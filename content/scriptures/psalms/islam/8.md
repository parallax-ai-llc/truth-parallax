Psalm 8's celebration of divine majesty and human dignity resonates powerfully with Quranic theology. The psalm's opening — "how majestic is your name in all the earth!" (8:1) — aligns with the Islamic glorification of God expressed in the *takbir* ("Allahu Akbar" — God is greater) and in the Quranic declaration: "To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth and whatever is within them" (5:120). The psalm's wonder at creation — "When I look at your heavens, the work of your fingers" (8:3) — corresponds to the many Quranic verses that invite contemplation of the natural world as signs (*ayat*) of God's power.

The question "What is man that you are mindful of him?" (8:4) and the answer — that God has given humanity dominion over creation — corresponds directly to the Quranic concept of *khilafah* (vicegerency). The Quran relates: "And when your Lord said to the angels, 'Indeed, I will make upon the earth a *khalifah* (vicegerent)'" (2:30). Humanity's role as God's representative on earth carries enormous responsibility in Islamic ethics — stewardship of the environment, just governance, and the establishment of *ma'ruf* (good) on earth are all dimensions of this vicegerency.

However, Islam carefully qualifies the psalm's language about human dignity to prevent any confusion with divine attributes. Human beings are *honored* (*mukarram*, Quran 17:70) by God's choice, not by any inherent divinity. The Islamic understanding of *tawhid* maintains an absolute distinction between Creator and creature. Whatever "glory and honor" humanity possesses is a gift of God's grace, not an inherent quality. The psalm's frame — beginning and ending with God's majesty — is read by Muslim scholars as correctly subordinating human dignity to divine sovereignty, a balance that Islam regards as essential.
