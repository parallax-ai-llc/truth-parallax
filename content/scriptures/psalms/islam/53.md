## The Denial of God as Moral Corruption

Psalm 53's opening — "The fool says in his heart, 'There is no God'" (v. 1) — describes what Islamic theology calls *kufr* (denial/covering of truth). The Quran identifies the fundamental human problem not as ignorance of God but as willful rejection of what is innately known. Every soul is created with *fitrah* — the natural recognition of God's existence and sovereignty — and the "fool" of this psalm is one who has suppressed that knowledge. Surah ar-Rum 30:30 declares, "So direct your face toward the religion, inclining to truth — the *fitrah* of Allah upon which He has created people."

The psalm's assertion that "there is none who does good, not even one" (v. 3) must be qualified in Islamic reading. Islam teaches that human nature is fundamentally sound though susceptible to corruption. The Quran affirms that humanity was created "in the best of stature" (Surah at-Tin 95:4) and that moral failure results from the rejection of divine guidance, not from an inherent defect in human nature. The psalm's sweeping condemnation may reflect prophetic frustration with a particular generation's corruption rather than a universal theological statement about human nature.

The concluding cry for salvation from Zion (v. 6) is understood by Muslim scholars within the framework of prophetic continuity. The deliverance that the psalmist longs for is the guidance that Allah has sent through successive prophets, culminating in the final revelation to Prophet Muhammad. The Quran teaches, "Indeed, We sent Our messengers with clear proofs and sent down with them the Scripture and the balance that the people may maintain justice" (Surah al-Hadid 57:25). The psalm's longing finds its answer, from the Islamic perspective, in the complete guidance of the Quran and Sunnah.
