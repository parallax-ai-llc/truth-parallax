## God's Triumph Over Falsehood

Psalm 68's opening — "God shall arise, his enemies shall be scattered" (v. 1) — expresses the certainty of divine victory that the Quran affirms through numerous narratives. The stories of Nuh (Noah), Ibrahim (Abraham), Musa (Moses), and Muhammad all demonstrate the same pattern: despite overwhelming opposition, God's truth prevails and His enemies are scattered. The Quran summarizes: "Allah has decreed, 'I will surely overcome, I and My messengers.' Indeed, Allah is Powerful and Exalted in Might" (Surah al-Mujadila 58:21).

The psalm's social vision — God as "father of the fatherless and protector of widows" (v. 5) — aligns with the Quran's extensive legislation protecting orphans and the vulnerable. The Quran warns severely, "Indeed, those who devour the property of orphans unjustly are only consuming fire, and they will be burned in a Blaze" (Surah an-Nisa 4:10). The Prophet Muhammad himself was an orphan, and his personal experience shaped Islamic law's emphatic protection of orphans' rights. The psalm's portrayal of God as defender of the defenseless resonates with the Islamic understanding of divine justice as fundamentally concerned with the most vulnerable members of society.

The psalm's reference to Sinai (v. 8) is significant for Islamic theology, which honors Sinai as the location where Allah spoke to Prophet Musa (Moses). The Quran refers to "the Mount" (*at-Tur*) as a sacred place (Surah at-Tur 52:1), and the theophany at Sinai is presented as one of the great moments of divine-human encounter in prophetic history. While Muslim scholars note that the present text of the psalm may have been altered from its original form, the core themes of divine triumph, care for the vulnerable, and the Sinai revelation are affirmed as reflecting authentic prophetic tradition.
