Psalm 38's anguished cry — "LORD, do not rebuke me in your anger" (38:1) — expresses the awareness of divine judgment that Islam calls *khashyah* (reverential fear). The Quran describes believers as those "who fear their Lord in the unseen and who are apprehensive of the Hour [of Judgment]" (21:49). This holy fear is not paralyzing terror but the motivation for sincere repentance and moral vigilance.

The vivid description of suffering — "My guilt has overwhelmed me like a burden too heavy to bear" (38:4) — illustrates the Islamic concept of sin as a weight upon the soul. The Quran uses this same metaphor: "Did We not expand for you your breast? And We removed from you your burden (*wizr*) which had weighed upon your back" (94:1-3). Sin burdens the soul; repentance and divine mercy lift that burden. Islam teaches that Allah's forgiveness is comprehensive: He removes the weight completely.

The psalm's closing appeal — "Come quickly to help me, my Lord and my Savior" (38:22) — demonstrates the urgency of *dua* that Islam values highly. The Prophet Muhammad taught that no one should say, "O Allah, forgive me if You will" but should be definite in asking (Bukhari). Islamic supplication is characterized by directness, persistence, and the certainty that Allah hears and responds — qualities this psalm models with striking clarity.
