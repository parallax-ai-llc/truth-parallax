## The Soul's Thirst for the Divine

Psalm 63's passionate longing — "my soul thirsts for you; my flesh faints for you, as in a dry and weary land" (v. 1) — expresses an intensity of devotion that Islamic mysticism (*tasawwuf*) affirms as the highest aspiration of the human heart. The great Sufi poet Rumi wrote, "The thirst for the Beloved is the only water that can quench the soul." While mainstream Islamic theology cautions against language that blurs the distinction between Creator and creature, it affirms that the soul's deepest longing is directed toward Allah, the source of all satisfaction.

The declaration "your steadfast love is better than life" (v. 3) finds a parallel in the Islamic concept of *hubb Allah* (love of God) that takes priority over all worldly attachments. The Quran describes believers as "those who love Allah most" (Surah al-Baqarah 2:165), and the Prophet Muhammad taught, "None of you truly believes until I am more beloved to him than his parent, his child, and all of mankind" (Sahih al-Bukhari). This psalm's radical prioritization of divine love over life itself echoes the willingness of the early Muslims to sacrifice everything — wealth, family, homeland — for the sake of Allah.

The nocturnal meditation described in verse 6 — "I meditate on you in the watches of the night" — corresponds directly to the voluntary night prayer (*Tahajjud* or *Qiyam al-Layl*) that holds special status in Islam. The Quran commands the Prophet: "And during the night, perform Tahajjud as additional prayer — perhaps your Lord will raise you to a praised station" (Surah al-Isra 17:79). The night hours are considered especially conducive to intimacy with Allah, and this psalm's description of nocturnal contemplation reflects a practice that transcends religious boundaries and speaks to a universal human capacity for encountering God in the stillness of the night.
