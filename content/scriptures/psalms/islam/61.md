## The Elevated Rock of Divine Refuge

Psalm 61's plea — "lead me to the rock that is higher than I" (v. 2) — expresses the human recognition that divine strength transcends all human capacity. In Islamic theology, this corresponds to the acknowledgment embedded in every *rak'ah* of prayer: the prostration (*sujud*) in which the worshiper places the highest part of the body (the forehead) on the lowest ground, acknowledging that Allah alone is exalted. The psalm's "rock" is not a created being but the Creator Himself, and the plea for guidance toward that rock mirrors the Quranic prayer "Guide us to the straight path" (Surah al-Fatihah 1:6).

The prayer for the king (vv. 6-7) is evaluated by Muslim scholars through the principle that legitimate sovereignty derives from God alone. The Quran teaches, "Say, 'O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will'" (Surah Ali 'Imran 3:26). While Islam respects the institution of righteous leadership, it insists that no human authority is absolute — all earthly rule is delegated and contingent upon obedience to divine command. The psalm's idealized king finds his truest expression, in Islamic reading, in the just ruler (*imam 'adil*) who governs by *Shariah* (divine law).

The psalm's integration of praise and daily obedience — "I will ever sing praises to your name, as I perform my vows day after day" (v. 8) — mirrors the Islamic framework of *'ibadah* (worship) that encompasses all of life. Islam does not confine worship to ritual moments but extends it to every action performed with the intention of pleasing Allah. The psalm's "day after day" vows correspond to the Muslim's daily renewal of submission to Allah through the five prayers, the recitation of the Quran, and the conscious performance of good deeds.
