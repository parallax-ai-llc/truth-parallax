Psalm 25's prayer for guidance and forgiveness encompasses two of the most central themes in Islamic spirituality: *hidayah* (divine guidance) and *tawbah* (repentance/turning to God). The plea "Make me to know your ways, O Lord; teach me your paths" (25:4) echoes the fundamental request of every *rak'ah* in the Muslim prayer: *"Ihdina al-sirat al-mustaqim"* — "Guide us to the straight path" (Quran 1:6). Islamic theology teaches that guidance is ultimately God's gift, not a human achievement, and that the believer must continuously seek it through prayer, study, and righteous living.

The psalm's plea for forgiveness — "Remember not the sins of my youth or my transgressions" (25:7) — aligns with the Islamic teaching on *tawbah* (repentance). The Quran promises: "Say, O My servants who have transgressed against themselves, do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins. Indeed, it is He who is the Forgiving, the Merciful" (39:53). The Prophet Muhammad said: "Allah is more pleased with the repentance of His servant than one of you who finds his lost camel in a barren land" (*Sahih Muslim*). Islam teaches that sincere repentance not only erases sins but can transform them into good deeds (Quran 25:70).

The verse "The friendship of the Lord is for those who fear him" (25:14) resonates with the Quranic concept of *wilayah* (divine friendship or patronage). The Quran identifies the *awliya' Allah* (friends of God): "Verily, the friends of Allah — no fear shall be upon them, nor shall they grieve. Those who believed and were God-conscious" (10:62-63). In Islamic spirituality, particularly in the Sufi tradition, the attainment of divine friendship is the highest spiritual station, achieved through a combination of outward obedience to the *Shariah* and inward purification of the heart. The psalm's promise that God "makes known his covenant" to those who fear Him corresponds to the Islamic teaching that spiritual knowledge (*ma'rifah*) is granted to those whose hearts have been purified through worship and moral discipline.
