## The Just Ruler and Universal Peace

Psalm 72's portrait of an ideal king who "judges your people with righteousness and your poor with justice" (v. 2) resonates deeply with the Islamic model of just governance. The Quran commands rulers to judge with justice: "Indeed, Allah orders you to render trusts to whom they are due and when you judge between people to judge with justice" (Surah an-Nisa 4:58). The Prophet Muhammad established a model of governance in Medina that prioritized justice for the weak and protection of the vulnerable — precisely the qualities this psalm attributes to its ideal king.

The psalm's social vision — "He delivers the needy when he calls... precious is their blood in his sight" (vv. 12, 14) — aligns with the Islamic principle that the ruler's primary responsibility is to protect the most vulnerable members of society. The second Caliph, Umar ibn al-Khattab, famously said, "If a mule stumbles on the banks of the Euphrates, I fear that Allah will ask me why I did not pave the road for it." This extreme sense of accountability for every creature under one's care echoes the psalm's vision of a king for whom every life is precious.

The psalm's universal scope — "May he have dominion from sea to sea, and from the River to the ends of the earth" (v. 8) — expresses an aspiration that Islamic eschatology associates with the coming of the Mahdi and the return of Prophet Isa (Jesus), who will establish justice on the earth. While Islam does not identify this psalm's king with any particular figure in Islamic prophecy, the vision of universal justice, peace, and divine worship that it describes is compatible with the Islamic expectation of a final era of righteousness before the Day of Judgment. The doxology (vv. 18-19) praising the God "who alone does wondrous things" is fully compatible with Islamic *tawhid* and could be prayed by any Muslim without reservation.
