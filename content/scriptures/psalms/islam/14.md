Psalm 14's opening declaration — "The fool says in his heart, 'There is no God'" (14:1) — addresses what Islamic theology terms *kufr* (disbelief or ingratitude). The Arabic root *k-f-r* means "to cover," suggesting that the disbeliever covers or conceals the innate knowledge of God (*fitrah*) that Islam teaches is implanted in every human soul. The Quran records the primordial covenant (*mithaq*) in which all human souls testified to God's lordship: "Am I not your Lord? They said, 'Yes, we have testified'" (7:172). Denial of God thus requires actively suppressing what the soul already knows.

The universal indictment — "there is none who does good, not even one" (14:3) — is read in Islamic perspective as a description of specific corrupt societies rather than an absolute statement about human nature. Islamic anthropology is more optimistic than some traditions: human beings are created in the best form (*ahsan taqwim*, Quran 95:4) with a natural disposition (*fitrah*) toward goodness and God-consciousness. Sin corrupts this original nature but does not destroy it. Every person retains the capacity to repent and return to God, and the door of *tawbah* (repentance) remains open until the moment of death.

The psalm's hope for salvation "out of Zion" (14:7) is read by Muslim scholars within the framework of prophetic succession. God's salvation comes through the chain of prophets — from Ibrahim through Musa, Dawud, and Isa to Muhammad (peace be upon them all). Each prophet renewed the original message of *tawhid* for his generation. The final and complete expression of this salvation came through the Prophet Muhammad and the Quran, which the Islamic tradition regards as God's definitive guidance for humanity. The psalm's longing for divine intervention is thus understood as a longing fulfilled in the succession of prophetic revelation.
