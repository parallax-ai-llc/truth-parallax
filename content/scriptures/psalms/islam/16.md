Psalm 16's expression of trust and satisfaction in God alone resonates with the Islamic concept of *ridha* (contentment with God's decree). The declaration "I say to the Lord, 'You are my Lord; I have no good apart from you'" (16:2) aligns with the Islamic principle that all good comes from Allah alone: "Whatever you have of favor, it is from Allah" (Quran 16:53). The spiritual satisfaction described in this psalm — finding one's portion and inheritance in God rather than in material possessions — is the state the Quran calls *itmi'nan* (tranquility of the soul): "Those who have believed and whose hearts are assured by the remembrance of Allah — verily, in the remembrance of Allah do hearts find rest" (13:28).

The affirmation that God is the psalmist's "chosen portion" and "cup" (16:5) corresponds to the Islamic understanding that the believer's ultimate inheritance is Paradise (*Jannah*) and, beyond even Paradise, the pleasure of God (*ridwan Allah*). The Quran describes this supreme reward: "And the greatest reward is the pleasure of Allah. That is the great attainment" (9:72). Material blessings in this world are temporary provisions; the eternal inheritance is God's favor and the abode of peace.

The promise "you will not abandon my soul to Sheol, or let your holy one see corruption" (16:10) is understood in Islamic eschatology within the framework of the afterlife. Islam teaches that the souls of the righteous are in comfort after death, experiencing a foretaste of Paradise in the *barzakh* (the intermediate realm between death and resurrection). The Prophet Muhammad taught that the grave is either "a garden from the gardens of Paradise or a pit from the pits of Hell" (*al-Tirmidhi*). The psalm's assurance of divine preservation beyond death resonates with this teaching, though Islam attributes such preservation to all righteous believers, not to a single messianic figure.
