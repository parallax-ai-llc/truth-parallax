## Turning to God After Communal Defeat

Psalm 60's lament over military defeat — "O God, you have rejected us, broken our defenses" (v. 1) — resonates with the Quranic treatment of the Muslim community's experience after the Battle of Uhud, where the believers suffered a painful setback. The Quran addresses this defeat directly: "Why, when a single disaster struck you — although you had struck with one twice as great — did you say, 'From where is this?' Say, 'It is from yourselves'" (Surah Ali 'Imran 3:165). Both psalm and Quran interpret communal defeat as a divine test that calls for self-examination and renewed faith, not despair.

The psalm's divine oracle (vv. 6-8), claiming sovereignty over the territories, expresses a principle that Islamic theology affirms absolutely: all sovereignty belongs to Allah alone. The Quran declares, "To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth and whatever is within them" (Surah al-Ma'idah 5:120). No territory, nation, or people exists outside divine sovereignty. While the psalm's specific territorial claims relate to ancient Israel, the theological principle that God governs all lands and peoples is fully compatible with Islamic *tawhid*.

The psalm's conclusion — "With God we shall do valiantly, for it is he who will tread down our foes" (v. 12) — combines human effort with divine reliance in a way that resonates with the Islamic concept of *tawakkul* balanced with *amal* (action). The Prophet Muhammad taught the famous principle: "Tie your camel and then trust in Allah" (Tirmidhi) — human effort and divine trust are not contradictory but complementary. This psalm's integration of valiant human action with confidence in divine victory exemplifies that balance.
