Psalm 37's counsel — "Do not fret because of those who are evil" (37:1) — addresses the question of evil's apparent prosperity, which the Quran also confronts directly: "Let not the movement of the disbelievers throughout the land deceive you. It is but a brief enjoyment; then their ultimate refuge is Hell" (3:196-197). Islamic theology is emphatic: the prosperity of the wicked is temporary, and the *akhirah* (hereafter) will reveal the true balance of justice.

The psalm's instruction to "trust in the LORD and do good" (37:3) encapsulates the Islamic integration of *tawakkul* (trust in Allah) and *amal salih* (righteous action). The Quran consistently pairs faith (*iman*) with good works: "Those who believe and do righteous deeds — they are the best of creatures" (98:7). Islam rejects both quietistic trust without effort and self-reliant effort without trust; the believer must combine inner reliance on Allah with external obedience.

The promise that "the meek will inherit the land" (37:11) is echoed in the Quran: "We wrote in the Zabur, after the reminder, that the earth will be inherited by My righteous servants" (21:105). This Quranic verse explicitly references the Zabur (the scripture given to Dawud) and Psalm 37, making it one of the clearest links between the Psalms and the Quran. The righteous — defined by humility, justice, and faith — are promised ultimate inheritance of the earth.
