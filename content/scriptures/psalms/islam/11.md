Psalm 11's declaration of trust despite counsel to flee resonates with the Islamic virtue of *tawakkul* (reliance on God). The Quran teaches: "And whoever relies upon Allah — then He is sufficient for him" (65:3). The Prophet Muhammad demonstrated this trust most dramatically during the *hijrah*, when he hid in the Cave of Thawr with Abu Bakr. When Abu Bakr expressed fear, the Prophet said: "Do not grieve; indeed, Allah is with us" (Quran 9:40) — an echo of the psalm's refusal to flee in panic.

The statement "The Lord tests the righteous" (11:5) aligns with the Quranic teaching on *ibtila'* (divine testing): "Do the people think that they will be left to say, 'We believe,' and they will not be tested?" (29:2). Islamic theology understands trials as a means of spiritual purification and elevation, not as punishment. The Prophet Muhammad said: "The greatest reward comes with the greatest trial" (*al-Tirmidhi*). The psalm's distinction between testing the righteous and judging the wicked corresponds to the Islamic understanding that the same event can be a *bala'* (test leading to reward) for the believer and a *'adhab* (punishment) for the disbeliever.

The promise that "the upright shall behold his face" (11:7) is understood in Islamic theology through the lens of the *ru'yah* (vision of God) in the Hereafter. Mainstream Sunni theology, based on the hadith "You will see your Lord as you see the full moon" (*Sahih al-Bukhari*) and the Quranic verse "Some faces, that Day, will be radiant, looking at their Lord" (75:22-23), affirms that the believers will see Allah in Paradise. This vision is considered the greatest reward of Paradise, surpassing even its physical pleasures — a teaching that resonates remarkably with the psalm's climactic promise.
