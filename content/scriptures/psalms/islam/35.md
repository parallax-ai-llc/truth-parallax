Psalm 35's appeal for divine justice — "Contend, LORD, with those who contend with me" (35:1) — aligns with the Islamic principle that ultimate justice (*adl*) belongs to Allah. The Quran declares Allah to be *al-Adl* (the Just) and *al-Hakam* (the Judge), who will establish perfect justice on the Day of Judgment. Islam teaches believers to seek justice through lawful means while entrusting final vindication to Allah.

The psalmist's account of repaying kindness with treachery (35:12-14) resonates with the Quranic accounts of prophets betrayed by their own communities. Nuh (Noah), Hud, Salih, Shu'ayb, Musa (Moses), and Muhammad himself all experienced the ingratitude described in this psalm. The Quran's repeated narration of prophetic rejection teaches that faithfulness to God often provokes hostility from those who benefit from injustice.

David's restraint — appealing to God rather than taking personal vengeance — models the Islamic ethic regarding retaliation. The Quran states: "The repayment of a bad action is one equivalent to it. But whoever pardons and makes reconciliation — his reward is with Allah" (42:40). While Islam permits measured self-defense, forgiveness is presented as the higher path. This psalm shows Dawud choosing the path of appeal to divine justice over personal retribution.
