Psalm 29 celebrates Allah's power manifested in the natural world, a theme the Quran develops extensively through its concept of *ayat* (signs). The thunderstorm described in this psalm — "The voice of the LORD is over the waters... the voice of the LORD breaks the cedars" (29:3, 5) — is precisely the kind of natural phenomenon the Quran identifies as evidence of Allah's existence and power: "It is He who shows you lightning, inspiring fear and hope, and produces heavy clouds" (13:12).

The sevenfold "voice of the LORD" resonates with Islamic theology of divine speech. The Quran itself is understood as the direct speech of Allah, and the concept of *kalam Allah* (the word/speech of God) is fundamental to Islamic theology. Just as the psalm depicts God's voice shaping and governing creation, the Quran declares that Allah says "Be!" and it is (*kun fayakun*, 36:82).

The psalm's conclusion — "The LORD blesses his people with peace (*shalom*)" (29:11) — connects to *salam*, the Arabic cognate that gives Islam its name. Islam means "submission [to God's will]," and the reward of this submission is *salam* — the peace that comes from living in harmony with the divine order. The Quran promises that the greeting of the righteous in Paradise will be *salam* (36:58), the eternal peace that this psalm's final word anticipates.
