## Divine Reckoning for Unjust Rulers

Psalm 58's confrontation of corrupt judges — "Do you indeed decree what is right, you gods?" (v. 1) — resonates powerfully with the Quran's emphasis on just governance. The Quran commands, "Indeed, Allah orders justice and good conduct" (Surah an-Nahl 16:90), and the Prophet Muhammad warned, "Each of you is a shepherd and each of you is responsible for his flock" (Sahih al-Bukhari). Islamic political theology holds rulers to the highest standard of accountability, and this psalm's indictment of those who pervert justice aligns with the Quranic denunciation of oppression (*zulm*).

The psalm's vivid judgment imagery — serpents, dissolved snails, stillborn children (vv. 4-8) — employs metaphorical language that Islamic exegesis finds parallel in the Quran's own graphic descriptions of divine punishment. The Quran warns that on the Day of Judgment, "the earth will shine with the light of its Lord, and the record will be placed, and the prophets and witnesses will be brought" (Surah az-Zumar 39:69). Both psalm and Quran insist that injustice is not permanent — divine reckoning is certain, even if its timing is in God's hands.

The psalm's conclusion — "Surely there is a God who judges on earth" (v. 11) — affirms the Islamic conviction that Allah's justice operates both in this world and in the *Akhirah* (Hereafter). While full justice may await the Day of Judgment (*Yawm al-Qiyamah*), the Quran also teaches that God intervenes in history to punish oppressive nations. The stories of 'Ad, Thamud, and Pharaoh illustrate this principle: divine judgment falls upon unjust rulers and civilizations within historical time, vindicating the truth of what this psalm declares.
