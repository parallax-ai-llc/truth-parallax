Psalm 39's meditation on life's brevity — "You have made my days a mere handbreadth" (39:5) — parallels the Quranic teaching on the transience of *dunya* (worldly life). The Quran declares: "Know that the life of this world is but amusement and diversion... and what is the life of this world except the enjoyment of delusion?" (57:20). Islam does not despise the created world but insists that attachment to its fleeting pleasures is spiritual blindness.

The psalmist's declaration that "everyone is but a breath" (39:5) uses language remarkably similar to the Quran's description of human life: "Every soul will taste death, and you will only be given your full compensation on the Day of Resurrection" (3:185). Islamic eschatology provides the framework that gives meaning to earthly transience: this life is a test (*ibtila*), and the *akhirah* is the permanent abode where deeds receive their true recompense.

David's self-identification as "a foreigner and a stranger" (39:12) resonates with the Prophet Muhammad's teaching: "Be in this world as though you were a stranger or a traveler" (Bukhari). Islamic spirituality, particularly the tradition of *zuhd* (ascetic detachment), cultivates this sense of being a pilgrim through temporal existence. The goal is not to reject the world but to hold it lightly, maintaining focus on the eternal destination.
