## The Urgency of Supplication

Psalm 70's urgent cry — "Make haste, O God, to deliver me!" (v. 1) — exemplifies the kind of fervent *du'a* (supplication) that Islam values highly. The Prophet Muhammad taught, "Call upon Allah while being certain of being answered, and know that Allah does not respond to a supplication from a heart that is heedless" (Tirmidhi). The psalm's brevity and intensity model the authentic urgency that transforms routine prayer into genuine spiritual communication. The Quran promises, "And when My servants ask you concerning Me — indeed I am near. I respond to the invocation of the supplicant when he calls upon Me" (Surah al-Baqarah 2:186).

The psalm's self-identification as "poor and needy" (v. 5) corresponds to the Islamic concept of *faqr* (spiritual poverty) — the recognition that the human being is utterly dependent on Allah for everything. The Quran declares, "O mankind, you are those in need of Allah, while Allah is the Free of need, the Praiseworthy" (Surah Fatir 35:15). This awareness of fundamental need is not humiliation but liberation — it frees the believer from the exhausting pretense of self-sufficiency and opens the door to receiving divine generosity.

The prayer that those who seek God may "rejoice and be glad" (v. 4) while the wicked are put to shame reflects the Islamic eschatological vision of *Yawm al-Qiyamah* (the Day of Resurrection), when the ultimate distinction between believers and deniers will be made manifest. The Quran describes this division vividly: "Some faces, that Day, will be bright, laughing, rejoicing at good news. And other faces, that Day, will have upon them dust, blackness will cover them" (Surah 'Abasa 80:38-41). The psalm anticipates, in miniature, the great separation that Islamic eschatology presents as the culmination of history.
