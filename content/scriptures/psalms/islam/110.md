## A Contested Psalm

Psalm 110 is among the most theologically contested texts between Islam and Christianity. Christian tradition reads it as a prophecy of Christ's divine priesthood; Islamic theology categorically rejects both the divinity of Jesus (*Isa*) and the concept of a divine-human priesthood.

## Islamic Response

### Rejection of Divine Sonship

The psalm's language of "my Lord" sitting at God's right hand is read by Islamic scholars as incompatible with *tawhid*. The Quran states: "It is not befitting for Allah to take a son. Exalted is He!" (Quran 19:35). Any interpretation that implies a second divine person is considered *shirk* (associating partners with God).

### Melchizedek in Islamic Tradition

Some Islamic scholars identify Melchizedek with Khidr or with Shem son of Nuh (Noah). The concept of an eternal priesthood, however, has no parallel in Islamic theology, which recognizes prophets and messengers but not a permanent priestly caste mediating between God and humanity.

### Isa (Jesus) in Islam

Islam honors Isa as a prophet and messenger, born of the Virgin Maryam, but denies his crucifixion, resurrection, and divinity. The Christological reading of Psalm 110 is therefore rejected entirely. "The Messiah, son of Maryam, was not but a messenger" (Quran 5:75).

## Theological Significance

- **Tawhid**: All themes are read through the lens of God's absolute oneness
- **Prophetic continuity**: The psalm's context within the chain of prophets
- **Quranic authority**: The Quran serves as the criterion for evaluating biblical texts
- **Ethical guidance**: Moral teachings that accord with Islamic values
