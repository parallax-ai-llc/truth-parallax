Psalm 28 presents a model of *dua* (supplication) that Islam affirms as the essence of worship. The Prophet Muhammad declared that "supplication is the very essence of worship" (Tirmidhi), and David's urgent cry — "Hear my cry for mercy as I call to you for help" (28:2) — demonstrates the humility and desperation that characterize sincere *dua*. Islam teaches that Allah is *al-Mujeeb* (the Responsive), who answers the prayer of the supplicant when they call (2:186).

The psalm's concern with the wicked who "speak cordially with their neighbors but harbor malice in their hearts" (28:3) aligns with the Quranic condemnation of hypocrisy (*nifaq*). The Quran devotes extensive attention to the *munafiqun* (hypocrites), whose outward show of faith conceals inner disbelief (63:1-4). Islam considers hypocrisy among the gravest spiritual dangers, and the Prophet warned that the signs of a hypocrite include speaking falsehood, breaking promises, and betraying trust (Bukhari).

The psalm's closing praise — "The LORD is my strength and my shield" (28:7) — mirrors the Islamic practice of relying on Allah as protector. The opening chapter of the Quran, *al-Fatiha*, petitions Allah for guidance and protection, while the closing chapters (*al-Falaq* and *al-Nas*) seek refuge in Allah from all forms of evil. This pattern of seeking divine protection pervades Islamic devotional life, just as it pervades the Psalms of Dawud.
