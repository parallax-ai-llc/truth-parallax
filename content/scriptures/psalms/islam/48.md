Psalm 48 celebrates God's holy city as "the joy of the whole earth" (48:2). While Islam does not accord the same theological significance to Jerusalem as Judaism does, *al-Quds* (the Holy) holds a unique place in Islamic tradition as the site of the Prophet Muhammad's Night Journey (*Isra*) and Ascension (*Mi'raj*). The Quran describes the journey "from al-Masjid al-Haram to al-Masjid al-Aqsa, whose surroundings We have blessed" (17:1).

The psalm's account of enemy kings who "fled in terror" (48:5) at the sight of God's city parallels the Quranic narratives of communities that rejected prophetic warnings and were destroyed. The Quran recounts the fates of 'Ad, Thamud, and Pharaoh's people as warnings to those who oppose Allah's purposes. The security of God's city is not in its walls but in divine protection — a principle Islam applies to the *ummah* (community of believers) as a whole.

The closing instruction — "tell it to the next generation" (48:13) — resonates with the Islamic emphasis on intergenerational transmission of faith. The Quran charges parents with their children's spiritual education, and the Prophet Muhammad emphasized teaching children prayer from age seven. Islamic civilization developed extensive educational institutions — *madrasas*, *kuttabs*, and circles of knowledge — to ensure that each generation received the heritage of faith from the previous one.
