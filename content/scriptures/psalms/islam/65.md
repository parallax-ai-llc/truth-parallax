## The Creator's Generous Provision

Psalm 65's celebration of God as both forgiver of sins (v. 3) and provider of rain and harvest (vv. 9-13) reflects a unity that Islamic theology affirms absolutely. Allah is simultaneously *al-Ghaffar* (the Forgiver) and *al-Razzaq* (the Provider), and these attributes are not separate functions but unified expressions of divine generosity. The Quran repeatedly connects natural provision with divine mercy: "And it is He who sends the winds as good tidings before His mercy, and We send down from the sky pure water" (Surah al-Furqan 25:48).

The psalm's creation theology — "You still the roaring of the seas... the tumult of the peoples" (v. 7) — affirms God's sovereignty over both natural and human chaos, a principle central to Islamic *tawhid*. The Quran invites humanity to contemplate creation as evidence of God's power: "Indeed, in the creation of the heavens and the earth and the alternation of the night and the day are signs for those of understanding" (Surah Ali 'Imran 3:190). This psalm models such contemplation, moving from natural observation to theological praise.

The abundant harvest imagery — "the valleys deck themselves with grain, they shout and sing together for joy" (v. 13) — is received in Islamic reading as a celebration of God's *ni'mah* (blessings) that demand *shukr* (gratitude). The Quran warns that ingratitude leads to loss of blessing: "If you are grateful, I will surely increase you; but if you deny — indeed, My punishment is severe" (Surah Ibrahim 14:7). The psalm's joyful description of abundance implicitly calls for the grateful response that Islam identifies as fundamental to the servant's relationship with the Lord.
