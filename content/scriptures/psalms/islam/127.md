## Overview

Psalm 127 — *Unless the Lord Builds* — contains 5 verses. In Islamic tradition, the *Zabur* (Psalms) is recognized as divine revelation given to the Prophet Dawud (David), peace be upon him. The Quran affirms: "And to Dawud We gave the Zabur" (Quran 4:163, 17:55). However, Muslim scholars maintain that the present biblical Psalter has undergone *tahrif* (textual alteration) and must be read critically in light of Quranic revelation.

## Quranic Connections

The themes of Psalm 127 — including divine sovereignty, praise, and human dependence on God — find resonance throughout the Quran. Islamic theology reads these themes through the lens of *tawhid* (absolute divine oneness), which serves as the criterion for evaluating all scriptural material.

Key Quranic parallels to Psalm 127's themes include:

- **Divine praise**: "Whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on the earth glorifies Allah" (Quran 59:24)
- **Trust in God**: "And whoever puts their trust in Allah, He is sufficient for them" (Quran 65:3)
- **God's sovereignty**: "To Allah belongs the dominion of the heavens and the earth" (Quran 3:189)

## Views of Major Commentators

**Al-Tabari** (d. 923) references the *Zabur* in his tafsir and historical works, noting that David was given a scripture of praise and supplication rather than legislative law. He preserves *Isra'iliyyat* traditions about David's devotional practices.

**Ibn Kathir** (d. 1373) takes a more cautious approach, affirming the divine origin of the *Zabur* while maintaining that its current form cannot be considered fully reliable. He emphasizes that any teaching in the Psalms must be evaluated against the Quran.

**Al-Ghazali** (d. 1111) in *Ihya Ulum al-Din* discusses the spiritual practices of David, including his fasting (alternating days) and his intense devotional life, as models for Islamic spirituality. The Psalms' themes of penitence and praise align with *dhikr* (remembrance of God).

## Theological Assessment

Islamic scholarship appreciates the devotional power of the Psalms while maintaining theological boundaries:

- Passages affirming God's oneness, power, and mercy are considered consistent with Quranic teaching
- Anthropomorphic language is reinterpreted or attributed to *tahrif*
- The Psalms' emphasis on praise (*hamd*) resonates deeply with Islamic worship, where *al-Hamd* opens the Quran itself
