Psalm 31 is profoundly significant in Orthodox worship because Christ spoke its words from the cross: "Into your hands I commit my spirit" (31:5; Luke 23:46). This verse is chanted at every Orthodox funeral and at Great Compline, transforming the act of dying — and the act of falling asleep — into an imitation of Christ's own self-surrender to the Father. Orthodox theology understands death not as annihilation but as *koimesis* — a falling asleep in the hands of God.

The psalm's description of extreme suffering — "I am the utter contempt of my neighbors... I am forgotten as though I were dead" (31:11-12) — is read through the lens of *kenosis*, Christ's self-emptying described in Philippians 2:7. Orthodox Christology emphasizes that the Logos experienced the fullness of human suffering, not merely its appearance. The icon of the Extreme Humility (*Akra Tapeinosis*) — Christ dead in the tomb — gives visual expression to the depths this psalm describes.

The confession "My times are in your hands" (31:15) expresses the Orthodox understanding of divine providence that permeates the liturgical calendar. Every feast and fast, every day and hour, is understood as belonging to God's economy of salvation. The Orthodox faithful are invited to receive each moment — joyful or painful — as providentially ordered for their salvation and *theosis* (divinization).
