Psalm 26 expresses the soul's desire for purity before approaching God's altar, a theme that resonates deeply with Orthodox liturgical theology. The psalmist's declaration, "I wash my hands in innocence and go around your altar, O LORD" (26:6), finds its liturgical echo in the Great Entrance of the Divine Liturgy, when the gifts are carried to the altar amid prayers of purification. Orthodox worship understands that approaching the Holy requires inner transformation.

The psalm's plea, "Vindicate me, LORD, for I have led a blameless life; I have trusted in the LORD and have not faltered" (26:1), is read by the Eastern Fathers not as self-justification but as *parrhesia* — the boldness that comes from a clear conscience maintained through ongoing repentance. Saint John Chrysostom taught that integrity before God is not sinlessness but consistent return to God through confession and amendment of life.

The psalmist's love for "the house where you live, the place where your glory dwells" (26:8) resonates with the Orthodox theology of sacred space. The church building, adorned with icons and sanctified by the Holy Mysteries, is understood as a participation in heavenly worship. The iconostasis, the incense, the chanting — all serve to manifest the glory that the psalmist yearns to behold.
