Psalm 17 (Psalm 16 in the Septuagint) is a prayer of the righteous sufferer whose ultimate hope is the vision of God. Orthodox tradition reads this psalm christologically: Christ is the supremely righteous one whose prayer is heard by the Father, whose innocence is proven by the resurrection, and whose vision of the Father is the eternal communion of the Trinity into which believers are invited. The psalm is also read as the voice of the persecuted Church throughout history — the Orthodox experience of suffering under Ottoman rule, Soviet persecution, and contemporary pressures gives this psalm immediate relevance.

The petition "Keep me as the apple of your eye; hide me in the shadow of your wings" (17:8) has been connected in Orthodox hymnography to the protection of the Theotokos. The wing imagery is applied to Mary's *maphorion* (veil), which shelters the faithful — a connection celebrated in the Feast of the Protection of the Theotokos (*Pokrov*), one of the most beloved feasts in the Slavic Orthodox tradition. The icon of the *Pokrov* depicts the Mother of God spreading her veil over the worshipping assembly, fulfilling the psalm's prayer for divine shelter.

The final verse — "when I awake, I shall be satisfied with your likeness" (17:15) — is a key text for the Orthodox doctrine of *theosis*. To be "satisfied with God's likeness" means to be transformed into the divine likeness — not absorption into God (which would be pantheism) but participation in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4) while remaining a distinct person. St. Athanasius's formula — "God became man so that man might become god" — finds scriptural support here. The "awakening" points to both the daily renewal of spiritual life and the eschatological resurrection, when the transformation will be complete.
