Psalm 7 presents David's appeal for divine justice, a theme that Orthodox theology treats with careful nuance. The oath of innocence in verses 3-5, where David invites God's judgment if he has committed the alleged wrongs, is understood by the Fathers not as boasting but as the legitimate cry of one falsely accused. St. John Chrysostom commented that David's willingness to be judged demonstrates true humility — only the genuinely innocent can stand before God's tribunal with such openness, and even then, their innocence is a gift of grace rather than personal achievement.

The image of God as righteous Judge (7:11) is central to Orthodox eschatology, which teaches both a particular judgment at death and a general judgment at the Second Coming. Orthodox iconography depicts the Last Judgment in monumental compositions on the western walls of churches, so that the faithful see it as they depart the liturgy. The psalm's language of divine testing (7:9, "you who test the minds and hearts, O righteous God") resonates with the Orthodox understanding that the judgment is not an external imposition but a revelation of what the soul has freely become through its choices in life.

The principle of self-recoiling evil (7:14-16) — the wicked falling into their own pit — is understood in Orthodox theology not as karmic retribution but as the natural consequence of turning away from the Source of life. Sin, in the Orthodox understanding following Maximus the Confessor, is fundamentally parasitic — it has no independent existence but corrupts what is good. When the wicked "conceive evil and are pregnant with mischief" (7:14), they are nurturing their own destruction, for evil ultimately consumes itself. The psalm's closing praise (7:17) affirms that God's justice, rightly understood, is itself a cause for thanksgiving.
