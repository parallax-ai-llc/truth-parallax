Psalm 20 (Psalm 19 in the Septuagint) is a royal intercessory psalm that Orthodox tradition applies to the Church's prayers for its leaders and for the triumph of the faith. In the Byzantine liturgical tradition, psalms for the king were adapted for use in prayers for the emperor and later for civil authorities, reflecting the Orthodox understanding that all authority is delegated by God and accountable to Him. The modern Orthodox practice of commemorating civil authorities in the litanies of the Divine Liturgy continues this tradition.

The declaration "Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the Lord our God" (20:7) is a principle deeply embedded in Orthodox consciousness through centuries of experience as a minority faith under hostile rule. The Orthodox churches that survived Ottoman domination, Soviet persecution, and various forms of marginalization did so not through military power but through faithfulness to the liturgy, the sacraments, and the name of the Lord. This verse has been cited by Orthodox martyrs and confessors as the foundation of their resistance to tyranny through spiritual means rather than armed force.

The psalm's intercessory character — the congregation praying for the king — reflects the Orthodox theology of corporate prayer. In Orthodox ecclesiology, the Church is a communion (*koinonia*) in which each member bears the burdens of all. The litanies of the Divine Liturgy, in which the deacon calls the congregation to pray "for the peace of the whole world, for the stability of the holy churches of God, and for the union of all," extend the psalm's intercessory vision to embrace the entire cosmos. Prayer is not a private transaction but a communal act that participates in Christ's own intercession before the Father.
