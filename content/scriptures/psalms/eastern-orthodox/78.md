## Sacred History as Liturgical Memory

The Orthodox Church reads Psalm 78 as *sacred history* (*hiera historia*) — not merely a record of past events but a living anamnesis that makes God's saving acts present in the worshipping community. The psalm's retelling of Israel's story from Exodus to David is incorporated into the Church's hymnographic tradition.

## Key Themes

### Transmission of the Faith (78:1-8)

The psalm's opening mandate to teach the next generation resonates with the Orthodox emphasis on Holy Tradition (*Paradosis*). The faith is not a dead letter but a living reality passed from generation to generation through liturgy, iconography, and the witness of the saints.

### Typological Reading (78:12-55)

The Fathers read the Exodus events typologically: the crossing of the Red Sea prefigures baptism, the manna prefigures the Eucharist, the rock that gave water prefigures Christ (1 Corinthians 10:4). St. Cyril of Jerusalem's *Mystagogical Catecheses* draw extensively on these parallels.

### The Election of Zion (78:65-72)

God's choice of Zion and David is read in Orthodox theology as a type of God's choice of the Church and Christ. David the shepherd-king prefigures Christ the Good Shepherd, whose icon adorns the apse of every Orthodox church.

## Theological Significance

- **Christological reading**: The Fathers hear Christ's voice in this psalm
- **Liturgical life**: The psalm shapes daily Orthodox worship
- **Theosis**: The psalm illuminates the path toward divine participation
- **Patristic continuity**: Orthodox interpretation stands in unbroken tradition
