## Humanity's Fall and God's Saving Initiative

Psalm 53's declaration that "the fool says in his heart, 'There is no God'" (v. 1) is read in Orthodox theology not primarily as a statement about philosophical atheism but about the practical godlessness that results from the fall. St. John Chrysostom preached that the true atheist is not the one who argues against God's existence but the one who lives as though God makes no difference — who acts without reference to divine truth and judgment. This reading situates the psalm within the Orthodox understanding of the *nous* — the spiritual faculty of perception that, when darkened by sin, loses its capacity to perceive God's reality.

The universal condemnation — "There is none who does good, not even one" (v. 3) — is understood in Orthodox anthropology through the lens of the fall's effects on human nature. While humanity retains the image of God (*eikon*), the likeness (*homoiosis*) has been distorted. The psalm does not describe an absolute incapacity for good (which would deny free will, a position the Orthodox Church rejects) but rather the comprehensive effects of sin that require divine healing through the Incarnation and the sacramental life.

The longing for salvation — "Oh, that salvation for Israel would come out of Zion!" (v. 6) — is read in the Orthodox Paschal liturgy as finding its answer in Christ's resurrection. The annual celebration of Pascha, the feast of feasts, is the Church's definitive response to this psalm's cry. God has indeed "restored the fortunes of his people" through the resurrection, and the Orthodox faithful celebrate this restoration with the joyful proclamation: "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life."
