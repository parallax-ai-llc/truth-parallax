Psalm 40 begins with patient waiting and culminates in a profound statement about sacrifice that Orthodox theology connects directly to the Incarnation. "Sacrifice and offering you did not desire — but my ears you have opened" (40:6). The Orthodox Fathers understood this as the eternal Son's willingness to take on human nature — to "open ears" of human flesh through which the divine Word could hear and respond to human suffering.

The declaration "Here I am, I have come — it is written about me in the scroll" (40:7) is read in the Orthodox lectionary as Christ's voice, spoken before the ages, consenting to the Father's plan of salvation. Orthodox Christology emphasizes the voluntary nature of the Incarnation — the Son was not compelled but freely chose to unite himself with human nature for its healing and divinization.

The psalm's movement between praise and petition (40:1-10, 11-17) mirrors the structure of Orthodox prayer, which constantly oscillates between thanksgiving and supplication. The faithful do not graduate from petition to pure praise but hold both in dynamic tension, as the psalm itself demonstrates. This pattern reflects the Orthodox understanding that even the saints continue to grow in grace, always receiving from God's inexhaustible fullness.
