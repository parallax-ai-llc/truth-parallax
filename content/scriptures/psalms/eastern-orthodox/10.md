Psalm 10 (part of the combined Psalm 9 in the Septuagint) wrestles with God's seeming absence amid the triumph of evil. The question "Why, O Lord, do you stand far away?" (10:1) is a cry that Orthodox theology takes seriously, not as a failure of faith but as an expression of authentic relationship with God. The great Orthodox spiritual writers — from the desert fathers to modern elders like St. Silouan of Athos — testify that the experience of God's hiddenness is a normal stage in the spiritual life, often a precursor to deeper communion.

The portrait of the wicked who says "There is no God" (10:4) in his heart is understood in Orthodox patristic thought as describing the condition of the *nous* (spiritual intellect) darkened by sin. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that the *nous* in its natural state perceives God's presence in all things, but when obscured by the passions, it loses this capacity and falls into practical atheism. The elaborate description of the wicked man's schemes (10:2-11) thus depicts not merely individual sin but the comprehensive corruption of the spiritual faculty designed to know God.

The psalm's resolution — "The Lord is king forever and ever" (10:16) — and its special attention to "the fatherless and the oppressed" (10:18) connect to the Orthodox understanding of Christ's kingship as expressed through solidarity with the suffering. The Orthodox icon of Christ "Extreme Humility" (*Akra Tapeinosis*) — depicting Christ dead or suffering — reveals that divine sovereignty is exercised through compassionate identification with the weakest. God's kingship is not exercised from a distance but from within the depths of human suffering, transforming it from the inside.
