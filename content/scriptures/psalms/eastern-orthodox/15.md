Psalm 15 (Psalm 14 in the Septuagint) asks who may dwell in God's presence and answers with a portrait of moral integrity. Orthodox theology reads this psalm within the framework of *theosis* — the character described is not merely ethical but iconic, reflecting the character of God Himself. The person who "walks blamelessly" (15:2) is being conformed to the divine likeness, and the ethical qualities listed are not a legal checklist but a description of what humanity looks like when the image of God is being restored through grace.

The Orthodox tradition places special emphasis on truthfulness in speech: "speaks truth in his heart" (15:2). The desert fathers taught that truthfulness is intimately connected to humility, for lying arises from the fear of being known as one truly is. St. Dorotheos of Gaza taught that every lie is an act of self-creation — constructing a false self rather than allowing God to reveal and heal the true self. The person who dwells on God's holy hill has abandoned this project of self-construction and stands before God and neighbor in transparency.

The promise of immovability — "He who does these things shall never be moved" (15:5b) — speaks to the Orthodox concept of *apatheia* (not apathy but freedom from the dominion of the passions). The person established on God's holy hill has achieved, through grace, a stability that is not rigidity but rootedness — like the tree of Psalm 1. This stability is the fruit of sustained ascetical effort (*askesis*) and divine cooperation, the goal of the monastic life and, in its own way, of every Orthodox Christian's baptismal calling. The psalm thus describes not an unattainable ideal but the real possibility opened up by grace for every human being.
