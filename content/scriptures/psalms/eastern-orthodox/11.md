Psalm 11 (Psalm 10 in the Septuagint) expresses the firm trust of the believer who refuses to flee when danger approaches. Orthodox spiritual theology reads the counsel to "flee like a bird to your mountain" (11:1) as the temptation of *acedia* (spiritual listlessness) — the desire to abandon one's spiritual post when difficulties arise. The desert fathers warned against the "noonday demon" who tempts monks to leave their cells, and this psalm's refusal to flee has been applied to the steadfastness required in prayer, monastic commitment, and daily Christian life.

The declaration "The Lord is in his holy temple" (11:4) has deep resonance in Orthodox liturgical theology. The Orthodox church building is understood as a microcosm of heaven and earth, with the iconostasis (icon screen) representing the boundary between the visible and invisible worlds. When the Orthodox faithful enter the temple for worship, they are entering the space where heaven and earth intersect — the Lord is genuinely present "in his holy temple" through the Eucharist, the icons, and the assembly of the faithful who constitute the living temple of the Holy Spirit.

The promise that "the upright shall behold his face" (11:7) is the scriptural foundation for the Orthodox doctrine of the vision of the uncreated light — the divine energies by which God makes Himself known to the saints. St. Gregory Palamas defended the hesychast monks of Mount Athos who claimed to see the uncreated light of Christ's Transfiguration through ascetical prayer. This vision of God's "face" is not a metaphor in Orthodox theology but a genuine experience of deification, in which the human person is illuminated by the same light that shone on Tabor. The psalm thus points to the ultimate goal of Orthodox spiritual life: seeing God.
