## Sheltered in God's Mercy

Psalm 57's opening plea — "Be merciful to me, O God, be merciful to me, for in you my soul takes refuge" (v. 1) — resonates with the Orthodox prayer tradition's constant refrain of *Kyrie eleison* (Lord, have mercy). The doubling of the plea for mercy expresses the intensity of need that Orthodox prayer acknowledges without embarrassment. St. John Chrysostom taught that repeated petition is not a sign of weak faith but of earnest desire — God delights in the persistence of those who seek His mercy.

The image of refuge "in the shadow of your wings" (v. 1) connects to Orthodox iconography's depiction of Christ as the sheltering mother hen (Matthew 23:37) and to the cherubim whose wings overshadow the mercy seat. Orthodox churches are adorned with icons that express this same theology of divine shelter — the faithful enter the temple, surrounded by images of saints and angels, and find themselves enfolded in the communion of heaven. The psalm's cave setting is read as a type of the tomb from which Christ rose, transforming the place of hiding into a place of glory.

The psalm's cosmic praise — "Be exalted, O God, above the heavens! Let your glory be over all the earth!" (vv. 5, 11) — expresses the Orthodox understanding of *doxologia*, the glorification of God that is the purpose of all creation. The Great Doxology sung at Sunday Matins echoes this psalm's expansive vision. The psalmist's "steadfast heart" (v. 7) and morning praise — "I will awake the dawn!" (v. 8) — mirror the Orthodox practice of rising before dawn for Matins, greeting the new day with praise as a participation in Christ's resurrection, which the dawn symbolically represents.
