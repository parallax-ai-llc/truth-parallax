## The Messianic Priesthood

Psalm 110 is central to Orthodox Christology and eucharistic theology. The psalm's declaration of an eternal priesthood "after the order of Melchizedek" is understood as a prophetic revelation of Christ's unique priesthood, which is made present in every celebration of the Divine Liturgy.

## Key Themes

### "The Lord said to my Lord" (110:1)

The Orthodox Fathers, following the New Testament, read this as the Father addressing the Son. This verse is foundational to the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed's affirmation that Christ "is seated at the right hand of the Father." The icon of the Ascension depicts this enthronement.

### The Priesthood of Christ (110:4)

Christ's priesthood after Melchizedek's order is permanent and non-transferable. In Orthodox theology, the ordained priesthood does not replace Christ's priesthood but participates in it through the mystery of ordination. Every priest serves *in persona Christi* at the holy altar.

### Eschatological Victory (110:5-7)

The psalm's martial imagery is read eschatologically — Christ's victory over death, sin, and the powers of darkness. The Paschal troparion ("Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death") echoes this psalm's confidence in ultimate divine triumph.

## Theological Significance

- **Christological reading**: The Fathers hear Christ's voice in this psalm
- **Liturgical life**: The psalm shapes daily Orthodox worship
- **Theosis**: The psalm illuminates the path toward divine participation
- **Patristic continuity**: Orthodox interpretation stands in unbroken tradition
