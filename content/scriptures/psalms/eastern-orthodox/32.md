Psalm 32 is a psalm of forgiveness that Orthodox penitential practice has embraced for centuries. The opening beatitude — "Blessed is the one whose transgressions are forgiven" (32:1) — is chanted during the preparation for Holy Communion, reminding the faithful that they approach the chalice as forgiven sinners, not as the self-righteous. Orthodox theology insists that the awareness of forgiveness, not moral perfection, qualifies one for communion.

The vivid description of unconfessed sin — "When I kept silent, my bones wasted away" (32:3) — illustrates the Orthodox understanding that sin affects the whole person, body and soul. The sacrament of Confession (*Exomologesis*) addresses this holistic damage: the penitent confesses before the icon of Christ with the priest as witness, and absolution restores not merely juridical standing but spiritual and even physical well-being.

God's promise to "instruct you and teach you in the way you should go; I will counsel you with my loving eye on you" (32:8) resonates with the Orthodox tradition of spiritual direction (*starets* or *geronda*). The experienced elder guides the disciple not by imposing rules but by discerning God's will through prayer and wisdom — watching over the soul with the same loving attentiveness that God promises in this psalm.
