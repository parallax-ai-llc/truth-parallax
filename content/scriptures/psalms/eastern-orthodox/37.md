Psalm 37 is a wisdom psalm that Orthodox theology reads through the lens of patient endurance (*hypomonē*) and trust in divine providence. David's counsel — "Be still before the LORD and wait patiently for him" (37:7) — resonates with the hesychast tradition's emphasis on inner stillness (*hesychia*) as the prerequisite for encountering God. The restless anxiety that comes from watching the wicked prosper is healed by the deep stillness of prayer.

The promise that "the meek will inherit the land" (37:11) is central to Orthodox social ethics, which emphasize humility and self-emptying (*kenosis*) over assertiveness and self-promotion. Orthodox saints such as Seraphim of Sarov and Silouan of Mount Athos embodied this meekness — acquiring inner peace and thereby, as Seraphim taught, enabling "thousands around you to be saved."

The psalm's repeated assurance of the righteous person's ultimate vindication (37:17-20, 28-29, 37-40) is understood eschatologically in Orthodox theology. The Orthodox Church does not expect perfect justice in this age but looks toward the Kingdom of God, when all things will be made new. In the meantime, the faithful are called to imitate Christ's patient endurance, trusting that "the LORD loves the just and will not forsake his faithful ones" (37:28).
