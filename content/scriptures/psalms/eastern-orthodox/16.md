Psalm 16 (Psalm 15 in the Septuagint) is read in Orthodox tradition as both a personal expression of trust and a christological prophecy of the resurrection. The apostolic preaching at Pentecost (Acts 2:25-31) established the authoritative interpretation: David spoke prophetically of the Holy One who would not see corruption. The Orthodox Paschal liturgy draws on this psalm's imagery of life conquering death, and the troparion of Pascha — "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death" — is the liturgical fulfillment of verse 10.

The declaration "The Lord is my chosen portion and my cup" (16:5) carries profound eucharistic significance in Orthodox worship. The chalice of the Eucharist is the "cup" par excellence, and the Orthodox faithful who receive communion are participating in the reality this psalm describes — God Himself as their inheritance and portion. The Septuagint rendering of verse 5 uses the word *klēros* (lot, inheritance), from which the English word "clergy" derives, but Orthodox theology insists that every baptized Christian shares in this inheritance, not merely the ordained.

The climactic vision of verse 11 — "in your presence there is fullness of joy; at your right hand are pleasures forevermore" — describes the reality of *theosis* in its eschatological fullness. Orthodox theology teaches that the joy of divine communion begins in this life through prayer, sacraments, and virtue, but reaches its perfection in the age to come. The "pleasures forevermore" are not sensory gratifications but the inexhaustible delight of participating in God's own life — what St. Gregory of Nyssa called *epektasis*, the eternal progress into the infinite depths of God, where every discovery reveals further depths to explore.
