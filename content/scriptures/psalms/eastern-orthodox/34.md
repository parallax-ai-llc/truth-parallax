Psalm 34 holds a place of supreme importance in Orthodox Eucharistic theology. The verse "Taste and see that the LORD is good" (34:8) is chanted by the choir immediately before the faithful receive Holy Communion in the Divine Liturgy. This is not metaphor but sacramental reality: in the Eucharist, the faithful literally taste the goodness of the Lord, receiving the deified Body and Blood of Christ for the healing of soul and body.

The psalm's wisdom instruction — "Come, my children, listen to me; I will teach you the fear of the LORD" (34:11) — reflects the catechetical tradition of the Orthodox Church. The fear of the Lord is understood not as terror but as *awe* before the holy — the response of creatures who encounter the living God. This awe pervades Orthodox worship: the removal of shoes at the altar, the prostrations of Great Lent, the reverent reception of antidoron all express the fear that is the beginning of wisdom.

"The LORD is close to the brokenhearted" (34:18) speaks directly to the Orthodox emphasis on *penthos* — holy compunction or godly sorrow. The great ascetic writers, from Ephrem the Syrian to Isaac of Nineveh, taught that the broken heart is the heart most open to God's presence. This is not despair but the fertile ground in which divine consolation takes root and the soul begins its journey toward *theosis*.
