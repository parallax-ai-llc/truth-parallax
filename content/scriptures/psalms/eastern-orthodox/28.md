Psalm 28 begins with a cry to God as "my rock" (28:1) — imagery the Orthodox Fathers understood christologically. Christ is the rock from which living water flows (1 Corinthians 10:4), and the psalmist's appeal to the rock is an appeal to the eternal Logos who sustains all creation. The raising of hands "toward your Most Holy Place" (28:2) mirrors the priestly gesture in the Divine Liturgy during the anaphora.

The psalm's concern with the wicked — those "who speak cordially with their neighbors but harbor malice in their hearts" (28:3) — reflects the Orthodox emphasis on the heart (*kardia*) as the center of spiritual life. The Philokalia repeatedly teaches that the heart must be guarded and purified, for out of it flow the springs of life. Hypocrisy — the disconnect between lips and heart — is the antithesis of Orthodox spirituality.

The closing doxology, "The LORD is my strength and my shield; my heart trusts in him" (28:7), and the pastoral imagery of verse 9 — "be their shepherd and carry them forever" — are chanted in Orthodox worship as an affirmation of Christ's care for his flock through the ministry of the Church. The bishop as shepherd of his diocese embodies this psalm's vision of divine pastoral care mediated through the community of faith.
