## Illumination in the Sanctuary

Psalm 73's crisis — envy of the wicked who prosper while the righteous suffer — is resolved not through philosophical argument but through liturgical encounter: "until I went into the sanctuary of God; then I discerned their end" (v. 17). Orthodox theology finds this profoundly significant. The sanctuary is the place of *photismos* (illumination), the second stage of the spiritual life after purification. Within the sacred space of worship, the *nous* (spiritual intellect) is healed and enabled to perceive reality as God sees it. The psalm thus validates the Orthodox conviction that theology is done properly not in the academy alone but in the worshiping community.

The psalm's near-despair — "my feet had almost stumbled" (v. 2) — is recognized by Orthodox spiritual writers as a common experience on the path toward God. St. John Climacus, in his *Ladder of Divine Ascent*, describes stages of discouragement and near-apostasy that monks encounter, and this psalm provides scriptural language for navigating such crises. The key is not to resolve the crisis through reason alone but to bring it into the sanctuary, where God's perspective replaces the distorted vision produced by the passions.

The psalm's climactic confession — "Whom have I in heaven but you? And there is nothing on earth that I desire besides you" (v. 25) — has been cherished in Orthodox monastic tradition as the quintessential expression of apatheia (freedom from disordered passions) and divine eros. St. Symeon the New Theologian heard in these words the soul's attainment of union with God, where all earthly attachments are transcended not through rejection but through fulfillment in the incomparably greater reality of divine love. The psalm's final declaration — "it is good to be near God" (v. 28) — summarizes the entire Orthodox understanding of salvation as proximity to and participation in the divine life.
