Psalm 48 celebrates Zion with language that Orthodox theology applies to the Church and, ultimately, to the heavenly Jerusalem. "Great is the LORD, and most worthy of praise, in the city of our God, his holy mountain" (48:1). Orthodox ecclesiology understands the local parish as a manifestation of the heavenly city — each community gathered around the Eucharistic table is the city of God in its fullness, not merely a branch of a larger institution.

The psalm's account of kings who "saw her and were astounded" (48:5) is read in Orthodox tradition as describing the power of the Church's witness through holiness. The conversion of Prince Vladimir of Kyiv — whose envoys to Constantinople reported that in Hagia Sophia they "knew not whether we were in heaven or on earth" — embodies this psalm's vision of God's city astonishing those who encounter her.

"Walk about Zion, go around her, count her towers" (48:12) is interpreted as an invitation to contemplate the Church's enduring witnesses — her saints, her liturgy, her theology, her sacred art. Orthodox tradition counts these "towers" — the great councils, the patristic consensus, the iconographic tradition — as visible testimonies to God's faithfulness across the centuries. The closing affirmation, "this God is our God for ever and ever" (48:14), is a confession of the unchanging faith that Orthodoxy treasures as its defining characteristic.
