Psalm 1 holds a place of honor in Orthodox liturgical life as the opening of the Psalter, which is divided into twenty sections called *kathismata* for use in the daily cycle of services. The Orthodox Church recites the entire Psalter weekly (and twice weekly during Great Lent), making the psalms the backbone of Orthodox prayer. The "blessed man" of verse 1 is read in Orthodox tradition as a prophetic image of Christ, the New Adam, who perfectly fulfilled the Law and never walked in the counsel of the ungodly. St. Basil the Great's homily on Psalm 1 established the patristic template for reading the Psalter as simultaneously historical, moral, and christological.

The tree planted by streams of water (1:3) is a beloved image in Orthodox spirituality, interpreted by the Fathers as the person rooted in the life-giving waters of baptism and nourished by the Holy Spirit. St. Gregory of Nyssa connected the "streams" to the four rivers of Paradise (Genesis 2:10-14), suggesting that the righteous person recovers the paradisal state through participation in divine grace. The fruitfulness "in its season" speaks to the Orthodox concept of *kairos* — God's appointed time — and the patience required in the spiritual life. The monastic tradition, which is the heart of Orthodox spirituality, understands the "meditation day and night" (1:2) as the practice of the Jesus Prayer and continuous remembrance of God.

The contrast between the righteous and the wicked in verses 4-6 reflects the Orthodox understanding of the two fundamental orientations of the human will: toward God (*theosis*) or away from God (spiritual death). The chaff driven by wind represents a soul without the weight of divine grace — insubstantial, directionless, at the mercy of passions. Orthodox anthropology teaches that human beings are created for communion with God, and apart from that communion they lose their ontological stability. The psalm thus serves as an overture to the entire Psalter's drama of fall, repentance, and restoration.
