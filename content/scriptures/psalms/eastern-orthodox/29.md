Psalm 29 is one of the great theophanic psalms, and Orthodox theology reads the sevenfold "voice of the LORD" (29:3-9) as a revelation of divine *energeia* — God's uncreated energies acting upon creation. The distinction between God's essence (unknowable) and his energies (experienced in creation and worship) is central to Orthodox theology, and this psalm demonstrates God's energies manifested in the natural world.

The psalm's association with water — "The voice of the LORD is over the waters" (29:3) — connects it to the Orthodox feast of Theophany (Epiphany), when the Church commemorates Christ's baptism in the Jordan. The Great Blessing of Waters, one of Orthodoxy's most dramatic liturgical rites, echoes this psalm: the priest plunges the cross into the water while prayers invoke the sanctifying power of God's voice over the deep.

The conclusion — "The LORD sits enthroned over the flood; the LORD is enthroned as King forever" (29:10) — affirms divine sovereignty with a cosmic scope characteristic of Orthodox theology. God does not merely govern human affairs but reigns over the entirety of creation, visible and invisible. The Pantocrator icon in every Orthodox dome visually represents this truth: Christ the Almighty, enthroned above all, blessing and sustaining the cosmos.
