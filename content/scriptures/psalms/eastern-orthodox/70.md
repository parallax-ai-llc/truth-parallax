## The Arrow Prayer of the Church

Psalm 70's urgent plea — "Make haste, O God, to deliver me!" (v. 1) — exemplifies what Orthodox spiritual writers call the "arrow prayer" — a brief, concentrated petition shot upward to God in moments of acute need. St. John Cassian identified this verse as particularly suited to ceaseless prayer, recommending it as a formula to be repeated throughout the day, much like the Jesus Prayer. The psalm's brevity is itself instructive: authentic prayer does not require eloquence or length but only sincerity and dependence.

The contrast between the enemies' shame and the faithful's joy (vv. 2-4) reflects the Orthodox understanding of the Last Judgment, where the final separation between those who sought God and those who refused Him will be revealed. Orthodox iconography of the Last Judgment, found in the narthex of most Orthodox churches, visually represents this psalm's binary: confusion for the wicked, exultation for the righteous. The icons serve as a visual catechesis, reminding the faithful each time they enter the church of the stakes of the spiritual life.

The self-identification as "poor and needy" (v. 5) expresses the fundamental humility that Orthodox spirituality requires. The Publican's prayer — "God, be merciful to me, a sinner" (Luke 18:13) — stands alongside this psalm as a model of the disposition God honors. The Fathers taught that spiritual poverty is not a deficiency but a prerequisite for receiving the fullness of divine grace. The psalm's urgent cry for God to "not delay" (v. 5) reflects not impatience but the earnest desire of a soul that knows its own need and the sufficiency of God's help.
