## The Messianic King's Universal Dominion

Psalm 72 describes an ideal king whose reign encompasses the whole earth, and Orthodox Christology identifies this king unequivocally with Christ. The psalm's opening — "Give the king your justice, O God" (v. 1) — is heard in Orthodox worship as a prayer fulfilled in the Incarnation, when the divine Logos assumed human kingship to reign with perfect justice. The Orthodox feast of Christ the King (not a separate feast as in Western Christianity, but a theme pervading the liturgical year) draws on this psalm's vision of a reign characterized by righteousness, mercy, and the defense of the poor.

The psalm's vision of kings bearing tribute — "May the kings of Tarshish and of the coastlands render him tribute; may the kings of Sheba and Seba bring gifts!" (v. 10) — is associated in Orthodox hymnography with the Nativity of Christ, when the Magi brought gold, frankincense, and myrrh. The Nativity kontakion and other hymns echo this psalm's imagery, presenting Christ's birth as the moment when the nations' long pilgrimage toward the true King finds its destination.

The psalm's social vision — "He delivers the needy when he calls... precious is their blood in his sight" (vv. 12, 14) — informs the Orthodox understanding of the Church's responsibility toward the vulnerable. The concluding doxology — "Blessed be his glorious name forever; may the whole earth be filled with his glory!" (v. 19) — is echoed in the Orthodox Liturgy's exclamation after the Great Entrance. As Book II of the Psalter closes with this vision of cosmic glory, Orthodox theology sees in it an icon of the eschatological fulfillment toward which all creation moves: the whole earth filled with the glory of the Lord, a glory already partially manifested in the liturgy.
