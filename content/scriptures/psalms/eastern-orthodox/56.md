## God's Tender Record of Human Suffering

Psalm 56's honest confession — "When I am afraid, I put my trust in you" (v. 3) — is valued in Orthodox spiritual theology as an example of *nepsis*, watchful awareness of one's inner state. The Desert Fathers taught that acknowledging fear without being governed by it is a mark of spiritual progress. The psalm does not deny the reality of fear but redirects it through trust, demonstrating the Orthodox principle that the passions are healed not by suppression but by reorientation toward God.

The image of God storing tears in a bottle (v. 8) has deeply influenced Orthodox hymnography. The funeral service and the prayers for the departed include images of God's tender care for human suffering. St. Isaac the Syrian, one of the most beloved mystics in the Orthodox tradition, taught that tears are a gift of the Holy Spirit — a sign of the heart's softening before God. This psalm's assurance that God counts every tear affirms the Orthodox conviction that suffering is never meaningless but is always held within divine awareness and compassion.

The psalm's refrain — "In God I trust; I shall not be afraid. What can man do to me?" (v. 11) — has been a rallying cry for the Orthodox Church during centuries of persecution, from the Roman Empire through Ottoman rule to Soviet oppression. The martyrs and confessors of the faith lived this psalm, trusting God amid humanly impossible circumstances. The concluding commitment to walk "before God in the light of the living" (v. 13) is understood as a reference to the resurrected life — the light of Pascha that illumines every believer who walks the path of faith through suffering to glory.
