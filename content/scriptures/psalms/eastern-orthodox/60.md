## Corporate Lament and Trust in God's Promises

Psalm 60's cry — "O God, you have rejected us, broken our defenses" (v. 1) — has been prayed by the Orthodox Church during periods of national catastrophe, from the fall of Constantinople to the suffering of Orthodox peoples under persecution. The psalm gives the faithful language for expressing devastation without abandoning faith. The Orthodox liturgical tradition incorporates such psalms of lament into the cycle of prayer, ensuring that the full range of human experience — including defeat and bewilderment — is brought before God rather than suppressed or denied.

The divine oracle in verses 6-8, claiming sovereignty over the entire land, is read by the Fathers as an expression of God's providence over all nations, not merely Israel. St. John Chrysostom preached that God's governance extends to every people and every territory, and that the fall of kingdoms and the rise of others are all within His sovereign plan. Orthodox political theology, shaped by this conviction, has historically maintained that earthly powers are accountable to divine justice and that no earthly defeat negates God's ultimate sovereignty.

The psalm's concluding confession — "With God we shall do valiantly, for it is he who will tread down our foes" (v. 12) — has sustained Orthodox communities through centuries of subjugation and suffering. The phrase "with God" (*syn Theo*) captures the Orthodox understanding of divine-human cooperation (*synergeia*) that characterizes both salvation and historical agency. Human effort is not negated but empowered by divine grace. The psalm thus provides a theological foundation for the Orthodox belief that faithful endurance in the face of defeat is itself a form of spiritual victory.
