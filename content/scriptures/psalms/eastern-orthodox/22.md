Psalm 22 (Psalm 21 in the Septuagint) is the psalm of the Passion in Orthodox Holy Week services. It is chanted during the service of the Royal Hours on Great and Holy Friday, and its imagery permeates the hymns of the Bridegroom Matins services of Holy Week. The cry "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (22:1) is understood by Orthodox theologians not as a rupture within the Trinity but as Christ's identification with the full depth of humanity's experience of alienation from God. In His human nature, Christ entered the darkness that sin had created, in order to fill it with divine light.

The Orthodox reading of the psalm's suffering (22:1-21) emphasizes the reality of Christ's human experience. The Chalcedonian definition (451) affirmed that Christ possesses a complete human nature with a human will and human experiences. The agony described — pierced hands and feet (22:16), bones out of joint (22:14), heart melted like wax (22:14) — is genuine human suffering, not a divine performance. Orthodox theology insists on this point against Docetic tendencies: the Son of God truly suffered in His humanity, and this real suffering is what makes salvation real.

The triumphant second half (22:22-31) is read as the voice of the risen Christ proclaiming victory to the Church. The "great congregation" (22:25) in which praise is offered is the Eucharistic assembly, and the universal worship prophesied — "all the ends of the earth shall remember and turn to the Lord" (22:27) — describes the missionary expansion of the Orthodox faith to all nations. The psalm's final declaration, "he has done it" (22:31), is the proclamation of completed salvation that every Divine Liturgy makes present. The Orthodox faithful do not merely remember the Passion and Resurrection; through the liturgy, they participate in these events as present realities.
