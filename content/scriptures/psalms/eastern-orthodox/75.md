## The Cup of Divine Judgment

Psalm 75 opens with thanksgiving — "We give thanks to you, O God... your name is near" (v. 1) — a nearness that Orthodox theology understands through the theology of the divine energies. God's Name is not merely a word but His real presence available to those who call upon it. The Hesychast tradition teaches that the invocation of God's Name brings genuine encounter with God, and this psalm's assertion that His Name "is near" affirms the accessibility of divine presence in prayer.

The divine oracle — "At the set time that I appoint I will judge with equity" (v. 2) — and the declaration "When the earth totters... it is I who keep steady its pillars" (v. 3) express the Orthodox understanding of divine providence. God's judgment operates within His own timing, and His sovereignty sustains the cosmos even when it appears to be collapsing. The Orthodox liturgical calendar, with its ordered progression through feasts and fasts, embodies this psalm's conviction that divine time has structure and purpose beyond human comprehension.

The cup of foaming wine (v. 8) — drained to the dregs by the wicked — has deep liturgical resonance in Orthodox worship. The Eucharistic cup transforms this psalm's image of judgment into an image of mercy: in the Liturgy, the faithful receive the cup not of wrath but of salvation, the blood of Christ shed for the remission of sins. The contrast is theologically decisive for Orthodox sacramental theology — the same cup that judges the impenitent saves the repentant. The psalm's final praise (v. 9) and the promise that "the horns of the righteous shall be exalted" (v. 10) point to the Orthodox confidence that the humble who receive God's cup in faith will share in Christ's exaltation.
