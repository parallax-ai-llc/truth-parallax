Psalm 21 (Psalm 20 in the Septuagint) celebrates the king's joy in God's strength and the blessings of answered prayer. Orthodox Christology reads this psalm as describing the exalted Christ, who receives from the Father "length of days forever and ever" (21:4) through the resurrection and whose crown of gold (21:3) is the glory of the divine nature now manifest in His glorified humanity. The Orthodox icon of Christ Pantocrator, with its golden halo inscribed with the Greek letters for "I AM" (ho ōn), is the visual counterpart of this psalm's vision.

The blessing that the king "asked life of you; you gave it to him" (21:4) is interpreted in Orthodox theology as the restoration of immortality through Christ's victory over death. Adam's sin introduced death into creation; Christ's resurrection reverses this curse. The Paschal troparion — "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life" — is the Orthodox Church's most beloved hymn and expresses exactly the theology of Psalm 21: life requested, life given, death conquered.

The vision of the king's enemies consumed by fire (21:9-12) is read in Orthodox eschatology through the patristic understanding of divine fire as simultaneously purifying and destructive. St. Isaac the Syrian taught that the fire of hell is nothing other than the fire of God's love experienced by those who have rejected it — the same reality that is joy for the saints is torment for the impenitent. The psalm's closing doxology (21:13) directs all attention back to God's strength and power, reflecting the Orthodox principle that all glory ultimately belongs to the Holy Trinity.
