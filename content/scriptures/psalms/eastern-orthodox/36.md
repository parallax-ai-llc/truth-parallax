Psalm 36 presents a profound contrast between the darkness of sin and the light of divine love. "Your steadfast love, O LORD, extends to the heavens" (36:5). Orthodox theology sees in this psalm a meditation on the divine attributes — God's love, faithfulness, righteousness, and justice — that are not abstract concepts but living realities experienced through prayer and the sacramental life.

The luminous declaration of verse 9 — "In your light do we see light" — is perhaps the most important verse in all of Eastern Orthodox theology of illumination. Gregory of Nazianzus called God "the Light beyond all lights," and Symeon the New Theologian described mystical experience as being "wholly light, wholly made light." The Uncreated Light experienced by the hesychasts on Mount Athos is not created illumination but participation in God's own radiance.

The psalm's opening description of the wicked — "an oracle is within my heart concerning the sinfulness of the wicked" (36:1) — is read by Orthodox ascetical theology as describing the *logismoi*, the sinful thoughts that whisper within the heart and seek to lead it away from God. The practice of *nepsis* (watchfulness) taught by the Philokalia is precisely the discipline of guarding the heart against these inner whispers, remaining attentive to God's light rather than the darkness of temptation.
