## The Ascension of the Divine Warrior

Psalm 68 is one of the great processional psalms of the Orthodox Church, and its verse "God shall arise, his enemies shall be scattered" (v. 1) is sung at the Paschal Liturgy as the faithful process with candles around the darkened church before the proclamation of the Resurrection. This liturgical use transforms the psalm from a historical recollection of the Exodus into a present experience of Christ's victory over death. The Orthodox Paschal celebration is not a mere commemoration but a participation in the mystery, and this psalm provides the triumphant proclamation that accompanies the Church's annual passage from Good Friday darkness to Resurrection light.

Verse 18 — "You ascended on high, leading a host of captives" — is central to Orthodox Ascension theology. The feast of the Ascension celebrates Christ's return to the Father bearing the redeemed humanity He assumed in the Incarnation. The "captives" led in triumph are the righteous dead liberated from Hades, a theme depicted in the iconic image of the Resurrection (Anastasis) where Christ descends into Hades and raises Adam and Eve. The "gifts" received among men are understood as the Holy Spirit, given to the Church at Pentecost — the gift that empowers all other gifts.

The psalm's description of God as "father of the fatherless and protector of widows" (v. 5) is taken seriously in Orthodox social ethics. The Church's ancient tradition of caring for orphans, widows, the sick, and prisoners flows from this understanding of God's character. Orthodox monasteries have historically served as hospitals, schools, and refuges, enacting the psalm's vision of a God who "settles the solitary in a home" (v. 6). The psalm concludes with a vision of universal worship (v. 32) that the Orthodox Church understands as realized in the Divine Liturgy, where heaven and earth unite in praise of the ascended Christ.
