## The Agony of Betrayal and the Wings of the Dove

Psalm 55's poignant cry of betrayal has been read in Orthodox tradition primarily as a prophetic utterance of Christ in Gethsemane. The description of the betrayer — "my companion, my familiar friend... we used to take sweet counsel together; within God's house we walked in the throng" (vv. 13-14) — is applied directly to Judas in the hymns of Holy Week. The Orthodox Matins of Great Friday includes hymns that echo these verses, lamenting the betrayal of Christ by one who shared His table and His prayers.

The psalmist's desire for the "wings of a dove" to "fly away and be at rest" (v. 6) has been interpreted by the Fathers in two complementary ways. St. Basil the Great sees the dove as a symbol of the Holy Spirit, and the desire for wings as the soul's longing for the Spirit's power to rise above earthly suffering. The monastic tradition reads it as expressing the call to the contemplative life — the flight from the world's turmoil into the stillness of the desert, where the soul finds rest in God. Both readings converge in the conviction that true rest is found not in escape but in divine encounter.

The psalm's prayer rhythm — "Evening and morning and at noon I utter my complaint and moan, and he hears my voice" (v. 17) — has shaped the Orthodox pattern of daily prayer. The three-fold division of the day into morning, noon, and evening prayers reflects the psalmist's practice and corresponds to the canonical hours. The exhortation to "cast your burden on the LORD" (v. 22) is recited as a word of comfort in Orthodox pastoral care, reminding the suffering that the God who hears prayers "evening and morning and at noon" is attentive to every cry of His people.
