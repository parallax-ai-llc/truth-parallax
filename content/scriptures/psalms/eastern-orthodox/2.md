Psalm 2 is one of the most frequently cited psalms in Orthodox Christology and is chanted during the services of Holy Week. The declaration "You are my Son; today I have begotten you" (2:7) was central to the Trinitarian theology developed at the First and Second Ecumenical Councils. Orthodox dogmatic theology, following the Cappadocian Fathers, understands this "begetting" as the eternal generation of the Son — not a temporal event but an eternal relationship within the Trinity. The "today" is the eternal now of divine life, beyond time.

The rebellion of the nations against the Lord and His Anointed (2:1-3) is read in Orthodox theology as the cosmic struggle between the kingdom of God and the powers of darkness. The Orthodox liturgy of Great and Holy Friday applies this psalm to the conspiracy of the chief priests and Pilate against Christ. Yet God "who sits in the heavens laughs" (2:4) — not with cruelty but with the sovereign assurance that no created power can frustrate the divine economy (*oikonomia*) of salvation. St. John Chrysostom preached that the laughter of God is the most terrifying thing the wicked can face, for it reveals the absolute futility of opposition to the Almighty.

The command to "kiss the Son" (2:12) is interpreted in Orthodox worship as the call to venerate Christ — literally, in the liturgical practice of kissing icons. The faithful kiss the icon of Christ not as an act of idolatry but as an expression of love and reverence for the Person depicted, a practice defended at the Seventh Ecumenical Council (787). The psalm's final blessing — "blessed are all who take refuge in him" — is understood as an invitation to enter the life of the Church, where refuge is found through the sacraments, the prayers of the saints, and the protection of the Theotokos.
