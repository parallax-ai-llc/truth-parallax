Psalm 23 (Psalm 22 in the Septuagint) is among the most cherished psalms in Orthodox devotion and is used in the funeral service, at the anointing of the sick, and in personal prayer. The Orthodox reading emphasizes the sacramental dimensions of the psalm's imagery: the "still waters" (23:2) evoke the baptismal font, the "table" (23:5) is the Eucharistic altar, the "oil" of anointing (23:5) points to Chrismation and Holy Unction. The entire psalm can be read as a journey through the sacramental life of the Church, guided by Christ the Good Shepherd.

The "valley of the shadow of death" (23:4) is given particular interpretation in Orthodox funeral and memorial services. The Orthodox Church prays extensively for the departed, understanding death not as annihilation but as a passage through which the shepherd accompanies the soul. The prayers for the departed — memorial services (*panikhida*), prayers on the third, ninth, and fortieth days after death, and the Saturday of Souls observances — express the Orthodox conviction that Christ's rod and staff comfort the soul even beyond the grave. The communion of saints bridges the divide between living and dead, united in the one Shepherd.

The Orthodox reading of "I shall dwell in the house of the Lord forever" (23:6) connects to the eschatological hope of the new creation. The "house of the Lord" is ultimately the restored cosmos, where God dwells with His people in unmediated communion (Revelation 21:3). Orthodox theology of icons and sacred space anticipates this eschatological reality: every church building, adorned with icons of Christ, the Theotokos, and the saints, is a foretaste of the heavenly dwelling. The psalm's pastoral intimacy — the shift from "he leads" to "you are with me" in the darkest moment — captures the essence of Orthodox prayer: personal encounter with the living God.
