Psalm 27 opens with the confession "The Lord is my light and my salvation" (27:1), language that Orthodox theology connects to the Uncreated Light — the divine energy that the hesychast tradition teaches is directly experienced in prayer. Gregory Palamas defended the reality of this light against those who would reduce it to metaphor, insisting that the light the apostles saw on Mount Tabor was God's own eternal radiance.

The psalmist's singular desire — "to gaze upon the beauty of the LORD" (27:4) — describes what Orthodox theology calls *theoria*, the contemplative vision of God that is the goal of the spiritual life. The Philokalia, that treasury of Orthodox mystical writings, is essentially a guide to attaining what Psalm 27:4 describes: the unceasing beholding of divine beauty in the temple of the heart.

The closing exhortation, "Wait for the LORD; be strong and take heart" (27:14), reflects the Orthodox understanding of spiritual patience (*hypomonē*). The ascetic tradition teaches that the spiritual life requires long endurance — the willingness to persist in prayer and repentance through seasons of darkness, trusting that God's light will be revealed. The monastics of Mount Athos have embodied this patient waiting for centuries.
