Psalm 24 (Psalm 23 in the Septuagint) is one of the most dramatically liturgical psalms, and Orthodox worship has developed its antiphonal character to the fullest. The Feast of the Ascension uses this psalm's imagery of the gates lifting up to receive the King of Glory, and the Great Entrance of the Divine Liturgy — when the consecrated gifts are carried from the prothesis (preparation table) to the altar — is a ritual enactment of the King's entry into His holy temple. The priest's prayer during the Great Entrance draws on the psalm's theology of divine sovereignty over all creation.

The entrance requirements (24:3-4) — "clean hands and a pure heart" — are understood in Orthodox ascetical theology as describing the fruit of the spiritual struggle. "Clean hands" refers to purified actions, while "pure heart" refers to the inner transformation achieved through *praxis* (ascetical effort) and *theoria* (contemplation). St. Maximus the Confessor taught that purification of the passions (*praktikē*) precedes the illumination of the *nous* (*physikē*), which in turn precedes the vision of God (*theologikē*). The psalm thus describes the person who has progressed through all three stages and is ready to stand in God's presence.

The repeated question "Who is this King of glory?" (24:8, 10) and its answer — "The Lord of hosts, he is the King of glory" — is echoed in the Orthodox Paschal liturgy. When the procession returns to the darkened church on Pascha night and the doors are closed, the dialogue between those outside and inside reenacts this psalm's exchange. The doors open to admit the light of the Risen Christ, and the church erupts in the Paschal hymn. This dramatic liturgical moment is perhaps the most powerful experience in Orthodox worship, making Psalm 24's ancient words a living reality.
