Psalm 45 is a royal wedding psalm that Orthodox theology reads on multiple levels. The king addressed as divine — "Your throne, O God, will last for ever and ever" (45:6) — is identified with Christ the Bridegroom, whose coming the Church celebrates during Holy Week. The Bridegroom Matins services, with their haunting chant "Behold, the Bridegroom comes at midnight," draw on this psalm's vision of the royal procession.

The queen at the king's right hand (45:9) is identified in Orthodox tradition with the *Theotokos* — the Mother of God, who occupies a place of supreme honor in the Kingdom. Orthodox Mariology does not worship Mary but recognizes her as the human person most fully transformed by divine grace, the first to receive and bear the incarnate Word. The psalm's description of the queen "all glorious within" (45:13) reflects the Orthodox understanding of Mary's inner sanctity.

The bridal imagery also points to the Church as the Bride of Christ — adorned not with external splendor but with the beauty of holiness. The iconographic tradition of depicting the Church as a woman in royal attire draws on this psalm's imagery. Orthodox ecclesiology understands the Church's beauty as the manifestation of divine grace working through human nature, adorning the Bride for the eschatological wedding feast.
