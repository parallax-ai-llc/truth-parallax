Psalm 35 is an imprecatory psalm that Orthodox theology reads within the framework of spiritual warfare. David's cry, "Contend, LORD, with those who contend with me" (35:1), is understood as a prayer against the demonic powers that assail the soul. Orthodox monasticism, especially in the tradition of Mount Athos, teaches that the Christian life is a constant battle against the "principalities and powers" (Ephesians 6:12), and the imprecatory psalms provide weapons for this warfare.

The psalmist's account of repaying good with evil — "I put on sackcloth and humbled myself with fasting... yet when I stumbled, they gathered in glee" (35:13, 15) — resonates with the lives of countless Orthodox saints who suffered persecution for their faithfulness. The icon of the Holy Forty Martyrs of Sebastia, for example, depicts soldiers who chose death by freezing rather than deny Christ — facing the very malice this psalm describes.

The petition "Vindicate me in your righteousness, LORD my God" (35:24) expresses the Orthodox trust in God's ultimate justice. The Last Judgment icon in every Orthodox narthex serves as a visual reminder that God will ultimately set all things right. Orthodox theology teaches that the cry for divine justice is not vindictiveness but faith — the confidence that God will not allow evil to have the last word.
