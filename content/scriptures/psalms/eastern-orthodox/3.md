Psalm 3 occupies a central place in Orthodox morning prayer. It is one of the six psalms (*hexapsalmos*) read at the beginning of Orthros (Matins) — Psalms 3, 37, 62, 87, 102, and 142 — a grouping that has defined Orthodox morning worship since at least the sixth century. The *hexapsalmos* is read in near-darkness with the church lights extinguished, creating a solemn atmosphere of watchfulness. Psalm 3's themes of waking and divine protection make it the natural opening: "I lay down and slept; I woke again, for the Lord sustained me" (3:5).

The Orthodox Fathers read Psalm 3 christologically: David's flight from Absalom prefigures Christ's rejection by His own people, and the sleep and waking of verse 5 prophesy Christ's death and resurrection. St. Athanasius, in his *Letter to Marcellinus on the Interpretation of the Psalms*, taught that every psalm can be read as the voice of Christ, the voice of the Church, or the voice of the individual believer. In Psalm 3, all three voices converge: Christ sleeps in death and rises, the Church endures persecution and is vindicated, and the individual Christian faces daily trials and finds God's sustaining grace each morning.

The declaration "Salvation belongs to the Lord" (3:8) is a foundational principle of Orthodox soteriology, which understands salvation as *theosis* — the process by which human beings, through divine grace, participate in the life of God without losing their creaturely identity. Salvation is not merely juridical (a legal declaration) but ontological (a real transformation of human nature). The psalm's movement from danger to deliverance mirrors the paschal pattern that shapes all of Orthodox life: through death to resurrection, through suffering to glory, through the cross to the empty tomb.
