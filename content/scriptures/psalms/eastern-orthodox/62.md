## Stillness Before the God of Power and Love

Psalm 62's opening — "For God alone my soul waits in silence" (v. 1) — is a foundational text for the Orthodox Hesychast tradition. The practice of *hesychia* (inner stillness) developed by the Desert Fathers and systematized by St. Gregory Palamas finds scriptural warrant in this psalm's insistence on silent waiting. The Hesychasts taught that the soul must be stilled — freed from distraction, passion, and discursive thought — in order to perceive the uncreated light of God. This psalm models the interior disposition that makes contemplative prayer possible.

The psalm's warning against trusting in human power and wealth (vv. 9-10) resonates with the Orthodox emphasis on *kenosis* — the self-emptying that characterizes Christ's incarnation and the Christian life that imitates it. The assertion that "those of high estate are a delusion" (v. 9) challenges all forms of worldly self-aggrandizement. Orthodox monasticism, with its vows of poverty and obedience, embodies this psalm's radical detachment from human securities. The monk's entire life is an enactment of verse 1: waiting for God alone, in silence.

The psalm's concluding revelation — "power belongs to God, and... steadfast love" (vv. 11-12) — articulates what Orthodox theology expresses through the distinction between God's essence and energies. God's power and love are uncreated divine energies through which creatures participate in the divine life without comprehending the divine essence. St. Gregory Palamas taught that these energies are God himself in His outward activity, and this psalm's pairing of power and love describes the two fundamental modes of God's engagement with creation: sovereign governance and merciful tenderness, inseparable in the divine life.
