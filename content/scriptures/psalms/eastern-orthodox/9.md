Psalm 9, which in the Septuagint numbering used by the Orthodox Church is combined with Psalm 10 as a single psalm, opens with whole-hearted thanksgiving: "I will give thanks to the Lord with my whole heart" (9:1). Orthodox liturgical worship is fundamentally eucharistic — the word *eucharistia* means thanksgiving — and this psalm models the total self-offering that characterizes authentic Orthodox prayer. The emphasis on recounting "all of your wonderful deeds" (9:1b) aligns with the Orthodox practice of *anamnesis* (remembrance), by which the saving acts of God are not merely recalled but made present in liturgical celebration.

The affirmation that "the Lord sits enthroned forever" (9:7) resonates with the Orthodox understanding of Christ Pantocrator — the All-Ruler — depicted in the central dome of every Orthodox church, looking down upon the worshipping assembly with both authority and compassion. The icon of Christ Pantocrator is the visual equivalent of this psalm's theology: the Judge of all the earth is simultaneously the Savior who hears the cry of the afflicted. This paradox of justice and mercy, judgment and compassion, is held together in Orthodox theology without resolution into either pole.

The psalm's concern for the oppressed and afflicted (9:9, 12, 18) connects to the Orthodox Church's social witness, which has historically manifested through philanthropic institutions, hospitals, and care for the poor. The Byzantine Empire's network of charitable institutions — orphanages, hospitals, hospices — was directly inspired by the biblical mandate to defend the vulnerable. Contemporary Orthodox social theology, articulated by figures like Metropolitan John Zizioulas, grounds the obligation to the poor in the eucharistic vision: since all are called to the same table, systemic exclusion of the poor is a eucharistic scandal.
