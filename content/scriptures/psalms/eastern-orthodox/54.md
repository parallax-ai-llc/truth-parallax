## Calling on the Divine Name for Deliverance

The invocation "O God, save me by your name" (v. 1) connects in Orthodox practice to the profound theology of the divine Name developed by the Hesychast tradition. The Jesus Prayer — "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner" — is built on the conviction that God's Name carries His presence and power. St. Gregory Palamas taught that the Name of God is not an empty sign but a vehicle of divine energy (*energeia*). This psalm's plea to be saved "by your name" thus anticipates the entire Orthodox tradition of unceasing prayer through the invocation of the Name.

The psalm's description of enemies as "strangers" who "do not set God before themselves" (v. 3) is interpreted by the Fathers as describing the spiritual forces that assail the soul. St. Athanasius reads the Psalms as weapons in the spiritual warfare that every Christian wages against the passions and the demonic powers. The psalm's movement from threat to confidence — "Behold, God is my helper" (v. 4) — mirrors the progression of the spiritual life from fear through struggle to trust, a pattern the ascetical Fathers describe as characteristic of growth in prayer.

The vow to "sacrifice to you with a freewill offering" (v. 6) is understood in Orthodox liturgical theology as fulfilled in the Eucharist, the free and grateful offering of the faithful in response to God's saving acts. The Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom includes the prayer "Thine own of Thine own we offer unto Thee," expressing the truth that every offering to God is first a gift received from God. This psalm models the eucharistic pattern: God saves (v. 7), and the saved respond with grateful offering (v. 6).
