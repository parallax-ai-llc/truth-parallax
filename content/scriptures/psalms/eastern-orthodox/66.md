## The Testimony of Deliverance

Psalm 66's opening call — "Shout for joy to God, all the earth!" (v. 1) — expresses the universal scope of Orthodox worship. The Orthodox Church understands herself as embracing all cultures and nations within the one liturgical tradition, and this psalm's invitation to "all the earth" finds expression in the multilingual, multicultural character of Orthodox Christianity — from the Greek-speaking patriarchates to the Slavic, Arabic, Georgian, and other Orthodox churches.

The psalm's recollection of the Red Sea crossing — "He turned the sea into dry land; they passed through the river on foot" (v. 6) — is read in Orthodox baptismal theology as a type of the sacrament. Just as Israel passed through the waters from slavery to freedom, the catechumen passes through the baptismal waters from death to life. The blessing of baptismal water includes prayers that recall the Exodus, and this psalm provides the scriptural warrant for that typological reading. The testing imagery — "You have tried us as silver is tried" (v. 10) — speaks to the Orthodox understanding of purification (*katharsis*) as the first stage of the spiritual life, in which the soul is refined through trials.

The personal testimony that closes the psalm — "Come and hear, all you who fear God, and I will tell what he has done for my soul" (v. 16) — has influenced the Orthodox practice of spiritual autobiography and the tradition of sharing one's experience of God's grace. The Lives of the Saints (*Synaxarion*), read daily in Orthodox worship, are extended testimonies of what God has done for particular souls. The psalm's warning against cherishing iniquity (v. 18) reinforces the Orthodox emphasis on confession as a prerequisite for authentic prayer and worthy reception of the Eucharist.
