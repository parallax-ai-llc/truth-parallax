Psalm 6 is the first of the Penitential Psalms and holds special significance in Orthodox worship during Great Lent, the forty-day season of fasting and repentance before Pascha (Easter). The cry "O Lord, rebuke me not in your anger" (6:1) is the voice of the penitent soul recognizing its distance from God and pleading for mercy rather than justice. Orthodox theology emphasizes that God's "anger" is not a passion like human anger but the experience of divine love by a soul that has turned away — the same fire that warms the righteous burns those who resist it, as St. Isaac the Syrian taught.

The theme of tears in Psalm 6 — "every night I flood my bed with tears; I drench my couch with my weeping" (6:6) — connects to the Orthodox tradition of *penthos* (compunction), the gift of tears that the Fathers regarded as a sign of spiritual progress. St. John Climacus devoted an entire step of his *Ladder of Divine Ascent* to the gift of tears, teaching that they cleanse the soul more effectively than any ascetical practice. The Orthodox funeral service includes the petition for "a place of refreshment, a place of repose, where all sickness, sighing, and lamentation have fled away" — language echoing the psalm's movement from weeping to consolation.

The psalm's turning point — "The Lord has heard the sound of my weeping" (6:8) — affirms a core principle of Orthodox prayer: God hears and responds to the contrite heart. The Orthodox sacrament of Holy Unction (anointing of the sick) draws on this psalm's union of physical and spiritual healing. In Orthodox understanding, body and soul are so intimately united that physical suffering and spiritual distress cannot be separated — the psalm's groaning bones and troubled soul are one integrated experience, and God's healing addresses the whole person, body and soul together.
