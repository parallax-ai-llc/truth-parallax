Psalm 8 is foundational to Orthodox theological anthropology and the doctrine of *theosis* (divinization). The declaration that humanity has been made "a little lower than the angels" and crowned "with glory and honor" (8:5) describes, in Orthodox reading, humanity's original vocation: to serve as the mediator between God and creation, uniting the material and spiritual worlds in a cosmic liturgy of praise. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that the human person is a *microcosm* and *mediator*, containing within itself elements of every level of creation and called to offer all things back to God.

The Septuagint rendering of verse 5, which translates *elohim* as "angels" rather than "God," was the version known to the Church Fathers and remains the standard in Orthodox liturgical use. The christological reading follows Hebrews 2:6-9: Christ, the incarnate Logos, became "a little lower than the angels" by assuming human nature, and through His death and resurrection has been crowned with glory and honor, raising human nature to the right hand of the Father. The Feast of the Ascension celebrates this cosmic event: human nature, in the Person of Christ, now sits enthroned in the divine life of the Trinity.

The psalm's vision of human dominion over creation (8:6-8) is read in Orthodox ecology through the lens of the priestly vocation. Humanity is called to be creation's priest, offering the world back to God in thanksgiving and receiving it back as gift — a dynamic beautifully expressed in the Eucharistic liturgy, where bread and wine (fruits of creation and human labor) are offered to God and returned as the Body and Blood of Christ. Ecumenical Patriarch Bartholomew, known as the "Green Patriarch," has drawn on this theology to develop an Orthodox environmental ethic rooted in the liturgical worldview of Psalm 8.
