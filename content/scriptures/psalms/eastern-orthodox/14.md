Psalm 14 (Psalm 13 in the Septuagint) diagnoses the universal human condition apart from divine grace. The fool who says "There is no God" (14:1) is understood in Orthodox anthropology as the person whose *nous* (spiritual intellect) has been darkened by the passions. Orthodox theology distinguishes between *logos* (reason) and *nous* (spiritual perception), teaching that the *nous* is the faculty designed to perceive God, and when it is obscured, the result is functional atheism — not necessarily intellectual denial but existential blindness to the divine presence that permeates all reality.

The declaration that "there is none who does good, not even one" (14:3) is interpreted in Orthodox theology with more nuance than in some Western traditions. While affirming the universal effects of the Fall, Orthodoxy teaches that the *imago Dei* was obscured but not obliterated. Human beings retain the capacity for natural goodness, though this goodness cannot save apart from divine grace. St. Gregory Palamas distinguished between the image of God (which remains) and the likeness of God (which was lost and must be restored through *theosis*). The psalm describes humanity in its fallen condition, not in its created nature.

The messianic hope of verse 7 — "Oh, that salvation would come out of Zion!" — is understood in Orthodox theology as fulfilled in the Incarnation, when the eternal Logos took flesh from the Theotokos in the land of Zion. The restoration of Israel's fortunes is expanded ecclesiologically to encompass all humanity, for in Orthodox understanding the Church is the true Israel, the community of all peoples united in Christ through baptism and Eucharist. The psalm's movement from universal corruption to eschatological hope traces the entire arc of salvation history as Orthodox theology understands it.
