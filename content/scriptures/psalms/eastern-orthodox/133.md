## Overview

Psalm 133 — *Unity of Brethren* — contains 3 verses. The Eastern Orthodox tradition reads the Psalms primarily through liturgical practice and patristic commentary, understanding them as the prayer book of the Church from its earliest days.

Psalm 133 belongs to the Songs of Ascent (*Anabathmoi*), which hold a special place in Orthodox worship. These psalms appear in the *Orthros* (Matins) service as the *Anabathmoi* hymns — liturgical compositions modeled on these psalms that are sung every Sunday, reflecting the soul's ascent toward the divine light.

## Patristic and Byzantine Interpretation

#

## The Cappadocians and Chrysostom

**Basil the Great** in his *Homilies on the Psalms* emphasized the Psalms' capacity to unite the soul with God through prayer. **John Chrysostom** preached extensively on the Psalter, urging his congregations to internalize the psalms as daily spiritual nourishment.

**Theodoret of Cyrus** provided verse-by-verse commentary that balanced historical context with Christological typology, reading Psalm 133 as simultaneously David's prayer and a prophecy of Christ and the Church.

#

## Hesychast Tradition

In the hesychast tradition of the Orthodox East, the Psalms serve as a foundation for contemplative prayer. Psalm 133's themes of trust, praise, and divine encounter resonate with the inner prayer of the heart that the *Philokalia* describes.

## Liturgical Use

The Orthodox Church divides the entire Psalter into twenty *kathismata* (sections), read through completely each week (twice during Great Lent). Psalm 133 falls within this cycle and is encountered regularly in the divine services.

In the *Typikon* (liturgical rubrics), specific psalms are appointed for particular feasts and occasions. The Orthodox liturgy weaves psalm verses throughout every service, from *Vespers* to the *Divine Liturgy*.

## Theological Significance

Orthodox theology reads the Psalms within the living Tradition of the Church, where Scripture, liturgy, and the communion of saints form an indivisible unity. Psalm 133 participates in this tradition as both inspired text and living prayer.
