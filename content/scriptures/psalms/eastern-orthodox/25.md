Psalm 25 (Psalm 24 in the Septuagint) is an acrostic prayer for guidance and forgiveness that Orthodox tradition has incorporated into the prayers before Holy Communion and into various penitential services. The opening gesture — "To you, O Lord, I lift up my soul" (25:1) — describes the fundamental orientation of Orthodox prayer: the soul's ascent toward God, facilitated by the liturgy, the sacraments, and the ascetical life. The physical posture of Orthodox prayer — standing with arms raised — embodies this upward movement.

The repeated plea "Make me to know your ways, O Lord; teach me your paths" (25:4) reflects the Orthodox understanding of spiritual life as a journey of learning guided by the Holy Spirit through the Church. The role of the spiritual father (*pneumatikos*) or spiritual mother (*gerontissa*) in Orthodox tradition is to mediate this divine guidance, helping the believer discern God's path amid the confusions of daily life. The institution of spiritual direction, formalized in monasticism but available to all the faithful, is the pastoral expression of the psalm's prayer for guidance.

The verse "The friendship of the Lord is for those who fear him" (25:14) employs a word (*sod*) that suggests intimate counsel, the sharing of secrets between trusted companions. Orthodox mystical theology identifies this intimate divine-human communion as the goal of *theosis* — not mere acquaintance with God but genuine friendship, the sharing of divine life. St. Symeon the New Theologian spoke of experiencing the divine light as a Friend who revealed Himself in prayer, and the hesychast tradition treasures this psalm's promise as a description of what contemplative prayer actually yields: not information about God but communion with God Himself.
