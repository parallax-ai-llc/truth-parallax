Psalm 30 is a psalm of thanksgiving for deliverance from death that Orthodox theology reads in the light of Christ's Pascha — his passage through death to resurrection. "You brought me up from the realm of the dead; you spared me from going down to the pit" (30:3). The Paschal troparion — "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death" — echoes this psalm's celebration of God's victory over the grave.

The pivotal verse, "Weeping may stay for the night, but rejoicing comes in the morning" (30:5), is central to the Orthodox experience of Pascha. The darkness of Great and Holy Saturday gives way to the midnight proclamation of the Resurrection — the most joyful moment in the Orthodox liturgical year. Every Saturday evening Vespers service anticipates this movement from sorrow to gladness, from death to life.

The psalm's confession of self-sufficiency — "When I felt secure, I said, 'I will never be shaken'" (30:6) — is read by Orthodox ascetical theology as describing the spiritual danger of *prelest* (spiritual delusion). The Desert Fathers warned that those who feel most secure in their own spiritual attainments are often closest to a fall. True spiritual stability comes not from self-confidence but from the continual awareness of one's dependence on God's mercy.
