## Prophetic Judgment on Unrighteous Powers

Psalm 58's challenge to corrupt judges — "Do you indeed decree what is right, you gods?" (v. 1) — is read in Orthodox tradition as a prophetic indictment that extends to all who wield authority unjustly. The Orthodox understanding of *symphonia* — the harmonious cooperation between civil and ecclesiastical authority — requires that human governance reflect divine justice. When it fails to do so, the Church, following this psalm, speaks prophetically against injustice, as Orthodox bishops have done throughout history in confronting tyrannical rulers.

The vivid imprecatory imagery — adders that refuse to hear (v. 4), snails dissolving (v. 8) — is handled in Orthodox exegesis through the hermeneutical principle of *theoria*, the spiritual vision that perceives deeper meaning beneath the literal surface. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that the enemies in the Psalms are ultimately the passions and demonic forces, and that prayers for their destruction are prayers for liberation from spiritual bondage. Orthodox monks pray these imprecatory psalms daily in their kathisma readings, understanding them as petitions against the spiritual enemies that threaten the soul's journey toward God.

The psalm's confident conclusion — "Mankind will say, 'Surely there is a reward for the righteous; surely there is a God who judges on earth'" (v. 11) — affirms the Orthodox eschatological conviction that divine justice will be manifested both within history and at the Last Judgment. The Orthodox Church prays for the departed, trusting that God's judgment is tempered by mercy, while simultaneously affirming that justice is real and that the righteous will ultimately be vindicated. This psalm sustains the Church's hope in the midst of a world where injustice often seems to prevail.
