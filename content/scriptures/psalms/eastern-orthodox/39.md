Psalm 39 meditates on human transience with an intensity that Orthodox theology channels into its understanding of repentance and the spiritual life. "You have made my days a mere handbreadth; the span of my years is as nothing before you" (39:5). Orthodox funeral services draw on this awareness of mortality, not to inspire despair but to awaken the soul to the urgency of repentance and the hope of resurrection.

The psalmist's confession that "everyone is but a breath" (39:5) resonates with the Orthodox liturgical tradition of the *Trisagion* service for the departed, which acknowledges human fragility while affirming faith in the God who conquers death. The kontakion of the funeral service — "With the saints give rest, O Christ, to the soul of your servant, where there is no pain, no grief, no sighing, but life everlasting" — responds to the psalm's existential cry with resurrection hope.

David's identification as "a foreigner, a stranger" (39:12) connects to the Orthodox understanding of the Christian life as *xeniteia* — voluntary exile or pilgrimage. The monastic tradition especially valued this sense of being a stranger in the world, not from hatred of creation but from longing for the heavenly homeland. The great monastic centers — Sinai, Athos, Meteora — embody this psalm's vision of earthly life as sojourn toward the eternal.
