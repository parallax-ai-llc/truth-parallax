Psalm 43 continues the prayer of Psalm 42 with the petition "Send me your light and your faithful care, let them lead me" (43:3). Orthodox theology identifies this light with the Uncreated Light that Gregory Palamas defended — the divine energy that is not created illumination but God's own radiance, accessible to the purified heart. The hesychast tradition's entire spiritual program is oriented toward receiving this light.

The psalmist's desire to come "to the altar of God, to God, my joy and my delight" (43:4) expresses the liturgical heart of Orthodox spirituality. The altar (*trapeza*) in every Orthodox church represents both the throne of God and the tomb of Christ — the place where death is transformed into life and bread into the Body of Christ. To approach the altar is to approach the center of reality itself.

The refrain "Why, my soul, are you downcast? Put your hope in God" (43:5) models the practice Orthodox spiritual directors call *logismos* — the rational engagement with one's own thoughts and feelings in the light of faith. Rather than being passively swept along by emotional states, the faithful are taught to address their souls with the truth of God's promises, redirecting the heart from despair toward hope.
