Psalm 42's opening cry — "As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you, my God" (42:1) — has been central to Orthodox baptismal liturgy since the earliest centuries. During the catechumenate, this psalm expressed the convert's burning desire for the sacramental waters that would unite them with Christ. The connection between spiritual thirst and the waters of baptism is not incidental but theologically essential in Orthodox understanding.

The psalmist's memory of "going with the multitude, leading the procession to the house of God, with shouts of joy and praise" (42:4) resonates with the Orthodox emphasis on communal worship as the primary context of spiritual life. Orthodoxy has always resisted the privatization of faith; the individual's relationship with God is mediated through and sustained by the worshipping community. Exile from the liturgical assembly is a genuine form of spiritual suffering.

"Deep calls to deep in the roar of your waterfalls" (42:7) is interpreted by Orthodox mystical theology as describing the encounter between the depths of the human soul and the depths of God's being. Maximus the Confessor taught that the human person is a microcosm, containing within itself the capacity to commune with the infinite God. The "deep" of the human heart calls out to the "deep" of divine love, and this mutual calling is the essence of prayer.
