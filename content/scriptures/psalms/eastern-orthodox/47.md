Psalm 47 celebrates God's ascension to his throne, and Orthodox liturgical tradition assigns it to the Feast of the Ascension. "God has ascended amid shouts of joy, the LORD amid the sounding of trumpets" (47:5). The Ascension is not understood as Christ's absence but as his enthronement — the moment when glorified human nature is seated at the right hand of the Father. In Orthodox theology, the Ascension completes the work of deification: human nature is now enthroned in the Godhead.

The psalm's declaration that "God reigns over the nations" (47:8) is visually expressed in every Orthodox church through the icon of Christ Pantocrator in the dome. This icon — Christ the Almighty Ruler — proclaims the universal sovereignty that Psalm 47 celebrates. The faithful who gaze upward during the Divine Liturgy behold the One whom the psalm commands all peoples to praise.

The eschatological vision of "the nobles of the nations" gathering as "the people of the God of Abraham" (47:9) anticipates the Orthodox understanding of the Church as the new Israel, encompassing all nations. Orthodox missiology, from the evangelization of the Slavs by Cyril and Methodius to contemporary mission in Africa and Asia, is driven by this psalm's vision of universal worship under the one God who is "King of all the earth" (47:7).
