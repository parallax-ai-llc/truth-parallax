## The Light of God's Face Upon All Nations

Psalm 67's prayer that God would "make his face to shine upon us" (v. 1) connects deeply with Orthodox theology of divine light. The uncreated light that the apostles witnessed at the Transfiguration is the same light that this psalm invokes — God's radiant presence illuminating His people. St. Gregory Palamas defended the reality of this light against those who would reduce it to metaphor, and the psalm's prayer for God's face to shine is understood in the Palamite tradition as a petition for the vision of uncreated divine glory.

The refrain "Let the peoples praise you, O God; let all the peoples praise you!" (vv. 3, 5) expresses the missionary vision that has shaped Orthodox evangelism from the apostolic era through Saints Cyril and Methodius (apostles to the Slavs) to modern Orthodox missions. Unlike Western missionary movements often associated with colonial expansion, Orthodox mission has traditionally emphasized the translation of liturgy and Scripture into local languages, making the worship of God accessible to every people in their own tongue — a practice that fulfills this psalm's inclusive vision.

The psalm's harvest imagery — "The earth has yielded its increase" (v. 6) — is incorporated into Orthodox agricultural blessings and the Feast of the Transfiguration, when the first fruits of the harvest are blessed. Orthodox theology sees the blessing of first fruits as a sanctification of the material order, acknowledging that the earth's productivity is God's gift. The psalm's final verse, praying that "all the ends of the earth" would fear God (v. 7), expresses the Orthodox conviction that mission is not complete until every corner of creation is drawn into the worship of the Trinity.
