Psalm 38 is one of the seven Penitential Psalms and occupies a central place in Orthodox Great Lenten worship. The psalm's raw honesty about the consequences of sin — "My wounds fester and are loathsome because of my sinful folly" (38:5) — reflects the Orthodox conviction that repentance must begin with unflinching self-knowledge. The Great Canon of Saint Andrew of Crete, chanted during the first week of Great Lent, mirrors this psalm's anguished self-examination.

The physical suffering described — "I am feeble and utterly crushed; I groan in anguish of heart" (38:8) — illustrates the Orthodox understanding that sin disorders the whole person, body and soul. Orthodox anthropology teaches that the human person is a psychosomatic unity; what wounds the soul also wounds the body. Conversely, the healing offered through confession and communion restores the whole person.

The psalm's closing cry — "Come quickly to help me, my Lord and my Savior" (38:22) — is echoed in the Jesus Prayer: "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner." This ceaseless prayer of the heart, central to Orthodox spirituality, distills the entire movement of Psalm 38 into a single breath: the honest acknowledgment of sin and the confident appeal to Christ's mercy. Both the psalm and the Jesus Prayer teach that salvation comes not through self-improvement but through crying out to the Savior.
