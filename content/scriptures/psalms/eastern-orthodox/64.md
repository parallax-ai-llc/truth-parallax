## God's Arrow Against the Schemers

Psalm 64's depiction of enemies who "whet their tongues like swords" and "aim bitter words like arrows" (vv. 3-4) is read in Orthodox ascetical tradition as describing the attacks of the passions and demonic forces that assail the soul in prayer. St. Evagrius Ponticus classified the various "arrows" of the enemy — thoughts of anger, lust, pride, and despair — and taught monks to respond with specific scriptural verses, using the Psalms as spiritual weapons. This psalm provides both diagnosis (the nature of the attack) and remedy (confidence in God's counter-arrow).

The principle of divine reversal — "God shoots his arrow at them; they are wounded suddenly" and "their own tongues are turned against them" (vv. 7-8) — reflects the Orthodox conviction that evil is ultimately self-defeating. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that evil has no substance of its own; it is a privation of good, and therefore carries within itself the seeds of its own destruction. The psalm dramatizes this metaphysical truth: the wicked's weapons recoil upon themselves, demonstrating that malice cannot ultimately prevail against divine truth.

The psalm's conclusion — "Let the righteous one rejoice in the LORD and take refuge in him" (v. 10) — directs the faithful to respond to God's vindication not with triumphalism but with deepened trust and joy. Orthodox worship consistently follows this pattern: the commemoration of God's victories over evil (celebrated in feast days and hymnography) leads not to boasting but to grateful praise. The "upright in heart" who "exult" are those whose inner disposition has been purified through prayer and ascetical struggle, enabling them to rejoice rightly in God's justice.
