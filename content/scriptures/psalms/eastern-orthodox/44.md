Psalm 44 is a communal lament of God's people suffering despite their faithfulness, and Orthodox theology reads it through the experience of the persecuted Church. "For your sake we face death all day long" (44:22) — words that describe the reality of Orthodox Christians under Ottoman rule, Soviet persecution, and continuing oppression in many parts of the world. This psalm has been the prayer of the Orthodox Church in its darkest hours.

The psalm's unflinching honesty — "You gave us up to be devoured like sheep" (44:11) — is not read as accusation against God but as the bold speech of covenant partners. Orthodox liturgical prayer includes moments of such boldness, particularly in the hymns of Holy Saturday, which dare to describe God as "sleeping" while Christ lies in the tomb. This is not irreverence but the deepest form of trust.

The closing cry, "Rise up and help us; rescue us because of your unfailing love" (44:26), is understood eschatologically in Orthodox theology. The Church awaits the final vindication of God's justice at the Second Coming, when every wrong will be set right and every tear wiped away. Until that day, the faithful continue to cry out with the psalmist, holding fast to the covenant promises even when history seems to contradict them.
