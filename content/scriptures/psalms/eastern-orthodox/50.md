Psalm 50 presents a divine theophany — "Our God comes and will not be silent" (50:3) — that Orthodox theology connects to the Second Coming. The psalm's imagery of fire and tempest echoes the prophetic visions of divine judgment and anticipates the eschatological renewal of all things. Orthodox iconography of the Last Judgment draws on precisely this kind of theophanic language.

God's declaration — "I have no need of a bull from your stall" (50:9) — is read by Orthodox theology not as rejecting sacrifice but as demanding the interior disposition that gives sacrifice meaning. The Divine Liturgy is understood as the perfect sacrifice precisely because it is Christ's own self-offering, not a human attempt to appease God. The faithful are called to unite their hearts to Christ's sacrifice, making the Eucharistic offering a genuine "sacrifice of thanksgiving" (50:14).

The psalm's rebuke of the wicked who "recite my laws" but "hate my instruction" (50:16-17) speaks to the Orthodox concern with the gap between liturgical participation and moral transformation. The Fathers consistently warned against reducing Orthodoxy to mere ritual observance. John Chrysostom declared that the true liturgy extends beyond the church doors into acts of mercy and justice. The psalm's closing promise — "to the blameless I will show my salvation" (50:23) — affirms that authentic worship and ethical living are inseparable in God's economy.
