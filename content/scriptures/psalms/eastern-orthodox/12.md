Psalm 12 (Psalm 11 in the Septuagint) contrasts the corruption of human speech with the flawless purity of God's words. Orthodox theology, which holds a high view of the *logos* (word) as reflecting the divine Logos (Christ the Word of God), sees in this psalm a meditation on the ontological significance of language. Human words are meant to participate in the truth of the Logos, but sin corrupts them into instruments of deception. The "flattering lips" and "double heart" of verse 2 describe the fragmentation of the person that sin causes — a division between interior and exterior that is the opposite of the *integrity* (*holoklēria*) that characterizes the saints.

The image of God's words as "silver refined in a furnace, purified seven times" (12:6) is understood in Orthodox tradition as describing not only Sacred Scripture but the entire deposit of Holy Tradition — the living transmission of the faith through the councils, the Fathers, the liturgy, and the canonical Scriptures. The Orthodox Church does not separate Scripture from Tradition but views both as the single, pure word of God preserved by the Holy Spirit in the life of the Church. The sevenfold purification suggests the perfection of the Holy Spirit's work in guarding this deposit.

God's promise to "arise" because "the poor are plundered" and "the needy groan" (12:5) connects to the Orthodox theology of divine *kenosis* (self-emptying) — God enters human history precisely at the point of greatest need. The Incarnation itself is the ultimate fulfillment of this promise: God arose from His throne and descended into the depths of human suffering to raise the poor and oppressed to share in divine life. Every liturgical celebration of the Eucharist is an encounter with this God who arises for the sake of His people.
