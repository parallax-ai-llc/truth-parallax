Psalm 4 is used in Orthodox evening worship, complementing Psalm 3's place in the morning office. The closing verse — "In peace I will both lie down and sleep, for you alone, O Lord, make me dwell in safety" (4:8) — appears in the prayers before sleep (*Apodeipnon*/Compline) that Orthodox Christians are encouraged to pray nightly. The rhythm of morning and evening psalmody structures the Orthodox day around the sanctification of time, transforming ordinary moments into occasions of prayer.

The injunction "Be angry, and do not sin; ponder in your own hearts on your beds, and be silent" (4:4) is important in Orthodox ascetical theology. The desert fathers, particularly Evagrius Ponticus and St. John Cassian, developed extensive teachings on the management of the passions (*pathe*), of which anger (*orgē*) is among the most destructive. The psalm's counsel to reflect silently rather than act impulsively aligns with the Orthodox practice of *nepsis* (watchfulness) — the disciplined awareness of one's inner states that is cultivated through the Jesus Prayer and hesychastic prayer.

The verse "You have put more gladness in my heart than they have when their grain and wine abound" (4:7) speaks to the Orthodox understanding of spiritual joy as distinct from worldly pleasure. The Fathers distinguished between *hēdonē* (sensory pleasure, often associated with the Fall) and *chara* (spiritual joy, a fruit of the Holy Spirit). The gladness God places in the heart is a foretaste of the eschatological joy of the Kingdom — the joy that Christ promised "no one will take from you" (John 16:22). This inner joy, independent of external circumstances, is a hallmark of Orthodox sanctity, visible in the radiant faces of saints depicted in iconography.
