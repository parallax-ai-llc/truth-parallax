Psalm 5 is a morning prayer that Orthodox tradition has incorporated into the cycle of daily worship. The verse "O Lord, in the morning you hear my voice; in the morning I prepare a sacrifice for you and watch" (5:3) establishes the ancient Christian practice of morning prayer as a spiritual sacrifice. Orthodox liturgical theology understands all prayer as participation in Christ's eternal intercession before the Father, and the morning offering described in Psalm 5 is thus joined to the heavenly liturgy celebrated by the angels and saints.

The psalm's declaration that God "hates all evildoers" (5:5) is read in Orthodox theology through the distinction between God's essence and His energies, as developed by St. Gregory Palamas. God's hatred of evil is an expression of His uncreated energies — His active opposition to everything that corrupts and destroys His creation. This is not a passion or emotion in the human sense but an expression of divine holiness that is simultaneously judgment for the wicked and purifying fire for those being saved. The same divine energy that destroys sin also deifies the saints.

The petition "Lead me, O Lord, in your righteousness" (5:8) resonates with the Orthodox understanding of salvation as a journey — a *podvig* (spiritual struggle) requiring ongoing divine guidance. The Orthodox faithful are not saved by a single moment of decision but are "being saved" (*sōzomenoi*, 1 Corinthians 1:18) through lifelong cooperation with divine grace (*synergeia*). The psalm's plea for guidance is thus the daily prayer of every Orthodox Christian walking the path of *theosis*, seeking to be led not by personal judgment alone but by the Holy Spirit working through the Church, the Scriptures, the Fathers, and the spiritual father or mother who guides the soul.
