## The Soul's Burning Desire for God

Psalm 63 is prescribed for Matins on most days of the week in the Orthodox liturgical cycle, making it one of the most frequently recited psalms in the tradition. Its opening — "O God, you are my God; earnestly I seek you; my soul thirsts for you" (v. 1) — expresses the fundamental orientation of the baptized soul toward God that Orthodox theology calls *eros* in its sanctified sense. St. Maximus the Confessor taught that divine eros — the soul's passionate love for God — is the purified form of the desire implanted in human nature at creation.

The declaration "your steadfast love is better than life" (v. 3) has been central to Orthodox martyrology. The cloud of witnesses who chose death over apostasy — from the early martyrs through the New Martyrs under Ottoman and Soviet persecution — lived this verse with ultimate seriousness. The Orthodox veneration of martyrs is not merely historical commemoration but liturgical participation in their witness, and this psalm's radical prioritization of God's love above life itself provides the spiritual foundation for their sacrifice.

The psalm's nocturnal meditation — "I meditate on you in the watches of the night" (v. 6) — corresponds to the Orthodox monastic practice of rising for prayer during the night. The midnight office (*Mesonyktikon*) and the vigil services give liturgical structure to this psalm's devotional practice. St. Isaac the Syrian described the night hours as the time when the soul is most receptive to divine illumination, free from the distractions and engagements of daily life. The psalm's image of God's "right hand" upholding the soul (v. 8) assures the night-prayer that divine support continues even in the darkness, and that morning praise will follow the night's vigil, as resurrection follows the tomb.
