Psalm 24 (Psalm 23 in Septuagint/Vulgate) has been associated in Catholic tradition with the Feast of the Ascension, when Christ the King of Glory entered the heavenly sanctuary. The antiphonal exchange — "Lift up your heads, O gates! Who is this King of glory?" (24:7-8) — was imagined by the Church Fathers as a dialogue between the ascending Christ and the angelic powers guarding the gates of heaven. St. Augustine envisioned the angels astonished at a human nature ascending to the divine throne, prompting their repeated question, "Who is this King of glory?"

The entrance requirements of verses 3-4 — "clean hands and a pure heart" — connect to Catholic sacramental discipline regarding the reception of Holy Communion. Canon law requires that Catholics approach the Eucharist in the state of grace, having been absolved of mortal sin through the sacrament of Reconciliation. The "clean hands and pure heart" are not achieved by human effort alone but are the fruit of sacramental grace — the cleansing of baptism, the ongoing purification of confession, and the sanctification of the Eucharist itself.

The cosmic declaration that opens the psalm — "The earth is the Lord's and the fullness thereof" (24:1) — undergirds Catholic social teaching on the universal destination of goods. If all creation belongs to God, then human ownership is stewardship, not absolute dominion. This principle, articulated in *Rerum Novarum* and reaffirmed in every subsequent social encyclical, challenges both collectivist and individualist ideologies by insisting that property rights are real but subordinate to the common good. The psalm thus moves seamlessly from creation theology to entrance liturgy to eschatological hope — a sweep characteristic of Catholic worship at its best.
