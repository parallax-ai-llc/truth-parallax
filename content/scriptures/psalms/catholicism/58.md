## Divine Judgment on Corrupt Authority

Psalm 58 confronts unjust judges with the question "Do you indeed decree what is right, you gods?" (v. 1). Catholic social teaching, rooted in the Thomistic tradition of natural law, has drawn on such psalms to articulate the principle that human authority is legitimate only insofar as it participates in God's justice. When rulers pervert justice, they violate not merely human convention but the divine order itself. The Compendium of the Social Doctrine of the Church echoes this psalm's insistence that political authority is accountable to a higher standard.

The imprecatory language of this psalm — asking that the wicked be dissolved like a snail, swept away like stillborn children (vv. 8-9) — has been interpreted by Catholic exegetes through the lens of the *sensus plenior*. St. Augustine reads these images not as prayers for literal physical harm but as calls for the destruction of wickedness itself. The Church prays for the conversion of sinners while simultaneously affirming that unrepentant evil will be judged. The psalm thus expresses a double truth held in tension within Catholic moral theology: absolute commitment to justice and absolute commitment to the possibility of mercy.

The psalm's conclusion — "Mankind will say, 'Surely there is a reward for the righteous; surely there is a God who judges on earth'" (v. 11) — affirms the Catholic teaching on divine retribution. The Catechism teaches that God "renders to each person according to his works" (CCC 1021-1022), and this psalm provides scriptural foundation for the doctrine of particular judgment. Catholic faith holds that God's justice will ultimately be vindicated, encouraging the faithful to trust in divine equity even when human justice fails.
