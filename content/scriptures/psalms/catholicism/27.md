Psalm 27 is prayed regularly in the Liturgy of the Hours, where its opening confession — "The Lord is my light and my salvation" (27:1) — serves as a powerful antiphon for the faithful. Catholic tradition reads this psalm as expressing the soul's fundamental orientation toward God, what the Catechism calls the *desiderium Dei* — the desire for God implanted in every human heart (CCC 27).

The psalmist's singular desire — "to gaze upon the beauty of the LORD and to seek him in his temple" (27:4) — is understood in Catholic mystical theology as the beginning of contemplative prayer. Saints such as Teresa of Avila and John of the Cross described this gazing upon divine beauty as the highest form of prayer, one that transforms the soul into the likeness of what it beholds. The Catechism teaches that contemplation is "a gaze of faith, fixed on Jesus" (CCC 2715).

The psalm's plea, "Do not hide your face from me" (27:9), speaks to the Catholic understanding of spiritual desolation described by Ignatius of Loyola. Seasons of darkness are not abandonment but purification, drawing the soul into deeper dependence on God. The closing verse — "Wait for the LORD; be strong and take heart" (27:14) — encourages the virtue of patience (*patientia*), which Catholic tradition counts among the fruits of the Holy Spirit.
