Psalm 16 (Psalm 15 in Septuagint/Vulgate) is among the most important christological psalms in Catholic liturgy. Peter's Pentecost sermon (Acts 2:25-31) established the authoritative interpretation: since David died and his tomb is known, the promise "you will not abandon my soul to Sheol, or let your holy one see corruption" (16:10) must refer to Christ's resurrection. The Catholic liturgy assigns this psalm to the Easter Vigil and to feasts of martyrs, connecting Christ's victory over death to the hope of all the faithful.

The confession "I say to the Lord, 'You are my Lord; I have no good apart from you'" (16:2) has been treasured in Catholic mystical tradition. St. Augustine heard in this verse the soul's fundamental orientation toward God — the *desiderium naturale* (natural desire) for the beatific vision that Catholic theology affirms in every human heart. The psalm's satisfaction in God alone as one's "chosen portion" and "cup" (16:5) resonates with the Eucharistic chalice: the cup of Christ's Blood is the ultimate portion in which the Catholic faithful find their inheritance.

The closing verses — "in your presence there is fullness of joy; at your right hand are pleasures forevermore" (16:11) — describe the beatific vision toward which all of Catholic spiritual life is oriented. The *Summa Theologiae* (I-II, q. 3, a. 8) argues that ultimate human happiness consists in the vision of God's essence, and Psalm 16:11 is a primary scriptural warrant. The psalm's "path of life" that leads to this vision is understood as the way of the sacraments, prayer, and virtue that the Church maps for the faithful — a journey begun in baptism and consummated in eternity.
