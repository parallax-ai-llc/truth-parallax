## The Church's Cry Amid Desolation

Psalm 74's lament over the destruction of the sanctuary — "the enemy has destroyed everything in the sanctuary" (v. 3) — has been read by Catholic tradition as applicable to every period in which the Church has faced devastation, whether through persecution, heresy, or scandal. The psalm's question "O God, why do you cast us off forever?" (v. 1) gives voice to the anguish that the Church's members experience when her institutions are attacked and her witness seems eclipsed. The Fathers applied this psalm to the Arian crisis, to the Islamic conquests, and to every era of ecclesial trial.

The creation theology in the psalm's center — "You divided the sea by your might; you broke the heads of the sea monsters on the waters" (v. 13) — is read typologically as referring to baptism (the sea divided) and Christ's victory over evil (the monsters defeated). Catholic exegesis, following the patristic tradition of reading Old Testament cosmology through the lens of the paschal mystery, sees in these verses a confession that the God who conquered chaos at creation conquers it still through Christ's death and resurrection.

The psalm's repeated appeal to the covenant — "Have regard for the covenant, for the dark places of the land are full of the habitations of violence" (v. 20) — grounds the Church's prayer in God's irrevocable promises. Catholic theology, following the Second Vatican Council's *Dei Verbum*, understands the covenant as progressing through history — from Noah through Abraham, Moses, and David to its definitive fulfillment in Christ. The psalm's plea is therefore heard by the Church as ultimately answered in the New Covenant sealed by Christ's blood, which guarantees that God's presence with His people will never finally be withdrawn, no matter how desolate present circumstances may appear.
