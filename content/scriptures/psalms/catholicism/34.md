Psalm 34 occupies a uniquely privileged place in Catholic Eucharistic theology because of verse 8: "Taste and see that the LORD is good." This verse is sung as the communion antiphon in the Roman Missal, directly connecting the act of receiving the Eucharist with the psalmist's invitation. In Catholic understanding, the faithful literally taste and see God's goodness in the Body and Blood of Christ.

The psalm's wisdom instruction — "The LORD is close to the brokenhearted and saves those who are crushed in spirit" (34:18) — informs Catholic pastoral care and the Church's preferential option for the poor. The Catechism teaches that Christ identified himself with the suffering (Matthew 25:40), making service to the afflicted an encounter with Christ himself. This psalm assures the faithful that God's presence is not reserved for the powerful but is given especially to the lowly.

David's teaching on the "fear of the LORD" (34:11) is understood in Catholic theology as the gift of the Holy Spirit identified by Isaiah (11:2). This reverential awe is not servile terror but filial love — the response of a child who fears to offend a beloved parent. Aquinas taught that this gift of fear perfects the virtue of hope, keeping the believer in humble dependence on God's mercy rather than presumptuous self-confidence.
