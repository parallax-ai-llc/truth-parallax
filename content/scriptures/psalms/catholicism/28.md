Psalm 28 presents a powerful image of intercession that resonates with Catholic theology of prayer. David lifts his hands "toward your Most Holy Place" (28:2), a gesture preserved in the *orans* posture used by the priest during the Eucharistic Prayer. Catholic liturgical practice maintains this ancient connection between bodily posture and spiritual supplication.

The psalm's movement from desperate plea to confident praise (28:6-7) mirrors the structure of the Mass itself — from the Penitential Act through the Liturgy of the Word to the joyful celebration of the Eucharist. Catholic theology sees in this progression the pattern of all authentic worship: acknowledging human need, receiving God's word, and responding with thanksgiving.

The closing image of God as shepherd who carries his people forever (28:9) connects to Catholic ecclesiology's understanding of pastoral authority. Christ the Good Shepherd exercises his care through the ordained ministry of bishops and priests, who are called to shepherd the flock entrusted to them. Pope Francis has frequently emphasized that pastors should "smell like the sheep" — embodying the intimate care this psalm envisions.
