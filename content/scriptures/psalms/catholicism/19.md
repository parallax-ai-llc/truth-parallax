Psalm 19 (Psalm 18 in Septuagint/Vulgate) has been central to Catholic theology of revelation. The First Vatican Council cited the psalm's opening — "The heavens declare the glory of God" (19:1) — in its teaching that God can be known through created things (Dei Filius, Chapter 2). This natural revelation, accessible to reason, is the first step in what Catholic theology calls the *praeambula fidei* (preambles of faith). The Catechism (CCC 32) affirms that "the world and man attest that they contain within themselves neither their first principle nor their final end, but rather that they participate in Being itself."

The psalm's second section on the Torah (19:7-11) is read by Catholic theology as pointing to the fullness of revelation in Christ and the Church. The "law" (*torah*) that is "perfect, reviving the soul" (19:7) finds its completion in the New Law of the Gospel, which St. Thomas Aquinas describes as principally the grace of the Holy Spirit received through faith in Christ (*Summa Theologiae* I-II, q. 106). The psalm's praise of divine instruction resonates with the Catholic emphasis on the Magisterium as the authentic interpreter of both Scripture and Tradition.

The personal prayer that concludes the psalm — "Let the words of my mouth and the meditation of my heart be acceptable in your sight, O Lord, my rock and my redeemer" (19:14) — is frequently used in Catholic liturgy as a preparation for preaching. The word *go'el* (redeemer, kinsman-redeemer) carries covenantal and familial connotations that Catholic theology connects to Christ's assumption of human nature: He became our kinsman through the Incarnation in order to become our Redeemer through the Cross. The Blessed Virgin Mary, through whom this kinship was established, is thus intimately connected to the redemptive work this psalm celebrates.
