## The Miserere: Prayer of Contrition

Psalm 51 — known by its Latin incipit *Miserere mei, Deus* — holds a singular place in Catholic liturgy and spirituality. It is the preeminent penitential psalm, prayed daily in the Liturgy of the Hours at Lauds on Fridays and during Lent, and it forms the heart of the Church's penitential practice. St. Augustine's *Enarrationes in Psalmos* devotes one of its longest commentaries to this psalm, reading it as David's model of sacramental confession: the sinner approaches God not with excuses but with total honesty — "I know my transgressions, and my sin is ever before me" (v. 3).

The psalm's theology undergirds the Catholic sacrament of Reconciliation. The plea "Wash me thoroughly from my iniquity, and cleanse me from my sin" (v. 2) speaks of purification that the Church understands as effected through sacramental absolution. Verse 5, "Behold, I was brought forth in iniquity," has been cited by the Council of Trent as supporting the doctrine of original sin transmitted through generation. The petition "Create in me a clean heart" (v. 10) is understood not as a one-time conversion but as an ongoing process of sanctification cooperating with grace — what Catholic theology calls *synergeia*.

The psalm's conclusion, "The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit; a broken and contrite heart, O God, you will not despise" (v. 17), does not negate liturgical sacrifice in Catholic reading but deepens it. The Catechism of the Catholic Church teaches that exterior sacrifice is authentic only when accompanied by interior contrition. The final verses (vv. 18-19), praying for the rebuilding of Jerusalem's walls and the restoration of proper sacrifice, have been read ecclesiologically — the Church is the new Jerusalem where the sacrifice of the Eucharist is continually offered, and the contrite heart is its necessary companion.
