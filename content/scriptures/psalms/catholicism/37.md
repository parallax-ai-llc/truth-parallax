Psalm 37 is a wisdom psalm that Catholic moral theology has used extensively to address the problem of evil and the virtue of patience. David's counsel — "Be still before the LORD and wait patiently for him; do not fret when people succeed in their ways" (37:7) — is understood as an exercise of the theological virtue of hope. Catholic teaching distinguishes hope from mere optimism: hope is the confident expectation that God will fulfill his promises, even when circumstances suggest otherwise.

The psalm's promise that "the meek will inherit the land" (37:11) — quoted by Jesus in the Beatitudes (Matthew 5:5) — is central to Catholic social teaching's understanding of justice. The "land" is read eschatologically as the new creation, but Catholic tradition also applies it to present social structures: the meek, those who renounce violence and trust in God, are the true inheritors of a just society.

Catholic theology's understanding of merit finds support in this psalm's repeated assurances that God rewards the righteous (37:25-26). The Council of Trent taught that the justified truly merit eternal life through works performed in grace — not because human works are intrinsically worthy, but because God has graciously promised to reward them. The psalmist's observation that "I was young and now I am old, yet I have never seen the righteous forsaken" (37:25) expresses this confidence in God's faithfulness to those who cooperate with his grace.
