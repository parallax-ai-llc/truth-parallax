Psalm 30 is a thanksgiving for healing that Catholic tradition has long associated with the Easter Vigil and the sacrament of the Anointing of the Sick. "O LORD my God, I cried to you for help, and you healed me. You brought me up from the grave" (30:2-3). The Church's ministry to the sick draws on this psalm's confidence that God hears the cry of the suffering and restores them — whether to bodily health or to the fullness of life in the resurrection.

The celebrated verse "Weeping may stay for the night, but rejoicing comes in the morning" (30:5) has profound resonance with Catholic theology of the Paschal Mystery. The "night" of Holy Saturday — Christ's descent among the dead — gives way to the "morning" of Easter. Every experience of suffering, when united to Christ's passion, participates in this movement from death to life. Catholic theology calls this the *mysterium paschale*.

The psalm's confession of false security — "When I felt secure, I said, 'I will never be shaken'" (30:6) — speaks to the Catholic understanding of the virtue of humility. The saints consistently taught that spiritual presumption is among the gravest dangers to the soul. Thomas Aquinas identified presumption as a sin against hope, a false confidence that bypasses the need for ongoing conversion and dependence on grace.
