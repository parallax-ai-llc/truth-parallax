Psalm 6 is the first of the seven Penitential Psalms, a collection that has shaped Catholic penitential practice since at least the seventh century. These seven psalms (6, 32, 38, 51, 102, 130, 143) were traditionally recited during Lent, on Ash Wednesday, and in preparation for the sacrament of Reconciliation. Pope Innocent III prescribed their use in the early thirteenth century, and they have been recited by saints, popes, and the faithful ever since. The dying Cassiodorus asked that the Penitential Psalms be read to him as he breathed his last.

The psalm's imagery of suffering — "my bones are troubled" (6:2), "my eye wastes away because of grief" (6:7) — has been connected in Catholic theology to the doctrine of purgatory and the purification of the soul. The cry "O Lord, rebuke me not in your anger" (6:1) expresses the soul's recognition that divine correction, while painful, is ultimately medicinal. The Council of Trent affirmed that temporal punishment for sin may remain even after sacramental absolution, and the Penitential Psalms have been used as prayers for the faithful departed undergoing purification.

The turning point — "The Lord has heard the sound of my weeping" (6:8) — affirms that God responds to genuine contrition, a principle central to the sacrament of Reconciliation. Catholic theology teaches that perfect contrition (sorrow motivated by love of God) obtains forgiveness even before sacramental confession, though confession remains necessary by divine precept. The psalm's movement from anguish to assurance mirrors the experience of sacramental reconciliation itself: the penitent approaches burdened with sin, confesses with tears, and departs with the assurance of divine pardon and the peace of absolution.
