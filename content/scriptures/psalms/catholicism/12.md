Psalm 12 (Psalm 11 in Septuagint/Vulgate) contrasts human deceit with the purity of God's Word. Catholic theology reads the declaration that God's words are "like silver refined in a furnace on the ground, purified seven times" (12:6) within the broader framework of divine revelation that includes both Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition, transmitted faithfully through the Magisterium. The purity of God's Word is preserved not only in the written text but in the Church's living interpretation guided by the Holy Spirit.

The psalm's lament over flattering lips and double hearts (12:2) has been applied by Catholic moral theology to the sin of duplicity — saying one thing while meaning another. The eighth commandment as explicated in the Catechism (CCC 2464-2513) treats truthfulness as a fundamental moral obligation, and the saints are held up as exemplars of integrity. St. Catherine of Siena, a Doctor of the Church, was known for her fearless truth-telling even to popes, embodying the psalm's implicit call for speech that matches the purity of God's own words.

God's promise to arise "because the poor are plundered, because the needy groan" (12:5) connects this psalm to Catholic social doctrine. The Church's teaching on the universal destination of goods — that the earth's resources are intended for all humanity, not merely the powerful few — addresses the economic oppression this psalm describes. Catholic institutions worldwide — from the Society of St. Vincent de Paul to Catholic Relief Services — embody the divine response to the groaning of the needy that the psalm anticipates.
