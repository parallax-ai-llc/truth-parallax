## From Doubt to Divine Communion

Psalm 73 addresses what Catholic theology calls the *mysterium iniquitatis* — the mystery of why the wicked prosper while the righteous suffer. Asaph's near-stumble into despair — "my feet had almost stumbled... for I was envious of the arrogant" (vv. 2-3) — is read by the Fathers as a universal temptation confronting every believer. St. Augustine devoted extensive commentary to this psalm, finding in it a paradigm for the interior journey from worldly envy to divine contemplation. The "sanctuary of God" (v. 17) where understanding comes is the Church's liturgy, where earthly perspectives are replaced by divine ones.

The psalm's turning point — "until I went into the sanctuary of God; then I discerned their end" (v. 17) — has been foundational for Catholic teaching on the relationship between worship and understanding. The liturgy is not merely an expression of belief but a source of illumination; in the sacred space, the faithful receive the perspective they could not achieve through human reasoning alone. The Catechism teaches that liturgical celebration "reveals the prayer of the Holy Spirit" and transforms the worshiper's understanding of reality.

The psalm's climax — "Whom have I in heaven but you? And there is nothing on earth that I desire besides you" (v. 25) — has been read by Catholic mystics as an anticipation of the beatific vision. St. John of the Cross cited this verse as expressing the soul's union with God in which all created desires are transcended. Catholic eschatology teaches that the beatific vision — the direct, face-to-face contemplation of God — is the ultimate fulfillment of the desire expressed in this verse. The psalm's final declaration, "it is good to be near God" (v. 28), summarizes the entire Catholic understanding of salvation: ultimate good is proximity to God, and the purpose of the Church, her sacraments, and her teaching is to draw souls into that divine nearness.
