## Betrayal and the Solace of Divine Faithfulness

Psalm 55 has been read throughout Catholic tradition as a prophetic voice of Christ betrayed by Judas. The agonizing description — "It is not an enemy who taunts me... But it is you, a man, my equal, my companion, my familiar friend. We used to take sweet counsel together; within God's house we walked in the throng" (vv. 12-14) — is cited by the Church Fathers as anticipating the betrayal at the Last Supper. St. John Chrysostom preached that these words gain their deepest meaning on the lips of Christ, who suffered betrayal from within His own circle of chosen apostles.

The psalm's expression of inner turmoil — "My heart is in anguish within me; the terrors of death have fallen upon me. Fear and trembling come upon me, and horror overwhelms me" (vv. 4-5) — is read in Catholic devotion as paralleling Christ's agony in Gethsemane. The desire to "fly away and be at rest" (v. 6) expresses a longing that the Fathers interpret as the soul's desire for heaven, the true rest that lies beyond earthly suffering. Catholic mystical tradition, from St. John of the Cross to St. Therese of Lisieux, has drawn on such psalmic expressions to articulate the dark night of the soul.

The exhortation "Cast your burden on the LORD, and he will sustain you" (v. 22) is prayed within the Liturgy of the Hours as an act of *fiducia* — trusting abandonment to divine providence. Catholic spiritual directors have consistently pointed to this verse as the foundation of the spirituality of abandonment taught by Jean-Pierre de Caussade and later by St. Therese. The psalm teaches that even the most devastating human betrayal does not sever the relationship between the faithful soul and its God, who "will never permit the righteous to be moved."
