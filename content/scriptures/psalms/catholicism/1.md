Psalm 1 has been read in Catholic tradition as the entrance to the entire Psalter and a summary of the spiritual life. The Church Fathers — particularly Augustine in his *Enarrationes in Psalmos* — interpreted the "blessed man" of verse 1 as ultimately Christ Himself, the one who perfectly avoided the counsel of the wicked. This christological reading, characteristic of patristic exegesis, sees every psalm as pointing to Christ, the Church, or both. The threefold descent into sin (walking, standing, sitting in 1:1) was read by the Fathers as a progression from temptation to habit to obstinacy.

The tree "planted by streams of water" (1:3) has rich sacramental resonance in Catholic theology. The streams recall baptismal waters, and the tree's fruitfulness is connected to the life of grace sustained by the sacraments. The Liturgy of the Hours incorporates Psalm 1 at the beginning of the weekly cycle, positioning it as the first psalm of Sunday Matins in the traditional breviary. St. Jerome noted that the psalm's emphasis on meditating on the law "day and night" (1:2) established the foundation for the monastic practice of *lectio divina*.

Catholic moral theology draws on the psalm's stark contrast between two ways — the way of the righteous and the way of the wicked — as confirmation that human choices have eternal consequences. The Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 1696) echoes this two-ways motif. Verse 5, "the wicked will not stand in the judgment," has been connected in Catholic teaching to the particular judgment each soul undergoes at death. The psalm thus serves as both an invitation to the contemplative life and a sober reminder of the four last things: death, judgment, heaven, and hell.
