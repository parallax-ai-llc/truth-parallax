Psalm 35 is an imprecatory psalm that Catholic tradition reads through the lens of the Church's theology of just warfare and the communion of saints. David's cry — "Contend, LORD, with those who contend with me" (35:1) — is not personal vendetta but an appeal to divine justice. Catholic moral theology distinguishes between sinful desire for revenge and righteous appeal to God's justice, and the imprecatory psalms model the latter.

The psalm's description of false witnesses (35:11) and betrayal by those David had cared for (35:13-14) resonates with the experience of the Church's martyrs throughout history. Catholic hagiography records countless saints who, like the psalmist, faced unjust persecution from those they had served. The psalm gives voice to the cry of the martyrs under the altar in Revelation 6:10: "How long, Sovereign Lord, holy and true, until you judge?"

Catholic liturgical use of this psalm, particularly during Passiontide, connects David's suffering to Christ's passion. The detail that David's enemies "repay my good with evil" (35:12) prefigures the rejection of Christ by those he came to save. The Fathers of the Church, including Augustine and Jerome, read the entire psalm as spoken in the voice of Christ, the innocent sufferer who entrusts his cause to the Father's righteous judgment.
