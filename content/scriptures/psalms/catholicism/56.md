## God's Tender Care for the Suffering

Psalm 56's confession "When I am afraid, I put my trust in you" (v. 3) has been a mainstay of Catholic devotional life, prayed especially by those facing persecution, illness, or spiritual trial. The Fathers read this psalm Christologically: Christ himself, in his human nature, experienced the full reality of fear and entrusted himself to the Father. The psalm thus becomes a model for every Christian who, united to Christ through baptism, faces fear not with stoic denial but with trusting surrender.

The image of God collecting tears in a bottle (v. 8) has profoundly shaped Catholic piety's understanding of redemptive suffering. The tears are not wasted or forgotten but preserved — an image that resonates with the Catholic theology of offering one's sufferings in union with Christ's passion. Pope St. John Paul II's *Salvifici Doloris* develops this theology, teaching that human suffering, when united to Christ's, participates in the redemptive work. The psalm's assurance that these tears are recorded "in your book" connects to the Catholic understanding of God's providential knowledge and care for each individual soul.

The psalm's refrain, "In God, whose word I praise — in God I trust" (vv. 4, 10-11), is prayed in the Liturgy of the Hours as an expression of the Church's collective faith. Catholic theology emphasizes that such trust is not merely individual but ecclesial — the Church as a whole trusts God's word, and the individual believer is strengthened by this communal faith. The concluding commitment to walk "before God in the light of the living" (v. 13) has been interpreted by the Fathers as a reference to the resurrected life that awaits all who die in Christ.
