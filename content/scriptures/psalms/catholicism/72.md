## The Universal Kingdom of the Messianic King

Psalm 72 occupies a central place in Catholic Epiphany theology. The verse "May the kings of Tarshish and of the coastlands render him tribute; may the kings of Sheba and Seba bring gifts!" (v. 10) has been read since the earliest centuries as prophesying the Magi's visit to the Christ child. The Epiphany liturgy incorporates this psalm, and Catholic iconography of the Adoration of the Magi draws on its imagery of distant kings bearing tribute to the newborn King. The psalm's vision of universal submission to a righteous ruler finds its fulfillment, Catholic theology teaches, in Christ's cosmic lordship.

The psalm's social vision — "He delivers the needy when he calls, the poor and him who has no helper. He has pity on the weak and the needy, and saves the lives of the needy" (vv. 12-13) — has profoundly shaped Catholic social teaching. The preferential option for the poor, articulated by the Latin American bishops at Medellin and Puebla and affirmed by subsequent popes, finds scriptural foundation in this psalm's portrait of the Messianic king whose reign is characterized by justice for the most vulnerable. Pope Benedict XVI, in *Deus Caritas Est*, connects the Church's charitable mission to precisely this biblical vision.

The psalm's concluding doxology — "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Israel, who alone does wondrous things. Blessed be his glorious name forever; may the whole earth be filled with his glory!" (vv. 18-19) — has been incorporated into Catholic liturgical prayer as a model of doxological praise. As the conclusion of Book II of the Psalter, it provides a template for the Gloria and other liturgical acclamations. Catholic theology sees this vision of the whole earth filled with God's glory as the ultimate goal of creation, inaugurated in Christ and progressively realized through the Church's mission until the Parousia.
