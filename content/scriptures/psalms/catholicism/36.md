Psalm 36 presents a stark contrast between human wickedness and divine love that illuminates Catholic theology of grace. The opening portrait of the sinner — "there is no fear of God before his eyes" (36:1) — describes what Catholic moral theology calls the state of mortal sin: a fundamental turning away from God that disorders the entire moral life. Without the fear of God, the sinner flatters himself and loses the ability to recognize evil (36:2).

The psalm's celebration of God's attributes — steadfast love, faithfulness, righteousness, justice (36:5-6) — is understood in Catholic theology as revealing the divine perfections that the soul is called to participate in through grace. The Catechism teaches that "the grace of Christ is the gratuitous gift that God makes to us of his own life, infused by the Holy Spirit into our soul to heal it of sin and to sanctify it" (CCC 1999). God's love does not merely cover sin but transforms the soul.

Verse 9 — "In your light do we see light" — has been foundational for Catholic epistemology and the theology of faith. Augustine drew on this verse to develop his doctrine of divine illumination: all genuine knowledge, whether natural or supernatural, is made possible by participation in God's light. The Catechism affirms that faith is both a human act and a supernatural gift, requiring divine light to perceive what reason alone cannot reach (CCC 153).
