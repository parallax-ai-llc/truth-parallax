Psalm 22 (Psalm 21 in Septuagint/Vulgate) is the psalm of the Passion *par excellence* in Catholic theology and liturgy. The Church assigns it to the Liturgy of the Hours on Good Friday and uses its responsorial form in the Mass of the Lord's Passion. Jesus' cry from the cross — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (22:1; Matthew 27:46) — is understood by Catholic theologians not as evidence of the Trinity's rupture but as Christ's assumption of humanity's experience of alienation from God. In bearing the weight of sin, Christ entered the deepest human darkness while remaining united to the Father in the bond of the Holy Spirit.

The specific details of the passion — "they have pierced my hands and my feet" (22:16), "they divide my garments among them, and for my clothing they cast lots" (22:18) — are read by Catholic tradition as prophetic testimony to the historicity of the crucifixion. The Church Fathers, particularly Justin Martyr in his *Dialogue with Trypho*, used these correspondences in early Christian apologetics. The Stations of the Cross, a Catholic devotional practice, incorporate the psalm's imagery into the meditation on Christ's journey to Calvary.

The psalm's dramatic shift at verse 22 — from suffering to praise, from isolation to congregation — has profound eucharistic significance. "In the midst of the congregation I will praise you" (22:22) is understood as Christ's words to His brethren after the resurrection, and the great assembly envisioned in verses 25-31 is the universal Church gathered at the Eucharistic table. The final phrase — "he has done it" (22:31) — parallels Christ's *tetelestai* ("It is finished," John 19:30), and the Eucharist makes this completed work present in every Mass, extending the psalm's universal worship "to a people yet unborn" across all generations.
