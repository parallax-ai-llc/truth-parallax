Psalm 13 (Psalm 12 in Septuagint/Vulgate) is among the shortest and most intense lament psalms. Its fourfold "How long?" (13:1-2) has been prayed by Catholics through centuries of persecution, plague, and personal suffering. The Church does not discourage lament; rather, the inclusion of such psalms in the Divine Office ensures that the full range of human experience finds expression in official worship. St. Teresa of Ávila, who endured decades of spiritual aridity, taught that honest questioning of God is not a failure of faith but a deepening of it.

Catholic spiritual theology reads the psalm's structure — lament, petition, trust — as a model of the prayer of *desolation* described in Ignatian spirituality. St. Ignatius of Loyola taught that desolation (spiritual dryness, darkness, and turmoil) is a normal part of the spiritual life, to be endured with patience and met with increased prayer. The psalmist's petition "Light up my eyes, lest I sleep the sleep of death" (13:3) resonates with the Ignatian counsel to resist desolation actively by seeking the light of Christ.

The psalm's turn to trust — "But I have trusted in your steadfast love" (13:5) — illustrates what Catholic theology calls the *obsequium fidei*, the obedience of faith that perseveres even when consolation is absent. The Catechism teaches that faith can be lived "in darkness" and that such trials "purify our faith" (CCC 164). The closing declaration, "I will sing to the Lord, because he has dealt bountifully with me" (13:6), anticipates the Eucharistic acclamation — *eucharistia* means thanksgiving — and models the Catholic understanding that gratitude is owed to God not only when blessings are felt but when they are trusted in the darkness.
