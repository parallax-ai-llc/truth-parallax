## Thanksgiving for Creation and Harvest

Psalm 65 is a hymn of thanksgiving that Catholic tradition has associated with the harvest season and with Rogation Days, when the Church prays for the blessing of crops and the fruits of the earth. The psalm's opening declaration that God is the one "who atones for our transgressions" (v. 3) places forgiveness and creation in the same theological frame — a connection Catholic theology maintains through the doctrine of creation's renewal in Christ. Pope Francis's encyclical *Laudato Si'* echoes this psalm's integrated vision, teaching that care for creation and care for the soul are inseparable aspects of the Christian vocation.

The psalm's creation theology — "By awesome deeds you answer us with righteousness, O God of our salvation, the hope of all the ends of the earth" (v. 5) — affirms God as both Creator and Sustainer. Catholic natural theology, building on St. Thomas Aquinas's *Five Ways*, finds in such psalms evidence of the God who can be known through the order and beauty of creation. The parallel between God's mastery over cosmic forces (stilling the seas, v. 7) and His care for agricultural abundance (watering the furrows, v. 10) expresses the Catholic conviction of divine providence operating at every scale.

The psalm's concluding vision of nature's abundance — "the hills gird themselves with joy, the meadows clothe themselves with flocks, the valleys deck themselves with grain, they shout and sing together for joy" (vv. 12-13) — presents creation as a living sacrament of God's generosity. Catholic sacramental theology holds that material reality is the medium through which God communicates grace, and this psalm supports that vision. The earth's fruitfulness is not merely natural but sacramental — a sign and instrument of God's steadfast love poured out upon His creation.
