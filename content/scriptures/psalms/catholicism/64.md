## God's Justice Against Hidden Evil

Psalm 64 confronts the evil of covert malice — enemies who "whet their tongues like swords" and "shoot from ambush at the blameless" (vv. 3-4). Catholic moral theology, drawing on St. Thomas Aquinas, classifies detraction, calumny, and conspiracy among the gravest sins against justice and charity. This psalm gives voice to the victim's prayer and to the Church's confidence that God perceives what humans conceal: "they hold fast their evil purpose; they talk of laying snares secretly, thinking, 'Who can see us?'" (v. 5).

The psalm's central assertion — "But God shoots his arrow at them; they are wounded suddenly" (v. 7) — expresses the Catholic doctrine of divine retribution within history. While Catholic eschatology emphasizes the final judgment as the definitive reckoning, the Church also recognizes God's providential governance within temporal affairs, where injustice often meets its reversal even before the eschaton. The image of the wicked "brought to ruin, with their own tongues turned against them" (v. 8) reflects the principle, articulated by the Fathers, that evil is ultimately self-defeating.

The psalm concludes with universal acknowledgment of God's works and the righteous rejoicing in the Lord (vv. 9-10). Catholic liturgical prayer incorporates this psalm as an expression of the Church's confidence in God's justice, especially in times of persecution or calumny. The communion of saints — including the Church Suffering, the Church Militant, and the Church Triumphant — all share in the psalmist's conviction that God's justice will prevail and that those who take refuge in Him will ultimately exult.
