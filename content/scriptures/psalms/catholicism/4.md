Psalm 4, the evening counterpart to Psalm 3, has been assigned to Compline (Night Prayer) in the Catholic Liturgy of the Hours. The closing verse — "In peace I will both lie down and sleep, for you alone, O Lord, make me dwell in safety" (4:8) — makes it perhaps the most natural bedtime prayer in all of Scripture. The Compline liturgy, one of the Church's most ancient prayer offices, uses this psalm to entrust the night to God's protection, a practice dating to the earliest monastic communities.

St. Ambrose's hymn *Te lucis ante terminum* ("Before the ending of the day"), sung at Compline for centuries, draws on the same themes of Psalm 4: divine protection during the vulnerability of sleep. Catholic spiritual theology reads verse 7 — "You have put more gladness in my heart than they have when their grain and wine abound" — as distinguishing between natural and supernatural joy. The joy of God's presence surpasses all material satisfaction, a teaching developed extensively by St. John of the Cross in his doctrine of detachment from created goods.

Verse 4, "Be angry, and do not sin; ponder in your own hearts on your beds, and be silent," has been important in Catholic moral theology's treatment of the passions. The Thomistic tradition teaches that anger is not inherently sinful — it is a natural passion that becomes sinful only when disordered. The psalm's counsel to channel anger through silent examination of conscience aligns with the Catholic practice of the nightly *examen* of conscience, recommended by St. Ignatius of Loyola in the Spiritual Exercises and practiced by the faithful before sleep as preparation for the sacrament of Reconciliation.
