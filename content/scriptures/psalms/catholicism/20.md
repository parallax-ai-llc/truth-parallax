Psalm 20 (Psalm 19 in Septuagint/Vulgate) is a royal psalm in which the congregation prays for the king's success in battle. Catholic liturgical tradition has applied this psalm to the Church's spiritual warfare and to prayers for those in authority. The prayer "May he grant you your heart's desire and fulfill all your plans" (20:4) has been used in Catholic ordination liturgies and blessings for those undertaking the work of the kingdom.

The decisive verse — "Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the Lord our God" (20:7) — articulates a principle that has shaped Catholic spirituality of detachment and trust in providence. St. Ignatius of Loyola's principle of "holy indifference" — seeking God's will rather than particular outcomes — resonates with this psalm's subordination of military means to divine reliance. The great Catholic missionaries, from St. Francis Xavier to St. Junípero Serra, went forth with minimal resources, trusting in the name of the Lord rather than human provisions.

Catholic reading applies the psalm christologically: the "king" for whom the Church prays is ultimately Christ, whose victory was accomplished not through earthly power but through the Cross. The psalm's liturgical use in Masses for peace and in times of conflict reflects the Catholic conviction that prayer is genuine participation in God's governance of the world. The communion of saints — the living, the dead in purgatory, and the blessed in heaven — joins in this intercessory work, forming a great prayer chorus that extends the psalm's communal intercession across time and eternity.
