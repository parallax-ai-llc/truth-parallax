Psalm 26 holds a significant place in Catholic liturgical tradition as a prayer of the just person approaching the altar. The psalmist's declaration, "I wash my hands in innocence and go around your altar, O LORD" (26:6), is echoed in the *Lavabo* — the ritual hand-washing performed by the priest during the Offertory of the Mass. This ancient liturgical gesture connects every celebration of the Eucharist to David's prayer of integrity.

Catholic moral theology reads this psalm through the lens of sanctifying grace. The psalmist's ability to "walk in integrity" (26:1) is not autonomous moral achievement but cooperation with divine grace. The Council of Trent affirmed that the justified can, with God's help, truly observe the commandments — not by their own strength but through the grace that sanctification confers.

The psalm's emphasis on loving "the house where you live, the place where your glory dwells" (26:8) resonates with Catholic sacramental theology, which understands the church building as a sacred space where God's presence is made tangibly available through the Blessed Sacrament. The psalmist's desire to dwell near God's glory anticipates the Catholic understanding of the tabernacle as the dwelling place of Christ's Real Presence.
