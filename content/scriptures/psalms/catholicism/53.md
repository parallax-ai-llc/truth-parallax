## The Folly of Godlessness

Psalm 53, a near-duplicate of Psalm 14, declares that "the fool says in his heart, 'There is no God'" (v. 1). Catholic theology, following the First Vatican Council's *Dei Filius*, affirms that God's existence can be known through natural reason (Romans 1:20), making the denial of God not merely an intellectual error but a culpable moral failure. St. Augustine interpreted the "fool" (*nabal*) as one who has been corrupted not in intellect alone but in will — the refusal to acknowledge God stems from the love of sin rather than from honest philosophical inquiry.

The psalm's survey of universal corruption — "There is none who does good, not even one" (v. 3) — has been foundational for Catholic teaching on the effects of original sin. The Council of Trent affirmed, citing this passage, that original sin has wounded human nature though not destroyed it entirely. Catholic anthropology maintains, against certain Protestant readings, that the *imago Dei* persists even in fallen humanity, and that grace perfects and heals nature rather than replacing it. The psalm's depiction of corruption is therefore read as describing the state prior to and apart from grace, not the permanent condition of the baptized.

The concluding cry, "Oh, that salvation for Israel would come out of Zion!" (v. 6), is read in Catholic tradition as a prophetic longing for Christ, who is the definitive salvation coming from the new Zion — the Church. St. Thomas Aquinas connected this verse to the Church's role as the instrument of salvation, through which God restores "the fortunes of his people." The psalm thus functions liturgically as a reminder of humanity's dependence on divine grace mediated through Christ and His Church.
