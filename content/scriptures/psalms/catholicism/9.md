Psalm 9 opens an acrostic pair with Psalm 10 and celebrates God's just governance of the nations. In the Septuagint and Vulgate numbering used in Catholic liturgy, Psalms 9 and 10 are combined as a single psalm, reflecting their literary and thematic unity. This numbering difference (Catholic Psalms 10-146 are one number behind the Hebrew) is important for understanding references in Catholic liturgical texts and patristic commentaries.

The psalm's declaration that God "has established his throne for judgment" (9:7) undergirds Catholic teaching on divine providence and the moral governance of history. The Church's social doctrine, from Rerum Novarum (1891) to Fratelli Tutti (2020), affirms that God's justice is not deferred to the afterlife alone but must be pursued in the present social order. Verse 9 — "The Lord is a stronghold for the oppressed" — has been cited in Catholic social teaching as a mandate for the Church's preferential option for the poor, formally articulated at the Latin American bishops' conferences at Medellin (1968) and Puebla (1979).

The psalm's attention to the "afflicted" and "needy" (9:12, 18) resonates with the Catholic understanding of the *anawim* — the poor of the Lord who depend entirely on God because they have no worldly recourse. Mary's Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55) echoes these themes: God "has put down the mighty from their thrones, and exalted those of low degree." Catholic Marian theology sees in the *anawim* tradition the spiritual context of the Incarnation itself — God chose to enter the world through a humble maiden, confirming that His kingdom belongs to the poor in spirit.
