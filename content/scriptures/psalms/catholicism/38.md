Psalm 38, one of the seven Penitential Psalms, has been prayed in Catholic penitential liturgy since the early centuries of the Church. David's cry — "LORD, do not rebuke me in your anger" (38:1) — expresses the penitent's awareness that sin merits divine judgment. Catholic theology distinguishes between God's punitive justice and his medicinal discipline: the suffering described in this psalm is understood as purifying rather than merely retributive.

The vivid physical descriptions — festering wounds, failing strength, dimming eyes (38:5-10) — illustrate Catholic teaching on the temporal consequences of sin. While the eternal punishment of sin is remitted through sacramental absolution, the temporal effects remain and must be addressed through penance, prayer, and works of mercy. This understanding underlies the Catholic doctrine of purgatory: even the forgiven soul may require further purification before entering God's full presence.

David's confession — "I am bowed down and brought very low; all day long I go about mourning" (38:6) — speaks to the Catholic emphasis on compunction (*compunctio cordis*), the piercing of the heart that accompanies genuine repentance. The great spiritual writers, from John Cassian to Ignatius of Loyola, taught that such sorrow is not depression but a grace — the Holy Spirit's work of breaking through the soul's resistance to conversion.
