Psalm 29 is one of the great theophanic psalms, celebrating the *vox Domini* — the voice of the Lord — thundering across creation. Catholic liturgical tradition assigns this psalm to the feast of the Baptism of the Lord, connecting the voice that thunders over the waters (29:3) with the Father's voice at Jesus's baptism: "This is my beloved Son" (Matthew 3:17). The waters of creation and the waters of baptism are thus liturgically united.

The sevenfold "voice of the LORD" (29:3-9) is read by Catholic theologians as anticipating the seven sacraments through which God's powerful word continues to act in the Church. Just as God's voice breaks cedars and shakes the wilderness, the sacramental word effects what it signifies — transforming bread into Body, washing away sin, and conferring grace.

The psalm's conclusion — "The LORD gives strength to his people; the LORD blesses his people with peace" (29:11) — is echoed in the dismissal rite of the Mass: *Ite, missa est* — "Go, you are sent." The peace that God bestows is not passive tranquility but the commissioning of a people strengthened to carry God's presence into the world. Catholic social teaching grounds its vision of peace (*shalom*) in this divine empowerment.
