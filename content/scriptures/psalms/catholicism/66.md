## Corporate and Personal Praise for Deliverance

Psalm 66's universal call — "Shout for joy to God, all the earth!" (v. 1) — expresses the Catholic Church's universal mission. The psalm's recollection of the Exodus — "He turned the sea into dry land; they passed through the river on foot" (v. 6) — is read in Catholic typology as prefiguring baptism, through which believers pass through the waters of death into new life. The Easter Vigil liturgy, which includes the reading of the Exodus narrative, gives sacramental expression to this psalm's theology.

The psalm's acknowledgment of divine testing — "You have tried us as silver is tried" (v. 10) — has shaped Catholic teaching on the purificatory role of suffering. The doctrine of purgatory, while not derived solely from this verse, resonates with its imagery of refining fire that removes impurities and produces something precious. Catholic spiritual writers, from St. Catherine of Genoa to Cardinal Newman, have developed theologies of purgation that echo the psalm's conviction that God's testing, though painful, is ultimately salvific.

The shift to personal testimony — "Come and hear, all you who fear God, and I will tell what he has done for my soul" (v. 16) — models the Catholic practice of witness within the community of faith. The psalm's principle that "if I had cherished iniquity in my heart, the Lord would not have listened" (v. 18) aligns with the Catholic teaching on the necessary disposition of the heart in prayer. The Catechism teaches that authentic prayer requires sincerity and a willingness to be converted — the "broken and contrite heart" that opens the soul to receive what God desires to give.
