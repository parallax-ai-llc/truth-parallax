## A Swift Cry for Divine Help

Psalm 70's opening verse — "Make haste, O God, to deliver me! O LORD, make haste to help me!" — has become one of the most frequently recited verses in Catholic liturgical prayer. It serves as the introductory versicle for every Hour of the Liturgy of the Hours (except the Office of Readings when it begins with the Invitatory). This placement ensures that every canonical hour begins with an acknowledgment of urgent dependence on God — the foundational posture that Catholic spirituality identifies as the starting point of all genuine prayer.

St. Benedict, in his *Rule*, singles out this verse (*Deus, in adjutorium meum intende*) as the monk's constant prayer, to be repeated at every transition throughout the day. The Benedictine tradition has thus embedded this psalm in the daily rhythm of millions of monks and nuns over fifteen centuries. The verse's power lies in its combination of urgency and humility: the one praying acknowledges both the reality of danger and the inadequacy of human resources to meet it.

The psalm's prayer that "all who seek you may rejoice and be glad in you" (v. 4) expresses the Catholic conviction that joy is the fruit of genuine seeking. The self-description "I am poor and needy" (v. 5) resonates with the *anawim* tradition — the "poor of the Lord" whom Catholic theology, following the Magnificat, identifies as the privileged recipients of God's grace. Mary's declaration that God "has filled the hungry with good things" (Luke 1:53) echoes this psalm's confidence that divine help will not be delayed for those who acknowledge their poverty before God.
