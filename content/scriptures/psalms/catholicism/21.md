Psalm 21 (Psalm 20 in Septuagint/Vulgate), the thanksgiving counterpart to Psalm 20, celebrates God's response to the king's petition. The declaration "You have given him his heart's desire" (21:2) and "you set a crown of fine gold upon his head" (21:3) have been read in Catholic tradition as fulfilled in Christ's coronation following the resurrection and ascension. The Feast of Christ the King, established by Pope Pius XI in 1925 with the encyclical *Quas Primas*, draws on psalms like this to celebrate Christ's universal sovereignty.

The promise of "length of days forever and ever" (21:4) transcends any earthly monarch and points, in Catholic interpretation, to Christ's eternal reign. The Nicene Creed's affirmation that Christ's "kingdom will have no end" echoes this verse. Catholic coronation liturgies for Christian monarchs historically drew on Psalm 21 to remind kings that their authority was derivative — borrowed from the true King whose reign is everlasting.

The psalm's vision of the king's enemies consumed by fire (21:9-12) has been interpreted in Catholic eschatology as pointing to the final judgment. The fires of divine justice purify creation of all evil, an image that connects to both purgatorial purification and the final conflagration described in 2 Peter 3:10-13. The psalm's conclusion — "Be exalted, O Lord, in your strength! We will sing and praise your power" (21:13) — is an acclamation of divine sovereignty that finds its liturgical expression in the doxologies and hymns of the Mass, especially the *Gloria in Excelsis Deo*.
