## National Lament and Divine Promise

Psalm 60's confession that "O God, you have rejected us, broken our defenses" (v. 1) is read in Catholic tradition as expressing the experience of the Church in times of crisis — whether external persecution or internal scandal. The Fathers recognized that God sometimes permits the Church to experience apparent defeat so that she might be purified and renewed. St. Gregory the Great found in such psalms a call to ecclesial humility, reminding the Church that her strength comes from God alone, not from human institutions or political alliances.

The divine oracle (vv. 6-8), in which God claims sovereignty over all the territories of Israel and surrounding nations, has been interpreted ecclesiologically by the Fathers. St. Augustine sees in God's sovereignty over Moab, Edom, and Philistia a prefiguration of the universal Church, which incorporates peoples from every nation under divine governance. This reading undergirds Catholic ecclesiology's claim to universality — the Church is not a national institution but God's instrument for the reconciliation of all peoples.

The psalm's concluding petition — "Oh, grant us help against the foe, for vain is the salvation of man! With God we shall do valiantly" (vv. 11-12) — resonates with Catholic teaching on grace and human cooperation. "Vain is the salvation of man" affirms that human effort apart from God is futile, while "with God we shall do valiantly" expresses the Catholic understanding of *cooperatio* — human agency empowered and directed by divine grace. The psalm thus balances the recognition of human weakness with confidence in what God accomplishes through and in His people.
