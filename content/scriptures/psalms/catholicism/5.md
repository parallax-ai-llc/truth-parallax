Psalm 5 is a morning prayer psalm that has been used in the Catholic Liturgy of the Hours at Lauds (Morning Prayer). The verse "O Lord, in the morning you hear my voice; in the morning I prepare a sacrifice for you and watch" (5:3) establishes the pattern of beginning each day with prayer — a practice the Church has formalized in the Divine Office. The sacrificial language (*'arak*, to arrange or set in order) connects morning prayer to the ancient temple sacrifices and, through them, to the Eucharistic sacrifice that Catholic theology understands as the fulfillment of all Old Testament offerings.

The psalm's strong language about God's hatred of evil (5:4-6) has been interpreted within the Catholic framework of God's justice and mercy. The Magisterium affirms that God truly abhors sin while always desiring the sinner's repentance. St. Thomas Aquinas distinguished between God's antecedent will (that all be saved) and consequent will (that the impenitent face judgment), a distinction that helps reconcile verses like 5:5 ("you hate all evildoers") with the universal salvific will affirmed in 1 Timothy 2:4.

The petition "Lead me, O Lord, in your righteousness" (5:8) has been connected in Catholic tradition to the guidance of the Magisterium and the sensus fidei. While individual conscience remains sacred (CCC 1776-1802), the faithful are led "in righteousness" through the Church's teaching authority, the sacraments, and the example of the saints. Verse 12 — "For you bless the righteous, O Lord; you cover him with favor as with a shield" — has been read as a promise fulfilled in the sacramental life, where divine grace is not merely forensic but transformative, actually making the believer righteous through infused grace.
