Psalm 10 (combined with Psalm 9 in the Septuagint/Vulgate numbering as Psalm 9B) confronts the problem of God's apparent absence during the triumph of evil. The raw question "Why, O Lord, do you stand far away?" (10:1) has been embraced by Catholic spiritual writers as a legitimate expression of the *dark night of the soul*. St. John of the Cross and St. Thérèse of Lisieux both experienced periods of desolation when God seemed absent, and the Church validates this experience as part of the journey toward deeper union with God.

The detailed portrait of the wicked in verses 2-11 describes a person who has effectively eliminated God from practical consideration: "In the pride of his face the wicked does not seek him; all his thoughts are, 'There is no God'" (10:4). Catholic moral theology identifies this as the sin of *practical atheism* — maintaining nominal religious identity while living as though God does not observe or judge. Pope Benedict XVI warned repeatedly against the "dictatorship of relativism," a modern expression of the ancient attitude this psalm describes.

The psalm's resolution — "You do see, for you note mischief and vexation" (10:14) — is an affirmation of divine providence that Catholic theology holds as a dogma of faith. The First Vatican Council (1870) defined that God governs all things with His providence, and nothing escapes His knowledge or power. The psalm's particular concern for "the fatherless and the oppressed" (10:18) connects to the Church's extensive charitable work and institutional care for orphans, a ministry reaching back to the earliest Christian communities described in Acts and the apostolic Fathers.
