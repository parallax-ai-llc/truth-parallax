Psalm 39 confronts human mortality with a directness that Catholic theology embraces in its meditation on the "Four Last Things" — death, judgment, heaven, and hell. David's plea, "Show me, LORD, my life's end and the number of my days" (39:4), mirrors the Catholic spiritual practice of *memento mori* — remembering death as a means of living well. The Rule of Saint Benedict instructs monks to "keep death daily before your eyes" (RB 4.47).

The psalm's declaration that "everyone is but a breath" (39:5) resonates with the Ash Wednesday liturgy: "Remember that you are dust, and to dust you shall return." Catholic theology does not read this as nihilism but as a summons to orient life toward eternity. The brevity of earthly existence heightens the urgency of conversion and the value of every moment lived in grace.

David's cry, "Hear my prayer, LORD... for I dwell with you as a foreigner, a stranger, as all my ancestors were" (39:12), is taken up by the Letter to the Hebrews (11:13) and has shaped Catholic spirituality's understanding of the Christian as *homo viator* — the pilgrim on the way to the heavenly homeland. This pilgrim theology pervades Catholic liturgy, from the funeral Mass ("May the angels lead you into paradise") to the Salve Regina's address to Mary as the guide of exiles.
