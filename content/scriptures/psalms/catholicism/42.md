Psalm 42 has profoundly shaped Catholic liturgical and mystical tradition. The opening image — "As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you, my God" (42:1) — was traditionally sung during the Easter Vigil as catechumens approached the baptismal font, the "living water" for which their souls thirsted. This psalm gave voice to the longing that baptism fulfills.

The psalmist's memory of processing "to the house of God, with shouts of joy and praise among the festive throng" (42:4) speaks to the Catholic understanding of liturgy as communal pilgrimage. The Church does not worship as isolated individuals but as a body moving together toward God. The loss of communal worship described in this psalm — exile from the temple — represents a spiritual desolation that Catholic theology takes with utmost seriousness.

The refrain, "Why, my soul, are you downcast? Put your hope in God, for I will yet praise him" (42:5, 11), models what Ignatius of Loyola called "consolation without previous cause" — the act of choosing hope when every natural feeling inclines toward despair. Catholic spiritual direction teaches that such moments of desolation, while painful, are opportunities for the deepening of faith. The soul that perseveres in hope during darkness emerges with a more purified and resilient love.
