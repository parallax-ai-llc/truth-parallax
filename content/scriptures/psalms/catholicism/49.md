Psalm 49 is a wisdom meditation on death and wealth that Catholic social teaching has invoked to challenge economic injustice. The psalm's central observation — "Those who trust in their wealth and boast of their great riches cannot redeem a single person" (49:6-7) — echoes Jesus's warning that "it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for someone who is rich to enter the kingdom of God" (Mark 10:25).

Catholic moral theology draws on this psalm to articulate the universal destination of goods — the principle that the earth's resources are intended for the benefit of all humanity, not merely for the accumulation of the wealthy. Pope Francis, in *Laudato Si'*, echoed the psalm's critique of those who "heap up wealth, not knowing who will get it" (cf. 49:10), calling for an economy that serves human dignity rather than the idolatry of money.

Verse 15 — "But God will redeem me from the realm of the dead; he will surely take me to himself" — is one of the Old Testament's most explicit expressions of hope beyond death. Catholic theology reads this as an anticipation of the resurrection and, more broadly, of the communion of saints. The faithful departed are not lost but are held in God's care, whether in the purification of purgatory or the joy of heaven. This psalm grounds the Catholic practice of praying for the dead.
