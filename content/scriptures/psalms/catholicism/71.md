## God's Faithfulness from Youth to Old Age

Psalm 71 is the prayer of a lifelong believer facing the vulnerabilities of old age, and Catholic tradition has treasured it for its witness to persevering faith. The psalmist's confession — "Upon you I have leaned from before my birth" (v. 6) — affirms the Catholic teaching that God's providential care begins before birth, supporting the Church's defense of the sanctity of human life from conception. The plea "Do not cast me off in the time of old age" (v. 9) has shaped Catholic pastoral care for the elderly, reminding the Church that aging members retain their dignity and their vocation to witness.

St. Augustine reads this psalm as the voice of the Church throughout history — born of God's ancient promises, sustained through centuries of trial, and still praising God in the present age. The commitment to proclaim God's "might to another generation" (v. 18) expresses the Catholic understanding of sacred tradition: each generation receives the faith and passes it on, not as a dead letter but as a living witness to God's continuing action. The Catechism's treatment of tradition echoes this psalm's intergenerational vision.

The psalm's persistent hope — "But I will hope continually and will praise you yet more and more" (v. 14) — has been a comfort to Catholic saints in their final years. Pope St. John XXIII, in his spiritual diary, reflected on this psalm during his final illness, finding in its words the assurance that advancing age deepens rather than diminishes the capacity for praise. Catholic teaching on the dignity of the elderly draws directly from such psalms, insisting that every stage of life, including its decline, is embraced by God's loving purpose.
