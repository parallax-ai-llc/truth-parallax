Psalm 8 is foundational to Catholic theological anthropology. The declaration that God has made humanity "a little lower than the heavenly beings and crowned him with glory and honor" (8:5) is cited in the Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 2566) as establishing the dignity of the human person. Vatican II's *Gaudium et Spes* (12-22) draws extensively on this vision of human dignity, rooted in creation in God's image and restored through Christ's redemption.

The psalm's assertion of human dominion over creation (8:6-8) has been central to Catholic social teaching on ecology and stewardship. Pope Francis's encyclical *Laudato Si'* (2015) engages directly with the question of dominion, insisting that it means responsible stewardship rather than exploitative mastery. The Catholic reading of Psalm 8 holds together human dignity and creaturely dependence — we are crowned with glory yet remain creatures sustained by God's care, called to tend the garden rather than ravage it.

The christological reading of Psalm 8 is anchored in Hebrews 2:6-9, where the psalm is applied to Christ's incarnation, suffering, and exaltation. Catholic theology reads this as the mystery of the *hypostatic union* — the Son of God took on human nature, was "made a little lower than the angels" in His earthly life, and was exalted above all creation through the resurrection and ascension. The Blessed Virgin Mary is also honored in Catholic tradition as the supreme exemplar of human dignity crowned with glory: the *Regina Caeli* (Queen of Heaven) who embodies the psalm's vision of a creature elevated to the highest honor by divine grace.
