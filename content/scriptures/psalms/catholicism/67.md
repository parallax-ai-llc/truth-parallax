## God's Blessing for All Peoples

Psalm 67, modeled on the Aaronic blessing (Numbers 6:24-26), expresses the universality of God's saving purpose — "that your way may be known on earth, your saving power among all nations" (v. 2). Catholic ecclesiology finds in this psalm a scriptural foundation for the Church's missionary mandate. The Second Vatican Council's *Ad Gentes* declares that the Church is "missionary by her very nature," and this psalm articulates the theological basis: God's blessing upon Israel (and the Church) is ordered toward the salvation of all peoples.

The refrain "Let the peoples praise you, O God; let all the peoples praise you!" (vv. 3, 5) envisions a universal liturgy in which every nation participates. Catholic theology sees this fulfilled in the Church's worldwide celebration of the Eucharist, where the same sacrifice is offered in every language and culture. The inculturation of the liturgy — adapting worship to diverse cultural expressions while maintaining essential unity — gives concrete form to this psalm's vision of all peoples praising the one God.

The psalm's connection between divine blessing and earthly harvest — "The earth has yielded its increase" (v. 6) — is incorporated into Catholic harvest blessings and the Offertory of the Mass, where bread and wine, "fruit of the earth and work of human hands," are offered to God. Catholic social teaching extends this psalm's logic: the earth's abundance is a gift meant for all, and the just distribution of resources is integral to the mission of blessing that the Church receives and extends to the world.
