Psalm 15 (Psalm 14 in Septuagint/Vulgate) asks the fundamental question "who shall sojourn in your tent?" (15:1) and answers with an ethical profile that Catholic moral theology recognizes as the life of virtue. The characteristics listed — truthfulness, justice, faithfulness, integrity — correspond to the cardinal virtues (prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance) as well as the theological virtues (faith, hope, charity) that Catholic ethics understands as the framework of the moral life. St. Thomas Aquinas's treatise on the virtues in the *Summa Theologiae* provides the systematic elaboration of what this psalm sketches poetically.

The requirement that the righteous person "does not put out his money at interest" (15:5) was historically important in Catholic economic ethics. The Church's centuries-long prohibition of usury drew on texts like this one, and while the specific application has been nuanced in light of modern economics, the underlying principle — that the powerful must not exploit the vulnerable through unjust financial practices — remains central to Catholic social teaching. The Compendium of the Social Doctrine of the Church continues to condemn exploitative lending practices that crush the poor.

The psalm's integration of worship and ethics — the same person who dwells on God's holy hill is the one who lives justly — reflects the Catholic principle that *lex orandi, lex credendi, lex vivendi* (the law of prayer is the law of belief is the law of living). Authentic worship and moral living are inseparable. The promise that such a person "shall never be moved" (15:5b) is understood not as guaranteed earthly prosperity but as the spiritual stability that comes from living in the state of sanctifying grace, a condition maintained through the sacraments and the practice of virtue.
