## God's Sovereign Judgment in His Time

Psalm 75 opens with thanksgiving — "We give thanks to you, O God" (v. 1) — and immediately introduces a divine oracle in which God himself speaks: "At the set time that I appoint I will judge with equity" (v. 2). Catholic eschatology draws on such passages to affirm that the timing of judgment belongs to God alone, not to human calculation. The Catechism warns against presuming to know "the time or the season" (Acts 1:7) of God's final reckoning, and this psalm supports that caution by placing judgment firmly within God's sovereign prerogative.

The psalm's warning against pride — "Do not lift up your horn on high, or speak with haughty neck" (v. 5) — echoes the Magnificat's declaration that God "has scattered the proud in the imagination of their hearts" (Luke 1:51). Catholic spiritual theology identifies pride as the root of all sin, following St. Augustine, and this psalm's imagery reinforces the teaching. The assertion that "it is God who executes judgment, putting down one and lifting up another" (v. 7) has been fundamental to Catholic political theology, reminding earthly powers that their authority is delegated and contingent.

The cup of judgment (v. 8) — "For in the hand of the LORD there is a cup with foaming wine... and all the wicked of the earth shall drain it down to the dregs" — is one of Scripture's most powerful images. Catholic exegesis connects this cup to Christ's prayer in Gethsemane — "Let this cup pass from me" (Matthew 26:39) — and to the Eucharistic cup, which transforms the cup of judgment into the cup of salvation. The Mass's chalice thus recapitulates and reverses this psalm's cup: where the wicked drink judgment, the faithful drink redemption. This liturgical theology makes Psalm 75 a text of profound sacramental significance in Catholic reading.
