## Overview

Psalm 133 — *Unity of Brethren* — comprises 3 verses. Catholic interpretation reads this psalm through the lens of Sacred Tradition, the Magisterium, and the Church Fathers, situating it within the Church's liturgical life and sacramental theology.

Psalm 133 is one of the Gradual Psalms (Psalms 120-134), traditionally recited or chanted on the steps of the Temple. In Catholic tradition, these psalms have been associated with the spiritual ascent of the soul toward God, a theme richly developed by the Church Fathers.

## Patristic Interpretation

#

## Augustine

In his *Enarrationes in Psalmos*, Augustine provided extensive commentary on every psalm. For Psalm 133, he characteristically sought the Christological and ecclesiological meaning, reading the psalm as the voice of Christ, of the Church, or of the individual soul journeying toward God.

#

## Other Fathers

**Cassiodorus** in his *Expositio Psalmorum* analyzed the rhetorical structure of Psalm 133, identifying its literary devices while drawing out theological meaning. **Jerome**, who produced the *Psalterium iuxta Hebraeos*, contributed important philological observations that continue to inform Catholic scholarship.

## Liturgical Context

In the *Liturgia Horarum* (Liturgy of the Hours), Psalm 133 is assigned to specific hours and days of the four-week psalter cycle established after the Second Vatican Council. The psalm's themes resonate with the Church's sacramental life and the rhythms of the liturgical year.

The Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 2585-2589) presents the Psalms as the masterwork of prayer in the Old Testament, where the Holy Spirit inspires both the words and the response of faith.

## Theological Significance

Catholic exegesis, guided by *Dei Verbum* and the Pontifical Biblical Commission, reads Psalm 133 within the unity of Scripture's two Testaments, recognizing both its historical meaning and its fuller spiritual sense (*sensus plenior*) fulfilled in Christ and the Church.
