Psalm 43 is liturgically inseparable from Psalm 42 in Catholic tradition. The opening verse — "Vindicate me, my God, and plead my cause" (43:1) — was formerly recited at the foot of the altar in the Tridentine Mass during the *Iudica me* prayers, making this psalm the gateway to Eucharistic worship. The priest identified with the psalmist's longing for God's altar, approaching the sacrifice with both reverence and urgent desire.

The petition "Send me your light and your faithful care, let them lead me" (43:3) is read by Catholic theology as a prayer for the gifts of the Holy Spirit — particularly the gifts of understanding and counsel that illuminate the path to God. Light and truth are personified as divine guides, anticipating Christ's declaration that he is both the light of the world and the way, the truth, and the life (John 14:6).

The psalm's resolution — "Then I will go to the altar of God, to God, my joy and my delight" (43:4) — expresses the Catholic understanding of the altar as the privileged place of encounter with God. Unlike traditions that minimize the altar's significance, Catholic theology maintains that the Eucharistic altar is where heaven and earth intersect, where the sacrifice of Calvary is made present, and where the faithful receive the Bread of Life.
