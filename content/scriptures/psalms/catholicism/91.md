## Divine Protection and the Temptation of Christ

Psalm 91 holds a distinctive place in Catholic theology both as a psalm of trust and as the text Satan misquoted during Christ's temptation (Matthew 4:5-7). Catholic interpretation balances the genuine promise of divine protection with the warning against *praesumptio* — the sin of presumption.

## Key Themes

### The Four Divine Names (91:1-2)

The psalm invokes God as Most High, Almighty, LORD, and God — a fourfold naming that Catholic commentators see as expressing the fullness of divine protection. The Fathers connected these names to the four cardinal virtues required of those who seek God's shelter.

### Angelic Protection (91:11-12)

Catholic angelology draws significantly on this psalm. The doctrine of guardian angels (CCC 336) finds biblical support here. The liturgical feast of the Guardian Angels (October 2) incorporates this passage, and St. Thomas Aquinas discussed angelic protection in *Summa Theologiae* I, q.113.

### The Temptation Narrative (91:11-12 in Matthew 4:6)

Satan's citation of this psalm to tempt Christ reveals the danger of isolating Scripture from its proper context. Catholic hermeneutics, guided by the Magisterium, insists that individual texts must be read within the whole of Scripture and Tradition — precisely the principle Christ demonstrated in His response.

## Theological Significance

- **Divine providence**: God's comprehensive care for those who trust Him
- **Guardian angels**: Angelic ministry to the faithful
- **Presumption vs. trust**: The distinction between faith and tempting God
- **Christological center**: Christ's victory over Satan's misuse of Scripture
