## Overview

The Psalms occupy a central place in Catholic worship, theology, and spirituality. They form the backbone of the Liturgy of the Hours (Divine Office), through which priests, religious, and laypeople pray the entire Psalter in a regular cycle. Catholic interpretation is shaped by patristic commentary, the Magisterium, and the liturgical context in which the Psalms are embedded.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Liturgical Context
The Psalms are not primarily studied as ancient texts but prayed as the living voice of the Church. Each psalm takes on additional meaning from its liturgical placement — Morning Prayer, Evening Prayer, the Mass, and the sacramental rites.

### Four Senses of Scripture
Following the patristic and medieval tradition:
1. **Literal**: The historical occasion and meaning of the psalm
2. **Allegorical**: The psalm as speaking of Christ and the Church
3. **Moral**: The psalm as guiding the soul's spiritual journey
4. **Anagogical**: The psalm as pointing to heavenly realities

### The Psalms as the Voice of Christ
St. Augustine's principle (*Enarrationes in Psalmos*): Christ speaks in the Psalms — as Head (in his own person), as Body (in the voice of the Church), or as the whole Christ (*Christus totus*).

## Theological Emphases

- **The suffering Christ**: Lament psalms as prophetic of the Passion
- **Mary in the Psalms**: Typological readings connecting psalms to Marian theology
- **Ecclesiology**: The assembly of Israel as a type of the Church
- **Eschatology**: Royal psalms pointing to Christ's eternal kingship

## Notable Catholic Commentators

| Commentator | Era | Contribution |
|-------------|-----|-------------|
| St. Augustine | 4th-5th century | *Enarrationes in Psalmos* — exhaustive commentary on all 150 psalms |
| St. Thomas Aquinas | 13th century | Commentary on Psalms 1-54 |
| Robert Bellarmine | 16th-17th century | *Explanatio in Psalmos* |
| Pius Parsch | 20th century | Liturgical-pastoral commentary |
| Joseph Ratzinger | Contemporary | *A New Song for the Lord* — theological reflection on psalmody |
