Psalm 17 (Psalm 16 in Septuagint/Vulgate) presents a prayer of the righteous sufferer whose ultimate hope is to "behold your face in righteousness" (17:15). Catholic commentators from Augustine to the present have read this psalm as a prayer of Christ Himself, the supremely righteous sufferer who endured the cross for the joy set before Him (Hebrews 12:2). The psalm's assertion of innocence is perfectly true only of Christ, and the faithful pray it in union with Him.

The petition "Keep me as the apple of your eye; hide me in the shadow of your wings" (17:8) has rich resonance in Catholic Marian devotion. The protective wing imagery recalls Revelation 12:14, where the woman (understood by Catholic tradition as both Mary and the Church) is given "the two wings of the great eagle" for protection. Catholic piety has understood Mary as the one who most perfectly shelters the faithful under her maternal protection, an extension of Christ's own sheltering care described in this psalm.

The concluding verse — "when I awake, I shall be satisfied with your likeness" (17:15) — has been interpreted by Catholic theologians as pointing to both the resurrection of the body and the beatific vision. The doctrine of the resurrection of the body, affirmed in every recitation of the Creed, promises that the faithful will not merely see God's face but be transformed into His likeness. St. Paul's teaching that "we shall be changed" (1 Corinthians 15:51-52) and that we will bear "the image of the man of heaven" (1 Corinthians 15:49) gives New Testament substance to the psalmist's hope. The *satisfaction* described is the complete fulfillment of every human longing in the eternal vision of God.
