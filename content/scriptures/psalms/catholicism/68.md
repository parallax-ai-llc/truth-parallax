## God's Victorious Procession

Psalm 68 is one of the great processional hymns of the Psalter, and Catholic liturgical tradition has employed it extensively, especially during the feast of the Ascension. The psalm traces God's march from Sinai through the wilderness to His dwelling on Mount Zion, and the Fathers read this journey as a type of Christ's passage from earth to heaven. St. Paul's citation of verse 18 in Ephesians 4:8 — "When he ascended on high he led a host of captives, and he gave gifts to men" — anchors the Catholic reading of this psalm in Ascension Christology and in the theology of spiritual gifts distributed through the Church.

The psalm celebrates God as "father of the fatherless and protector of widows" (v. 5), a characterization that Catholic social teaching takes as normative. The Church's preferential option for the poor, articulated from the Fathers through the social encyclicals, finds scriptural rootage here. Catholic charitable institutions — hospitals, orphanages, schools — embody this psalm's vision of God's special care for the most vulnerable, mediated through the Church's works of mercy.

The psalm's description of a liturgical procession — "Your procession is seen, O God, the procession of my God, my King, into the sanctuary — the singers in front, the musicians last, between them virgins playing tambourines" (vv. 24-25) — has informed Catholic liturgical aesthetics. The Corpus Christi procession, the entrance rite of the Mass, and the elaborate liturgies of the great feast days all reflect the psalm's conviction that God's people encounter Him through ordered, beautiful, communal worship. The psalm's final doxology — "Awesome is God from his sanctuary" (v. 35) — expresses the Catholic sensibility that liturgy is the place where God's awesome holiness becomes accessible to His people.
