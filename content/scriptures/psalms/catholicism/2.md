Psalm 2 holds a place of supreme importance in Catholic christological and ecclesiological reflection. The declaration "You are my Son; today I have begotten you" (2:7) was understood by the Council of Nicaea (325) and subsequent ecumenical councils as scriptural testimony to the eternal generation of the Son from the Father. Catholic dogmatic theology cites this verse in establishing the consubstantiality of the Son with the Father, a doctrine enshrined in the Nicene Creed recited at every Sunday Mass.

The psalm's depiction of the nations raging against the Lord and His Anointed (2:1-3) has been applied throughout Catholic history to the Church's relationship with hostile powers. Augustine read these verses as describing the persecutions of the early Church and, by extension, every era when secular authority opposes Christ's Body. The papal encyclical tradition has drawn on Psalm 2 to articulate the Church's confidence that Christ's reign will ultimately prevail over every temporal opposition, a theme developed notably in Leo XIII's *Immortale Dei*.

The invitation to "kiss the Son" (2:12) has been interpreted in Catholic devotional tradition as a call to worship and sacramental encounter with Christ. The Eucharist is the supreme act of "kissing the Son" — receiving Christ Himself in the communion of His Body and Blood. The psalm's final beatitude, "blessed are all who take refuge in him" (2:12b), has been applied to Marian devotion as well, since Mary is the Ark in whom the Son made His dwelling, and taking refuge in her leads to refuge in Christ Himself.
