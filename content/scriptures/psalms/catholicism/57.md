## Taking Refuge in God's Mercy

Psalm 57 opens with a doubled plea for mercy — "Be merciful to me, O God, be merciful to me" (v. 1) — that Catholic liturgical tradition has incorporated into penitential rites and prayers of supplication. The image of refuge "in the shadow of your wings" draws on the maternal dimension of God's care, an aspect of divine tenderness that Catholic Marian theology sees reflected in Our Lady's protective intercession. The psalm's setting in a cave, fleeing from Saul, is read by the Fathers as an image of the soul taking shelter in the Church, the spiritual cave of refuge amid worldly persecution.

St. Augustine's commentary on this psalm focuses on the theme of divine glory: "Be exalted, O God, above the heavens! Let your glory be over all the earth!" (vv. 5, 11). Augustine reads this as a prophetic cry that finds its fulfillment in Christ's Ascension, when God's glory was indeed exalted above the heavens. The Catholic liturgical year, with its celebration of the Ascension as a holy day of obligation, gives concrete expression to this psalm's vision. The glory of God, manifested in Christ, now fills the whole earth through the presence of the Church and her sacraments.

The psalmist's declaration "My heart is steadfast, O God, my heart is steadfast! I will sing and make melody!" (v. 7) expresses what Catholic spiritual theology calls *stabilitas* — the steadfastness of heart that enables persevering prayer. St. Benedict made stability a hallmark of monastic life, and this psalm is prayed within monastic communities as an expression of the monk's commitment to remain rooted in God despite all adversity. The psalm's movement from fear to praise — from the cave of danger to the cosmic proclamation of God's love and faithfulness (v. 10) — mirrors the paschal mystery that shapes all Catholic worship.
