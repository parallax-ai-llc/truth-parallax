Psalm 44 is a communal lament that Catholic theology reads through the lens of the Church's theology of redemptive suffering. The people protest their innocence — "All this came upon us, though we had not forgotten you" (44:17) — raising the agonizing question of unmerited suffering. Catholic tradition does not dismiss this protest but takes it into the heart of the Paschal Mystery: innocent suffering, united to Christ's cross, becomes redemptive.

Paul's quotation of verse 22 — "For your sake we face death all day long; we are considered as sheep to be slaughtered" (Romans 8:36) — has been applied by Catholic tradition to the martyrs of every age. The Roman Martyrology records thousands who suffered precisely because of their fidelity to God and his covenant. Psalm 44 gives canonical voice to their experience, validating the cry of the persecuted Church.

The psalm's bold demand — "Awake, Lord! Why do you sleep?" (44:23) — represents what Catholic theology calls the prayer of complaint or protest, which is a legitimate form of faith. The Catechism acknowledges that lamentation is authentic prayer (CCC 2630). Far from indicating lack of faith, such bold address to God demonstrates profound confidence in his covenant relationship. The psalmist does not turn away from God but toward him, even in the darkest hour.
