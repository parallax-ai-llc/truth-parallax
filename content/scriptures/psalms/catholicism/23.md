Psalm 23 (Psalm 22 in Septuagint/Vulgate) is the most widely known psalm in Catholic devotion and holds a central place in the Church's sacramental life. It is prescribed for use at funeral Masses, the Rite of the Anointing of the Sick, and the liturgy of the Fourth Sunday of Easter (Good Shepherd Sunday). The image of Christ as the Good Shepherd, drawn from John 10 and anchored in this psalm, is among the earliest in Christian art — catacombs from the second and third centuries depict the shepherd carrying a sheep on his shoulders.

The sacramental reading of Psalm 23 is especially rich. "He leads me beside still waters" (23:2) evokes baptism; "You prepare a table before me" (23:5) is the Eucharistic banquet; "you anoint my head with oil" (23:5) recalls the anointing of Confirmation and the Anointing of the Sick. The entire psalm can be read as a journey through the sacramental life of the Church, from the waters of initiation to the table of communion to the anointing that strengthens for the final passage. Catholic mystics such as St. John of the Cross interpreted "the valley of the shadow of death" (23:4) as the dark night of the soul through which God leads the faithful toward deeper union.

The promise "I shall dwell in the house of the Lord forever" (23:6) points to both the earthly church and the heavenly Jerusalem. Catholic ecclesiology understands the parish church as a true dwelling-place of the Lord through the reserved Eucharist, and the "house of the Lord" thus becomes every tabernacle where Christ is present. The psalm's final word — "forever" — reaches beyond this life to the eternal communion of saints, where the shepherd-host relationship is consummated in the beatific vision. Pope Francis has spoken of Psalm 23 as "the prayer that teaches us to trust, even through the darkest valley."
