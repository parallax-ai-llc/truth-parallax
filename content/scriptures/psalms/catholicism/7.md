Psalm 7, a *shiggaion* of David, presents an appeal for divine justice that Catholic moral theology reads through the lens of the virtue of justice as articulated by St. Thomas Aquinas. The psalmist's oath of innocence (7:3-5) is understood not as self-righteousness but as a legitimate appeal for vindication by one who has been falsely accused. Catholic teaching on the eighth commandment ("You shall not bear false witness") draws on texts like this to defend the right to one's good name and reputation.

The portrait of God as righteous Judge (7:11) is central to Catholic eschatology. The Catechism teaches both a particular judgment at death and a general judgment at the end of time (CCC 1021-1022, 1038-1041). The psalm's description of God who "feels indignation every day" (7:11) toward the impenitent is balanced in Catholic teaching by the doctrine of divine mercy — God's judgment is always preceded by abundant opportunity for repentance. The *Dies Irae*, the medieval hymn of judgment formerly part of the Requiem Mass, draws on the same tradition of divine justice tempered by mercy that runs through the Psalter.

The principle of self-recoiling evil in verses 14-16 — the wicked dig a pit and fall into it, their mischief returns on their own heads — has been important in Catholic moral philosophy. St. Augustine taught that sin carries its own punishment intrinsically: the disorder of sin disorders the sinner. This natural-law understanding of moral consequence, developed by the Thomistic tradition, sees in Psalm 7's imagery not merely poetic justice but a description of the moral structure of reality itself. The wicked are destroyed not by arbitrary divine punishment but by the inherent consequences of their own evil choices.
