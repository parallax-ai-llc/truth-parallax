Psalm 45 is a royal wedding psalm that Catholic theology reads on multiple levels: as a celebration of the Davidic king, as a messianic prophecy of Christ's kingship, and — distinctively — as a Marian text. The queen standing at the king's right hand "in gold of Ophir" (45:9) is identified by Catholic tradition with the Blessed Virgin Mary, Queen of Heaven, who shares in her Son's royal dignity.

The declaration "Your throne, O God, will last for ever and ever" (45:6), applied to Christ in Hebrews 1:8, affirms the Catholic dogma of Christ's divine kingship. Catholic theology insists that Christ reigns not only spiritually but over all creation, a truth celebrated in the Feast of Christ the King established by Pius XI in 1925. The psalm's vision of a righteous and eternal reign informs the Church's eschatological hope.

The bridal imagery — the queen adorned for her king, led with gladness into the royal palace (45:13-15) — is read ecclesiologically as depicting the Church as the Bride of Christ. Catholic nuptial theology draws on this psalm to illuminate the sacramental nature of marriage, which Ephesians 5:32 calls "a profound mystery" pointing to Christ and the Church. The beauty of the bride is understood as the beauty of holiness conferred by grace, not merely external adornment.
