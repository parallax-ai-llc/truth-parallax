## Silent Trust in God Alone

Psalm 62 opens with a profound statement of contemplative rest: "For God alone my soul waits in silence" (v. 1). Catholic mystical tradition has drawn extensively on this verse. St. John of the Cross saw in this silence the prerequisite for contemplative prayer — the stilling of all human faculties so that God may act upon the soul without impediment. The Carmelite tradition of interior silence, practiced in the prayer of quiet and the prayer of union, finds its scriptural warrant in this psalm's repeated insistence on waiting for God "alone."

The psalm's warning against trusting in riches — "if riches increase, set not your heart on them" (v. 10) — resonates with Catholic teaching on detachment from material goods. St. Francis of Assisi embodied this psalm's spirituality through radical poverty, and the Church's social encyclicals from *Rerum Novarum* to *Laudato Si'* echo its caution against making wealth an idol. The Catholic doctrine of the universal destination of goods — that material wealth exists to serve the common good — finds prophetic support in the psalm's deflation of human pretension (v. 9).

The concluding revelation — "power belongs to God, and... to you, O Lord, belongs steadfast love" (vv. 11-12) — presents two divine attributes that Catholic theology holds in inseparable unity. God's omnipotence is always exercised through love, and God's love is always backed by omnipotence. This integration prevents both a harsh theology of power without mercy and a sentimental theology of love without justice. The psalm's final affirmation that God "will repay each person according to what they have done" (v. 12) expresses the Catholic doctrine of judgment based on the totality of one's life lived in response to grace.
