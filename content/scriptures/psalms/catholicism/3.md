Psalm 3, set during David's flight from Absalom, has occupied a privileged place in the Catholic Liturgy of the Hours as a morning psalm. The traditional Roman breviary assigned it to Sunday Matins, and the current Liturgy of the Hours places it in the first week of the psalter cycle at Morning Prayer. Its themes of waking, rising, and trusting God make it especially apt for the beginning of the day. St. Benedict's Rule prescribed the Psalter's complete recitation each week, and Psalm 3 has thus been prayed by millions of monks and nuns at dawn for over fifteen centuries.

Augustine's commentary on verse 5 — "I lay down and slept; I woke again, for the Lord sustained me" — reads the psalm christologically: Christ lay down in death, slept in the tomb, and rose again because the Father sustained Him. This interpretation transforms the psalm from David's personal experience into a prophecy of the Paschal Mystery. The Catholic liturgical tradition reinforces this reading by placing resurrection-themed antiphons alongside Psalm 3 during the Easter season.

The declaration "Salvation belongs to the Lord" (3:8) resonates with Catholic soteriology, which affirms that salvation is entirely God's gift while also requiring human cooperation with grace. The psalm's movement from desperate petition to confident trust mirrors the spiritual journey described by Catholic mystics: the purgative way (acknowledging one's foes and helplessness), the illuminative way (recognizing God as shield and sustainer), and the unitive way (resting in the certainty of God's salvation and blessing). Verse 4, "I cried aloud to the Lord, and he answered me from his holy hill," has been connected to the communion of saints — the "holy hill" is both earthly Zion and the heavenly Jerusalem where the saints intercede.
