Psalm 41 opens with a beatitude that Catholic social teaching has embraced as foundational: "Blessed are those who have regard for the weak" (41:1). The Church's preferential option for the poor — articulated by the Latin American bishops at Medellin (1968) and Puebla (1979) and reiterated by successive popes — finds scriptural grounding in this verse. Care for the vulnerable is not optional charity but a constitutive element of Christian discipleship.

The psalm's reference to betrayal by "my close friend, one who shared my bread" (41:9) is applied by Jesus to Judas at the Last Supper (John 13:18), giving this verse central importance in Catholic Holy Week liturgy. The sharing of bread that Judas violated is understood as a Eucharistic betrayal — the most intimate communion turned into the most devastating treachery. Catholic theology sees in this the gravity of receiving the Eucharist unworthily (1 Corinthians 11:27-29).

The concluding doxology — "Praise be to the LORD, the God of Israel, from everlasting to everlasting" (41:13) — marks the end of Book I of the Psalter. Catholic liturgical tradition uses such doxologies to structure the prayer of the Church, always returning from lament and petition to praise. The *Gloria Patri* appended to every psalm in the Divine Office reflects this same instinct: all prayer finds its completion in the glorification of the Triune God.
