Psalm 5's morning plea for guidance amid deceitful enemies is read by Gnostic interpreters as the pneumatic soul's daily struggle against the deceptions of the archontic world. The "boastful" who cannot stand before God's eyes (5:5) are reinterpreted as the archons themselves, who boast of their power and divine status but cannot endure the gaze of the true, transcendent God. The Demiurge's chief characteristic, according to the *Apocryphon of John*, is his boastful declaration "I am God and there is no other" — a claim that reveals his ignorance of the Pleroma above.

The psalm's strong language about God's hatred of evil (5:4-6) creates an interpretive challenge for Gnostic readers. If the God speaking here is the Demiurge, then his "hatred of evil" is merely the tyrant's rage against those who disobey his arbitrary laws. But if the speaker is the true God, then the "evildoers" are the archons and their servants — those who perpetuate the cosmic deception. Valentinian exegesis, which was the most sophisticated Gnostic hermeneutic, would read the psalm on multiple levels simultaneously, finding both the Demiurge's self-righteous fury and the true God's genuine opposition to the evil of ignorance.

The plea "Lead me, O Lord, in your righteousness" (5:8) becomes, in Gnostic reading, a prayer for the guidance of Sophia (Wisdom) or the Holy Spirit, understood as feminine divine figures who lead the pneumatic soul through the labyrinth of the material world toward the Pleroma. The "way" to be made "straight" (5:8b) is the path of ascent through the archontic spheres — the journey the soul must make after death (or through mystical experience) to return to its divine origin. This ascent requires knowledge of the passwords, seals, and formulae that allow passage past each archontic guardian.
