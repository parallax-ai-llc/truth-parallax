Psalm 28's cry to God as "my rock" (28:1) is reinterpreted in Gnostic thought as an appeal to the immovable, transcendent God beyond the shifting instabilities of the material cosmos. The Demiurge's creation is characterized by flux and decay; the true God, by contrast, is the unchanging foundation upon which the pneumatic soul can rest. The *Apocryphon of John* describes the Monad as "immovable" and "not in any inferiority" — the ultimate rock.

The psalmist's fear of being "dragged away with the wicked" (28:3) takes on cosmic significance in Gnostic reading: it is the fear of the divine spark being permanently absorbed into material existence, losing its identity and its connection to the Pleroma. The archons — the rulers of the material world — seek to keep souls entrapped in ignorance, and the psalmist's plea for deliverance is the cry of the spirit against its cosmic captors.

The closing thanksgiving — "The LORD is my strength and my shield" (28:7) — affirms that the true God provides protection against the archonic powers. Gnostic texts such as the *Pistis Sophia* describe the ascent of the soul through hostile celestial spheres, each governed by archons who seek to prevent the soul's return to the Pleroma. The psalm assures the Gnostic that divine power is sufficient to overcome every obstacle on the path of ascent.
