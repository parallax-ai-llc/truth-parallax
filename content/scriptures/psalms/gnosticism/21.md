Psalm 21's celebration of royal blessings is reread by Gnostic interpreters as a description of the pneumatic soul's reception in the Pleroma upon successful ascent. The "crown of fine gold" (21:3) is the crown of *gnosis* — the luminous wreath or garland that Gnostic texts describe as placed upon the heads of the redeemed. The *Odes of Solomon* (Ode 1) opens: "The Lord is on my head like a crown, and I shall not be without Him." The crown symbolizes the restoration of the divine glory that the soul lost when it descended into matter.

The promise of "length of days forever and ever" (21:4) is the pneumatic's liberation from the cycle of birth and death that the archons impose. In some Gnostic systems, unawakened souls are recycled through the material world in successive incarnations, trapped in an endless loop. The pneumatic who achieves *gnosis* breaks this cycle and enters the timeless existence of the Pleroma, where "length of days" is not measured chronologically but signifies the infinite quality of Pleromatic life — existence without diminishment, joy without satiation, knowledge without limit.

The psalm's vision of enemies consumed by fire (21:9-12) is read as the final dissolution of the archontic powers. When all light-sparks have been extracted from the material cosmos, the Demiurge's world will have no more purpose and will be consumed by the purifying fire that separates the last traces of light from matter. This is not a punishment in the moral sense but a cosmological inevitability: the material world was never meant to be permanent; it was an accident, a product of Sophia's error, and its dissolution is not destruction but correction — the restoration of the proper order that existed before the Fall.
