Psalm 36's contrast between human wickedness (36:1-4) and divine love (36:5-9) maps directly onto the Gnostic distinction between the *kenoma* (the realm of deficiency and ignorance) and the *Pleroma* (the realm of fullness and light). The wicked person who has "no fear of God" (36:1) represents the hylic — the purely material being who is wholly identified with the Demiurge's creation and has no awareness of the transcendent God.

The luminous center of the psalm — "In your light do we see light" (36:9) — is arguably the most important verse for Gnostic theology in the entire Psalter. This is a precise description of the Gnostic epistemological principle: divine knowledge is only possible through divine light. The spark within recognizes the Light above because they share the same nature. *Gnosis* occurs when "like knows like" — when the divine light in the human encounters the divine light of the Pleroma.

The psalm's declaration that God is "the fountain of life" (36:9) identifies the true God — not the Demiurge — as the source of genuine existence. In Gnostic cosmology, the material world is not truly alive but is a shadow or imitation of the living Pleroma. Real life flows from the Invisible Spirit, and the pneumatic soul drinks from this fountain when it attains *gnosis*. The Demiurge can fashion bodies but cannot bestow the life that originates in the transcendent source.
