Psalm 20's intercessory prayer for the king is reread by Gnostic interpreters as a prayer for the pneumatic soul embarking on the perilous journey of ascent through the archontic spheres. The "day of trouble" (20:1) is the moment of death, when the soul must navigate past the hostile archons who guard each planetary gate. The "help from the sanctuary" (20:2) is the assistance of the divine Aeons who descend to guide the soul upward, and the "support from Zion" is the power of the Pleroma reaching down to draw the ascending spark homeward.

The decisive verse — "Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the Lord our God" (20:7) — receives a specialized reading in Gnostic liturgical practice. Knowledge of the divine names is essential for the soul's ascent: each archontic sphere is guarded by a power that can be bypassed only by pronouncing the correct name, password, or formula. The *Books of Jeu* and the *Pistis Sophia* contain elaborate catalogs of these names and seals. To "trust in the name" is thus not merely a general attitude of faith but a specific spiritual technology — the mastery of the sacred names that give power over the archons.

The psalm's vision of victory — "Some collapse and fall, but we rise and stand upright" (20:8) — describes the contrast between the fate of the unawakened (who "collapse" back into material existence, possibly through reincarnation) and the pneumatics (who "rise and stand upright" in the Pleroma). The final prayer — "O Lord, save the king!" (20:9) — is the community's petition for the successful ascent of each departing soul, a prayer that Gnostic funeral liturgies may have incorporated into their rites for the dead.
