Psalm 4 as an evening psalm of peace is read by Gnostic interpreters as a meditation on the inner tranquility available to the pneumatic who has received *gnosis*. The "relief in distress" (4:1) is not merely psychological comfort but the liberation that comes from knowing that the material world's afflictions are ultimately illusory — the real self, the divine spark, is beyond the reach of worldly suffering. The Valentinian distinction between the pneumatic (spiritual), psychic (soul-level), and hylic (material) dimensions of human existence means that the peace described in this psalm belongs to the pneumatic level alone.

The challenge "How long will you love vain words and seek after lies?" (4:2) is reread as a Gnostic rebuke directed at those who remain attached to the Demiurge's system of religion, morality, and worldly striving. The "vain words" are the teachings of the false god — the commandments, threats, and promises that keep humanity trapped in the material order. The "lies" are the Demiurge's claims to be the only God, the supreme being, when in truth he is merely a flawed emanation, ignorant of the Pleroma above him.

The psalm's declaration "You have put more gladness in my heart than they have when their grain and wine abound" (4:7) is read as the Gnostic experience of interior illumination. The gladness described is the joy of *gnosis* itself — the intoxicating recognition that one is not a creature of matter but a spark of divine light temporarily sojourning in darkness. The closing peace — "In peace I will both lie down and sleep" (4:8) — is not the ordinary peace of physical rest but the profound serenity of the awakened soul who knows that even death cannot destroy the divine spark within.
