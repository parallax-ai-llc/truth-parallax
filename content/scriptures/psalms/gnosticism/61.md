## The Rock Beyond the Demiurge's Reach

Psalm 61's plea — "lead me to the rock that is higher than I" (v. 2) — is read in Gnostic exegesis as the pneumatic soul's cry for something beyond the material world. The "rock" is the transcendent God, immovable and unreachable by the archontic powers. The cry from "the end of the earth" (v. 2) describes the soul's condition at the farthest remove from its true home — trapped at the bottom of the cosmic hierarchy, looking upward through layer upon layer of archontic heavens toward the Father who exists beyond them all.

The desire to "dwell in your tent forever" (v. 4) is interpreted as longing for permanent residence in the Pleroma. The *Tripartite Tractate* (NHC I,5) describes the Pleroma as the true dwelling of all pneumatic beings — the place from which they have been exiled and to which they will return. The material world, by contrast, is a temporary tent of flesh that the soul inhabits only during its sojourn in matter. The psalmist's desire for God's tent is the desire for the eternal, uncreated dwelling that predates and will outlast the Demiurge's material construction.

The prayer for the king to be "enthroned forever before God" (v. 7) is read as referring to the Gnostic Christ — the aeon sent from the Pleroma who reigns eternally in the presence of the true Father. Unlike the earthly kings who serve the Demiurge's purposes, the Gnostic Christ exercises a kingship of knowledge and liberation, enthroned not on a material throne but in the hearts of the pneumatics who recognize his saving message. The psalm thus becomes, in Gnostic reading, a hymn to the spiritual king who leads the elect back to their heavenly origin.
