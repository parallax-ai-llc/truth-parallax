## The Gnostic Resolution of Cosmic Injustice

Psalm 73's anguished question — why do the wicked prosper while the righteous suffer? — receives a distinctive answer in Gnostic thought. The prosperity of the wicked (vv. 3-12) is not a theological anomaly but an expected feature of the Demiurge's cosmos, which is structured to reward those who serve its system. The arrogant who say "How can God know?" (v. 11) are right in one sense: the Demiurge, limited in knowledge, genuinely may not perceive the injustice of his own creation. The true Father above does know — but the Demiurge's universe was not designed to implement the Father's justice.

The turning point — "until I went into the sanctuary of God; then I discerned their end" (v. 17) — describes the moment of *gnosis*. The "sanctuary" is not the Demiurge's temple but the inner sanctum of the soul, where the divine spark perceives truth directly. The Valentinian sacrament of the bridal chamber (*nymphon*) — described in the *Gospel of Philip* as the holiest of the mysteries — may be the "sanctuary" in which the pneumatic receives the illumination that resolves all questions. From this vantage point, the wicked's prosperity is seen for what it is: a brief flicker within a system destined for dissolution.

The psalm's climax — "Whom have I in heaven but you? And there is nothing on earth that I desire besides you" (v. 25) — is the supreme Gnostic confession. The pneumatic, having seen through the archontic heavens to the true Father, desires nothing that the material world offers. The "heaven" referenced is not the Demiurge's visible sky but the Pleroma beyond it. The closing declaration — "it is good to be near God" (v. 28) — expresses the *gnosis* that divine nearness is the only true good, and that the entire cosmos, with its apparent injustices, is simply the darkness through which the soul must pass to reach the light.
