Psalm 13's anguished "How long?" is read by Gnostic interpreters as the divine spark's cry during its prolonged imprisonment in the material world. The fourfold repetition (13:1-2) corresponds to the multiple layers of bondage — physical body, psychic illusion, archontic deception, and cosmic forgetfulness — that the soul must overcome to achieve liberation. The experience of God "hiding his face" (13:1) is not a temporary spiritual trial but the fundamental condition of incarnation: the Pleroma is hidden behind the archontic veil, and the soul experiences this hiddenness as abandonment.

The "sorrow in my heart all the day" (13:2) describes what Gnostic texts call the condition of the *psyche* trapped in *hyle* (matter). The *Exegesis on the Soul* describes the soul's grief in terms of sexual degradation and slavery — a far more radical portrait of suffering than conventional readings suggest. The soul has forgotten its divine bridegroom and has been forced into relationships with the archons, who abuse and exploit it. The plea "Light up my eyes" (13:3) is a prayer for *gnosis* — the illumination that dispels the darkness of ignorance and reveals the soul's true identity.

The psalm's turn to trust (13:5) — occurring without any external change — is particularly significant for Gnostic spirituality. The shift happens within consciousness itself: the soul remembers. This *anamnesis* (remembrance) is the essential Gnostic experience — not a new acquisition of knowledge but the recovery of knowledge that was always present but suppressed by the archontic conditioning. The closing song (13:6) is the hymn of the liberated soul, the same song sung by Sophia upon her restoration to the Pleroma in the *Pistis Sophia*, a song of gratitude for the knowledge that sets free.
