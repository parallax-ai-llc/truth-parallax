Psalm 43's prayer — "Send me your light and your faithful care, let them lead me" (43:3) — is read in Gnostic tradition as a petition for the descent of divine emanations that will guide the soul's ascent back to the Pleroma. In Valentinian theology, "light" and "truth" are understood as aeons — divine beings within the Pleroma whose function is to illuminate and guide the scattered pneumatic sparks back to their source.

The desire to come "to the altar of God, to God, my joy and my delight" (43:4) describes the Gnostic goal: not earthly worship at a material altar but the soul's arrival at the divine center, the innermost sanctuary of the Pleroma where the Invisible Spirit dwells. The *Gospel of Philip* describes this destination as the "Holy of Holies" or the "Bridal Chamber" — the place of ultimate union between the pneumatic soul and its divine counterpart.

The self-directed exhortation — "Why, my soul, are you downcast? Put your hope in God" (43:5) — models the Gnostic practice of self-remembrance. The pneumatic must continually remind the *psyche* (lower soul) of truths that the *pneuma* (spirit) already knows. This internal dialogue between the higher and lower aspects of the self is a characteristic feature of Gnostic spiritual practice, described extensively in texts like the *Teachings of Silvanus*.
