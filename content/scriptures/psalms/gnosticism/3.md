Psalm 3's depiction of a soul surrounded by foes is read by Gnostic interpreters as the predicament of the divine spark trapped in the material world. The "many foes" (3:1) are the archons and their agents — the cosmic powers that surround and oppress the pneumatic soul, seeking to keep it ignorant of its true origin. The taunt "There is no salvation for him in God" (3:2) is the voice of the archons themselves, who deny that there is any god above the Demiurge and any salvation beyond the material cosmos.

The pivotal declaration "But you, O Lord, are a shield about me" (3:3) takes on a different valence in Gnostic reading. The "Lord" addressed here is not the Demiurge but the true, hidden God — the source of the Pleroma, the ultimate reality beyond the archontic heavens. The pneumatic, in a moment of *anamnesis* (remembrance), recalls that there is a power greater than the archons, a shield not of material protection but of spiritual knowledge. The "lifting of the head" (3:3b) signifies the soul's upward turning — the recognition that its true home is above, not in the material prison below.

The sleep and awakening of verse 5 — "I lay down and slept; I woke again" — is perhaps the most significant phrase for Gnostic exegesis in this psalm. Gnostic mythology portrays the human condition as a state of sleep or drunkenness imposed by the archons. The *Hymn of the Pearl* (Acts of Thomas) describes the soul that falls asleep in Egypt (the material world) and forgets its royal identity. Awakening is the moment of *gnosis* — the sudden recognition of one's divine origin. The psalm's "I woke again" thus becomes a cry of liberation: the pneumatic has broken through the archontic spell and remembered who they truly are.
