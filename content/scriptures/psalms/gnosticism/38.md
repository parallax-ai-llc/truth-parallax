Psalm 38's anguished description of suffering — "My wounds fester and are loathsome because of my sinful folly" (38:5) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as describing the soul's torment within the material body. The "wounds" are not merely physical ailments but the spiritual damage inflicted by incarnation itself. The *Hymn of the Pearl* describes the prince who put on "their filthy and unclean garment" (the body) and became sick with the corruption of the material world.

The psalm's isolation — "my friends and companions avoid me because of my wounds; my neighbors stay far away" (38:11) — mirrors the Gnostic experience of alienation from a world that cannot comprehend the pneumatic's suffering. Those trapped in material consciousness (hylics and psychics) cannot understand the spiritual anguish of one who perceives the imprisonment of the divine spark. The Gnostic is a stranger in the Demiurge's cosmos, and this estrangement is the source of profound loneliness.

Yet the psalm's persistence in prayer — "LORD, I wait for you; you will answer" (38:15) — affirms the Gnostic confidence that the true God hears the cry of the imprisoned spark. Though the Demiurge may seem to rule the visible world, the pneumatic's prayer penetrates beyond the cosmic spheres to the transcendent Father. The *Pistis Sophia*'s repeated cries to the "Light of Lights" model this same persistent appeal through suffering to the God above all gods.
