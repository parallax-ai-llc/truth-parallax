Psalm 6's cry of physical and spiritual anguish is read by Gnostic interpreters as the divine spark's lamentation over its imprisonment in matter. The "troubled bones" (6:2) and "greatly troubled soul" (6:3) describe not ordinary illness but the fundamental condition of incarnation — the agony of a divine being trapped in a material body crafted by the Demiurge. The *Exegesis on the Soul* (NHC II,6) similarly describes the soul's suffering in the body as a form of prostitution and degradation, a fall from the heavenly bridal chamber into the squalor of the material world.

The plea "O Lord, rebuke me not in your anger" (6:1) raises the question: whose anger? For the Gnostic reader, the Demiurge's anger is the punitive system of the material cosmos — sickness, death, and suffering that the archontic powers impose on embodied souls. The true God, by contrast, has no anger; the Pleroma is characterized by fullness, joy, and peace. The psalmist's fear of divine wrath is thus a vestige of the Demiurge's conditioning — the false belief that suffering is divine punishment rather than archontic oppression.

The turning point — "The Lord has heard the sound of my weeping" (6:8) — is read as the moment when the call of the imprisoned spark reaches the Pleroma and triggers the descent of a salvific power. The *Trimorphic Protennoia* (NHC XIII,1) describes the divine Voice descending through the cosmic spheres in response to the cries of the trapped light-sparks, calling them to remember their origin and begin the journey home. The psalm's shift from despair to confidence mirrors the Gnostic pattern of awakening: the soul cries out in ignorance and suffering, the divine response comes as revelatory knowledge, and the soul is transformed from captive to liberated knower.
