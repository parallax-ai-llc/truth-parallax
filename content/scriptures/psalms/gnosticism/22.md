Psalm 22 is the most theologically contested psalm between Gnostic and orthodox Christian readings. The cry "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (22:1) is interpreted in diverse ways across Gnostic systems. In Docetic Christologies, the divine Christ-Aeon departed from the human Jesus before the crucifixion, and the cry of forsakenness comes from the abandoned human shell rather than from the divine being. The *Second Treatise of the Great Seth* (NHC VII,2) describes the crucifixion as an illusion: "It was another who drank the gall and vinegar; it was not I... It was another upon whom they placed the crown of thorns."

Other Gnostic readings are more nuanced. The Valentinian interpretation sees the cry as the moment when the divine Sophia — whose passion and fall initiated the entire cosmic drama — reaches her nadir of suffering. The cross is not the suffering of God in the orthodox sense but the ultimate expression of the cosmic tragedy: the divine trapped in matter, crying out for reunion with the Pleroma. Every pneumatic who experiences spiritual darkness echoes this cry — it is the universal lament of divinity imprisoned in the world of the Demiurge.

The psalm's dramatic reversal at verse 22 — from suffering to praise, from isolation to universal worship — corresponds to the Gnostic salvific narrative's resolution. The "great congregation" (22:25) is the assembly of the redeemed pneumatics, gathered in the Pleroma for the final celebration. The universal worship prophesied — "all the ends of the earth shall remember and turn to the Lord" (22:27) — describes the ultimate awakening, when all divine sparks have been recovered and the Pleroma is once again whole. The closing phrase "he has done it" (22:31) is the announcement that the cosmic drama is complete, the light has been gathered, and the original rupture — Sophia's fall — has been healed.
