## The Passion of the Spiritual Christ

Psalm 69 is central to the Gnostic understanding of Christ's passion, though Gnostic Christology radically reinterprets the meaning of the crucifixion. The cry "the waters have come up to my neck" (v. 1) describes the Savior's descent into the depths of material existence — the "deep waters" and "deep mire" (vv. 2, 14) represent the chaos of matter through which the spiritual Christ must pass to reach the imprisoned sparks. The *Second Treatise of the Great Seth* describes Christ's perspective during the crucifixion: "I did not die in reality but in appearance... it was another who drank the gall and the vinegar." The archons believed they were destroying the Savior but were in fact unable to touch his true spiritual nature.

The psalm's zeal — "zeal for your house has consumed me" (v. 9) — is the Savior's consuming passion not for the Jerusalem Temple (the Demiurge's house) but for the true "house" of the divine sparks trapped within material bodies. Each pneumatic human being is a "house" containing a fragment of the Pleroma's light, and the Savior's zeal is directed toward the liberation of every last spark. The offering of vinegar (v. 21) is read as the archons' attempt to administer the material world's bitter substitute for the living water of *gnosis*.

The imprecatory section (vv. 22-28) is applied to the fate of the archontic powers. The "table" that becomes a "snare" (v. 22) is the sacrificial system the Demiurge established — his worship traps the psychics and hylics, preventing them from seeking the true God beyond. The concluding hope — "God will save Zion" (v. 35) — is reinterpreted: the true Zion is not the earthly city but the Pleroma itself, and the "cities of Judah" are the aeons within the divine fullness that will be restored when all the scattered light is gathered home.
