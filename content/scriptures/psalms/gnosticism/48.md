Psalm 48's celebration of "the city of our God" (48:1) is read in Gnostic theology as describing the heavenly Pleroma rather than earthly Jerusalem. The "city" whose beauty "is the joy of the whole earth" (48:2) is the spiritual metropolis of the aeons — the ordered, luminous reality that stands in contrast to the chaos of the material cosmos. Earthly Jerusalem is, at best, a dim reflection of this heavenly city; at worst, a Demiurgic imitation.

The hostile kings who "saw her and were astounded" (48:5) represent the archons who occasionally glimpse the Pleroma's glory and are overwhelmed by its superiority to their own creation. The *Hypostasis of the Archons* describes moments when the rulers of the material world catch sight of a higher reality and are thrown into confusion. Their power, which seems absolute within the material cosmos, is revealed as trivial before the radiance of the Pleroma.

The invitation to "walk about Zion, go around her, count her towers" (48:12) becomes an invitation to contemplate the structure of the Pleroma — its aeons, its syzygies (divine pairs), its emanative order. Gnostic cosmological texts such as the *Apocryphon of John* and the *Tripartite Tractate* do precisely this: they "count the towers" of the divine fullness, mapping the spiritual architecture of ultimate reality so that the pneumatic soul can recognize its true home.
