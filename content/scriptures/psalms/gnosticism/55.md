## Betrayal and the Soul's Desire for Flight

Psalm 55's anguished desire — "Oh, that I had wings like a dove! I would fly away and be at rest" (v. 6) — speaks directly to the Gnostic soul's longing to escape the prison of material existence. The dove is a recurrent symbol in Gnostic texts, representing the Holy Spirit or the pneumatic element that yearns to return to its heavenly origin. The *Hymn of the Pearl* (Acts of Thomas) narrates the soul's journey from the heavenly kingdom into the material world and its eventual return — this psalm's wish for dove's wings expresses the same narrative of exile and longing for homecoming.

The betrayal by a companion (vv. 12-14) is read by Gnostic interpreters as depicting the treachery of the Demiurge, who initially appears to be humanity's benefactor but proves to be a jailer. The *Apocryphon of John* describes how the archons created the human body as a prison for the divine spark, then posed as its protectors. The psalmist's shock at being betrayed by "my companion, my familiar friend" mirrors the pneumatic's awakening to the truth that the god they have worshiped is not the true Father but a deceptive imitator.

The inner turmoil described in verses 4-5 — "the terrors of death have fallen upon me... fear and trembling come upon me" — is understood as the existential horror that accompanies the initial stages of *gnosis*. Before the pneumatic fully grasps the liberating truth, the shattering of previous certainties produces what the Valentinians called the experience of *kenoma* (emptiness/void) — the terrifying recognition that the entire familiar world is a construct of ignorance. The psalm's exhortation to "cast your burden on the LORD" (v. 22) is reread as an instruction to release one's attachment to the material world and trust in the transcendent God beyond the Demiurge.
