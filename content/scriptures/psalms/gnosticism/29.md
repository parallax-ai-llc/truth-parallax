Psalm 29's sevenfold "voice of the LORD" presents a complex text for Gnostic interpretation. The Gnostic reader must distinguish between the creative word of the Demiurge — who shapes the material world through speech — and the voice of the true God, whose word emanates the aeons of the Pleroma. The storm imagery (29:3-9) may be read as the Demiurge's display of power over the material cosmos, impressive but ultimately inferior to the silent emanation of the true God.

However, a more sympathetic Gnostic reading sees in the sevenfold voice an echo of the seven emanations or powers that proceed from the divine source. The Valentinian system describes a series of divine emanations (*probole*) through which the Pleroma unfolds, and the psalm's seven utterances can be mapped onto this emanative process. The voice that creates is not the Demiurge's crude command but the Logos expressing the divine fullness.

The psalm's conclusion — "The LORD sits enthroned over the flood" (29:10) — is read as affirming divine sovereignty over the waters of chaos, which in Gnostic cosmology represent the formless matter (*hyle*) from which the material world was fashioned. The true God's enthronement over chaos means that disorder and darkness are not ultimate realities but are contained within a larger divine order that the Gnostic gradually comes to perceive through *gnosis*.
