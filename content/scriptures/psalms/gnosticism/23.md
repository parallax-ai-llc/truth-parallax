Psalm 23 is reread by Gnostic interpreters as a description of the soul's guided journey through the archontic spheres back to the Pleroma. The "shepherd" (23:1) is not the Demiurge (who is a thief and a robber, in the Gnostic inversion of John 10) but the divine guide — whether Christ, Sophia, or an angelic emissary from the Pleroma — who leads the pneumatic soul safely past the hostile powers that guard each celestial gate. "I shall not want" (23:1b) expresses the sufficiency of *gnosis*: the soul that possesses knowledge needs nothing from the material world.

The "valley of the shadow of death" (23:4) is the material cosmos itself — a dark valley through which the soul passes during its sojourn in the body. The archons are the lurking dangers, and the "rod and staff" are the sacred passwords, seals, and knowledge that the guide provides for safe passage. Gnostic funerary texts — instructions for the dead — serve precisely this function, equipping the departing soul with the words and gestures needed to pass each archontic checkpoint. The *Pistis Sophia* contains detailed instructions for the soul's post-mortem journey, corresponding exactly to the psalm's scenario of guided passage through danger.

The table prepared "in the presence of my enemies" (23:5) is the Pleromatic banquet — the feast of reunion that awaits the soul that has successfully navigated past the archons. The "enemies" who watch helplessly are the archontic powers, stripped of their captive and powerless to reclaim it. The anointing with oil (23:5b) is the chrismation — the sealing with the Holy Spirit that many Gnostic communities practiced as a sacrament of initiation and protection. The promise of dwelling "in the house of the Lord forever" (23:6) is the soul's permanent reunion with the Pleroma, from which it will never again be separated.
