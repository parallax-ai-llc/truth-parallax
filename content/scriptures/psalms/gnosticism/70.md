## The Pneumatic's Cry for Swift Liberation

Psalm 70's urgent plea — "Make haste, O God, to deliver me!" (v. 1) — expresses the anguish of the pneumatic soul trapped in a material existence that feels unbearably prolonged. The *Exegesis on the Soul* describes the soul's impatience for redemption after it has awakened to its imprisonment: once *gnosis* dawns, every moment in the material world becomes a source of suffering, and the soul cries out for swift deliverance. The psalm's brevity intensifies its urgency — five verses of concentrated longing, stripped of all ornament.

The prayer that enemies would be "put to shame and confusion" while seekers of God "rejoice and be glad" (vv. 2-4) maps onto the Gnostic eschatological division between the archontic powers (and their hylic servants) who will be exposed and dissolved, and the pneumatics who will be gathered into the Pleroma. The shame of the enemies is not merely psychological but ontological — their entire reality is revealed as a deception, their power as an illusion, their cosmos as a counterfeit.

The self-identification as "poor and needy" (v. 5) takes on Gnostic resonance: the pneumatic soul is "poor" in material terms because it has renounced the Demiurge's wealth, recognizing it as a trap. But this poverty is also the condition of receptivity — the *Gospel of Thomas* (Saying 3) teaches that "the kingdom is inside of you, and it is outside of you. When you come to know yourselves, then you will become known." The pneumatic's poverty is the prerequisite for the richness of *gnosis*, just as the empty vessel is the one that can be filled with the true God's living water.
