Gnostic interpreters approached the Psalms as texts containing hidden wisdom (*gnosis*) accessible only to the pneumatic (spiritual) reader. Psalm 1's contrast between the righteous and the wicked is reread through the fundamental Gnostic hermeneutical lens: the surface meaning conceals a deeper teaching about the soul's imprisonment in matter and its liberation through knowledge. The "blessed man" of verse 1 is not simply someone who avoids sinners but the pneumatic who refuses to participate in the Demiurge's system of moral control — the law, the judgment, the entire apparatus of the lower god's dominion.

The "law of the Lord" in which the blessed man meditates "day and night" (1:2) is radically reinterpreted by Gnostic readers. For Valentinian Gnostics, the true "law" is not the Torah of the Demiurge (Yaldabaoth) but the hidden wisdom of the Pleroma — the fullness of the divine realm above the material cosmos. The tree planted by streams of water (1:3) becomes the pneumatic soul rooted in the living waters of the Pleroma, drawing sustenance not from the Demiurge's creation but from the transcendent Source beyond it. The *Gospel of Truth* (NHC I,3) speaks similarly of those who are "planted in the paradise" of the true Father.

The chaff driven by wind (1:4) represents, in Gnostic reading, the hylic (material) humans who are bound to the cycle of material existence and subject to the Demiurge's judgment. The "way of the wicked" that perishes (1:6) is not moral wickedness in the conventional sense but ignorance — the failure to recognize one's divine origin and to seek return to the Pleroma. Gnostic soteriology replaces moral categories with epistemological ones: the fundamental distinction is not between good and evil but between knowledge and ignorance, between those who remember their divine origin and those who have forgotten it entirely.
