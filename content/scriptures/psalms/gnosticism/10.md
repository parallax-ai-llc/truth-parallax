Psalm 10's cry about God's apparent absence — "Why, O Lord, do you stand far away?" (10:1) — receives a radical answer in Gnostic theology: the true God *is* far away, separated from the material cosmos by the vast gulf of the archontic spheres. The true God did not create this world, does not govern it directly, and is genuinely absent from the material order in a way that orthodox theology would never concede. The psalm's question, read Gnostically, is not a lament about divine testing but a recognition of cosmic reality: the Pleroma is distant, and the Demiurge who rules this world is not the true God.

The detailed portrait of the wicked (10:2-11) is read as a description of the Demiurge himself. The one who "says in his heart, 'God has forgotten'" (10:11) is Yaldabaoth, who denies the existence of any god above himself. The predatory behavior described — lurking, seizing the poor, crushing the helpless (10:9-10) — matches the Gnostic characterization of the Demiurge as a jealous, violent tyrant who devours the spiritual energy of trapped souls to sustain his own illegitimate power. The *Hypostasis of the Archons* portrays the Demiurge in just these terms.

The psalm's resolution — "But you do see" (10:14) — is the moment of hope in the Gnostic reading. Though the true God is distant, He is not indifferent. The divine realm *does* perceive the suffering of the trapped sparks, and it responds by sending salvific knowledge through its emissaries. The promise that God "hears the desire of the afflicted" (10:17) and does justice to "the fatherless" (10:18) describes the Pleroma's response to the cries of orphaned divine sparks — souls separated from their true Parent by the Demiurge's cosmic machinery. The ultimate liberation is assured, even if the present darkness persists.
