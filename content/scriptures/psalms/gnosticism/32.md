Psalm 32's celebration of forgiveness — "Blessed is the one whose transgressions are forgiven" (32:1) — is reinterpreted in Gnostic theology through the lens of *agnoia* (ignorance) rather than moral transgression. The fundamental human problem in Gnostic thought is not sin in the conventional sense but ignorance of one's divine nature. "Forgiveness" thus means the dissolution of this ignorance through the illumination of *gnosis*.

The torment of concealed sin — "When I kept silent, my bones wasted away" (32:3) — describes in Gnostic reading the suffering of the soul that suppresses its own divine nature. The spark within demands recognition; when it is denied, the entire person suffers. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 70) teaches: "If you bring forth what is within you, what you bring forth will save you. If you do not bring forth what is within you, what you do not bring forth will destroy you."

The psalm's image of God as guide — "I will instruct you and teach you in the way you should go" (32:8) — corresponds to the Gnostic role of the divine revealer who descends from the Pleroma to awaken sleeping souls. In Valentinian thought, the Christos-figure serves this revelatory function, bringing the *gnosis* that liberates the pneumatic from ignorance. The psalm's promise of guidance is the promise of revelation — the knowledge that sets the captive spark free.
