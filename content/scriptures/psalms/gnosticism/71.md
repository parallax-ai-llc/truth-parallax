## The Aging Pneumatic's Undiminished Gnosis

Psalm 71's prayer of the aging believer acquires distinctive meaning in Gnostic reading. The plea "Do not cast me off in the time of old age" (v. 9) expresses the pneumatic's concern that the weakening of the physical body might impair the soul's capacity for *gnosis*. However, the Gnostic understanding ultimately inverts this fear: as the material body declines, the pneumatic element within actually becomes more prominent, less encumbered by material distractions. The aging process, paradoxically, strips away the layers of matter that have obscured the divine spark.

The psalmist's testimony — "From my youth you have taught me" (v. 17) — is read as the progressive unfolding of *gnosis* across a lifetime. The *Gospel of Truth* teaches that the process of awakening is not instantaneous but gradual: the Father calls each pneumatic soul by name, and the soul responds over the course of its entire earthly sojourn. The commitment to "proclaim your might to another generation" (v. 18) is the Gnostic teacher's vocation — the transmission of saving knowledge from one generation of pneumatics to the next, ensuring that the chain of illumination is not broken.

The declaration "I will hope continually and will praise you yet more and more" (v. 14) is heard as the pneumatic's growing confidence as they approach the end of material existence. The *Odes of Solomon* (Ode 7) describes this confidence: "As the sun is joy to those who seek its daybreak, so is my joy in the Lord." For the Gnostic, death is not the end but the beginning of the soul's unimpeded ascent through the archontic spheres toward the Pleroma. The aged pneumatic's "yet more and more" praise reflects the increasing certainty that liberation is near.
