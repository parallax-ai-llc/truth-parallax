## The Divine Record of the Soul's Suffering

Psalm 56's image of God recording tears in a bottle (v. 8) is given a distinctive reading in Gnostic thought. The tears represent the suffering of the divine sparks scattered throughout the material cosmos — each tear is a fragment of Sophia's primal grief over her separation from the Pleroma. The Valentinian myth narrates that the material elements were produced from Sophia's passions: grief became water, fear became earth, perplexity became air. The tears in the bottle are thus not merely personal sorrows but cosmic evidence of the Pleroma's ongoing anguish over its lost light.

The refrain "In God I trust; I shall not be afraid. What can flesh do to me?" (v. 4) is read as the pneumatic's declaration of freedom from the tyranny of material existence. "Flesh" is not merely the human body but the entire archontic system that governs through fear. The *Gospel of Philip* teaches that the archons maintain control over humanity primarily through ignorance and fear — fear of death, fear of poverty, fear of social rejection. The pneumatic who possesses *gnosis* is liberated from these fears because they know that their true identity transcends the material order entirely.

The psalm's conclusion — walking "before God in the light of the living" (v. 13) — is reinterpreted as the pneumatic soul's journey toward the Pleroma, the realm of true life and light. The "light of the living" is contrasted with the false light of the material sun, which Gnostic texts identify as an archontic creation. The true light is the uncreated radiance of the Father, perceived not through physical eyes but through the awakened *nous* (spiritual intellect) of the pneumatic individual.
