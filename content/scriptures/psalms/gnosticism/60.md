## The Broken Cosmos and Divine Sovereignty

Psalm 60's opening lament — "O God, you have rejected us, broken our defenses" (v. 1) — is heard in Gnostic reading as the pneumatic community's cry from within a fractured cosmos. The "breaking" is not a temporary military setback but the fundamental rupture at the heart of existence: the fall from the Pleroma, the shattering of divine unity into the fragments of material creation. The "wine that made us stagger" (v. 3) represents the intoxicating ignorance that the Demiurge imposes on the divine sparks — a drunkenness of forgetfulness described extensively in the *Gospel of Truth*.

The divine oracle (vv. 6-8), in which God claims sovereignty over various territories, is read ironically by Gnostic interpreters. The Demiurge boasts of ownership over the material world, parceling out territories as though he were the supreme deity. But the pneumatic reader knows that this claim is hollow — the Demiurge's sovereignty extends only over the counterfeit cosmos, not over the Pleroma or the divine sparks trapped within his creation. True sovereignty belongs to the Father of All, who stands beyond and above the Demiurge's petty territorial claims.

The psalm's concluding confession — "With God we shall do valiantly" (v. 12) — is read as the pneumatic community's declaration of confidence in the true God's ultimate victory. "Valiantly" does not mean military conquest but the courage to pursue *gnosis* against all opposition. The "foes" whom God will "tread down" are the archontic powers, and their defeat will come not through physical warfare but through the irresistible power of truth penetrating the cosmic deception.
