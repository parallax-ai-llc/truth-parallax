Psalm 30's celebration of deliverance from death — "You brought me up from the realm of the dead" (30:3) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as describing the soul's awakening from the death-like sleep of material existence. In Gnostic theology, the unawakened soul is effectively dead — trapped in the tomb of the body, unaware of its divine origin. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 56) teaches that "whoever has come to understand the world has found only a corpse."

The pivotal verse — "Weeping may stay for the night, but rejoicing comes in the morning" (30:5) — maps onto the Gnostic narrative of fall and restoration. The "night" is the soul's sojourn in the material world, the *kenoma* (emptiness) that contrasts with the Pleroma (fullness). The "morning" is the dawn of *gnosis* — the moment when the divine spark recognizes its true nature and begins its journey home. This awakening is the source of all genuine joy.

David's confession that prosperity bred complacency (30:6) illustrates the Gnostic warning against *agnoia* (ignorance) — specifically, the ignorance that comes from material comfort. When the soul is well-fed and comfortable in the body, it forgets its divine origin. The *Hymn of the Pearl* describes a prince who falls asleep in Egypt and forgets his royal identity; only when a messenger awakens him does he remember his mission. Psalm 30's reversal of fortune mirrors this awakening.
