## Silence Before the Unknown God

Psalm 62's opening — "For God alone my soul waits in silence" (v. 1) — resonates profoundly with the apophatic (negative) theology that characterizes Gnostic descriptions of the ultimate deity. The *Apocryphon of John* describes the Father as "ineffable," "immeasurable light, pure, holy, immaculate," and beyond all categories. Silence is the only appropriate response to this God because human language, forged within the Demiurge's system, is inherently inadequate to express transcendent reality. The psalm's silence is not empty but pregnant with the *gnosis* that transcends words.

The psalm's dismissal of human power and wealth — "Those of low estate are but a breath; those of high estate are a delusion" (v. 9) — is read as a comprehensive deconstruction of all value systems within the material cosmos. Whether rich or poor, powerful or weak, all positions within the Demiurge's hierarchy are equally illusory. The *Gospel of Thomas* (Saying 56) teaches, "Whoever has come to understand the world has found only a corpse." The psalm's radical relativization of worldly distinctions aligns with this Gnostic insight that the material order, in its entirety, is without ultimate significance.

The concluding revelation — "power belongs to God, and... steadfast love" (vv. 11-12) — is heard in Gnostic reading as a statement about the true God beyond the Demiurge. The Demiurge claims power but lacks genuine love; the true Father possesses both in infinite measure. The assertion that God "repays each according to his work" is reinterpreted through the Valentinian tripartition: the hylic returns to matter, the psychic to the Demiurge's intermediate realm, and the pneumatic to the Pleroma — each receiving the destiny appropriate to their nature and degree of *gnosis*.
