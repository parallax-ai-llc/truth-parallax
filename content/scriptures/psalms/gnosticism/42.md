Psalm 42's image of the soul thirsting for God — "As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you" (42:1) — expresses the fundamental Gnostic experience: the divine spark's longing for the Pleroma from which it has been separated. This thirst cannot be satisfied by anything within the material cosmos. No earthly pleasure, no intellectual achievement, no social belonging can quench the soul's deepest desire, which is for reunion with its divine source.

The psalmist's exile from the temple — "My tears have been my food day and night" (42:3) — describes the condition that Gnostic theology calls the "homesickness of the soul." The *Hymn of the Pearl* narrates this same experience: the divine prince, sent into Egypt (the material world) to retrieve a pearl (the divine spark), forgets his identity and weeps without knowing why. The tears are the soul's unconscious mourning for a home it cannot remember but cannot forget.

"Deep calls to deep" (42:7) is one of the most evocative verses for Gnostic mysticism. The "deep" within the human — the pneumatic spark — calls out to the "deep" of the divine Pleroma. This mutual calling is the mechanism of *gnosis* itself: the spark recognizes its origin not through external instruction but through an internal resonance, a deep answering to deep that transcends rational thought and penetrates to the core of being.
