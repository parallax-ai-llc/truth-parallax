Psalm 34's invitation to "taste and see that the LORD is good" (34:8) is a key Gnostic text because it calls for experiential knowledge rather than mere belief. *Gnosis* is not intellectual assent to propositions but direct, participatory knowing — "tasting" the divine reality. The *Gospel of Philip* insists that truth cannot be communicated through words alone but must be received through sacramental experience and direct spiritual illumination.

The psalm's teaching that "the LORD is close to the brokenhearted" (34:18) is interpreted in Gnostic thought as describing the divine spark's special luminosity in moments of spiritual crisis. When the false constructions of the ego (*psyche*) are shattered, the pneumatic kernel is more fully exposed and accessible. The breaking of the heart is the breaking of the shell of ignorance — painful but necessary for the light within to shine forth.

David's instruction on the "fear of the LORD" (34:11) is reinterpreted in Gnostic terms as awe before the transcendent mystery of the true God — not the servile fear demanded by the Demiurge, who rules through intimidation and law. The *Tripartite Tractate* distinguishes between the fear that enslaves (belonging to the psychic realm) and the reverence that liberates (the pneumatic response to the majesty of the Invisible Spirit). This psalm's wisdom instruction, properly understood, teaches the latter.
