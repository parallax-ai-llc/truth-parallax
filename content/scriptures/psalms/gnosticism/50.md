Psalm 50 presents a divine theophany — "Our God comes and will not be silent" (50:3) — that Gnostic theology reads with characteristic ambivalence. The God who demands sacrifices, who owns "every animal of the forest" (50:10), and who threatens to "tear to pieces" those who disobey (50:22) displays characteristics that Gnostic thought associates with the Demiurge: possessiveness, wrath, and the demand for cultic worship. The true God of the Pleroma does not need, threaten, or demand.

However, the psalm also contains elements that transcend the Demiurgic framework. The critique of empty ritual — "I have no need of a bull from your stall" (50:9) — echoes the Gnostic rejection of material sacrifice. And the call to "sacrifice thank offerings" and "fulfill your vows" (50:14) can be read as pointing beyond animal sacrifice to the spiritual offering that the Gnostic makes: the offering of *gnosis* itself, the acknowledgment of the true God through direct knowledge rather than ritual performance.

The psalm's closing promise — "to the blameless I will show my salvation" (50:23) — is interpreted as the promise of revelation to those who have purified themselves of material attachments and Demiurgic deceptions. The "blameless" in Gnostic terms are not the morally perfect but the spiritually awake — those who have ceased to worship the Demiurge and have turned their attention to the true God. To these, the fullness of salvation (*soteria*) will be revealed: the return to the Pleroma and the restoration of all things.
