## The True King Beyond the Material Throne

Psalm 72's portrait of an ideal king raises the question central to Gnostic political theology: can any earthly ruler embody true justice? The Gnostic answer is emphatically no. All earthly governance operates within the Demiurge's system, subject to the limitations and corruptions of material power. The king described in this psalm — whose reign extends "from sea to sea" (v. 8) and who "delivers the needy" (v. 12) — cannot be a material ruler but must be the spiritual Christ, whose kingdom is the Pleroma and whose justice transcends the compromised order of the material world.

The vision of kings bringing tribute (v. 10) is read as the submission of the archontic rulers to the Gnostic Christ. The *Valentinian Exposition* (NHC XI,2) describes how the aeons of the Pleroma honor the Savior who accomplished the redemption, bringing their gifts of praise and knowledge. The earthly kings of Tarshish and Sheba are types of the cosmic powers who will ultimately bow before the authority of the true God — not the Demiurge's local authority but the Father's universal sovereignty.

The psalm's declaration that the king values the lives of the needy as "precious" in his sight (v. 14) is given radical Gnostic meaning: every human being, however humble or degraded in the Demiurge's social order, potentially contains a divine spark of infinite worth. The spiritual king's mission is to perceive and rescue these sparks, regardless of their material circumstances. The concluding doxology — "may the whole earth be filled with his glory" (v. 19) — is the pneumatic hope for the consummation: when all the divine light has been gathered from matter, the Pleroma's glory will fill everything, and the distinction between the heavenly and earthly will be abolished.
