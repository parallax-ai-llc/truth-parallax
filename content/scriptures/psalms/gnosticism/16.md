Psalm 16's expression of satisfaction in God as one's "portion" and "inheritance" (16:5-6) is read by Gnostic interpreters as the pneumatic's recognition that the material world offers nothing of genuine value — only the Pleroma is a worthy inheritance. The declaration "I have no good apart from you" (16:2) is the awakened soul's confession that all material goods are shadows and illusions; true "good" exists only in the fullness of the divine realm. The "pleasant places" and "beautiful inheritance" (16:6) describe the Pleromatic homeland from which the soul has been exiled and to which it longs to return.

The promise "you will not abandon my soul to Sheol, or let your holy one see corruption" (16:10) is read in Gnostic Christology as describing the descent and ascent of the divine Redeemer. In many Gnostic systems, the Christ-Aeon descends into the lower world (Sheol/Hades) to rescue trapped sparks but does not Himself suffer corruption — the divine nature cannot be degraded by contact with matter. The *Treatise on the Resurrection* (NHC I,4) teaches that the spiritual body is real and incorruptible, while the material body is merely a garment to be discarded.

The psalm's climax — "in your presence there is fullness of joy; at your right hand are pleasures forevermore" (16:11) — describes the Pleromatic experience in terms remarkably close to Valentinian theology. The word "fullness" (*pleroma* in Greek) is the technical term for the divine realm itself. The "joy" and "pleasures" are not sensory gratifications but the bliss of reunification with the Source — the divine spark returning to the divine fire from which it emanated. This is the *telos* (goal) of all Gnostic striving: not moral improvement, not obedience to law, not even mystical vision, but complete restoration to the Fullness of the true God.
