Psalm 27's opening declaration — "The Lord is my light" (27:1) — speaks directly to the Gnostic understanding of the divine as pure, uncreated light. In Gnostic cosmology, the true God is the Invisible Spirit or Monad, whose nature is light itself. The material world, created by the ignorant Demiurge, is a realm of darkness; yet within each pneumatic individual burns a spark of the original divine light. The psalmist's confession identifies the true God — not the world-ruler — as the source of illumination.

The desire "to gaze upon the beauty of the LORD" (27:4) describes the Gnostic aspiration for direct vision (*theoria*) of the divine Pleroma — the fullness of the Godhead that exists beyond the material cosmos. The *Gospel of Philip* teaches that those who enter the bridal chamber see the divine light directly, and this psalm's longing to dwell in God's house and behold divine beauty expresses the same mystical aspiration.

The psalm's alternation between confidence and fear reflects the Gnostic understanding of the soul's journey through the material world. The divine spark within provides moments of luminous certainty (27:1-6), but the hostile environment of the Demiurge's cosmos continually threatens to extinguish that light (27:7-12). The closing command to "wait for the LORD" (27:14) counsels the Gnostic to persist in seeking *gnosis* even when the darkness of the material world seems overwhelming.
