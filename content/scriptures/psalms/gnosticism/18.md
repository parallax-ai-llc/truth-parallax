Psalm 18's grand theophany — God descending with earthquake, smoke, and storm to rescue David — is read by Gnostic interpreters as a mythological account of the Pleroma's intervention in the material cosmos. The cosmic disturbances (18:7-15) describe the disruption of the archontic order when the divine power breaks through: the earth quakes because the foundations of the Demiurge's world are shaken; darkness descends because the archons' false light is eclipsed by the true light from above; the waters part because the barriers between the realms are breached.

The psalmist's description of being drawn "out of many waters" (18:16) parallels the Gnostic image of the soul rescued from the waters of chaos — the primordial matter (*hyle*) in which the light-sparks have been submerged. The *Apocryphon of John* describes creation as a process of trapping light in increasingly dense layers of matter, and salvation as the extraction of that light from the material depths. The "strong enemy" (18:17) from whom the psalmist is delivered is the Demiurge and his archons, the cosmic jailers who resist every attempt to free their prisoners.

The cascade of divine titles (18:2) — rock, fortress, deliverer, shield — is read by Gnostic interpreters as a partial catalog of the Aeons of the Pleroma, each representing a different aspect of the divine fullness that comes to the pneumatic's aid. The Valentinian system enumerated thirty Aeons arranged in pairs (*syzygies*), each embodying a specific divine quality. The psalm's overwhelming abundance of metaphors for God thus becomes, in Gnostic reading, a glimpse of the Pleromatic richness that awaits the liberated soul — a world of infinite divine qualities, each more wondrous than the last, stretching into incomprehensible depths of the Godhead.
