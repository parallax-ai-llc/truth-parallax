Psalm 46's declaration "God is our refuge and strength" (46:1) speaks to the Gnostic's fundamental security: no matter how hostile the material cosmos, the pneumatic soul is ultimately protected by the transcendent God who placed the divine spark within it. The archons can persecute the body and torment the psyche, but they cannot extinguish the pneumatic light. This is the unshakeable refuge the psalm proclaims.

The vision of cosmic destruction — "though the earth give way and the mountains fall into the heart of the sea" (46:2) — is read eschatologically as the dissolution of the Demiurge's cosmos at the end of the age. In Gnostic eschatology, the material world will eventually be consumed when all pneumatic sparks have been extracted and returned to the Pleroma. The cosmic upheaval described in this psalm is not catastrophe but liberation — the end of the prison, not the end of the prisoners.

"Be still, and know that I am God" (46:10) is the quintessential Gnostic imperative. *Gnosis* comes not through external activity but through the stilling of the mind and the turning of attention inward, where the divine spark burns silently. The *Allogenes* (a Nag Hammadi text) describes a practice of "withdrawal" and "stillness" through which the mystic transcends ordinary consciousness and encounters the divine. This psalm's command to be still is a command to enter the contemplative state in which *gnosis* becomes possible.
