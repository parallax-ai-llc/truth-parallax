## The True King Beyond the Demiurge

Psalm 110's imagery of a priest-king seated at God's right hand was read by Gnostic interpreters as referring not to the Demiurge's anointed but to a transcendent figure from the Pleroma — a heavenly being who embodies true divine authority as opposed to the counterfeit authority of the material cosmos.

## Gnostic Reading

### "The Lord says to my Lord" (110:1)

Valentinian interpreters read the two "Lords" as referring to different levels of divinity. The first Lord is the true, transcendent God (the Monad or Bythos); the second is the spiritual Christ — an aeon of the Pleroma who descends to liberate the divine sparks. This is not the earthly Jesus of orthodox Christianity but the heavenly Christos of Gnostic mythology.

### The Melchizedek Figure (110:4)

The Nag Hammadi library includes a text titled *Melchizedek* (NHC IX,1) that presents Melchizedek as a heavenly priest-figure who reveals secret knowledge. The eternal priesthood of Psalm 110 is thus detached from Jewish or Christian institutional religion and relocated to the purely spiritual realm of the Pleroma.

### Conquest of the Archons (110:5-7)

The psalm's martial imagery — striking kings, shattering heads, executing judgment — is read as the spiritual Christ's liberation of the divine sparks from archontic bondage. The "enemies" are the Demiurge and his archons, and their subjugation is the cosmic drama of redemption through gnosis.

## Theological Significance

- **Hermeneutical inversion**: The psalm's meaning is reversed through the Gnostic lens
- **Pneumatic knowledge**: True understanding requires spiritual illumination
- **Archontic framework**: Material existence as imprisonment requiring liberation
- **Transcendent hope**: The true God beyond the Demiurge offers salvation through gnosis
