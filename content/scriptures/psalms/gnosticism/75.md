## The Cup of Gnosis and the Cup of Ignorance

Psalm 75's divine oracle — "At the set time that I appoint I will judge with equity" (v. 2) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as the true God's announcement of the archontic system's end. The "set time" is the predetermined moment when the last divine spark will be gathered from matter and the Pleroma will be restored to its original fullness. The Valentinian system teaches that this consummation is certain and its timing fixed in the Father's will, not subject to the archons' interference or humanity's ignorance.

The warning against pride — "Do not lift up your horn on high" (v. 5) — is directed, in Gnostic reading, at the archontic powers whose fundamental sin is precisely this self-exaltation. The Demiurge's declaration "I am God and there is no other" is the ultimate lifting of the horn — the prideful claim that denies the existence of the transcendent Father. The psalm's declaration that "it is God who executes judgment, putting down one and lifting up another" (v. 7) is heard as the true Father's response: the archons will be put down, and the pneumatics will be lifted up to their rightful place in the Pleroma.

The cup of foaming wine (v. 8), drained to the dregs by the wicked, represents the cup of ignorance that the Demiurge administers to humanity. The *Gospel of Truth* describes how "Error" brews an intoxicating drink that keeps humanity unconscious of its divine origin. Those who drink this cup to the dregs are the hylics who are fully absorbed by the material world and never awaken. By contrast, the *Gospel of Philip* speaks of the cup of the Eucharist as containing the wine of *gnosis* — the true cup that awakens rather than intoxicates. The psalm's two cups — judgment for the wicked, salvation for the righteous — thus represent the fundamental Gnostic division between the cup of ignorance and the cup of knowledge.
