Psalm 25's prayer for guidance and forgiveness is reread by Gnostic interpreters through the lens of *anamnesis* (remembrance) — the recovery of the soul's lost knowledge of its divine origin. The acrostic structure, moving through the entire alphabet, suggests a comprehensive act of recollection: the soul must recover every fragment of its scattered knowledge to achieve complete *gnosis*. The opening — "To you, O Lord, I lift up my soul" (25:1) — is the upward orientation of the soul toward the Pleroma, the fundamental gesture of Gnostic spirituality: turning away from the material world and toward the transcendent Source.

The plea "Make me to know your ways, O Lord; teach me your paths" (25:4) is the pneumatic's request for the esoteric teaching that will map the path of ascent through the archontic spheres. The "ways" and "paths" are the specific routes, passwords, and techniques necessary for the soul's journey — knowledge that Gnostic teachers claimed to possess and transmit to their initiates. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 3) records Jesus saying: "When you come to know yourselves, then you will become known, and you will realize that it is you who are the sons of the living Father."

The prayer for forgiveness — "Remember not the sins of my youth" (25:7) — is reinterpreted as a plea to release the soul from the accumulated weight of material entanglement. The "sins" are not moral transgressions against the Demiurge's law but the soul's progressive involvement in the material world — its attachments, identifications, and forgetfulness. Each incarnation deepens the soul's amnesia, and liberation requires that these layers of forgetfulness be stripped away. The verse "The friendship (*sod*) of the Lord is for those who fear him" (25:14) is read as the innermost secret of *gnosis* — the esoteric knowledge shared only with the spiritually prepared, the final revelation that consummates the soul's journey from ignorance to knowledge, from the material prison to the Pleroma's embrace.
