Psalm 14's declaration "The fool says in his heart, 'There is no God'" (14:1) receives a stunning inversion in Gnostic reading: the "fool" is the Demiurge himself, who declares that there is no God above him. The *Apocryphon of John* records Yaldabaoth's boast: "I am God and there is no other God beside me," and immediately adds that this declaration proves his ignorance, for the very making of such a claim reveals that he suspects — but refuses to acknowledge — a higher power. The psalm thus becomes a critique of the Demiurge's self-deception rather than a condemnation of human atheism.

The universal corruption described — "They have all turned aside; together they have become corrupt" (14:3) — is read as a description of the entire material creation, which is inherently flawed because its creator is himself flawed. The Demiurge, produced through Sophia's error (or passion, depending on the Gnostic system), created a world that reflects his own ignorance and limitation. The statement "there is none who does good, not even one" is thus not a moral indictment of individual sinners but a cosmic assessment: nothing in the Demiurge's creation achieves genuine goodness, because genuine goodness belongs only to the Pleroma.

The messianic hope — "Oh, that salvation would come out of Zion!" (14:7) — is reinterpreted as the longing for the Pleromatic intervention that will liberate the trapped sparks. "Zion" is not the earthly Jerusalem but the heavenly realm from which the savior descends. In Valentinian Christology, the Christ-Aeon descends from the Pleroma to bring *gnosis* to the sleeping pneumatics, and this descent is the "salvation out of Zion" that the psalm anticipates. The "restoration of fortunes" is the gathering of all light-sparks back to their source — the final dissolution of the material order and the healing of Sophia's original breach.
