## The Archontic Tyrant and the Living Tree

In Gnostic reading, the "mighty man" who "boasts of evil" (v. 1) is identified with the Demiurge — Yaldabaoth — who boasts of his power over the material cosmos. The *Apocryphon of John* (NHC II,1) records the Demiurge's arrogant declaration: "I am God and there is no other God beside me," a boast born of ignorance of the true Father. This psalm's indictment of the boastful tyrant becomes a Gnostic critique of the creator-god's pretension to absolute sovereignty — he rules through deception and violence, trusting in "the abundance of his riches" (v. 7), meaning the material creation he mistakes for ultimate reality.

The contrast between the wicked who are "uprooted from the land of the living" (v. 5) and the righteous who are "like a green olive tree in the house of God" (v. 8) is read through the Gnostic myth of the Tree of Knowledge. In several Nag Hammadi texts, the Tree of Knowledge is valorized rather than condemned — it is the instrument through which the serpent (identified with Christ or Sophia in some Gnostic systems) awakened Adam and Eve to their true spiritual nature. The "olive tree in the house of God" thus represents the pneumatic soul rooted in true knowledge, flourishing not in the Demiurge's temple but in the Pleroma of the true God.

The psalm's concluding trust — "I will wait for your name, for it is good, in the presence of the godly" (v. 9) — is read as the pneumatic community's patient expectation of final liberation. The "godly" (*chasidim*) are identified with the elect pneumatics who possess the saving *gnosis*. Their waiting is not passive but involves the active cultivation of self-knowledge, which is simultaneously knowledge of the divine origin from which they have been separated by the Demiurge's cosmic deception.
