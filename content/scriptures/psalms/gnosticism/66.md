## Testing as Archontic Trial and Pneumatic Refinement

Psalm 66's recollection of the Red Sea crossing — "He turned the sea into dry land" (v. 6) — is given distinctive meaning in Gnostic reading. The passage through water represents the soul's transit through the material element, emerging on the other side transformed. Several Gnostic texts reinterpret the Exodus as a metaphor for the pneumatic soul's liberation from the archontic Pharaoh (the Demiurge) who holds the divine sparks in bondage. The "sea" is the chaotic matter through which the soul must pass to reach the promised land of *gnosis*.

The psalm's acknowledgment of divine testing — "You have tried us as silver is tried" (v. 10) — is interpreted through the Gnostic understanding of spiritual refinement. The trials of material existence serve, paradoxically, to awaken the divine spark. The *Exegesis on the Soul* (NHC II,6) describes the soul's descent into matter as a necessary journey through which it comes to know itself and its origin. The "crushing burden" on the back (v. 11) is the weight of material existence, the body itself, which the soul must bear until it achieves the *gnosis* that liberates it.

The personal testimony — "I will tell what he has done for my soul" (v. 16) — is the Gnostic convert's proclamation of awakening. The most important event in the pneumatic's life is not any external deliverance but the internal illumination — the moment of *gnosis* when the soul recognizes its divine origin. The psalm's warning that God does not listen to those who "cherish iniquity" (v. 18) is reread: the "iniquity" cherished in the heart is attachment to matter, the love of the material world that blocks the soul's receptivity to the Savior's call.
