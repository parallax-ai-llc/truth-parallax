Psalm 11's declaration "The Lord is in his holy temple; the Lord's throne is in heaven" (11:4) is read by Gnostic interpreters as a crucial distinction between the earthly temple (the Demiurge's domain) and the true temple of the Pleroma. The Gnostic understanding of sacred space is radically different from orthodox Judaism or Christianity: the Jerusalem temple was built for the Demiurge, and worship conducted there is misdirected. The true "holy temple" is above the visible heavens, beyond the archontic spheres, in the realm of the transcendent God whom no material temple can contain.

The counsel to "flee like a bird to your mountain" (11:1) is surprisingly embraced by some Gnostic interpreters rather than rejected. Flight from the material world — spiritual ascent through the archontic spheres to the Pleroma — is precisely the pneumatic's goal. The "mountain" is the divine realm itself, and the soul's flight is the journey of return that *gnosis* enables. The bird imagery connects to numerous Gnostic texts that portray the soul as a bird trapped in a cage (the body), longing for release. The *Gospel of Thomas* (saying 86) records Jesus saying: "Foxes have their holes and birds have their nests, but the Son of Man has no place to lay his head" — homelessness in this world is the condition of the awakened soul.

The promise that "the upright shall behold his face" (11:7) is the vision of the true God — not the Demiurge's face (which would be terrifying but ultimately insignificant) but the face of the ineffable Source of all. In Valentinian theology, this vision is the *gnosis* itself — not a future eschatological event but a present spiritual reality available to the pneumatic through contemplation, ritual, and the reception of secret teaching. To "behold God's face" is to know oneself as divine, to see one's own origin reflected in the ultimate light.
