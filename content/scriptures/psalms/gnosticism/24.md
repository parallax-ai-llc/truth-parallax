Psalm 24's antiphonal dialogue about the opening of gates is read by Gnostic interpreters as a dramatic liturgical text describing the soul's ascent through the archontic barriers. The "gates" (24:7) are the boundaries between the cosmic spheres — each guarded by an archon who demands identification before allowing passage. The repeated question "Who is this King of glory?" (24:8, 10) is the archons' challenge to the ascending soul: Who are you? By what authority do you pass? The correct answer — declaring the divine names and demonstrating knowledge of the heavenly geography — compels the gates to open.

The entrance requirements (24:3-4) — "clean hands and a pure heart" — are reinterpreted as the spiritual conditions for ascent rather than ethical qualifications for temple worship. "Clean hands" means freedom from attachment to material actions; "pure heart" means liberation from archontic conditioning. The soul that still clings to the values, desires, and beliefs of the material world cannot ascend — it is weighed down by its attachments and dragged back into incarnation. Only the soul purified by *gnosis* — stripped of every archontic residue — can pass through the gates and enter the Pleroma.

The cosmic declaration "The earth is the Lord's and the fullness thereof" (24:1) is radically inverted in Gnostic reading. If the "Lord" here is the Demiurge, then yes — the earth and everything in it belongs to him, for he made it. But the pneumatic's response is: we do not belong to the earth or to its lord. Our true citizenship is in the Pleroma, and our passage through his territory is temporary. The psalm's triumphant ending — "The Lord of hosts, he is the King of glory" — is reread as a declaration about the true God, whose glory infinitely surpasses the Demiurge's petty sovereignty over one small, flawed cosmos.
