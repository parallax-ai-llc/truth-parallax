Psalm 19's two-part structure — the heavens declaring glory (19:1-6) and the law reviving the soul (19:7-11) — is read by Gnostic interpreters as a distinction between two kinds of knowledge, corresponding to two gods. The heavens that "declare the glory of God" (19:1) are the archontic heavens — the planetary spheres governed by the archons. Their "glory" is the Demiurge's glory, impressive but ultimately counterfeit. The cosmic order is beautiful but deceptive: it appears to reveal the true God but actually conceals Him behind the veil of the visible sky.

The transition to the law (19:7-9) is reread as a shift from cosmic deception to hidden truth. But which law? The Gnostic reader distinguishes between the Torah of the Demiurge (which enslaves through commandments and guilt) and the "perfect law" of the true God (which liberates through knowledge). The description of a law that is "perfect, reviving the soul" (19:7) applies only to the latter — the secret teaching transmitted by Christ to the pneumatic elect, the wisdom that *gnosis* reveals beneath the surface of the scriptural text. The *Testimony of Truth* (NHC IX,3) teaches that the law of the Demiurge brings death, while the word of the true God brings life.

The personal prayer concluding the psalm — "Who can discern his errors? Declare me innocent from hidden faults" (19:12) — takes on special significance in Gnostic reading. The "hidden faults" are not moral failings but remnants of archontic conditioning — the unconscious beliefs and habits that keep the pneumatic attached to the material world even after initial awakening. Full *gnosis* requires the purification of these deep, hidden attachments, a process that some Gnostic schools formalized through ritual, meditation, and progressive initiation into higher levels of esoteric teaching.
