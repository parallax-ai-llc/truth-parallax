Psalm 8's celebration of human dignity within creation is radically reinterpreted in Gnostic thought. The question "What is man that you are mindful of him?" (8:4) receives a startling answer: humanity is special because it contains a divine element that the rest of creation lacks. The Gnostic myth, as told in the *Apocryphon of John*, relates that the archons created the human body in imitation of the divine Anthropos (Primal Human) whose image they glimpsed reflected on the waters of chaos. They could craft the outer form but could not make it live — until the Demiurge, tricked by Sophia, breathed the divine spirit into the clay figure.

The declaration that humanity is made "a little lower than *elohim*" (8:5) is thus reinterpreted: humanity is not lower than the archons (*elohim*) in essence but only in present condition. The divine spark within is of the same substance as the Pleroma — humanity's true nature is divine, not creaturely. The "glory and honor" with which humanity is crowned (8:5b) refers to the light-garment that the soul wore in its pre-incarnate state and will recover upon its return to the Pleroma. The *Hymn of the Pearl* describes this garment of glory that the soul left behind when it descended into Egypt (matter).

The dominion over creation (8:6-8) is read ambivalently by Gnostic interpreters. In one sense, human mastery over nature confirms humanity's superior status — the archons intended humanity to be a servant, but the divine spark within gives it a dignity the archons never planned. In another sense, dominion over the material world is itself a trap: attachment to the things of this world — animals, fields, the natural order — can deepen the soul's entanglement in matter. The truly awakened pneumatic exercises dominion by detachment, recognizing all material things as shadows of the Pleromatic reality they dimly reflect.
