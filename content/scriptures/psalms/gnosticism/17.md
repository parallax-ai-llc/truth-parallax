Psalm 17's prayer of the righteous sufferer is read by Gnostic interpreters as the cry of the pneumatic who has awakened but not yet been fully liberated. The assertion of innocence (17:3-5) is understood not as moral self-righteousness but as the pneumatic's recognition that the "crimes" the Demiurge charges against the awakened soul — disobedience to archontic law, refusal to worship the false god, sharing secret knowledge — are in truth virtues. The pneumatic is "innocent" because *gnosis* has revealed the true moral order, which is the inverse of the Demiurge's system.

The petition "Keep me as the apple of your eye; hide me in the shadow of your wings" (17:8) is addressed to the true God or to Sophia, the divine Wisdom who watches over the scattered sparks with maternal care. The "wings" recall the Gnostic image of the divine Mother hovering over her lost children, seeking to gather them back to the Pleroma. The *Apocryphon of John* describes Barbelo (the divine female principle) as constantly attending to the welfare of the trapped light, providing guidance and protection through the agents she sends into the material world.

The final verse — "when I awake, I shall be satisfied with your likeness" (17:15) — is the supreme Gnostic statement in this psalm. The "awakening" is the ultimate *gnosis*, and the "satisfaction with God's likeness" is the recovery of the divine image that the soul has carried all along but forgotten. In Gnostic anthropology, the human being *is* the likeness of God — not in a diminished, creaturely sense but in a real, substantial sense. To awaken is to discover that one's deepest self is identical with the divine, that the spark within is made of the same light as the Pleroma. This discovery is not intellectual but transformative — it changes not what one believes but what one *is*.
