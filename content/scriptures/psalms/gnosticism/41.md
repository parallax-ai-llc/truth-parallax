Psalm 41's beatitude concerning care for the weak — "Blessed are those who have regard for the weak" (41:1) — is interpreted in Gnostic thought as referring to compassion for those whose divine spark is especially buried under the weight of material existence. The "weak" are not merely the physically or economically disadvantaged but the spiritually impoverished — those whose *gnosis* is most deeply suppressed by the Demiurge's systems of control.

The betrayal by "my close friend, one who shared my bread" (41:9) takes on profound significance in Gnostic readings of the Christ narrative. Some Gnostic texts, such as the *Gospel of Judas*, reinterpret Judas's betrayal as a necessary act within the divine plan — Judas alone understood that Jesus needed to shed the material body to return to the Pleroma. The "betrayal" becomes, paradoxically, the moment of liberation from material imprisonment.

The doxology — "Praise be to the LORD, the God of Israel, from everlasting to everlasting" (41:13) — is read by the Gnostic not as praise of the God of Israel (whom many Gnostic traditions identify with the Demiurge) but as pointing beyond its literal referent to the true God whose existence is "from everlasting to everlasting" — eternal, unbounded by the Demiurge's temporal creation. The praise transcends its own words, reaching toward the Ineffable One whom no psalm can fully name.
