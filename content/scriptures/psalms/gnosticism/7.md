Psalm 7's appeal for divine justice is read by Gnostic interpreters as a plea to the true God to overrule the unjust judgment of the Demiurge. The archontic legal system — encompassing the Torah's commandments, the rewards and punishments of the material cosmos, and the cycle of incarnation — is understood as a mechanism of control rather than genuine justice. When the psalmist cries "O Lord my God, in you I take refuge" (7:1), the Gnostic hears an appeal over the head of the lower god to the transcendent source of authentic justice.

The oath of innocence (7:3-5) takes on a peculiar meaning in Gnostic exegesis. The pneumatic's "innocence" consists not in moral perfection by the Demiurge's standards but in knowledge — the pneumatic is innocent because they have awakened to the truth and refused to participate in the archontic deception. The Valentinian understanding of sin is fundamentally different from orthodox Christianity: sin is not primarily moral transgression but ignorance of one's divine origin. The "guilt" the psalmist denies is the false guilt imposed by the Demiurge's law.

The principle of self-recoiling evil (7:14-16) — the wicked falling into their own pit — is read as a description of the archons' ultimate fate. The *Hypostasis of the Archons* (NHC II,4) describes how the archontic powers will eventually be overthrown and dissolved when the light-sparks have all been gathered back to the Pleroma. The instruments of oppression will destroy their makers. The Demiurge's elaborate system of control — death, ignorance, forgetfulness — will collapse under the weight of its own contradictions when *gnosis* spreads and the trapped light is reclaimed.
