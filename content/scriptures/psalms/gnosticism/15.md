Psalm 15's question "who shall dwell on your holy hill?" (15:1) is reframed in Gnostic reading as: who shall ascend to the Pleroma? The ethical qualifications listed in the psalm are reinterpreted through the Gnostic distinction between psychic morality (the Demiurge's system of rules) and pneumatic ethics (the conduct that flows naturally from *gnosis*). The person who "walks blamelessly" (15:2) is not the law-abiding citizen of the Demiurge's regime but the pneumatic who has transcended the law entirely and acts from inner knowledge of the divine.

Valentinian ethics held that the pneumatic's behavior naturally surpasses the Demiurge's moral requirements without being bound by them. The *Gospel of Philip* teaches that the free person does not sin — not because they follow rules but because *gnosis* transforms the will so that harmful behavior becomes impossible. "Speaking truth in his heart" (15:2) means possessing the inner knowledge that dispels all illusion; "not slandering" (15:3) means refusing to propagate the Demiurge's false narrative about reality. Each ethical requirement is reread as a dimension of spiritual knowledge rather than moral compliance.

The promise "he who does these things shall never be moved" (15:5b) describes the stability of the pneumatic who has achieved *gnosis* — a stability that depends not on external circumstances but on the unshakable knowledge of one's divine identity. The material world and its archontic rulers can destroy the body, persecute the social self, and strip away every external support, but they cannot touch the divine spark within. This immovability is the Gnostic equivalent of salvation — not a future reward but a present reality experienced by the awakened soul who has passed beyond the reach of the Demiurge's power.
