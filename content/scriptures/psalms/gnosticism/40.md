Psalm 40's declaration that God desires obedience over sacrifice — "Sacrifice and offering you did not desire" (40:6) — is central to the Gnostic critique of the Demiurge's religious system. The Demiurge, identified with the God of the Old Testament's sacrificial laws, demands blood offerings and ritual compliance. The true God of the Pleroma desires not sacrifice but *gnosis* — the soul's awakening to its divine nature.

The psalmist's testimony — "He put a new song in my mouth" (40:3) — describes the Gnostic experience of revelation. The "new song" is the *gnosis* that transforms the soul's understanding of itself and the cosmos. The *Odes of Solomon*, a collection of Gnostic hymns, frequently describe the illuminated soul singing a "new song" — a song that could not be sung before awakening because the soul did not yet know its true identity or its true God.

The declaration "Here I am, I have come — it is written about me in the scroll" (40:7) is read in Gnostic Christology as the voice of the divine Revealer descending from the Pleroma into the material world. The Christos comes not to die as a sacrifice but to bring the knowledge that liberates. The "scroll" contains the divine plan for the restoration of all pneumatic sparks to the Pleroma — a plan written before the foundation of the material cosmos.
