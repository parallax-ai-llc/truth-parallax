Academic study of Abu Dawud's marriage and food traditions provides valuable evidence for the social history of family life and material culture in early Islam. The marriage hadiths reveal the legal structure of marital relations — contract forms, dower negotiations, grounds for dissolution — and allow scholars to trace how prophetic precedent shaped the development of Islamic family law across different regions and periods.

The food traditions are examined as sources for the dietary practices and culinary culture of the early Muslim community. Scholars analyze which foods were available, which were prohibited and why, and how Islamic dietary regulations related to pre-Islamic Arabian food customs and to Jewish and Christian dietary laws. The etiquette traditions — on table manners, hospitality, and communal eating — provide insights into the social norms and values of early Islamic society, revealing how prophetic practice functioned as a mechanism for cultural formation and community building.
