Academic scholars examine Abu Dawud's purification and prayer traditions as sources for understanding the standardization of Islamic ritual practice. The variation in ablution and prayer practices that Abu Dawud preserves — recording multiple methods and noting which scholars favored which approach — provides evidence that ritual practice was not yet fully uniform in the ninth century, and that hadith compilation was one of the mechanisms through which standardization gradually occurred.

The prayer traditions are studied for their relationship to the development of Islamic liturgical law. Scholars analyze the chains of transmission to map the geographical and intellectual networks through which specific prayer practices circulated, and they examine Abu Dawud's editorial choices — which variant practices he includes, how he frames disputed points — as evidence of his own jurisprudential commitments. The material also provides data for comparative liturgical studies, illuminating how Islamic prayer practice relates to earlier Jewish and Christian patterns of daily worship.
