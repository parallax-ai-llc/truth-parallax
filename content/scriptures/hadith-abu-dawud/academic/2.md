Academic study of Abu Dawud's zakat and fasting traditions yields important insights into the economic and social history of the early Muslim community. The zakat hadiths reveal the types of wealth and agricultural produce that were prevalent, the administrative mechanisms for charitable collection, and the social categories identified as deserving recipients. Scholars use these traditions to reconstruct the economic landscape of early Islamic society and the fiscal policies of the prophetic and post-prophetic periods.

The fasting traditions are examined for their contribution to understanding the development of the Islamic liturgical calendar. Academic researchers trace the origins and codification of Ramadan observance, the methods used for determining the lunar month's beginning and end, and the social practices surrounding communal fasting. Comparative analysis with Jewish and Christian fasting traditions illuminates both the distinctive features of the Islamic fast and its possible connections to earlier Near Eastern ascetic practices and liturgical rhythms.
