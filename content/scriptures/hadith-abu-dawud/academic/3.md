Academic scholarship examines Abu Dawud's jihad traditions within the context of early Islamic military history and the development of the laws of warfare. Scholars analyze these hadiths as reflections of the military challenges faced by the early Muslim community, the ethical principles invoked to govern conduct in war, and the processes by which prophetic precedent was codified into a formal legal framework governing armed conflict.

The rites traditions on sacrifice, oaths, and vows are studied for their contribution to understanding ritual practice in the early Islamic world. Academic researchers examine the continuities and discontinuities between Islamic sacrificial practices and the pre-Islamic Arabian rituals they partially replaced, the legal mechanisms by which oaths and vows became enforceable, and the social functions of communal sacrifice during the major Islamic festivals. These traditions also provide material for comparative religious studies, particularly in relation to sacrificial traditions in Judaism and other Near Eastern religions.
