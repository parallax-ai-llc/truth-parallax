Academic scholars examine Abu Dawud's concluding sections on the Sunnah and manners as evidence of how the concept of prophetic authority was articulated and defended in the ninth century. The creedal hadiths in the Sunnah section reveal the theological debates that animated the early Muslim community — questions about divine attributes, human free will, and the relative authority of scripture and prophetic tradition — and show how hadith compilation served as a vehicle for theological argument.

The manners traditions are studied as sources for the social history of adab (etiquette) in early Islamic culture. Scholars analyze these traditions alongside the broader Arabic literary tradition of adab literature, examining how prophetic manners functioned as a model for social behavior, how they were codified and transmitted, and how they interacted with pre-Islamic Arabian customs and the social norms of conquered societies. This material illuminates the processes by which a prophetic model of conduct was transformed into a comprehensive cultural framework governing daily life.
