Abu Dawud's sections on marriage and food address the legal framework governing family life and dietary practice in Islam. The marriage chapters record prophetic traditions on the marriage contract, the rights and obligations of spouses, the conditions for divorce, custody of children, and the *mahr* (bridal gift). Sunni family law draws extensively on these hadiths, with each legal school interpreting them according to its own jurisprudential methodology.

The food traditions establish the Islamic dietary code through prophetic precedent: the types of animals permissible and prohibited for consumption, the etiquette of eating and drinking, and the protocols of hospitality. Abu Dawud records hadiths addressing specific items whose permissibility was disputed, providing the evidence base for Sunni legal rulings on dietary matters. His inclusion of traditions on table manners and the prophetic practice of eating — with the right hand, saying *bismillah*, and expressing gratitude — extends beyond legal obligation to encompass the ethical and devotional dimensions of daily sustenance.
