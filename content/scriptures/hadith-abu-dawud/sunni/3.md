Abu Dawud's sections on jihad and religious rites provide Sunni jurisprudence with prophetic precedents for military ethics and ritual observances. The jihad chapters address the conditions for legitimate armed struggle, the treatment of combatants and non-combatants, the distribution of war spoils, and the ethics of peace treaties. Sunni scholars have drawn upon these traditions to develop the Islamic laws of warfare (*siyar*), emphasizing the constraints the Prophet placed on the conduct of hostilities.

The rites sections cover animal sacrifice (*udhiyah*), oaths and vows, and the ritual slaughter of animals for consumption. Abu Dawud records prophetic traditions governing the proper method of sacrifice, the types of animals acceptable for offering, and the conditions under which oaths become binding. These traditions form the legal basis for Sunni practice during Eid al-Adha and other occasions requiring ritual slaughter. The combination of jihad and rites in this section reflects the broad scope of Abu Dawud's legal concerns — encompassing both extraordinary circumstances of conflict and the regular ritual obligations of Muslim life.
