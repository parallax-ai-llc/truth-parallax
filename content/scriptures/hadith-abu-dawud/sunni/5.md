Abu Dawud concludes his collection with sections on the Sunnah as a source of authority and the prophetic manners (*adab*) that govern social conduct. The Sunnah chapters include creedal traditions — hadiths defining the attributes of God, the nature of predestination, and the authority of prophetic guidance alongside the Quran — providing the textual foundation for Sunni theological positions on these foundational questions.

The manners sections record prophetic guidance on interpersonal conduct: greeting, visiting the sick, attending funerals, maintaining kinship ties, and resolving disputes. Sunni scholars treat these traditions as establishing the normative etiquette of Muslim society, distinguishing between obligatory social duties (such as returning greetings) and recommended practices (such as visiting the sick). Abu Dawud's final sections thus extend the scope of prophetic legislation from ritual worship and commercial law to the full fabric of social life, reinforcing the Sunni conviction that the Prophet's guidance encompasses every dimension of human existence.
