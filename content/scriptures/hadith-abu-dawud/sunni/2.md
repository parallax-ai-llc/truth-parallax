Abu Dawud's sections on zakat and fasting address two of Islam's five pillars with the legal precision characteristic of his collection. The zakat traditions specify the categories of wealth subject to charitable taxation, the minimum thresholds (*nisab*) for each category, the rates of collection, and the eligible recipients. Sunni jurists rely heavily on these hadiths to determine the practical application of charitable obligations in diverse economic circumstances.

The fasting chapters provide comprehensive prophetic guidance on the Ramadan fast: its commencement and conclusion (tied to moon sighting), the conditions that permit breaking the fast, the regulations for travelers and the ill, and the expiation required for deliberate violations. Abu Dawud also records traditions on voluntary fasting — the Prophet's practice of fasting on specific days — which Sunni Muslims observe as recommended acts of devotion. His inclusion of both strong and weaker traditions, with appropriate notation, allows Sunni scholars to distinguish between established obligations and meritorious recommendations.
