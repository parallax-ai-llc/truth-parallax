Salafi scholars approach Abu Dawud's jihad traditions as prophetic legislation establishing the conditions, ethics, and limits of armed struggle. These hadiths are cited to argue that jihad must conform to specific prophetic guidelines — including the protection of non-combatants, the prohibition of treachery, and the just distribution of spoils — and that departures from these norms represent violations of the Sunnah rather than legitimate extensions of it.

The rites traditions on sacrifice, oaths, and vows are treated as binding prophetic prescriptions. Salafi scholars use Abu Dawud's narrations to determine the exact specifications of acceptable sacrificial animals, the proper method of slaughter, and the conditions under which oaths and vows become obligatory. Where cultural practices surrounding sacrifice have diverged from the prophetic model — through local customs, popular traditions, or innovations — Salafi scholars use Abu Dawud's authenticated traditions to call for a return to the original prophetic practice, distinguishing the essential Sunnah from later additions.
