Sufi interpreters engage with Abu Dawud's jihad traditions through the prophetic distinction between the "lesser jihad" (military struggle) and the "greater jihad" (struggle against the ego). While accepting the legal validity of outward jihad under proper conditions, Sufi tradition emphasizes that the most important battle is the inner one — the effort to subdue selfishness, pride, anger, and desire that obstruct the soul's journey to God.

The rites sections on sacrifice are read symbolically as well as literally. The ritual slaughter of an animal on Eid al-Adha commemorates Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son, and Sufi interpreters see in this a model for the spiritual sacrifice required of every seeker: the willingness to surrender one's most cherished attachments for God's sake. Abu Dawud's precise traditions on the conditions and methods of sacrifice are followed outwardly while the Sufi practitioner simultaneously contemplates the inner sacrifice — the offering of the ego on the altar of divine love.
