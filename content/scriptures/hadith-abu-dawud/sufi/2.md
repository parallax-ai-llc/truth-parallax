Sufi engagement with Abu Dawud's zakat and fasting traditions illuminates the spiritual dimensions of generosity and self-denial. Zakat is understood not merely as a financial obligation but as a training of the soul in detachment from material possessions. The prophetic traditions on charitable giving are read as descriptions of the generous heart — the heart that recognizes all wealth as a trust from God and finds joy in releasing it for the benefit of others.

Fasting holds a special place in Sufi practice as the most powerful discipline for subduing the lower self (*nafs*). Abu Dawud's traditions on Ramadan and voluntary fasting are embraced as prophetic prescriptions for spiritual transformation. The hunger and thirst of fasting create an inner emptiness that, in the Sufi understanding, becomes a receptive space for divine grace. Sufi masters teach that the fast must extend beyond food and drink to encompass the fasting of the tongue from hurtful speech, the fasting of the eyes from forbidden sights, and the fasting of the heart from everything other than God.
