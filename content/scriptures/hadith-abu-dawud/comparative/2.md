A comparative analysis of Abu Dawud's zakat and fasting traditions places Islamic charitable obligation and ascetic discipline within the broader context of religious duties across traditions. The zakat system — a mandatory wealth-based contribution for communal welfare — parallels the Jewish practice of *tzedakah* (righteousness through giving), the Christian tithe, the Sikh *dasvandh* (tenth part), and the Buddhist practice of *dana* (generosity). Each tradition develops mechanisms for translating spiritual values into material redistribution.

The fasting traditions recorded by Abu Dawud resonate with ascetic practices across religions. The month-long Ramadan fast can be compared in structure and purpose with Christian Lent, while the discipline of complete abstention from food and drink during daylight hours resembles the Yom Kippur fast in Judaism. Across traditions, fasting serves overlapping purposes: self-discipline, solidarity with the poor, spiritual purification, and commemoration of sacred history. Abu Dawud's legal precision in delineating the rules of fasting reflects the common tendency of religious communities to codify ascetic practices into detailed regulatory frameworks.
