A comparative reading of Abu Dawud's jihad and rites traditions situates Islamic military ethics and sacrificial practice within broader religious frameworks for sanctioned violence and ritual offering. The ethical constraints on warfare documented by Abu Dawud — protection of non-combatants, prohibition of environmental destruction, requirement of just cause — parallel similar frameworks in Christian just war theory, the Hindu dharma of *kshatriya* warriors, and the ethical codes governing warfare in classical Chinese and Japanese traditions.

The sacrificial rites recorded by Abu Dawud connect to the universal religious phenomenon of animal sacrifice, which features prominently in the Hebrew Bible's Temple cult, Vedic Hinduism's *yajna* rituals, and pre-Islamic Arabian religious practice. The Abrahamic narrative underlying the Islamic sacrifice — Ibrahim's willingness to offer his son — is shared with Judaism and Christianity, though each tradition interprets the story differently. Abu Dawud's precise legal regulations for sacrifice reflect the broader tendency of mature religious traditions to transform spontaneous ritual acts into carefully codified legal requirements.
