A comparative reading of Abu Dawud's purification and prayer traditions reveals shared patterns with ritual purity and liturgical prayer across world religions. The elaborate system of ritual cleanliness in Islam — distinguishing between minor and major impurities, prescribing specific purification methods for each — parallels the purity systems of Rabbinic Judaism (*tahorah*), Zoroastrian purity law, Hindu ritual cleanliness, and the baptismal traditions of Christianity. Each system reflects a common intuition that encounter with the sacred requires a prior state of ritual readiness.

The structured daily prayers documented by Abu Dawud invite comparison with the Jewish *Shacharit*, *Mincha*, and *Maariv* services, the Christian Liturgy of the Hours, and the Buddhist practice of regular meditation periods. Within Islam itself, Abu Dawud's preservation of variant prayer practices demonstrates the kind of internal liturgical diversity found in all major religious traditions, from the ritual differences between Ashkenazi and Sephardi Judaism to the liturgical variations among Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant Christianity.
