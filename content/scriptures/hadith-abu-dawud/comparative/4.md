A comparative analysis of Abu Dawud's marriage and food traditions reveals how religious communities invest domestic and dietary life with sacred significance. The Islamic marriage contract documented through Abu Dawud's hadiths can be compared with the Jewish *ketubah*, the Christian sacrament of matrimony, and the Hindu *vivaha* ceremony. Each tradition surrounds the formation of a family with religious ritual and legal structure, reflecting the shared conviction that marriage is not merely a social arrangement but a sacred covenant.

The dietary regulations in Abu Dawud parallel the kosher laws of Judaism, the fasting and dietary disciplines of various Christian traditions, the vegetarian principles of Jain and Buddhist practice, and the Hindu distinctions between *sattvic*, *rajasic*, and *tamasic* foods. Across traditions, what one eats — and how one eats — is invested with religious meaning. Abu Dawud's recording of prophetic table manners finds its closest parallel in the elaborate *berakhot* (blessings) that accompany eating in Jewish practice, where every type of food occasions a specific invocation of the divine.
