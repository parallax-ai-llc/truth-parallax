A comparative reading of Abu Dawud's final sections on the Sunnah and manners addresses the universal religious phenomenon of the exemplary life as normative for the community. The Islamic concept of Sunnah — the Prophet's practice as a binding model — parallels the Christian *imitatio Christi*, the Buddhist aspiration to follow the Noble Eightfold Path exemplified by the Buddha, the Confucian ideal of emulating the sage-kings, and the Jewish concept of walking in God's ways as interpreted through Rabbinic example.

The manners traditions in Abu Dawud illustrate how religious communities develop comprehensive behavioral codes from the example of their founders. The prophetic guidance on greetings, dress, hospitality, and social interaction can be compared with the monastic rules of Christianity (such as the Rule of St. Benedict), the Vinaya regulations of Buddhist monastic life, and the *derech eretz* (proper conduct) traditions of Rabbinic Judaism. Each system transforms the founder's personal habits into communal norms, investing everyday behavior with religious significance and creating a distinctive cultural identity.
