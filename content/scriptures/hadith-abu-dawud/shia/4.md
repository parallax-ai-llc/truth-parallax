Shia engagement with Abu Dawud's marriage traditions centers on points where Sunni and Shia family law diverge. The most prominent divergence concerns *mut'ah* (temporary marriage), which Shia jurisprudence permits based on Quranic and prophetic evidence, while Sunni law prohibits it based on traditions recording its abrogation. Shia scholars examine Abu Dawud's narrations on this topic to assess how the Sunni prohibition was constructed and whether the evidence supports the abrogation claim.

The food traditions are engaged more neutrally, with broad agreement on the basic Islamic dietary framework. Shia scholars note where Abu Dawud's dietary hadiths align with Ja'fari rulings on permissible and prohibited foods, using these convergences to demonstrate the shared prophetic basis of Islamic dietary law. Differences arise in specific areas — such as the permissibility of certain seafood — where Shia jurisprudence applies distinctive criteria. The etiquette traditions on meals and hospitality are largely shared, reflecting common prophetic teachings on generosity and mindful consumption.
