Shia scholars examine Abu Dawud's purification and prayer traditions with attention to divergences between his narrations and Ja'fari jurisprudence. In purification, the Shia practice of wiping (*mash*) over the feet during ablution — rather than washing them — finds partial support in certain narrations that Abu Dawud records, and Shia polemicists highlight these traditions as evidence that their practice reflects an authentic prophetic variant suppressed by later Sunni consensus.

The prayer chapters are scrutinized for traditions that may support Shia liturgical practices, such as combining the noon and afternoon prayers or the evening and night prayers. Shia scholars note where Abu Dawud preserves narrations allowing such combinations, arguing that these traditions attest to a flexibility in prophetic practice that Sunni legal rigidity later curtailed. The chains of narration are examined through Shia criteria, with traditions transmitted through Ahl al-Bayt narrators receiving special credence and those from politically controversial narrators being subjected to heightened criticism.
