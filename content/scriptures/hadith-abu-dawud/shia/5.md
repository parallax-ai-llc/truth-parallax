Shia scholars engage with Abu Dawud's final sections on the Sunnah and manners with particular sensitivity to the creedal traditions. The hadiths defining the authority of the Sunnah are examined alongside the Shia doctrine that authentic Sunnah must be transmitted and interpreted through the Imams. Where Abu Dawud records traditions on predestination, divine attributes, or the companions' authority, Shia scholars assess whether these reflect authentic prophetic teaching or later theological constructions.

The manners sections are received with greater harmony, as prophetic guidance on social etiquette — visiting the sick, attending funerals, greeting with peace — represents a shared heritage across Islamic traditions. Shia scholars read these traditions alongside the ethical teachings of the Imams, noting the continuity between the Prophet's manners and the conduct of Ali, al-Hasan, al-Husayn, and their descendants. This section provides common ground between Sunni and Shia, demonstrating that beneath doctrinal differences lies a shared commitment to the prophetic model of gracious and compassionate social conduct.
