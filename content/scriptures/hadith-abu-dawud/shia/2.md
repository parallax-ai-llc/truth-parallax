Shia engagement with Abu Dawud's zakat and fasting traditions focuses on the substantive legal content and its correspondence with Ja'fari rulings. In the area of zakat, Shia jurisprudence recognizes the obligation but adds *khums* (a one-fifth tax) as an additional requirement not found in the Sunni canonical collections. Shia scholars examine Abu Dawud's zakat traditions for their compatibility with the broader framework of religious taxation in Shia law.

The fasting traditions are engaged more harmoniously, as the fundamental regulations of the Ramadan fast — its timing, conditions for exemption, and spiritual significance — are largely shared across Sunni and Shia traditions. Shia scholars note where Abu Dawud records fasting traditions through narrators connected to Ahl al-Bayt, treating these as particularly reliable. Differences emerge on specific points, such as the criteria for determining the beginning and end of Ramadan, where Shia jurisprudence may apply distinct evidentiary standards for moon sighting.
