Shia scholars approach Abu Dawud's jihad traditions through the lens of the Imamate — the belief that only a divinely appointed Imam has the full authority to declare offensive jihad. This theological principle shapes Shia reading of the military traditions, distinguishing between the Prophet's own commands (which carry full authority) and the practices of later caliphs (whose authority Shia Islam does not recognize). Traditions about Ali's military leadership are highlighted as models of legitimate command.

The rites sections on sacrifice and oaths are engaged for their legal content, with Shia scholars comparing Abu Dawud's narrations against the rulings of the Twelve Imams. Ritual sacrifice during Hajj and Eid al-Adha follows broadly similar patterns across Sunni and Shia practice, and Abu Dawud's traditions on the proper method and conditions of sacrifice are acknowledged where they align with Shia jurisprudence. Differences in detail — such as the specific supplications recited during sacrifice — are noted and traced to distinct chains of transmission.
