## Overview

The Gospel of Mary is an early Christian text in which Mary Magdalene emerges as a privileged recipient of the Savior's teaching. Surviving in a fifth-century Coptic manuscript (Berlin Codex 8502) and two Greek fragments, the text is notable for the large sections that are missing — roughly half the original is lost. What remains presents a post-resurrection teaching by the Savior on the nature of matter and sin, followed by Mary's account of a private vision describing the soul's ascent past hostile powers, and concluding with a dispute among the disciples over the legitimacy of her testimony.

## Structure

The surviving text divides into three major sections:

1. **The Savior's Teaching on Matter and Sin** (Chapter 1): A dialogue in which the Savior explains that sin is not a moral category but a condition of attachment to matter
2. **Mary's Vision — The Soul's Ascent Past the Powers** (Chapter 2): Mary recounts a vision of the soul overcoming Desire, Ignorance, and Wrath during its return to the divine
3. **The Dispute — Peter, Andrew, and Levi on Mary's Authority** (Chapter 3): Peter and Andrew challenge Mary's authority; Levi defends her as one whom the Savior loved more than them

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Gospel of Mary has attracted renewed interest across traditions:

- **Gnosticism**: Valued as testimony to Mary Magdalene's role as a revealer of hidden knowledge
- **Catholicism / Eastern Orthodoxy**: Not canonical; the text challenges hierarchical and gendered models of authority
- **Protestantism**: Studied as a historical document illuminating early Christian diversity
- **Judaism**: Not part of Jewish tradition, though the text engages with Jewish angelology and cosmology
- **Academic**: A central text in studies of gender, authority, and diversity in early Christianity
