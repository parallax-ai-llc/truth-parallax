The authority dispute in chapter 3 is an entirely intra-Christian matter and does not directly engage Jewish tradition. Jewish scholars approach it primarily as evidence for the social dynamics of early Christian communities and their relationship to broader Mediterranean gender norms. The debate over whether a woman could serve as an authoritative teacher reflects tensions that existed within both Jewish and early Christian communities, though the specific terms of the Gospel of Mary's resolution — appeal to the Savior's personal preference — are distinctly Christian.

From a Jewish studies perspective, the text is significant as evidence for the diversity of early Christian attitudes toward women's religious authority. The Second Temple and early rabbinic periods saw a range of positions on women's participation in religious life, and the Gospel of Mary provides a data point for understanding how these negotiations played out within the Christian movement as it distinguished itself from its Jewish matrix.
