## Overview

The Gospel of Mary is not part of the Jewish canon and represents an intra-Christian text with no direct relevance to Jewish religious practice. Jewish scholarly engagement with the text is limited and focuses primarily on the Second Temple Jewish traditions that inform its theological framework — particularly the concept of the soul's ascent past hostile powers, which has parallels in Jewish Merkabah (chariot mysticism) literature and Hekhalot texts.

## Jewish Background Elements

The soul-ascent narrative in Mary's vision draws on a tradition well attested in Jewish apocalyptic and mystical literature. The idea of the soul encountering hostile heavenly beings during its journey resonates with descriptions in texts such as the Ascension of Isaiah and elements of early Jewish mysticism. Jewish scholars note these shared roots while recognizing that the Gospel of Mary deploys them within a distinctly Gnostic-Christian framework that has departed from their Jewish origins.

## Broader Significance

From a Jewish studies perspective, the Gospel of Mary is primarily significant as evidence for the ways in which Jewish traditions were transformed within early Christian communities. The text's concern with authority, revelation, and the nature of the human person reflects questions that were being negotiated across the Jewish-Christian boundary in the second century, even as these communities were increasingly defining themselves in distinction from one another.
