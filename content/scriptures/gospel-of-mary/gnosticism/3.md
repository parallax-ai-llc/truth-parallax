The dispute in chapter 3 dramatizes a conflict central to Gnostic self-understanding: the tension between institutional authority and charismatic, spirit-led authority. Peter's objection — "Did he really speak privately with a woman rather than openly to us?" — represents the perspective of the psychic Christian who relies on external criteria (gender, apostolic office) to validate teaching. Mary's weeping in response reveals the pain of having authentic spiritual experience dismissed on the basis of social categories irrelevant to the spirit.

Levi's defense of Mary resolves the dispute in favor of the Gnostic position: the Savior loved Mary more than the other disciples because of her spiritual capacity, not her social status. Levi's rebuke of Peter — "If the Savior made her worthy, who are you to reject her?" — articulates the Gnostic principle that spiritual authority derives from inner worthiness recognized by the divine, not from external credentials. The chapter thus serves as a foundational text for Gnostic communities that valued inspired teaching from any spiritually qualified person, regardless of gender or institutional standing.
