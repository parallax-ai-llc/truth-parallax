Orthodox theology rejects the understanding of sin presented in chapter 1. The Orthodox tradition understands sin primarily as a sickness of the soul — a turning away from God that leads to spiritual death — rather than as the mixing of spiritual and material natures. The therapeutic model of salvation in Orthodox thought (Christ as physician) addresses a very different diagnosis than the Gospel of Mary's ontological account of sin as material entanglement.

The teaching on the dissolution of matter conflicts with the Orthodox vision of creation's ultimate transfiguration. Orthodox theology, grounded in the incarnation and expressed in the theology of icons, affirms that matter can be sanctified, transfigured, and made transparent to divine glory. The Transfiguration of Christ on Mount Tabor — a central event in Orthodox theology — reveals that the material world is destined not for dissolution but for illumination by uncreated light. The Gospel of Mary's cosmology, which treats matter as destined to return to its root, cannot accommodate this vision.
