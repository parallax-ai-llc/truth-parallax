The soul-ascent narrative in chapter 2 invites comparison with the Orthodox tradition of the "aerial toll houses" — a popular (though debated) teaching that the soul, after death, passes through stations where demons attempt to claim it based on its sins. While the structural parallel is noteworthy, the theological content differs significantly. In the toll-house tradition, the soul is defended by angels and by the prayers of the Church, and the outcome depends on divine mercy and the person's life of repentance. In Mary's vision, the soul overcomes the powers through self-knowledge alone.

Orthodox spiritual theology does value personal experience of God (theoria), and the mystical tradition from the Desert Fathers through the Hesychasts emphasizes direct encounter with the divine. However, such experience is always situated within the ecclesial community, tested against the patristic consensus, and understood as the fruit of grace rather than autonomous spiritual achievement. Mary's vision, presented as authoritative independent of any ecclesial validation, represents a model of spiritual authority that Orthodox theology cannot endorse without significant qualification.
