Mary's vision of the soul's ascent past hostile powers raises significant concerns from a Protestant perspective. The principle of sola scriptura holds that Scripture alone is the final authority for Christian doctrine. Private visions, while not denied as possible experiences, cannot serve as the basis for authoritative teaching. The Gospel of Mary's elevation of Mary's private visionary experience to the status of definitive revelation represents a model of authority that Protestantism cannot accept.

The content of the vision itself — the soul negotiating its way past personified cosmic powers — also lacks biblical grounding. While Paul acknowledges the existence of cosmic powers (Ephesians 6:12), the New Testament consistently teaches that believers' security rests in Christ's finished work, not in the soul's own ability to navigate hostile spiritual territories. The Protestant assurance of salvation is rooted in Christ's victory over all powers and principalities (Colossians 2:15), not in the possession of esoteric knowledge about the soul's post-mortem journey.
