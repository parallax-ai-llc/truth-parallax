The dispute over Mary's authority in chapter 3 touches on questions that continue to divide Protestant denominations. The text's implicit critique of gender-based restrictions on teaching authority resonates with Protestant traditions that affirm women's full participation in ministry, while it challenges those that maintain complementarian positions. However, Protestant interpreters across the spectrum recognize that a non-canonical Gnostic text cannot settle these debates, which must be resolved through careful exegesis of the canonical Scriptures.

Protestant scholars note that the dispute also raises broader questions about the nature of apostolic authority. The Gospel of Mary presents a model in which spiritual intimacy with the Savior, rather than membership in the Twelve or institutional appointment, constitutes the basis for authoritative teaching. While some Protestant traditions, particularly those in the charismatic and Pentecostal movements, might find this model partially congenial, mainstream Protestantism generally affirms that apostolic authority was unique to the foundational generation and that subsequent authority derives from faithfulness to the apostolic witness preserved in Scripture.
