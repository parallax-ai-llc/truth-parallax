## Overview

Protestantism does not recognize the Gospel of Mary as canonical scripture. The text was unknown until its discovery in a Cairo manuscript in 1896 (Berlin Codex 8502) and was never part of any canonical list in church history. Protestant scholars engage with the text primarily as a historical document that sheds light on the diversity of early Christian communities, particularly regarding questions of authority, gender, and the nature of revelation.

## Theological Concerns

From a Protestant perspective, the Gospel of Mary presents several theological problems. Its teaching that matter will be dissolved and that sin is a product of mixing rather than moral transgression departs from the biblical understanding of sin as rebellion against God. The text's emphasis on private visionary revelation as a source of authoritative teaching also conflicts with the Protestant principle of sola scriptura — that Scripture alone is the final authority for faith and practice.

## Contemporary Relevance

Despite its non-canonical status, the Gospel of Mary has generated significant interest among Protestant scholars and theologians, particularly in discussions about the role of women in the early church. The text's portrayal of Mary Magdalene as a legitimate teacher and its critique of gender-based exclusion from leadership resonate with ongoing debates within Protestant denominations about women's ordination and spiritual authority.
