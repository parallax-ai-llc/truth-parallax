## Overview

The Catholic Church does not accept the Gospel of Mary as canonical scripture. The text was not included in any early canonical list and presents theological positions at odds with Catholic doctrine. However, Catholic engagement with the text has increased, particularly following Pope Francis's 2016 elevation of Mary Magdalene's liturgical feast to the rank of apostolic feast, acknowledging her as the "apostle to the apostles." The Gospel of Mary provides an early, if heterodox, witness to Magdalene's prominent role in the early Christian movement.

## Theological Assessment

Catholic theologians note that while the text's cosmology and soteriology diverge from orthodox teaching — particularly its view of matter as destined for dissolution and its understanding of sin as a condition of material entanglement — the Gospel of Mary raises authentic questions about the nature of spiritual authority. The Church, however, resolves these questions through apostolic succession and magisterial authority rather than through the charismatic model the text proposes.

## The Figure of Mary Magdalene

Catholic scholarship has increasingly recognized that the canonical Gospels themselves present Mary Magdalene in a role of considerable authority — as the first witness to the resurrection and the one commissioned to announce it to the apostles. The Gospel of Mary, while non-canonical, provides additional evidence that early Christian communities remembered Magdalene as a significant figure whose authority was contested by some and defended by others.
