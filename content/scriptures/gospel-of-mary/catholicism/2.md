The Catholic tradition distinguishes between public revelation — closed with the death of the last apostle — and private revelation, which may occur but does not constitute authoritative teaching for the universal Church. Mary's vision in chapter 2, as a form of private revelation presented as universally binding teaching, conflicts with this framework. The Magisterium, not individual visionary experience, holds the authority to interpret and teach the faith.

The soul-ascent narrative, while structurally interesting to Catholic scholars of mystical theology, differs fundamentally from Catholic eschatological teaching. The Catholic tradition describes the soul's post-mortem journey in terms of particular judgment, purgatory, and final union with God — categories shaped by grace, merit, and divine mercy. The Gospel of Mary's schema, in which the soul must defeat personified cosmic powers through self-knowledge, lacks any role for divine grace, Christ's redemptive work, or the intercession of the saints — elements central to Catholic eschatology.
