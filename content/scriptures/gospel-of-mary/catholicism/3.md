The dispute between Peter and Mary in chapter 3 is of particular interest to Catholic theology, given the Church's teaching on Petrine primacy. The text's portrayal of Peter as resistant to Mary's teaching and rebuked by Levi contrasts with the Catholic understanding of Peter as the rock upon which Christ built his Church (Matthew 16:18). Catholic scholars note that the text likely reflects second-century debates over authority rather than historical events, and that its implicit critique of Petrine authority serves the theological agenda of communities that favored charismatic over institutional models.

Catholic engagement with Mary Magdalene has evolved significantly. Pope Francis's 2016 decree elevating her feast day recognized her as the "apostle to the apostles," and Catholic biblical scholarship increasingly acknowledges her prominent role in the earliest Christian communities. However, the Church resolves questions of authority not through appeal to non-canonical texts but through the Magisterium's interpretation of Scripture and Tradition. The Gospel of Mary provides historical context for understanding early debates about women's roles but does not itself constitute authoritative testimony.
