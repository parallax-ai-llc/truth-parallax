Catholic theology rejects the Savior's teaching in chapter 1 on multiple grounds. The claim that sin arises from the mixing of spiritual and material natures contradicts the Catholic understanding of sin as a free act of the will in opposition to God's law. The Catechism defines sin as "an offense against reason, truth, and right conscience" (CCC 1849) — a moral and relational category, not an ontological condition of material entanglement.

The teaching that matter will dissolve into its own root also conflicts with the Catholic sacramental vision, in which material elements become vehicles of divine grace. The Eucharist, in which bread and wine become the body and blood of Christ, represents the most radical affirmation of matter's capacity to bear the divine. A theology that treats matter as inherently destined for dissolution cannot sustain the sacramental economy that lies at the heart of Catholic worship and spirituality.
