The dispute in chapter 3 has generated the most extensive academic discussion of any passage in the Gospel of Mary. Scholars read the conflict between Peter and Mary as reflecting genuine second-century debates over the legitimacy of women's teaching authority in Christian communities. Ann Graham Brock's comparative study of Peter and Mary traditions across early Christian literature has demonstrated a consistent pattern in which texts associated with "orthodox" communities favor Peter's authority while texts associated with Gnostic or heterodox communities elevate Mary Magdalene.

The character of Levi, who mediates the dispute, has received particular attention. His alignment with Mary against Peter may reflect historical tensions between different leadership models in early Christianity. Scholars debate whether Levi is to be identified with Matthew (as in Mark 2:14/Matthew 9:9) and what significance this identification might carry. The chapter's concluding scene — in which the disciples go out to preach — has been read both as a narrative resolution and as a programmatic statement about the kind of authority the text's community endorsed.
