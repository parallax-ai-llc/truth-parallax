## Overview

The Seven Valleys (Haft-Vádí) is a mystical treatise composed by Bahá'u'lláh around 1860 in Baghdad, written in response to questions posed by a Sufi judge. The text describes the soul's journey toward God through seven successive stages or "valleys," drawing extensively on the imagery and vocabulary of Persian Sufi literature — particularly Farid ud-Din Attar's Conference of the Birds (Mantiq ut-Tayr). While rooted in the Sufi tradition, the Seven Valleys transforms its sources within the framework of Bahá'u'lláh's own prophetic claim, presenting the mystical journey as the universal path of every soul toward the recognition of the divine.

## Structure

The Seven Valleys traces seven stages of spiritual ascent:

1. **The Valley of Search**: The beginning of the spiritual quest, requiring patience, ardor, and sincere seeking
2. **The Valley of Love**: The soul consumed by longing for the divine Beloved, surrendering reason to passion
3. **The Valley of Knowledge**: The attainment of inner wisdom and the discernment of spiritual meanings
4. **The Valley of Unity**: The vision of the oneness of all things in the divine reality
5. **The Valley of Contentment**: Detachment from worldly desire and the experience of inner peace
6. **The Valley of Wonderment**: Awe and bewilderment before the infinite mystery of God
7. **The Valley of True Poverty and Absolute Nothingness**: The annihilation of self (*fana*) and subsistence in God alone

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Bahá'í**: A treasured mystical text, studied as a guide to the inner spiritual life and the stages of the soul's approach to God
- **Academic**: A key document for the study of Bahá'í mysticism, its relationship to Sufi literature, and the transformation of Islamic mystical forms
- **Comparative**: Invites rich comparison with mystical stage-models across traditions — Sufi maqamat, Christian mystical theology, Kabbalistic sefirot, and Buddhist jhanas
- **Islamic**: Deeply rooted in the Sufi tradition of spiritual wayfaring, engaging with the legacy of Attar, Rumi, and Ibn Arabi
- **Christian**: Resonates with the via mystica — purgation, illumination, and union — and with John of the Cross's "dark night of the soul"
- **Interfaith**: A text of universal mystical appeal, used widely in interfaith contemplative settings
