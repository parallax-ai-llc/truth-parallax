Sufi tradition reads an-Nasa'i's mosque and prayer time traditions as guidance for sanctifying space and time through worship. The mosque is understood not merely as a building but as a symbol of the purified heart — just as the mosque is a space set apart for God's remembrance, so the heart must be emptied of worldly distractions and consecrated to divine awareness. Traditions about the merits of praying in mosques are read as pointing toward the spiritual merit of internal devotion.

The prayer time traditions take on mystical significance in Sufi reading. The five daily prayers mark stations in the spiritual day, each corresponding to a dimension of the soul's relationship with God. The pre-dawn prayer (*fajr*) represents the awakening of spiritual consciousness; the midday and afternoon prayers sustain awareness through the active hours; the evening and night prayers return the soul to its contemplative source. An-Nasa'i's precise delineation of prayer times provides the temporal architecture within which the Sufi practitioner structures a life of continuous remembrance.
