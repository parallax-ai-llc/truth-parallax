Sufi interpreters read an-Nasa'i's pilgrimage traditions as descriptions of the soul's journey to God. The Hajj is understood on multiple levels: the physical journey to Mecca mirrors the spiritual journey to the divine presence, the stripping away of ordinary clothing for the *ihram* symbolizes the stripping away of the ego, and the circumambulation of the Ka'bah represents the soul's orbit around the divine center that gives all existence its meaning.

The jihad traditions are engaged primarily through the lens of the "greater jihad" — the struggle against the lower self. Sufi masters teach that the outward battlefield is a reflection of the inner one, and that true victory lies in conquering the ego's resistance to God's will. An-Nasa'i's traditions on courage, sacrifice, and sincerity in battle are read as descriptions of the spiritual qualities needed for the inner struggle. The martyr who dies in battle is a symbol of the spiritual aspirant who dies to the self and is reborn in God — a death and resurrection that occurs within the living heart of the sincere seeker.
