Sufi engagement with an-Nasa'i's zakat and fasting traditions emphasizes the transformative power of generosity and self-denial. Zakat is understood as the outward expression of the heart's detachment from worldly possessions — the truly generous soul gives not out of obligation but out of the overflowing love that comes from recognizing God as the source of all provision. An-Nasa'i's authenticated traditions on charitable giving are embraced as prophetic models of the generosity that characterizes the spiritually advanced.

Fasting is treasured in Sufi practice as the most direct path to subduing the ego and opening the heart to divine illumination. An-Nasa'i's traditions on Ramadan and voluntary fasting are followed with the understanding that physical hunger creates a spiritual hunger — a longing for God that cannot be satisfied by anything less than divine presence. The Sufi practitioner who observes an-Nasa'i's authenticated fasting traditions does so not merely as legal compliance but as a deliberate entry into the state of spiritual emptiness that makes room for the fullness of divine encounter.
