A comparative reading of an-Nasa'i's mosque and prayer time traditions examines how religious communities sanctify space and time for worship. The Islamic mosque, as described in an-Nasa'i's traditions, can be compared with the Jewish synagogue, the Christian church, the Hindu temple, the Buddhist vihara, and the Sikh gurdwara. Each sacred space embodies a tradition's theology in architectural form and establishes behavioral norms that distinguish sacred from profane space.

The regulation of prayer times documented by an-Nasa'i parallels the temporal structuring of worship in other traditions: the Jewish division of the day into prayer services tied to the Temple sacrifices, the Christian canonical hours descended from monastic practice, and the Hindu *sandhyavandana* performed at dawn, noon, and dusk. Each tradition anchors its worship to natural temporal markers — sunrise, noon, sunset — while investing these moments with theological significance. The result is a sanctification of time that transforms the ordinary day into a sacred rhythm.
