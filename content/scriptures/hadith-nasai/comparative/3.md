A comparative analysis of an-Nasa'i's zakat and fasting traditions places Islamic economic ethics and ascetic discipline within the broader context of religious approaches to wealth and self-denial. The mandatory charitable contribution documented through an-Nasa'i's hadiths can be compared with Jewish *ma'aser* (tithing), Christian tithing traditions, and Sikh *dasvandh*, as well as the Buddhist concept of *dana* as a primary merit-producing practice.

The fasting traditions in an-Nasa'i invite comparison with ascetic disciplines across religious traditions. The communal, calendar-based character of the Ramadan fast distinguishes it from the more individualized fasting practices of some traditions, while its combination of physical abstention with spiritual intensification parallels the Lenten discipline of Christianity and the fasting practices associated with Jewish holy days. An-Nasa'i's strict authentication of fasting traditions adds a methodological dimension not found in most other tradition's treatments of ascetic practice — a critical evaluation of the very sources that prescribe the discipline.
