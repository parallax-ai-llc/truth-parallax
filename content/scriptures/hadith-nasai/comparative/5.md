A comparative reading of an-Nasa'i's transaction and judicial traditions examines the Islamic approach to economic and procedural law within the broader landscape of religious legal systems. The prophetic regulations on commerce documented by an-Nasa'i parallel the commercial ethics found in Jewish *halakha*, Christian canon law, Hindu *dharmashastra*, and Confucian economic thought. Each tradition develops a moral framework for economic activity grounded in its foundational values.

The judicial traditions invite comparison with the legal procedures of other religious courts: the Jewish *beit din*, the Christian ecclesiastical tribunal, and the traditional Confucian magistracy. Each system grapples with questions of judicial qualification, evidentiary standards, and the relationship between divine law and human judgment. An-Nasa'i's strict authentication methodology adds a distinctive layer to this comparison — the insistence that every legal precedent must be traceable through a verified chain of transmission to a divine or prophetic source, a methodological feature that has no exact parallel in other religious legal traditions.
