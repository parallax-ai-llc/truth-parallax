A comparative analysis of an-Nasa'i's purification and prayer traditions reveals the universal religious pattern of ritual preparation followed by structured worship. The Islamic ablution system documented by an-Nasa'i can be compared with the Jewish *mikveh* (ritual immersion pool), the Christian baptismal tradition, the Hindu practice of bathing in sacred rivers, and the Shinto rituals of *harae* (purification). Each tradition develops a ritual grammar linking physical cleanliness with spiritual readiness for the sacred.

An-Nasa'i's comprehensive treatment of prayer — its times, postures, recitations, and communal dimensions — invites comparison with the liturgical traditions of other religions. The prostration central to Islamic prayer parallels the *metanoia* (full prostration) in Eastern Orthodox worship, the *kowtow* in Confucian ritual, and the *sashtanga pranam* in Hindu devotion. Within Islam, an-Nasa'i's documentation of variant prayer practices mirrors the liturgical diversity within other traditions, demonstrating that internal pluralism is a common feature of living religious communities.
