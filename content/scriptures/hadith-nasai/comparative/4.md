A comparative reading of an-Nasa'i's pilgrimage traditions situates the Hajj within the global phenomenon of sacred pilgrimage. The journey to Mecca parallels the Jewish pilgrimage to the Temple in Jerusalem, the Christian pilgrimages to Jerusalem, Rome, and Santiago de Compostela, the Hindu *tirtha yatra* to sacred rivers and temples, and the Buddhist pilgrimage to the sites of the Buddha's life. Each tradition invests the physical journey with spiritual significance, understanding travel to a sacred center as a transformative encounter with the holy.

The jihad traditions invite cautious but instructive comparison with concepts of sanctioned struggle in other traditions. The Christian just war tradition, the Hindu concept of *dharma yuddha* (righteous warfare), and the Buddhist notion of defending the *dharma* each represent attempts to reconcile religious ethics with the reality of armed conflict. An-Nasa'i's authenticated traditions on the ethical constraints of jihad contribute to a comparative understanding of how religious communities establish moral boundaries around the use of force.
