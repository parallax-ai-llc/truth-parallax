Academic study of an-Nasa'i's sections on mosques and prayer times provides evidence for the social and architectural history of early Islamic worship spaces and the regulation of communal religious life. The mosque traditions reveal the functions these buildings served — not only as prayer spaces but as community centers, educational institutions, and sites of political assembly — and the norms that governed behavior within them.

The prayer time traditions are examined for what they reveal about the relationship between Islamic time-keeping and the astronomical and calendrical knowledge of the ninth-century Muslim world. Scholars analyze how the prophetic prescriptions for prayer times were interpreted and applied in different geographical contexts, from equatorial regions where sunrise and sunset vary little to northern latitudes where seasonal variation creates practical challenges. This material illuminates the processes by which a revelatory mandate was translated into practical time-keeping systems adapted to diverse environmental conditions.
