Academic scholarship examines an-Nasa'i's zakat and fasting traditions for their contribution to the economic and social history of early Islamic society. The zakat hadiths provide data on the types of wealth and agricultural produce prevalent in the prophetic and post-prophetic periods, the administrative mechanisms for charitable collection, and the social safety net that the zakat system was designed to provide.

The fasting traditions are studied within the broader context of Near Eastern ascetic practices and calendrical observances. Scholars trace possible connections between the Ramadan fast and pre-Islamic Arabian, Jewish, and Christian fasting traditions, examining how the Islamic practice both absorbed and transformed earlier models. An-Nasa'i's strict authentication standards make his fasting traditions particularly useful for distinguishing between practices that can be traced to the earliest Islamic period and those that may have developed later under various cultural influences.
