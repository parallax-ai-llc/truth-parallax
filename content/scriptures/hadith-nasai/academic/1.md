Academic scholars study an-Nasa'i's extensive purification and prayer sections as primary evidence for understanding the standardization of Islamic liturgical practice. The variant ablution and prayer practices he records — while subjecting each to strict authentication — reveal a period in which multiple legitimate forms of worship coexisted, and hadith compilation was one of the processes through which uniformity was gradually achieved.

Researchers examine the chains of transmission in these sections to map the geographical distribution of specific prayer practices, tracing how forms of worship circulated from Medina and Kufa to other centers of Islamic learning. An-Nasa'i's strict narrator criticism provides a methodological control: by comparing the traditions he accepted with those he rejected, scholars can reconstruct his evaluation criteria and assess the intellectual standards that governed ninth-century hadith scholarship. The prayer material is also valuable for comparative liturgical studies examining the relationship between Islamic, Jewish, and Christian patterns of daily worship.
