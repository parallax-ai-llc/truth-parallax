Academic scholarship studies an-Nasa'i's commercial and judicial traditions as sources for understanding the development of Islamic economic and procedural law. The transaction hadiths reveal the commercial practices of the early Muslim community — the types of contracts in use, the financial instruments available, and the regulatory principles invoked — providing evidence for the economic history of the early Islamic world.

The judicial traditions are examined for their contribution to understanding the formation of the Islamic legal system. Scholars analyze the qualifications prescribed for judges, the evidentiary standards required for different types of cases, and the procedures for adjudication and appeal. This material illuminates the processes by which prophetic precedent was transformed into a functioning judicial system, and how the hadith literature served as the primary source of legal authority in an era before the full codification of Islamic jurisprudence. An-Nasa'i's strict narrator standards provide additional data for studying how authentication criteria affected the content of legal hadith collections.
