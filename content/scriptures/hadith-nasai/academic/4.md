Academic study of an-Nasa'i's pilgrimage traditions examines the Hajj as a complex ritual system that both transformed and preserved elements of pre-Islamic Arabian religious practice. Scholars analyze the pilgrimage hadiths for evidence of how the Prophet reformed existing rites, maintained continuity with Abrahamic precedent, and established the distinctive Islamic form of pilgrimage that has continued for over fourteen centuries.

The jihad sections are studied within the context of early Islamic military history and the development of the Islamic laws of warfare. Academic researchers examine how prophetic precedents were invoked to establish ethical constraints on armed conflict, how the concept of jihad evolved in the post-prophetic period, and how the hadith literature on warfare relates to the actual military practices of the early Muslim community. An-Nasa'i's strict authentication is particularly valuable here, as it helps scholars distinguish between traditions with strong claims to early provenance and those that may reflect later developments.
