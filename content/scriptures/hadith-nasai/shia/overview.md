## Overview

Shia scholarship engages with Sunan an-Nasa'i with particular interest because of an-Nasa'i's known sympathies toward Ahl al-Bayt. Historical sources report that an-Nasa'i authored a work specifically on the virtues of Ali ibn Abi Talib (*Khasa'is Amir al-Mu'minin*), and that he was killed in Damascus for refusing to acknowledge the superiority of Mu'awiya over Ali. These biographical details lend his collection special significance for Shia scholars.

## Interpretive Approach

Shia engagement with an-Nasa'i is shaped by the belief that his personal sympathies toward Ahl al-Bayt make his collection a more trustworthy Sunni source than compilations by scholars perceived as less sympathetic. Traditions he records that praise Ali and the Prophet's family are given particular weight, as they come from a compiler whom Shia scholars regard as less likely to have suppressed pro-Alid material. His strict narrator standards are also appreciated, as they suggest a commitment to truth over political convenience.

## Points of Engagement

Shia scholars highlight an-Nasa'i's *Khasa'is* as evidence that a major Sunni hadith compiler recognized Ali's distinctive merit. The traditions on Ali's virtues that appear in the Sunan itself are cited in inter-traditional dialogue as confirmations of Shia positions from within the Sunni canon. An-Nasa'i's martyrdom for his refusal to disparage Ali is remembered in Shia historiography as evidence of the political pressures that shaped Sunni hadith transmission.
