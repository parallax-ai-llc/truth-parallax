Shia scholars engage with an-Nasa'i's zakat traditions while noting that Shia jurisprudence adds the requirement of *khums* — a one-fifth tax on certain categories of income and wealth — which is not systematically addressed in the Sunni canonical collections. The zakat traditions themselves are evaluated for their compatibility with Ja'fari rulings on the categories, thresholds, and distribution of obligatory charity. Where convergences exist, they are noted as evidence of shared prophetic foundations.

The fasting chapters are engaged with broad agreement, as the fundamental Ramadan regulations are common across Islamic traditions. Shia scholars note where an-Nasa'i records fasting traditions through Ahl al-Bayt narrators, treating these as particularly reliable given both the narrators' proximity to the Prophet and an-Nasa'i's own sympathies. Specific points of divergence — such as the criteria for moon sighting and the treatment of intentional fast-breaking — are examined by comparing an-Nasa'i's narrations with the rulings transmitted through the Twelve Imams.
