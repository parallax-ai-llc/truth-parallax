Shia scholars approach an-Nasa'i's purification and prayer traditions with particular openness, given his known sympathies toward Ahl al-Bayt. His recording of variant prayer practices is examined for narrations that may support Shia liturgical positions — such as the permissibility of combining prayers and the specific form of the *adhan*. Where an-Nasa'i authenticates traditions that align with Ja'fari practice, Shia scholars cite these as evidence from a sympathetic yet rigorous Sunni compiler.

The purification chapters are examined for traditions relevant to the Shia practice of wiping (rather than washing) the feet during ablution. An-Nasa'i's strict authentication standards mean that any purification tradition he includes carries significant evidentiary weight, and Shia scholars leverage this credibility in inter-traditional dialogue. His comprehensive coverage of prayer regulations also provides numerous points of comparison between Sunni and Shia liturgical practice, with convergences noted as evidence of shared prophetic origin and divergences attributed to the politics of hadith transmission.
