Shia scholars approach an-Nasa'i's pilgrimage traditions with attention to the Prophet's Farewell Hajj, during which — according to Shia historiography — the Prophet publicly designated Ali as his successor at Ghadir Khumm. While the Hajj rituals themselves are largely shared across traditions, the events surrounding the Farewell Pilgrimage carry deep theological significance for Shia Islam, and any traditions an-Nasa'i preserves about this occasion are examined with care.

The jihad sections are read through the Shia doctrine that offensive jihad requires the authority of the divinely appointed Imam. An-Nasa'i's traditions on the Prophet's military campaigns are accepted as descriptions of legitimate warfare under prophetic authority, but the extension of this authority to subsequent caliphs is questioned. Shia scholars highlight that an-Nasa'i, who reportedly died for his refusal to praise Mu'awiya over Ali, would have understood the political stakes of military authority, and they read his selections and editorial choices in this light.
