Shia scholars engage with an-Nasa'i's commercial and judicial traditions by comparing them with the legal rulings transmitted through the Twelve Imams. The transaction hadiths are assessed for their compatibility with Ja'fari commercial law, which shares many fundamentals with Sunni commercial jurisprudence — the prohibition of usury, the requirement of contractual transparency — while differing on specific types of contracts and conditions.

The judicial traditions are of special interest because Shia theology holds that Ali ibn Abi Talib was the most qualified judge after the Prophet, and traditions about Ali's judicial wisdom appear throughout Islamic hadith literature. An-Nasa'i's recording of Ali's legal decisions and judicial pronouncements is highlighted by Shia scholars as evidence of Ali's unparalleled legal knowledge — a point that supports the broader Shia argument for his rightful succession. An-Nasa'i's sympathetic disposition toward Ahl al-Bayt makes his judicial selections a trusted source for traditions about the legal acumen of the Prophet's household.
