Shia engagement with an-Nasa'i's sections on mosques and prayer times centers on traditions that bear on the sacred geography of Islam and the regulation of congregational worship. The traditions on the merits of the three holy mosques are broadly accepted, though Shia scholars give additional emphasis to the sanctity of sites associated with Ahl al-Bayt — such as the mosque of Kufa and the shrines of the Imams — which are not addressed in the Sunni canon.

The prayer times traditions are compared with Ja'fari jurisprudence on the scheduling and combination of daily prayers. Shia practice allows the combination of the noon and afternoon prayers and the evening and night prayers, effectively reducing the five prayer times to three. Where an-Nasa'i records traditions that permit or describe such combinations — narrated from the Prophet's practice during travel or other circumstances — Shia scholars cite these as evidence that their practice reflects an authentic prophetic option that Sunni tradition narrowed without prophetic warrant.
