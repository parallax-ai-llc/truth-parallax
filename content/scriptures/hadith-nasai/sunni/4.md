An-Nasa'i's sections on pilgrimage and jihad cover two domains of Islamic practice that involve significant physical and legal complexity. The Hajj traditions detail every stage of the pilgrimage — from the state of consecration (*ihram*) to the farewell circumambulation — with an-Nasa'i's characteristic precision. Sunni jurists rely on his authenticated narrations to establish the obligatory, recommended, and prohibited acts during Hajj and Umrah.

The jihad sections record prophetic traditions on the conditions, ethics, and merit of military struggle. An-Nasa'i preserves hadiths on the preparation for battle, the treatment of prisoners and non-combatants, the distribution of spoils, and the spiritual merit ascribed to those who fight in defense of the Muslim community. Sunni scholars treat these traditions as the prophetic legislation governing armed conflict, emphasizing the ethical constraints that distinguish legitimate jihad from mere aggression. An-Nasa'i's strict authentication ensures that these consequential traditions rest on the firmest possible evidential foundations.
