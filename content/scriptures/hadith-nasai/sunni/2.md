An-Nasa'i's sections on mosques and prayer times address the spatial and temporal framework within which Islamic worship takes place. The mosque traditions cover the etiquette of entering and leaving, the designation of sacred precincts, the merits of specific mosques (particularly the three holy mosques in Mecca, Medina, and Jerusalem), and the congregational obligations that attach to the mosque as a communal space.

The prayer times sections are valued by Sunni scholars for their precise delineation of the windows within which each prayer must be performed. An-Nasa'i records traditions specifying the earliest and latest acceptable times for each of the five daily prayers, the preferred time within each window, and the special circumstances (such as travel or extreme weather) that may affect timing. These traditions form the evidential basis for the prayer timetables used throughout the Muslim world, and an-Nasa'i's strict authentication ensures that the timings rest on reliable prophetic guidance.
