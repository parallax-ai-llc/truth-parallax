An-Nasa'i's sections on commercial transactions and judicial proceedings provide Sunni legal scholars with authenticated prophetic precedents for economic and procedural law. The transaction traditions address the permissibility of various contract forms — sales, partnerships, advance payment, agency — and the prohibitions against usury, fraud, and excessive uncertainty. An-Nasa'i's strict narrator standards lend these legal traditions particular authority in jurisprudential debates.

The judicial traditions cover the qualifications of judges, the rules of evidence, the administration of oaths, and the procedures for dispute resolution. Sunni scholars value these traditions for establishing the prophetic model of justice that Islamic courts seek to emulate. An-Nasa'i's authenticated narrations on testimony, including the number and character of witnesses required for different types of cases, provide the textual basis for Islamic procedural law. The combination of commercial and judicial traditions in this section reflects the comprehensive scope of Islamic legal concern, extending prophetic authority over both the marketplace and the courtroom.
