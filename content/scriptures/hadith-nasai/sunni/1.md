An-Nasa'i's opening sections on purification and prayer constitute the most extensive and detailed treatment of these subjects in the canonical hadith collections. Sunni scholars prize this material for its comprehensiveness: an-Nasa'i records traditions covering every conceivable aspect of ritual cleanliness — from the water used for ablution to the handling of specific impurities — and every element of the daily prayers — from the opening invocation to the closing greeting.

The strictness of an-Nasa'i's narrator standards means that the traditions in these sections carry strong presumptions of authenticity, making them particularly reliable for deriving jurisprudential rulings. Sunni jurists across all four schools draw upon an-Nasa'i's prayer traditions to support their respective positions, appreciating that his collection preserves multiple authentic variant practices that reflect the genuine diversity of early Muslim worship. His meticulous recording of the Prophet's prayer — its movements, recitations, and supplications — provides the most detailed prophetic blueprint for the central act of Islamic worship.
