Salafi scholars engage with an-Nasa'i's pilgrimage traditions to establish the precise prophetic method for performing Hajj and Umrah. Every element of the pilgrimage — from the type of *ihram* to the specific supplications at each station — is determined from authenticated narrations, with Salafi practitioners seeking to replicate the Prophet's Farewell Hajj as exactly as possible. An-Nasa'i's strict standards ensure that the traditions used for this reconstruction meet the highest thresholds of reliability.

The jihad sections are treated as prophetic legislation defining the legitimate conditions and ethical constraints of armed struggle. Salafi scholars insist that jihad must conform to the specific parameters established by authenticated hadith — just cause, proper authority, protection of innocents, prohibition of treachery — and that deviations from these prophetic norms are themselves violations of Islamic law. An-Nasa'i's traditions on the merit and conduct of jihad are used to counter both those who reject the concept entirely and those who invoke it without regard for its prophetic constraints.
