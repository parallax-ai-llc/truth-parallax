Salafi scholars apply an-Nasa'i's commercial and judicial traditions to contemporary legal questions, insisting that prophetic precedent provides binding guidance for economic and procedural law. The transaction traditions are used to evaluate modern financial instruments and practices against prophetic standards — with particular attention to the prohibition of interest-based lending and the requirement of contractual transparency. Where modern economic practices conflict with authenticated prophetic norms, Salafi scholars advocate for prophetic compliance.

The judicial traditions are engaged as the foundation for Islamic legal procedure. Salafi scholars use an-Nasa'i's authenticated narrations to argue that legitimate justice must be administered according to divine law, with judges who possess knowledge of the Quran and Sunnah and who apply evidence-based reasoning rather than personal opinion. The strictness of an-Nasa'i's compilation is seen as a model for the rigor that should characterize Islamic legal proceedings — every ruling grounded in authenticated evidence, every judgment traceable to prophetic precedent.
