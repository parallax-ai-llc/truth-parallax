The Gnostic reading of Pistis Sophia chapter 1 opens with a scene of extraordinary cosmic significance: the risen Jesus, eleven years after the resurrection, sits with his disciples on the Mount of Olives when a tremendous light descends and he is taken up into the heavens. He returns clothed in the light-vesture, radiating a brilliance so intense that the disciples cannot look upon him. For Gnostic readers, this scene establishes Jesus as the supreme emissary of the realm of Light, far beyond any earthly teacher or prophet.

The light-vesture is not a metaphor but a description of Jesus's true nature as revealed after the resurrection. In Gnostic cosmology, the material body is a garment of flesh that conceals the being of light within. Jesus's investiture with the light-vesture reverses this concealment, showing the disciples — and the reader — what lies beyond the material veil. The three garments of light that Jesus receives correspond to the three powers of the First Mystery, representing the fullness of divine authority.

The disciples' terror and confusion at Jesus's transformed appearance reflects the Gnostic understanding that direct encounter with the divine overwhelms ordinary human perception. Only through the progressive revelation of the mysteries — which the remaining chapters will unfold — can the disciples learn to perceive and participate in the light that Jesus now embodies.
