Protestant scholars analyze the final chapter's sacramental descriptions with particular interest. The three baptisms — water, fire, and Spirit — recall the canonical distinction between John's water baptism and Jesus's baptism with the Holy Spirit and fire (Matthew 3:11). Protestant interpreters note how the Gnostic text elaborates what is a brief canonical reference into an extensive sacramental system with specific ritual procedures and cosmic effects.

The chapter's commissioning of the disciples to share the mysteries invites comparison with the Great Commission (Matthew 28:18-20). In both cases, the risen Jesus sends disciples to transmit what they have received. The difference lies in the content: the canonical commission centers on making disciples of all nations through baptism and teaching; the Gnostic commission centers on transmitting mysteries to the worthy elect. Protestant scholars identify this as a fundamental ecclesiological difference between the inclusive mission of the canonical Church and the esoteric selectivity of Gnostic communities.
