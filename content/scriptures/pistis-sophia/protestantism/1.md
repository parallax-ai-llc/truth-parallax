Protestant scholars examine Pistis Sophia chapter 1 as a post-resurrection narrative that extends far beyond the canonical accounts. The forty days of post-resurrection teaching mentioned in Acts 1:3 have here become eleven years, during which Jesus reveals an elaborate cosmological system unknown to the canonical Gospels. Protestant theology, grounded in the sufficiency of Scripture, regards this extension as characteristic of Gnostic literature's claim to possess secret teachings beyond the apostolic witness.

The light-vesture scene, in which Jesus ascends and returns clothed in overwhelming brilliance, is compared by Protestant scholars to the Transfiguration (Matthew 17:1-8) and the ascension (Acts 1:9-11). The canonical accounts, however, are restrained and Christologically grounded, whereas the Gnostic narrative elaborates the scene into a complex cosmological drama. Protestant interpreters note that the simplicity and restraint of the canonical accounts reflect their character as genuine apostolic witness rather than speculative embellishment.
