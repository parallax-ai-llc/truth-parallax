Protestant analysis of the mysteries of the First Mystery focuses on the text's sacramental theology, which presents salvation as dependent on the reception of specific mysteries or sacraments. This framework conflicts with the Protestant doctrine of sola gratia — salvation by grace alone through faith, not through sacramental works. The hierarchical system of mysteries, in which higher mysteries confer greater salvation, resembles what the Reformers critiqued in medieval Catholic sacramental theology, though in a far more elaborate cosmic form.

The chapter's moral teachings, in which Jesus insists that the mysteries must be accompanied by righteous conduct, interest Protestant ethicists. The integration of sacrament and ethics in Pistis Sophia challenges the stereotype that Gnosticism was antinomian. Protestant scholars note this as evidence that Gnostic communities could be as ethically demanding as orthodox ones, complicating simplistic distinctions between Gnostic libertinism and orthodox moral seriousness.
