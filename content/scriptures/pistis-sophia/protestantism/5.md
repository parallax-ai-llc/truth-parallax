The chapter's detailed eschatology — its descriptions of the soul's post-mortem journey, reincarnation, and ultimate liberation — stands in stark contrast to Protestant teachings on the afterlife. Protestant eschatology, based on canonical Scripture, affirms bodily resurrection, final judgment, and eternal communion with God. Pistis Sophia's vision of the soul cycling through reincarnation until it receives the Gnostic mysteries and escapes embodiment reflects a fundamentally different anthropology.

Protestant scholars note that the chapter's teaching on reincarnation has no basis in canonical Scripture and reflects the influence of Platonic and possibly Eastern philosophical traditions on Gnostic thought. The text's detailed topography of the afterlife — the specific punishments, the cosmic spheres, the guardian archons — is compared to the canonical restraint of passages like 2 Corinthians 12:2-4, where Paul declines to describe what he saw in the third heaven.
