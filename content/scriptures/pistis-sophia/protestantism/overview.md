Protestant scholars classify Pistis Sophia as a non-canonical Gnostic text with no authority for Christian doctrine. The text's extensive post-resurrection dialogues, in which Jesus teaches elaborate cosmological and soteriological mysteries to his disciples, represent a genre of "secret teaching" that Protestant theology rejects. The sufficiency of the canonical Scriptures — a foundational principle of the Reformation — excludes the kind of esoteric supplementary revelation that Pistis Sophia claims to provide.

Protestant biblical scholars have shown particular interest in how Pistis Sophia reinterprets the canonical Psalms. Sophia's thirteen repentances are explicitly correlated with specific Psalms, which are given radical new meanings in the Gnostic framework. This practice of allegorical reinterpretation illustrates the hermeneutical methods of Gnostic communities and provides a contrast to Protestant principles of biblical interpretation, which emphasize the grammatical-historical sense of the text.

Despite its doctrinal distance from Protestant Christianity, the text is valued as a historical witness to second- and third-century Christian diversity. The prominence of Mary Magdalene has contributed to Protestant scholarly discussions about women's roles in early Christianity.
