Orthodox eschatology firmly rejects the reincarnation taught in this chapter. The Fifth Ecumenical Council (553 CE) is sometimes cited as having condemned the doctrine of the pre-existence and transmigration of souls. Orthodox teaching affirms the uniqueness of each human life, the particular judgment after death, and the general resurrection at the end of history. The Gnostic cycle of reincarnation, in which the soul passes through body after body until it achieves liberation, contradicts the Orthodox understanding of bodily existence as good, meaningful, and destined for resurrection.

The chapter's detailed descriptions of post-mortem punishments, however, have been compared to Orthodox and Byzantine visionary literature, including the Apocalypse of Paul and various monastic descriptions of the afterlife. Orthodox scholars note these generic similarities while insisting on the fundamental theological differences between Gnostic and Orthodox eschatological frameworks.
