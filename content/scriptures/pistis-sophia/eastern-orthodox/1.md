Eastern Orthodox scholars read Pistis Sophia chapter 1 with attention to its departure from the Orthodox understanding of the Resurrection. In Orthodox theology, the Resurrection of Christ is the victory over death and the beginning of the new creation — the entire cosmos is transformed. The Gnostic post-resurrection teaching, by contrast, uses the resurrection as a launching point for esoteric cosmological instruction that has no interest in the renewal of creation or the victory over physical death.

The light-vesture imagery is compared by Orthodox scholars to the Orthodox theology of divine light, particularly the Tabor Light celebrated in the Feast of the Transfiguration. The Palamite distinction between God's essence and energies holds that the divine light perceived by the apostles on Mount Tabor was the uncreated energy of God. The Gnostic light-vesture, while superficially similar, operates within a dualistic framework that the Palamite theology explicitly rejects.
