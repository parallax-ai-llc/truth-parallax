The Eastern Orthodox Church does not accept Pistis Sophia as Scripture. The text is recognized as a product of Gnostic theology that, despite sharing certain vocabulary with Orthodox Christianity — repentance, light, mystery, baptism — operates within a fundamentally different theological framework. Orthodox scholars study the text as evidence for the diversity of early Christian movements and the challenges the patristic Fathers faced in defining orthodox doctrine.

Orthodox readers note that Sophia's thirteen repentances share the language but not the theology of Orthodox metanoia (repentance). In Orthodox spirituality, repentance is a turning of the whole person toward God within the sacramental life of the Church — confession, Eucharist, and ongoing ascetic practice. In Pistis Sophia, repentance is a cosmic drama in which a divine aeon cries out from chaos for rescue.

The text's elaborate system of mysteries and baptisms contrasts with the Orthodox liturgical tradition, which is grounded in the seven sacraments celebrated within the historical continuity of the Church. Orthodox scholars view the Gnostic sacramental system as an alternative that developed alongside but apart from the apostolic liturgical tradition preserved in Orthodoxy.
