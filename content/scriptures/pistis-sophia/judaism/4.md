The mysteries described in this chapter bear some structural resemblance to the ma'aseh merkavah (work of the chariot) and ma'aseh bereshit (work of creation), the esoteric traditions that rabbinic Judaism restricted to qualified practitioners. Both Gnostic and rabbinic systems distinguish between levels of knowledge and restrict access to the highest teachings. However, the content and purpose differ fundamentally: rabbinic esotericism explores the glory of God as Creator and King, while Gnostic mysteries provide escape routes from creation.

Jewish scholars also note the chapter's ethical dimensions, which parallel rabbinic emphasis on the integration of knowledge and righteous conduct. The Gnostic insistence that mysteries must be accompanied by moral behavior echoes the rabbinic principle that Torah study without good deeds is fruitless.
