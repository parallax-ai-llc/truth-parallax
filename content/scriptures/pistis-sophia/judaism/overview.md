Pistis Sophia is not a Jewish text and holds no place in Jewish tradition. Jewish scholars approach it as evidence for how Jewish Scripture — particularly the Psalms — and Jewish theological concepts — particularly Hokmah (Wisdom) — were appropriated by Gnostic communities in late antiquity. The text's systematic reinterpretation of canonical Psalms as expressions of a cosmic Sophia myth demonstrates the malleability of biblical texts in the hands of interpreters operating outside the rabbinic framework.

The most directly relevant aspect of Pistis Sophia for Jewish scholars is its treatment of the Psalms. Sophia's thirteen repentances are explicitly linked to specific Psalms, which are reread as expressions of the divine aeon's cosmic suffering. Jewish scholars note the radical decontextualization involved: Psalms written for Temple worship, personal lament, or communal prayer become vehicles for an alien cosmological narrative.

The figure of Pistis Sophia draws on the Jewish Hokmah tradition — personified Wisdom who participates in creation and calls humanity to understanding. But the Gnostic Sophia falls, suffers, and requires rescue, a trajectory foreign to the Wisdom of Proverbs 8, who is confident, creative, and secure in her relationship with God.
