The chapter's teaching on reincarnation has been compared by some scholars to the gilgul (transmigration of souls) tradition in Jewish mysticism, particularly in Kabbalah. While gilgul is a minority view within Judaism and is not found in the classical rabbinic period, its later development in Kabbalistic thought raises questions about possible shared sources or parallel developments between Gnostic and Jewish mystical traditions.

Jewish scholars generally maintain that the Kabbalistic doctrine of gilgul developed independently of Gnostic influence, drawing instead on internal Jewish resources and possibly Platonic philosophy. The Gnostic version in Pistis Sophia, however, frames reincarnation within a cosmological dualism foreign to Jewish thought: the goal is escape from embodiment rather than the tikkun (repair) that Kabbalistic gilgul envisions.
