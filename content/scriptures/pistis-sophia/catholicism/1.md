Catholic scholars examine Pistis Sophia chapter 1's post-resurrection setting in light of the Church's teaching on public revelation. Catholic doctrine holds that public revelation closed with the death of the last apostle, and that no new public revelation is to be expected. The extended post-resurrection teaching in Pistis Sophia — eleven years of secret instruction — represents precisely the kind of supplementary revelation that the Church has consistently excluded from the deposit of faith.

The light-vesture imagery, however, resonates with Catholic mystical traditions. The saints' visions of Christ in glory, the theology of the glorified body in Catholic eschatology, and the liturgical celebration of the Transfiguration all engage with the theme of divine radiance made visible. Catholic scholars note these resonances while maintaining the distinction between orthodox mystical theology and Gnostic speculation.
