Catholic eschatology and the Gnostic eschatology of this chapter diverge sharply on the question of reincarnation. The Catholic Church teaches that "it is appointed for man to die once, and after that comes judgment" (Hebrews 9:27), explicitly rejecting reincarnation. Pistis Sophia's teaching that souls cycle through multiple lives until they receive the mysteries represents a cosmological framework incompatible with Catholic doctrine.

However, Catholic scholars note that the chapter's moral seriousness — its insistence that actions in this life have consequences for the soul's destiny — resonates with Catholic moral theology. The detailed descriptions of punishments for various sins, while operating within a Gnostic cosmological framework, reflect a moral universe in which choices matter and accountability is real.
