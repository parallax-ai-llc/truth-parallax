Catholic scholarship treats Pistis Sophia as a significant but non-canonical Gnostic text. The Church does not accept its teachings as authoritative, but Catholic scholars study it for its contribution to understanding early Christian diversity, Gnostic soteriology, and the development of sacramental theology. The text's elaborate system of baptisms, seals, and mysteries invites comparison with Catholic sacramental practice, though the theological frameworks differ fundamentally.

Catholic scholars have explored the resonances between the Gnostic Sophia and Catholic Marian theology. Both figures represent a feminine dimension of salvation history: Sophia falls and is redeemed; Mary cooperates with grace and is exalted. However, Catholic theology locates Mary within the orthodox framework of Incarnation and redemption, whereas the Gnostic Sophia inhabits a dualistic cosmos.

The text's detailed sacramental descriptions — multiple baptisms, anointings, and seals — provide evidence that Gnostic communities developed liturgical practices parallel to but distinct from those of the emerging Catholic Church. Catholic scholars study these parallels to understand the diversity of early Christian worship.
