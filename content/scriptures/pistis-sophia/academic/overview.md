Academic scholarship on Pistis Sophia has focused on questions of dating, provenance, literary structure, and theological classification. The text is preserved in the Askew Codex (British Library Add. MS 5114), purchased in 1785. Carl Schmidt's critical edition established the scholarly foundation, and subsequent work by Violet MacDermot and others has refined the translation and analysis. The text is generally dated to the third century CE, though some scholars argue for portions dating to the late second century.

The relationship of Pistis Sophia to the major Gnostic schools remains debated. Unlike the clearly Sethian or Valentinian texts from Nag Hammadi, Pistis Sophia does not fit neatly into any single Gnostic system. Some scholars classify it as a late synthesis drawing on multiple Gnostic traditions. Others see it as representing an independent Gnostic tradition with its own distinctive mythology and ritual practice.

The text's portrayal of Mary Magdalene as Jesus's primary interlocutor and most insightful disciple has made Pistis Sophia a key document in scholarly discussions of women's roles in early Christianity. Karen King, Anne Pasquier, and other scholars have analyzed Mary's prominence as evidence for competing models of religious authority in early Christian communities.
