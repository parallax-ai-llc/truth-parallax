## Overview

The Dasavaikalika Sutra (Daśavaikālika Sūtra, "The Ten Evening Lectures") is one of the most important texts of Jain monastic literature, belonging to the Mula Sutras (root texts) of the Shvetambara canon. Traditionally attributed to Acharya Sayyambhava, it was composed as a concise manual of monastic discipline to be studied within ten evening sessions. It covers the essential rules of conduct, the nature of food and begging, the practice of austerities, and the mental disciplines required of a Jain monk or nun.

## Structure

The text is organized into 10 chapters (adhyayanas):

1. **Chapter 1 — The Noble Path**: The monk's commitment to non-violence and self-restraint
2. **Chapter 2 — Begging for Food**: Rules governing the collection and consumption of alms
3. **Chapter 3 — Carefulness in Walking**: Mindfulness to avoid harming living beings
4. **Chapter 4 — Carefulness in Speech**: Right speech and the avoidance of harmful words
5. **Chapter 5 — Carefulness in Receiving**: Proper acceptance of food and requisites
6. **Chapter 6 — Austerities**: The practice of physical and mental tapas
7. **Chapter 7 — The Three Controls**: Controlling mind, speech, and body
8. **Chapter 8 — Endurance**: Bearing hardship with equanimity
9. **Chapter 9 — The True Monk**: Qualities of an ideal ascetic
10. **Chapter 10 — Liberation**: The culmination of monastic practice in moksha

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Digambara**: Maintain parallel codes of monastic conduct with stricter requirements
- **Shvetambara**: Use it as a foundational text in monastic training
- **Academic**: Essential for understanding Jain monastic institutions
- **Comparative**: Parallels with Buddhist Vinaya rules and Hindu sannyasa traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Monastic Discipline | Detailed rules for daily conduct of monks and nuns |
| Non-Harm | Extreme care to avoid injuring any living being |
| Austerity | Fasting, endurance, and self-mortification as spiritual practices |
| Mindfulness | Constant awareness in every action |
| Begging Practice | The ethics and procedures of collecting alms |
