---
title: "Dasavaikalika - Chapter 4"
scripture: "dasavaikalika"
chapter: 4
---

**1** The monk should practise the restraint of the senses with unwavering diligence, for the senses are the gateways through which karma enters the soul.

**2** The sense of touch craves pleasant sensations and recoils from unpleasant ones. The monk should be equanimous toward both, accepting whatever comes without preference.

**3** The sense of taste craves delicious food and is repelled by unpleasant food. The monk should eat without attachment to taste, regarding food only as sustenance.

**4** The sense of smell craves pleasant fragrances and is repelled by unpleasant odours. The monk should be indifferent to both, not seeking the one nor avoiding the other.

**5** The sense of sight craves beautiful forms and is repelled by ugly ones. The monk should look upon all forms with equanimity, seeing the soul within, not the body without.

**6** The sense of hearing craves pleasant sounds and is repelled by harsh ones. The monk should listen with equal attention to praise and blame, to music and noise.

**7** The mind, the sixth sense, craves pleasant thoughts and is repelled by unpleasant ones. The monk should observe his thoughts without attachment, letting them come and go like clouds in the sky.

**8** The restraint of the senses does not mean the suppression of experience. It means the freedom from compulsive reaction to experience.

**9** The monk who has mastered his senses is free to experience the world without being bound by it. He sees, hears, smells, tastes, and touches, but he is not enslaved by these experiences.

**10** This freedom from sensory bondage is the fruit of long and patient practice. It is the gateway to the deeper levels of meditation and the higher stages of spiritual development.
