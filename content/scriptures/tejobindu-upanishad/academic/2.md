Section 2's analysis of the mind as a product of ignorance reflects a philosophical position more radical than most Upanishadic texts. While the principal Upanishads generally treat the mind as a real faculty that can be purified, the Tejobindu's denial of the mind's ultimate reality aligns more closely with the *ajativada* (non-origination) position of Gaudapada and the *sunyavada* (emptiness) of Mahayana Buddhism.

Scholars debate whether this radical position represents indigenous Upanishadic development or Buddhist influence. The philosophical sophistication of the analysis — treating the mind itself as an object of inquiry rather than an unquestioned subject — marks a significant advance in Indian philosophy of mind.
