## The Virtuous Ruler and Good Governance

Volume III provides the richest material for Confucian interpretation, as its more historical narratives of imperial governance align closely with the Confucian concern for ethical rulership, social harmony, and the moral cultivation of the sovereign.

### Emperor Nintoku as Confucian Ideal

The story of Nintoku observing the absence of cooking smoke and suspending taxation is the paradigmatic Confucian moment in the Kojiki. Tokugawa-era scholars cited this passage extensively as evidence that Japanese governance had always embodied the Confucian ideal of benevolent rule (*jinsei*), sometimes even surpassing Chinese exemplars.

### Poetry as Moral Cultivation

Confucian interpreters valued the poems of Volume III as expressions of cultivated sentiment. Following the Chinese tradition that poetry reveals character, they read the imperial *kayo* as windows into the moral state of each sovereign. Elegant, restrained poetry indicated virtuous rule; excessive or passionate verse suggested moral failing.

### Succession and Propriety

The succession disputes and fraternal conflicts recorded in Volume III are analyzed through the Confucian lens of proper ritual succession (*teisei*). When successions proceed smoothly according to established precedent, social harmony prevails; when they are disrupted by ambition or passion, disorder follows -- a pattern that Confucian readers traced through both Japanese and Chinese history.
