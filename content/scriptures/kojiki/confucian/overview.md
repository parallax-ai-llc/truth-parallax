## Overview

Confucian interpretation of the Kojiki reads the text through the lens of moral philosophy, governance ethics, and the cultivation of virtue. During the Tokugawa period (1603-1868), when Confucianism served as the dominant intellectual framework of the ruling class, scholars frequently interpreted Kojiki narratives using Confucian categories of filial piety, benevolent governance, and ritual propriety.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Moral Governance

Confucian readers emphasize the Kojiki's portrayal of exemplary rulers who govern with benevolence and wisdom. The virtuous reigns of emperors such as Nintoku, who famously observed smoke rising from his subjects' hearths to assess their welfare, are read as illustrations of Confucian ideals of compassionate leadership.

### Filial Piety and Loyalty

The narratives of imperial succession, sibling relationships, and ancestral veneration in the Kojiki are interpreted through the Confucian framework of the Five Relationships, particularly the bonds between ruler and subject, parent and child.

### Ritual and Propriety

The elaborate descriptions of court ceremonies, offerings, and diplomatic protocols in the later volumes align with the Confucian emphasis on *li* (ritual propriety) as the foundation of social harmony.
