## Heavenly Order and Moral Cosmology

Confucian interpreters of Kojiki Volume I read the creation narratives through the framework of moral cosmology. The spontaneous emergence of order from chaos, the hierarchical arrangement of kami, and the differentiation of heaven and earth all mirror the Confucian understanding of a morally ordered universe.

### Heaven and Earth as Moral Categories

In Confucian readings, the separation of the High Heavenly Plain (Takamagahara) from the terrestrial realm reflects the distinction between heavenly principle (*tenri*) and earthly manifestation. The descent of divine authority from heaven to earth parallels the Confucian concept of the Mandate of Heaven (*tenmei*).

### Izanagi and Izanami as Moral Exemplars

Confucian commentators read the relationship between Izanagi and Izanami through the lens of proper conjugal relations and ritual propriety. Izanami's transgression in speaking first during their marriage ceremony, which produces the malformed child Hiruko, is interpreted as a cautionary tale about the disruption of proper hierarchical order.

### The Problem of Amaterasu

The supreme authority of a female deity posed interpretive challenges for Confucian scholars who emphasized male precedence. Some resolved this tension by emphasizing Amaterasu's association with the virtue of brilliance and illumination rather than her gender, while others subordinated Shinto mythology to Confucian moral categories entirely.
