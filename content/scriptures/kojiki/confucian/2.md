## Governance, Loyalty, and the Moral Sovereign

Confucian readers of Volume II focus on the themes of legitimate governance, loyal service, and the moral qualities required of a true sovereign. The transition from divine to human rule is read as establishing the ethical framework for earthly kingship.

### Emperor Jimmu as Sage-King

Confucian interpreters cast Jimmu in the mold of the Chinese sage-kings (Yao, Shun, Yu), emphasizing his wisdom, virtue, and the providential signs that validated his rule. His eastward expedition is read not as military conquest but as the extension of civilizing virtue to benighted regions.

### Loyalty and Self-Sacrifice

The stories of loyal retainers who sacrifice themselves for their sovereigns in Volume II resonate strongly with Confucian ethics. The ideal of *chu* (loyalty to one's lord) finds vivid illustration in figures such as Oto Tachibana Hime, who calms the sea by offering herself so that Yamato Takeru may complete his mission.

### The Five Relationships

Confucian interpreters map the narratives of Volume II onto the Five Relationships: ruler-subject, parent-child, husband-wife, elder-younger sibling, and friend-friend. The text's accounts of succession disputes, fraternal rivalries, and marital alliances are all read as illustrating (positively or negatively) the proper fulfillment of these relational duties.
