Nationalist interpretation of the Kamiyo section insisted on its literal truth as a historical account of Japan's divine creation. Motoori Norinaga argued that the spontaneous generation of the kami and the creation of the Japanese islands by Izanagi and Izanami were actual events that demonstrated Japan's unique status among nations. The birth of Amaterasu, supreme among the kami, established Japan as the land of the sun goddess, superior to all other nations by virtue of its divine origin.

State Shinto ideology drew heavily on this chapter to construct the doctrine of kokutai — the sacred national polity centered on the emperor as a living descendant of Amaterasu. School textbooks presented the creation myths as factual history, and questioning them was treated as an offense against the state. The postwar reassessment of this tradition has led to a more nuanced understanding of how mythological narratives were transformed into political ideology, a process with parallels in other nationalist movements worldwide.
