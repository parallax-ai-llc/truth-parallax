Nationalist readings of the Izumo Cycle focused on the kuniyuzuri (land-cession) as a paradigm of willing submission to divinely ordained authority. Ōkuninushi's surrender of sovereignty to the envoys of Amaterasu was interpreted as demonstrating the natural and right order of Japanese political life — all regional powers properly subordinate to the central imperial authority descended from the heavenly kami.

This reading supported the centralization policies of both the ancient Ritsuryō state and the modern Meiji government. The construction of the massive Izumo Taisha shrine was cited as evidence that the Yamato court honorably rewarded submission, a model for the incorporation of diverse regional identities into a unified national identity under the emperor. Critical scholarship has since examined how this narrative of willing submission obscured historical realities of conquest and resistance.
