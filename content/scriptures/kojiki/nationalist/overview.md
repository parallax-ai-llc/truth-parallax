## Overview

The nationalist reading of the Kojiki, which reached its fullest development during the Edo period through the kokugaku (national learning) movement and was institutionalized during the Meiji through early Shōwa periods, treated the text as a literal account of Japan's divine origins. Scholars such as Motoori Norinaga (1730-1801) championed the Kojiki over the more Chinese-influenced Nihon Shoki, arguing that its language and mythology preserved the authentic spirit of ancient Japan untainted by foreign influence.

## Theological Emphases

This interpretive tradition emphasized the uniqueness of Japan as a divinely created nation (shinkoku), the unbroken imperial line descending from Amaterasu, and the concept of kokutai (national polity) as a sacred and eternal political order. During the period of State Shinto (1868-1945), these readings were promoted through the education system and served as ideological foundations for Japanese nationalism and imperial expansion.

## Interpretive Context

The postwar disestablishment of State Shinto and the emperor's renunciation of divinity in 1946 fundamentally altered the context for nationalist readings. Contemporary scholarship critically examines this interpretive tradition as a historical phenomenon, analyzing how mythological texts were mobilized for modern political purposes while also acknowledging the genuine philological contributions of kokugaku scholars.
