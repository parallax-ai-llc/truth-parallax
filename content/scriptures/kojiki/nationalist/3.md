The imperial lineage section was the most politically significant portion of the Kojiki for nationalist interpreters. The concept of bansei ikkei — the unbroken imperial line from Jimmu to the present — was treated as historical fact and as proof of Japan's unique national destiny. Emperor Jimmu's legendary founding of the state in 660 BCE was commemorated as National Foundation Day (Kigensetsu), and the three imperial regalia (mirror, sword, jewel) described in the text were venerated as sacred objects connecting the present emperor to the age of the gods.

During the period of State Shinto, the Kojiki's imperial genealogy served as the foundation for emperor worship (tennō sūhai) and the ideology of the emperor as a living kami (akitsumikami). The 1945 defeat and the emperor's subsequent declaration of humanity fundamentally challenged this reading, prompting a reassessment of the relationship between mythology, history, and political authority that continues in Japanese intellectual life today.
