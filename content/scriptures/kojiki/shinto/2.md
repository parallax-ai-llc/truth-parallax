The Izumo Cycle holds special significance in Shinto as the narrative of the earthly kami and their relationship to the heavenly kami. Ōkuninushi's building of the land (kuni-zukuri) and his eventual cession of authority to the descendants of Amaterasu is understood not as a conquest narrative but as the proper ordering of divine governance, in which the earthly kami willingly cooperate with the heavenly mandate.

Shinto shrines throughout the Izumo region, most notably Izumo Taisha, preserve the worship of Ōkuninushi and the kami of this cycle. The annual gathering of kami at Izumo in the tenth month (Kannazuki, the 'godless month' elsewhere in Japan) reflects the continuing ritual significance of these myths. The Izumo traditions represent a complementary stream of Shinto theology that honors the earthly, chthonic dimensions of kami activity alongside the celestial.
