Shinto interpretation of the Kamiyo narrative reads the creation of heaven and earth as the foundational act from which all kami, all land, and all life emerge. The spontaneous generation of the first kami in the plain of high heaven (Takamagahara) reveals the principle of musubi — the creative, generative force that is the very essence of divine activity. Each kami that comes into being represents a distinct aspect of reality, from growth and fertility to form and completion.

The account of Izanagi and Izanami standing upon the Floating Bridge of Heaven and stirring the primordial brine to create Onogoro-jima (the first island) is understood as the paradigmatic act of world-making. Their subsequent birth of the Japanese islands and the kami of nature establishes the sacred geography that Shinto ritual continues to honor. The death of Izanami and Izanagi's descent to and flight from the underworld introduces the fundamental themes of pollution (kegare) and purification (harae) that structure Shinto practice to this day.
