## Approaching Historical Record

Volume III of the Kojiki comes closest to conventional historical chronicle and is therefore of particular interest to academic historians. The narratives of imperial reigns from Nintoku onward can be partially corroborated by Chinese and Korean historical sources, providing external reference points for chronological reconstruction.

### Chronological Problems

One of the central academic debates concerns the Kojiki's chronology. The text assigns implausibly long reign-periods to early emperors, and the regnal dates do not align with those in the Nihon Shoki or with Chinese historical records. Scholars have proposed various corrective schemes, but no consensus has been reached.

### Korean Peninsula Relations

Volume III contains important references to diplomatic and military interactions with the Korean kingdoms of Silla, Baekje, and Goguryeo. Academic historians compare these accounts with Korean sources such as the Samguk Sagi to reconstruct a more balanced picture of ancient East Asian interstate relations.

### Textual Transmission

The oldest surviving manuscript of the Kojiki dates from 1371-1372 (the Shinpukuji manuscript). Academic study of textual variants between different manuscript lines reveals the processes of scribal transmission, editorial intervention, and interpretive glossing that shaped the received text over six centuries.
