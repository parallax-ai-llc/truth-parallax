## Source Analysis and Mythological Layers

Academic study of Kojiki Volume I focuses on identifying the multiple source traditions that were woven together to create the received text. Scholars recognize that O no Yasumaro drew upon diverse oral traditions, court genealogies, and regional myths to compose a unified narrative serving the political interests of the early eighth-century Yamato court.

### Cosmogonic Traditions

Textual analysis reveals at least two distinct creation traditions in Volume I. The first describes the spontaneous generation of kami in the High Heavenly Plain; the second narrates the creative activity of Izanagi and Izanami. These likely originated in different regional or ritual contexts before being harmonized in the Kojiki.

### Linguistic Evidence

The Kojiki's unique writing system, using Chinese characters for both their semantic and phonetic values, provides invaluable data for reconstructing Old Japanese phonology. Volume I preserves archaic vocabulary and grammatical forms that had already become obsolete by the Nara period, suggesting that some passages faithfully transmit very old oral traditions.

### Political Function

Academic interpreters emphasize that the myths of Volume I served to legitimate the Yamato imperial house by tracing its lineage to Amaterasu, the supreme deity. This political dimension does not diminish the text's religious or literary value, but it must be recognized as shaping the selection and arrangement of mythological material.
