## Everyday Mind as Sacred

Volume III's more worldly, historical narratives align with the Zen emphasis on finding awakening within ordinary experience. As the mythological recedes and the human comes to the foreground, Zen interpreters find an analogue to the teaching that "everyday mind is the Way" (*heijoshin kore michi*).

### Nintoku's Compassion as Bodhisattva Action

Emperor Nintoku's concern for his starving subjects, expressed through his willingness to forgo palace repairs and suspend taxation, is read through the lens of *karuna* (compassion) -- one of the two wings of awakening alongside wisdom. His attentive observation of the cooking smoke (or its absence) exemplifies the Zen virtue of careful, present-moment awareness applied to the welfare of all beings.

### Poetry as Direct Pointing

The numerous poems in Volume III are valued by Zen interpreters as examples of direct, non-conceptual expression -- *kyoge betsuden* (transmission outside the scriptures). Like the best Zen poetry, the Kojiki's *kayo* capture fleeting moments of beauty, sorrow, and insight in language that points beyond itself to immediate experience.

### The Uncarved Block

As the Kojiki narrative moves toward its conclusion, the decreasing intervention of supernatural forces can be read as pointing toward the Zen ideal of naturalness (*jinen*). The most profound sacredness is not found in spectacular divine manifestations but in the unadorned flow of human life lived with awareness, compassion, and sincerity.
