## Emptiness Before the Gods

Zen Buddhist interpreters approach Volume I of the Kojiki through the lens of *sunyata* (emptiness) and *tathata* (suchness). The primordial state before the appearance of kami is read not as mere nothingness but as the pregnant emptiness from which all phenomena spontaneously arise -- a direct parallel to the Buddhist understanding of dependent origination.

### Spontaneous Arising and Buddha-Nature

The appearance of the first three kami "of their own accord" (*hitorigami*) resonates with the Zen understanding that buddha-nature is not created or produced but simply reveals itself when conditions are appropriate. The kami do not create the world through deliberate action; the world unfolds through them as naturally as flowers bloom.

### Amaterasu's Cave as Zen Koan

The narrative of Amaterasu's withdrawal into the cave and subsequent emergence has been read as a natural koan. The world of darkness when the sun goddess hides parallels the darkness of ignorance; the mirror held before her as she emerges suggests the moment of self-recognition that constitutes awakening. One does not discover something new but sees what was always already present.

### Purification as Practice

Izanagi's purification after returning from Yomi -- washing away the pollutions of death in a flowing river -- resonates with Zen practice traditions that emphasize physical and mental cleansing as inseparable. The new kami born from this act of purification suggest that genuine practice transforms the practitioner at the deepest level.
