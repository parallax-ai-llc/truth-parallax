## Overview

Zen Buddhist interpretation of the Kojiki draws on the long history of *shinbutsu shugo* (the synthesis of kami and buddhas) that characterized Japanese religion for over a millennium before the Meiji-era separation of Shinto and Buddhism. From a Zen perspective, the Kojiki's mythological narratives can be read as pointing toward fundamental insights about the nature of mind, reality, and awakening.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Honji Suijaku Framework

The medieval *honji suijaku* ("original ground, manifest traces") theory held that kami are local manifestations of universal buddhas and bodhisattvas. Under this framework, Amaterasu was identified with Dainichi Nyorai (Mahavairocana), and other Kojiki kami were mapped onto the Buddhist pantheon.

### Non-Dual Reading

Zen interpreters approach the Kojiki's dualities -- Izanagi and Izanami, heaven and earth, light and darkness -- as expressions of the non-dual nature of reality. The apparent oppositions dissolve upon deeper contemplation, much as form and emptiness are recognized as inseparable in the Heart Sutra.

### Spontaneous Arising

The Kojiki's account of kami arising spontaneously in the primordial void resonates with the Zen understanding of reality as self-arising and self-displaying, beyond causation in the ordinary sense.
