## Overview

Philosophical interpretation of the Kojiki approaches the text as a repository of Japanese ontological and cosmological thought. Rather than treating the myths as literal historical accounts or purely religious narratives, this tradition reads the Kojiki as expressing fundamental principles about the nature of reality, existence, and the relationship between the visible and invisible worlds.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Motoori Norinaga's Mono no Aware

Motoori Norinaga (1730-1801) pioneered the philosophical reading of the Kojiki through his landmark commentary *Kojiki-den*. He argued that the text reveals an authentic Japanese sensibility (*mono no aware*) that precedes and transcends Chinese philosophical categories.

### Ontology of Musubi

Philosophical interpreters focus on *musubi* (generative, creative power) as the foundational principle of Kojiki cosmology. The spontaneous generation of kami from the primal chaos suggests an ontology where creative becoming, rather than static being, is the ground of reality.

### Nature and Cosmos

The Kojiki's depiction of the natural world as inherently sacred and alive resonates with process philosophy and ecological thought. Mountains, rivers, and natural phenomena are not mere objects but living presences with their own agency.
