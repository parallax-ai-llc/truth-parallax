## History as Philosophical Reflection

Volume III of the Kojiki moves furthest from pure mythology toward what resembles historical chronicle. Philosophical interpreters read this transition as the text's own reflection on the nature of historical understanding and the fading of direct divine presence in human affairs.

### The Ethical Emperor

The reign of Emperor Nintoku, who upon seeing no smoke rising from his subjects' homes suspended taxation for three years, presents a philosophical ideal of governance grounded in empathetic perception. The ruler's primary virtue is not power but attentiveness to the condition of the people.

### Poetry and Philosophical Expression

Volume III contains numerous poems (*kayo*) exchanged between emperors, consorts, and courtiers. These lyric moments interrupt the narrative prose and offer philosophical reflections on impermanence, longing, and the beauty of the natural world. They represent a distinctly Japanese mode of philosophical expression through aesthetic sensibility.

### The Thinning of the Sacred

As the narrative progresses, divine interventions become less frequent and human agency more prominent. This pattern suggests a philosophical understanding of history as the gradual "thinning" of direct sacred presence, requiring new modes of relating to the divine through ritual, poetry, and moral cultivation.
