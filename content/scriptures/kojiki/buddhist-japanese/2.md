Buddhist interpreters read Ōkuninushi's career in the Izumo Cycle as a bodhisattva narrative. His compassion toward the white hare of Inaba, his endurance of trials imposed by Susanoo, and his patient labor in building the land all reflect the bodhisattva virtues of compassion (karuṇā), perseverance (kṣānti), and skillful means (upāya). His voluntary cession of the land to the heavenly kami was read as the supreme act of selfless generosity (dāna).

The Izumo shrines that honored Ōkuninushi were integrated into Buddhist temple-shrine complexes (jingūji) during the medieval period. Buddhist monks served alongside Shinto priests, and the kami of Izumo were given Buddhist names and identities. This syncretic practice, though disrupted by Meiji-era policies, left a deep imprint on the religious culture of the region and on how the Izumo myths are understood in popular piety.
