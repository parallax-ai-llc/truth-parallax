Japanese Buddhist tradition read the imperial lineage recorded in the Kojiki through the concept of the dharma-protecting king (gohō no ō). The emperors were understood not only as descendants of the kami but as rulers whose legitimacy was confirmed by their protection and promotion of the Buddhist dharma. Several emperors recorded in the latter portion of the Kojiki were celebrated in Buddhist tradition for their patronage of temples and their personal practice.

The concept of ōbō buppō sōi (the mutual dependence of imperial law and Buddhist law) provided the framework within which Buddhist thinkers understood the political theology of the Kojiki. The divine mandate described in the text was reinterpreted as a mandate to protect the dharma, and the prosperity or decline of imperial reigns was read as reflecting the merit or demerit accumulated through support of Buddhist institutions.
