## Overview

Japanese Buddhist interpretation of the Kojiki developed primarily through the honji suijaku (original ground, manifest trace) framework, which understood the kami described in the text as local manifestations of universal buddhas and bodhisattvas. This syncretic reading allowed Buddhist thinkers to honor the Kojiki's narratives while situating them within a broader Buddhist cosmological framework.

## Theological Emphases

Medieval Buddhist commentators read the creation myths as expressions of dependent origination and the interplay of form and emptiness. The generation and destruction described in the Kamiyo section were interpreted through the lens of impermanence (mujō), while the suffering of Izanami in the underworld resonated with Buddhist teachings on the realms of existence and the cycle of rebirth.

## Interpretive Context

This tradition of reading reached its fullest expression in the Ryōbu Shinto and Sannō Shinto schools, which produced elaborate correspondences between the Kojiki's divine genealogies and Buddhist mandala structures. Though the Meiji-era separation of Shinto and Buddhism disrupted these interpretive traditions, they continue to inform Japanese religious consciousness.
