The third volume of the Kojiki presents a literary challenge as it transitions from myth to chronicle. The narrative becomes more episodic, organized around individual reigns, yet the text maintains its literary character through the embedded songs that punctuate the genealogical framework. These songs — love poems, war songs, elegies, and drinking songs — preserve a range of human emotion and social occasion that enlivens the dynastic record.

The story of Yamato Takeru stands out as the volume's greatest literary achievement. His tragic career — a warrior prince sent on impossible missions by a father who fears him, disguising himself as a woman to defeat enemies, falling in love with a priestess, and dying far from home transformed into a white bird — combines heroic epic with personal tragedy in a way that deeply moved later Japanese readers. His death poem, lamenting the beautiful land of Yamato visible from a distance, became one of the most celebrated passages in Japanese literature and established the topos of longing for home that pervades the tradition.
