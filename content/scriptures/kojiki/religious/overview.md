## Overview

The religious interpretation of the Kojiki treats the text as sacred scripture (shinten) within the Shinto tradition. For Shinto practitioners and priests, the Kojiki is not merely a literary or historical artifact but a living record of divine revelation that informs ritual practice, shrine worship, and spiritual understanding.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Liturgical Reading

Within shrine Shinto, the Kojiki's narratives are recited, dramatized, and reenacted in matsuri (festivals) throughout the year. The myths of Amaterasu's emergence from the cave, for example, inform the structure of kagura performances.

### Kami Theology

The religious tradition reads the Kojiki as revealing the nature and hierarchy of kami. Each narrative establishes the character, domain, and proper veneration of specific kami enshrined across Japan's approximately eighty thousand shrines.

### Purification and Restoration

The narrative of Izanagi's purification after visiting Yomi (the land of the dead) provides the mythological foundation for misogi and harae rituals that remain central to Shinto practice.

## Sectarian Perspectives

| Tradition | Emphasis |
|-----------|----------|
| Shrine Shinto | Kami enshrined and worshipped based on Kojiki narratives |
| Kokugaku | Recovery of pure Shinto uncontaminated by foreign influence |
| Sect Shinto | Various movements drawing on Kojiki for doctrinal authority |
| State Shinto (historical) | Imperial mythology as national ideology |
