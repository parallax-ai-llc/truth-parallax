## From Myth to History: A Comparative View

Volume III's transition from mythological to quasi-historical narrative presents an opportunity for comparing how different cultures negotiate the boundary between sacred time and historical time.

### The Problem of Euhemerization

Volume III's more human-scale narratives raise the question of euhemerization -- the process by which historical figures are elevated to divine status, or divine figures are rationalized as historical persons. Comparative scholars note similar processes in Greek tradition (the hero cults), Roman history (divine emperors), and Hindu tradition (the avataras).

### Royal Poetry Traditions

The exchange of poems between rulers and courtiers in Volume III parallels similar traditions in other court cultures: the Arabic tradition of court poetry, the troubadour tradition of medieval Europe, and the Sanskrit *kavya* tradition of Indian courts. In each case, poetic exchange serves as a medium for political negotiation, romantic communication, and the display of cultural refinement.

### Divine Kingship in Decline

The gradual diminishing of supernatural elements in Volume III mirrors patterns seen in other traditions where an original age of gods gives way to an age of heroes and finally to an age of ordinary humans. This descending pattern appears in Hesiod's ages of man, the Hindu yugas, and numerous other traditions that imagine history as a progressive distancing from divine origins.
