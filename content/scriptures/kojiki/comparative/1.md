## Universal Patterns in Creation Mythology

Volume I of the Kojiki presents creation narratives that invite extensive comparison with cosmogonic myths from cultures worldwide. Comparative scholars identify both structural parallels that suggest universal patterns in human mythmaking and distinctive features that mark the Kojiki's cosmogony as uniquely Japanese.

### The Primordial Chaos

The Kojiki's description of the original state as resembling "an ocean of oil" that "floated about like a jellyfish" finds parallels in numerous traditions: the watery chaos of Mesopotamian Tiamat, the primordial ocean of Egyptian Nun, and the cosmic waters of Vedic cosmogony. The motif of solid land emerging from liquid chaos appears to be one of the most widespread cosmogonic patterns.

### The Divine Pair

Izanagi and Izanami as creative siblings who generate the world through their union parallel the Maori Rangi and Papa, the Egyptian Geb and Nut, and numerous other divine pairs. The motif of the failed first attempt (Hiruko) followed by successful creation appears in other traditions as well.

### The Withdrawn Sun Deity

Amaterasu's retreat into the cave, plunging the world into darkness, parallels the disappearing-deity motif found in Hittite mythology (Telepinu), Norse mythology (Balder's death), and Egyptian mythology (Ra's withdrawal). The communal celebration that lures the deity back suggests a ritual substrate underlying the myth.
