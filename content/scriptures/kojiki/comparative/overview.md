## Overview

Comparative interpretation of the Kojiki places the text alongside creation myths, sacred histories, and cosmogonic narratives from cultures worldwide. This approach reveals both universal patterns in human mythmaking and the distinctive features of the Japanese mythological imagination.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Cosmogonic Parallels

The Kojiki's creation narrative -- in which the cosmos emerges from primordial chaos through the spontaneous generation of deities -- invites comparison with creation myths from Polynesia, Mesopotamia, Greece, and India. The sibling pair Izanagi and Izanami who stir the cosmic waters find parallels in numerous traditions.

### Descent-to-the-Underworld Motif

Izanagi's journey to Yomi to retrieve Izanami closely parallels the myths of Orpheus and Eurydice, Inanna's descent to the underworld, and Persephone's abduction. The motif of the broken taboo (looking back) appears across cultures as a way of explaining the finality of death.

### Divine Kingship

The Kojiki's narrative of Amaterasu's grandson descending to rule the terrestrial realm (tenson korin) parallels divine-kingship ideologies in Egypt, Mesopotamia, and Mesoamerica. The comparison illuminates how mythological narratives legitimate political authority.

## Comparative Framework

| Motif | Kojiki | Parallel Tradition |
|-------|--------|-------------------|
| Creation pair | Izanagi-Izanami | Rangi-Papa (Maori), Geb-Nut (Egypt) |
| Sun deity | Amaterasu | Ra (Egypt), Sol (Norse) |
| Underworld descent | Izanagi to Yomi | Orpheus (Greek), Inanna (Sumerian) |
| Storm god vs. sun | Susanoo vs. Amaterasu | Set vs. Horus (Egypt), Thor (Norse) |
