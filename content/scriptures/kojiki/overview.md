## Overview

The Kojiki (古事記, "Record of Ancient Matters") is the oldest surviving chronicle of Japan, compiled in 712 CE by O no Yasumaro at the command of Empress Gemmei. It stands as the foundational text of Shinto and the primary source for Japanese mythology, cosmogony, and early imperial history.

## Structure

The Kojiki is organized into three volumes:

1. **Volume I: Age of the Gods** (Kamiyo no Maki): The creation of the cosmos, the divine generations, and the myths of Izanagi, Izanami, Amaterasu, and Susanoo
2. **Volume II: Age of Heroes** (Nakatsumaki): The transition from divine to human rule, from Emperor Jimmu through Emperor Ojin
3. **Volume III: Human Age** (Shimotsumaki): The reigns from Emperor Nintoku through Empress Suiko, blending myth with historical record

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Kojiki occupies a unique position across multiple interpretive traditions:

- **Shinto**: The primary scriptural source for understanding kami, ritual, and sacred narrative
- **Confucian**: Read through the lens of moral governance and filial piety during the Tokugawa period
- **Buddhist/Zen**: Interpreted alongside Buddhist cosmology in medieval Japan
- **Academic**: A critical text for comparative mythology and ancient Japanese linguistics

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Musubi | The creative, generative power underlying all existence |
| Kami | Divine beings pervading the natural and spiritual worlds |
| Imperial Lineage | The unbroken descent from Amaterasu to the imperial house |
| Purification | Misogi and harae as restoration of cosmic harmony |
| Land and Sovereignty | The intimate connection between kami, land, and rulership |
