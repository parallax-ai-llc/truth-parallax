## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox tradition classifies the Gospel of Truth as a Valentinian Gnostic text that falls outside the canon of Scripture as defined by the ecumenical councils and the patristic consensus. The Fathers of the Church — Irenaeus, Hippolytus, Clement of Alexandria, and Epiphanius — engaged extensively with Valentinian thought, and their refutations form part of the living tradition that shapes Orthodox theology. The Gospel of Truth is studied as a historical artifact that illuminates the challenges the early Church faced in preserving the apostolic deposit of faith.

## Theosis and the Problem of Gnosis

Orthodox theologians note both superficial resemblances and deep divergences between the Gospel of Truth and Orthodox mystical theology. Both traditions speak of return to God, divine knowledge, and the overcoming of ignorance. However, Orthodox *theosis* (deification) is rooted in the incarnation of Christ — God becomes human so that humans might become divine (Athanasius, *On the Incarnation*) — while the Gospel of Truth offers a disembodied gnosis that bypasses the incarnation's redemptive significance. The Orthodox emphasis on liturgical participation, bodily resurrection, and the sacramental transformation of matter stands in sharp contrast to the text's implicit rejection of materiality.

## Patristic Legacy

The Orthodox reception of the Gospel of Truth is mediated through the patristic response to Valentinianism. Irenaeus's insistence on the "rule of faith," apostolic succession, and the fourfold Gospel canon emerged in direct response to Valentinian claims of secret tradition and alternative scriptures. In this sense, the Gospel of Truth played an indirect but significant role in shaping the Orthodox understanding of authority, tradition, and the boundaries of the faith.
