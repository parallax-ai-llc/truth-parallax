## Rest in the Father

The concluding chapter of the Gospel of Truth envisions the final rest of pneumatic beings in the Father's Pleroma — a state of complete knowledge and union that resolves the cosmic deficiency. Orthodox eschatology shares the hope for ultimate rest in God but frames it very differently. The Orthodox vision of the eschaton includes the bodily resurrection of all the dead, the transfiguration of the material cosmos, and the eternal liturgy of heaven — not the dissolution of the material world and the reabsorption of spiritual beings into a divine fullness.

The Orthodox tradition's emphasis on cosmic transfiguration — rooted in Romans 8:19-21 and the liturgical celebration of Pascha — affirms the ultimate value of the material creation. The icons, the liturgy, the consecrated bread and wine — all point toward a future in which matter itself is transfigured by divine glory, not abandoned or dissolved. The Gospel of Truth's vision of rest in the Pleroma, while spiritually appealing, ultimately devalues the material order that Orthodox theology insists will be renewed and glorified. The Orthodox hope is not escape from creation but the transfiguration of all creation in the light of the risen Christ.
