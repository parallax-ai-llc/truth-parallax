## The Word of the Father

Chapter 2 presents Jesus as the Word of the Father who reveals the hidden God. Orthodox Christology, defined at the Councils of Nicaea (325), Constantinople (381), Ephesus (431), and Chalcedon (451), affirms that Christ is the eternal Logos — fully God and fully human, one person in two natures, "without confusion, without change, without division, without separation." The Gospel of Truth's Christology, while employing Logos language, tends toward docetism — it emphasizes Christ's revelatory function while diminishing the reality and significance of his human nature and suffering.

The Orthodox tradition insists that the incarnation — the Word becoming flesh — is not merely a vehicle for revelation but the means of humanity's salvation. Through the incarnation, Christ unites divine and human nature, healing what was broken in the fall and opening the way to theosis. The Gospel of Truth's image of Jesus on the cross as the "fruit of knowledge" reinterprets Calvary as a revelatory rather than redemptive event. Orthodox theology maintains that the cross is both: Christ reveals the Father's love precisely through his self-offering for the salvation of the world. The separation of revelation from redemption that characterizes the Gospel of Truth's Christology is foreign to the Orthodox theological synthesis.
