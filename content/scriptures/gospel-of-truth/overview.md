## Overview

The Gospel of Truth is a Valentinian Gnostic text discovered among the Nag Hammadi codices (NHC I,3 and XII,2) in 1945. Many scholars identify it with the work of the same name attributed to Valentinus by Irenaeus (*Against Heresies* 3.11.9), which would make it one of the few surviving texts by the founder of Valentinian Gnosticism, composed in Rome around 140-180 CE. The text is not a gospel in the narrative sense but a meditative homily on the nature of ignorance, the saving work of Jesus as the Word of the Father, and the return of all beings to the divine Pleroma.

## Structure

The Gospel of Truth unfolds as a sustained theological meditation in four movements:

1. **The Gospel of Joy** — The Father and the Deficiency: how ignorance (Error) arose and why the gospel is "good news"
2. **The Word of the Father** — Jesus as the revealer who brings knowledge of the Father
3. **Knowledge and Return** — The fog of Error dispersed through gnosis
4. **Rest in the Father** — The final return to the Pleroma and eternal rest

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Gospel of Truth is significant across multiple interpretive traditions:

- **Gnosticism**: Possibly the earliest surviving Valentinian text and a window into Valentinus's own thought
- **Christianity**: Evidence of sophisticated theological reflection in second-century Christianity
- **Judaism**: Engages with Wisdom traditions and the Hebrew concept of the divine Name
- **Academic**: Central to debates about the relationship between Gnosticism and early orthodoxy

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Error/Ignorance | The root cause of suffering — not sin but lack of knowledge of the Father |
| The Father | The transcendent source of all being, hidden by the fog of Error |
| The Word | Jesus as the living book and utterance of the Father |
| Pleroma | The divine fullness to which all emanated beings must return |
| Knowledge (Gnosis) | The saving recognition of one's origin in and belonging to the Father |
