## Overview

Protestant scholars approach the Gospel of Truth as a non-canonical text that, while historically illuminating, carries no theological authority for the Christian faith. The Reformation commitment to *sola scriptura* places this text firmly outside the boundaries of authoritative revelation. Nevertheless, Protestant scholarship has been at the forefront of Nag Hammadi studies, and the Gospel of Truth is valued as a primary source for understanding the theological landscape of second-century Christianity and the challenges that prompted the formation of the New Testament canon.

## Theological Assessment

From a Protestant standpoint, the Gospel of Truth presents a vision of salvation fundamentally at odds with the biblical gospel. Where the canonical writings proclaim salvation by grace through faith in the atoning work of Christ (Ephesians 2:8-9), the Gospel of Truth offers salvation through knowledge of the Father — a form of spiritual enlightenment that bypasses the cross. The text's understanding of sin as ignorance rather than moral rebellion, and its implicit denial of the goodness of creation, conflict with core Protestant convictions drawn from Scripture.

## Contributions to Historical Understanding

Despite these theological objections, Protestant historians and New Testament scholars recognize the Gospel of Truth as an invaluable window into early Christian diversity. The text demonstrates the sophistication of Valentinian thought and helps explain why figures like Irenaeus devoted such energy to refutation. It also enriches understanding of how early Christians read and interpreted the Hebrew Bible and the emerging New Testament writings.
