## Knowledge and Return

Chapter 3 describes salvation as the dispersal of the "fog of Error" through knowledge of the Father. Protestant theology affirms that knowing God is essential to the Christian life (Jeremiah 9:23-24, John 17:3), but it insists that saving knowledge is not an intellectual achievement or mystical awakening — it is the gift of the Holy Spirit who enables sinners to trust in Christ (1 Corinthians 12:3, Ephesians 2:8-9). The chapter's account of salvation through interior illumination, while resonant with certain aspects of the Reformed doctrine of effectual calling, lacks the framework of grace, faith, and repentance that structures Protestant soteriology.

The metaphor of "return" to the Father echoes the parable of the prodigal son (Luke 15:11-32), but in the canonical parable the return involves repentance, confession, and the father's forgiveness — moral categories that are absent from the Gospel of Truth's account. Protestant interpreters note that the text's elimination of moral categories from salvation (replacing sin with ignorance, repentance with recognition, forgiveness with awakening) produces a soteriology that addresses a different problem than the one diagnosed by the canonical Scriptures.
