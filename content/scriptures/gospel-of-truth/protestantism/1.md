## The Gospel of Joy

The opening chapter of the Gospel of Truth proclaims the "good news" that the hidden Father can be known, identifying ignorance rather than sin as the fundamental human predicament. Protestant theology, grounded in the canonical Scriptures, diagnoses the human condition differently. Romans 3:23 teaches that "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" — the problem is moral rebellion, not epistemological deficit. While Scripture affirms that knowledge of God matters (John 17:3), this knowledge is inseparable from repentance, faith, and the atoning work of Christ.

The chapter's personification of Error as a cosmic force that produces the material world through ignorance stands in tension with the Protestant affirmation of creation as the good and purposeful work of the sovereign God (Genesis 1:31, Psalm 19:1). Protestant interpreters note that the text's redefinition of the "gospel" — from the proclamation of Christ's death and resurrection for sinners (1 Corinthians 15:1-4) to the revelation of the Father's hidden nature — represents a fundamental departure from the apostolic message. The chapter's theology, while intellectually sophisticated, substitutes Valentinian speculation for biblical proclamation.
