## The Word of the Father

Chapter 2 presents Jesus as the Word of the Father whose primary function is to reveal hidden knowledge. Protestant Christology, while affirming Jesus as the revelation of God (John 1:18, Hebrews 1:1-3), insists that his role as revealer cannot be separated from his work as redeemer. The Heidelberg Catechism, the Westminster Confession, and other Protestant confessions teach that Christ fulfills the threefold office of prophet, priest, and king — revelation is one dimension of his work, not its totality.

The chapter's treatment of the crucifixion as a revelatory event rather than a substitutionary atonement diverges from a central tenet of Protestant soteriology. The Reformers — Luther, Calvin, and their successors — emphasized that Christ's death on the cross was the satisfaction of divine justice and the propitiation for human sin (Romans 3:25, 1 John 2:2). The Gospel of Truth's image of Jesus as the "fruit of the knowledge of the Father" — inverting the Tree of Knowledge — is theologically creative but subordinates the atoning significance of the cross to a Gnostic hermeneutical framework that the New Testament does not support.
