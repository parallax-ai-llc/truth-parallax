## Rest in the Father

The concluding chapter of the Gospel of Truth describes the final rest of the pneumatic beings in the Father's Pleroma — a state of complete knowledge and union that dissolves the deficiency. Protestant eschatology anticipates the return of Christ, the resurrection of the dead, the final judgment, and the new creation (Revelation 21-22). While the concept of "rest" resonates with the biblical language of sabbath rest (Hebrews 4:9-11), the Protestant understanding is concrete and corporate: the redeemed people of God dwell in a renewed creation, not in a disembodied divine fullness.

The chapter's universalist tendency — its suggestion that all pneumatic beings will inevitably return to the Father — raises questions about the biblical teaching on judgment and the possibility of eternal separation from God. Protestant theology has historically affirmed the reality of final judgment (Matthew 25:31-46, 2 Thessalonians 1:5-10), though some Protestant theologians have argued for universal reconciliation. The Gospel of Truth's serene confidence that the Father's will for reunion will be accomplished lacks the note of moral urgency that characterizes the canonical call to repentance and faith.
