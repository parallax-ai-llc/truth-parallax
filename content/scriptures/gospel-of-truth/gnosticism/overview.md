## Overview

The Gospel of Truth holds a place of exceptional importance within the Gnostic tradition, as it is widely believed to be the work of Valentinus himself — the most influential Gnostic teacher of the second century. Irenaeus mentions a "Gospel of Truth" used by Valentinians (*Against Heresies* 3.11.9), and the Nag Hammadi text's content aligns closely with what patristic sources report about Valentinus's theology. If this identification is correct, the text offers an unmediated encounter with the thought of Gnosticism's most sophisticated theologian.

## The Theology of Error

Central to the Gnostic reading of this text is the personification of Error (*plane*) as a quasi-cosmic force that arises from ignorance of the Father. Error is not a moral failing but an ontological condition — a "fog" that obscures the Aeons' knowledge of their origin. The gospel (good news) is precisely the dissipation of this fog through the revelation brought by Jesus, the Word of the Father. Valentinian communities understood this text as articulating the fundamental human predicament: not sin but forgetfulness, not guilt but disorientation.

## Pleroma and Return

The Gospel of Truth describes the drama of emanation and return that structures Valentinian cosmology. All beings originate in the Father's Pleroma (fullness), but through Ignorance and Error they experience deficiency. The saving work of Christ is to reveal the Father's name and nature, enabling the Aeons — and by extension, the pneumatic (spiritual) members of the Valentinian community — to remember their origin and return to the Pleroma. This return is not a future eschatological event but a present awakening through gnosis.
