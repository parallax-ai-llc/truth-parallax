## The Word of the Father

Chapter 2 presents Jesus as the Word (*Logos*) of the Father — the one who reveals the hidden God to those trapped in ignorance. For Gnostic readers, Jesus is not primarily a sacrificial victim who atones for sin but a revealer who brings the saving knowledge of the Father. The text describes him as a "living book" — the embodiment of divine truth that Error sought to destroy but could not, because the truth he carries is indestructible.

The chapter's treatment of the crucifixion is characteristic of Valentinian Christology. Jesus is "nailed to a tree" and becomes "fruit of the knowledge of the Father" — a deliberate inversion of the Genesis narrative in which the fruit of the Tree of Knowledge brought death. In the Valentinian reading, Jesus on the cross is the Tree of Knowledge fulfilled: his death is not punishment for sin but the occasion for the ultimate revelation. Those who "eat" this fruit receive gnosis rather than condemnation. The crucifixion thus becomes a revelatory event rather than an atoning sacrifice.

The concept of Jesus as the "living book" reflects the Valentinian understanding of Scripture itself. The canonical Scriptures are written books that can be read but not fully understood without the key of gnosis. Jesus is the living interpretation — the one who opens the sealed book (cf. Revelation 5) and makes its meaning accessible to those with pneumatic capacity. This hermeneutical Christology is distinctive to the Valentinian tradition and shapes the entire Gospel of Truth's approach to Scripture and knowledge.
