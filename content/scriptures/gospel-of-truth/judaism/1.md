## The Gospel of Joy

The opening chapter of the Gospel of Truth describes the Father as hidden and unknowable, and identifies ignorance of the Father as the source of Error and cosmic deficiency. Jewish theology engages with the theme of divine hiddenness (*hester panim*) — Isaiah 45:15 declares "Truly you are a God who hides himself, O God of Israel, the Savior" — but the Jewish understanding differs fundamentally from the Valentinian account. In Jewish thought, God's hiddenness is relational and historical (a response to human sin or a test of faith), not ontological (an inherent quality of the divine nature that requires special revelation to overcome).

The chapter's concept of "deficiency" — a lack in being that produces the material world — stands in direct tension with the Jewish doctrine of creation. The opening verse of the Torah, "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" (Genesis 1:1), affirms that the Creator is the supreme and only God, and that creation is an act of sovereign will, not a consequence of cosmic ignorance. Jewish scholars note that the Valentinian system, while drawing on Jewish concepts of divine transcendence and hiddenness, inverts the Jewish affirmation that creation is purposeful and good.
