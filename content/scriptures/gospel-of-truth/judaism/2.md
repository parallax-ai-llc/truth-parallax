## The Word of the Father

Chapter 2 presents Jesus as the Word (*Logos*) of the Father — a concept with deep roots in Jewish tradition. The Aramaic Targumim use the term *Memra* (Word) as a way of speaking about God's active presence in the world, and Philo of Alexandria developed an elaborate Logos theology that mediated between Jewish monotheism and Greek philosophy. The Gospel of Truth's use of Logos language thus draws on a Jewish tradition that was subsequently appropriated by both Christian and Gnostic writers.

Jewish scholars note that the text's description of Jesus as a "living book" echoes the Jewish reverence for Torah as the embodiment of divine wisdom and the blueprint of creation. The Midrash teaches that God looked into the Torah and created the world (Genesis Rabbah 1:1). However, the Valentinian identification of a human figure (Jesus) as the living Word represents a departure from Jewish theology, which does not personify the Word in a single human being. The chapter's reinterpretation of the crucifixion as the fruit of the Tree of Knowledge engages with Genesis in a way that is creative but unrecognizable from the perspective of rabbinic exegesis.
