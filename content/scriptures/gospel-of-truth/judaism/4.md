## Rest in the Father

The concluding chapter of the Gospel of Truth describes the final rest of pneumatic beings in the Father's Pleroma. The concept of rest resonates deeply with the Jewish tradition of Shabbat — the weekly sabbath that anticipates the ultimate rest of the messianic age. The Talmud describes the world to come (*olam ha-ba*) as "a day that is entirely Shabbat and rest for eternal life" (Mishnah Tamid 7:4). The Gospel of Truth's eschatological vision of rest thus draws on Jewish sabbath theology, though it transforms it substantially.

Jewish eschatology envisions the messianic age as a time of universal peace, justice, and knowledge of God — not the dissolution of the material world into a divine fullness. The prophets describe a renewed earth where "the wolf will live with the lamb" (Isaiah 11:6) and "nation will not take up sword against nation" (Isaiah 2:4). This vision is concrete, historical, and this-worldly, in contrast to the Gospel of Truth's otherworldly Pleroma. Jewish scholars note that the Valentinian appropriation of sabbath language for a non-material eschatology represents a spiritualization of Jewish hope that strips it of its historical and communal character.
