## Knowledge and Return

Chapter 3 describes salvation as the dispersal of Error's fog through knowledge (*gnosis*) of the Father, enabling the lost to return to their origin. The Jewish concept of *teshuvah* (repentance/return) provides an intriguing parallel: the prophets repeatedly call Israel to "return" to God (Hosea 14:2, Jeremiah 3:12-14), and rabbinic literature develops *teshuvah* as a fundamental spiritual practice. The Hebrew word *da'at* (knowledge) also plays a central role in Jewish theology — "the earth will be filled with the knowledge of the Lord" (Isaiah 11:9).

However, the Jewish understanding of return is moral and covenantal, not cosmological. *Teshuvah* involves repentance for sin, confession, and the commitment to righteous action — it is directed toward restoring the covenant relationship between God and Israel, not toward dissolving the material world or returning to a pre-cosmic divine fullness. Jewish scholars note that the Gospel of Truth's replacement of moral categories (sin, repentance, forgiveness) with epistemological ones (ignorance, knowledge, awakening) represents a fundamental transformation of Jewish concepts into something foreign to the Jewish theological tradition.
