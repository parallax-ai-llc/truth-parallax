## Overview

The Gospel of Truth is not a Jewish text and occupies no place in the Jewish canon or liturgical tradition. However, Jewish scholars have taken interest in the text because of its deep engagement with Jewish theological concepts — particularly the divine Name, Wisdom (Sophia) theology, and the language of hiddenness and revelation that pervades Second Temple Jewish literature. The text's Valentinian framework transforms these Jewish elements into something foreign to rabbinic Judaism, but the transformation itself is instructive for understanding the complex relationship between Judaism and early Gnosticism.

## Jewish Themes Transformed

The Gospel of Truth's central metaphor — that the Father is hidden and must be revealed through his Name — resonates with Jewish traditions about the ineffable Name of God (*Shem ha-Meforash*) and the theology of divine hiddenness found in Isaiah and the Psalms. The text's description of Jesus as the "living book" echoes Jewish reverence for Torah as the embodiment of divine wisdom. Yet these parallels are ultimately inverted: where Judaism affirms the Creator God as the supreme and good deity, the Gospel of Truth implicitly devalues the created order as a product of deficiency and Error.

## Scholarly Contributions

Jewish scholars, including Gershom Scholem and Gedaliahu Stroumsa, have explored the hypothesis that certain Gnostic ideas — including those found in the Gospel of Truth — may have roots in heterodox Jewish speculation of the Second Temple period. The discovery of the Nag Hammadi texts has enriched this discussion, providing primary evidence for the ways in which Jewish concepts were adopted, transformed, and sometimes inverted by Gnostic teachers. The Gospel of Truth remains a significant text for understanding the Jewish matrix from which early Christianity and its Gnostic variants emerged.
