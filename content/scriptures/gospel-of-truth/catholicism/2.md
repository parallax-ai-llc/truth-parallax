## The Word of the Father

Chapter 2 presents Jesus as the Word (*Logos*) of the Father — a designation that resonates with the Prologue of John's Gospel (John 1:1-18) and the Catholic Christological tradition. The Church affirms that Jesus is the eternal Word made flesh, consubstantial with the Father (Council of Nicaea, 325), who reveals the Father and redeems humanity through his incarnation, death, and resurrection. The Gospel of Truth's Christology, while employing similar language, reduces Jesus's role to that of revealer, diminishing the salvific efficacy of the cross.

Catholic soteriology teaches that Christ's death is a true sacrifice — the offering of himself for the redemption of sin (Council of Trent, Session XXII). The Gospel of Truth's image of Jesus as the "fruit of knowledge" on the tree reinterprets the crucifixion as a revelatory event rather than an atoning sacrifice, conflicting with the Church's teaching that "without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness" (Hebrews 9:22). The Catholic tradition affirms both the revelatory and redemptive dimensions of Christ's work; the Gospel of Truth retains only the former.
