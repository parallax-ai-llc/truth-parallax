## Overview

The Catholic Church regards the Gospel of Truth as a heterodox Valentinian composition that was rightly excluded from the canon of Scripture. Irenaeus of Lyon, whose *Against Heresies* (c. 180 CE) remains the foundational Catholic response to Gnosticism, specifically mentions a "Gospel of Truth" produced by the followers of Valentinus and condemns it as a departure from apostolic faith. The rediscovery of this text at Nag Hammadi confirmed Irenaeus's account and vindicated the patristic judgment that Valentinian theology distorted the Christian message.

## Doctrinal Concerns

Catholic theologians identify several points of fundamental incompatibility between the Gospel of Truth and Catholic doctrine. The text's understanding of evil as ignorance rather than the result of free will contradicts the Council of Trent's teaching on original sin. Its implicit docetism — the suggestion that Christ's suffering was revelatory rather than truly redemptive — conflicts with the Catholic dogma of the salvific efficacy of the cross. The absence of any ecclesial structure, apostolic succession, or magisterial authority in the text's vision of salvation through private knowledge stands in tension with Catholic ecclesiology.

## Scholarly Engagement

Catholic scholars have nonetheless made important contributions to the study of the Gospel of Truth. Raymond Brown, Hans-Martin Schenke, and others have analyzed the text's Christology, its use of Johannine language, and its relationship to developing Catholic sacramental theology. The Gospel of Truth's meditative, homiletic style has also been compared to the writings of Church Fathers like Clement of Alexandria, suggesting that the boundaries between "orthodox" and "Gnostic" were sometimes more porous than later polemics imply.
