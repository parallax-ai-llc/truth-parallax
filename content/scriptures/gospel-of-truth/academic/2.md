## The Word of the Father

Chapter 2 develops the text's Christology, presenting Jesus as the Word (*Logos*) of the Father and the "living book" that reveals divine knowledge. Scholars have analyzed this section's extensive intertextuality with the New Testament — particularly the Gospel of John (Logos theology), Colossians (the image of the invisible God), and Hebrews (the word of God as living and active). The question of whether the Gospel of Truth directly depends on canonical texts or draws on shared traditions remains debated.

The chapter's treatment of the crucifixion has generated significant academic discussion. The image of Jesus on the cross as the "fruit of the knowledge of the Father" inverts the Genesis narrative of the Tree of Knowledge, and scholars have analyzed this as an example of "counter-reading" — a hermeneutical strategy in which a text deliberately reverses the meaning of its source. Bentley Layton and Birger Pearson have compared this strategy to similar inversions in other Gnostic texts. The chapter's implicit docetism — its emphasis on revelation over suffering — has also been examined in relation to the broader spectrum of early Christian Christologies, from the "low" Christology of the Synoptic Gospels to the "high" Christology of John and the Valentinians.
