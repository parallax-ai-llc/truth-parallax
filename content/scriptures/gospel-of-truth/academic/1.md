## The Gospel of Joy

The opening chapter of the Gospel of Truth introduces the text's central theological argument: the Father is hidden, ignorance of the Father produces Error, and the gospel is the joyful announcement that this Error can be overcome. Scholars have noted the rhetorical sophistication of this opening — the text begins not with a mythological narrative (as in the Apocryphon of John) but with a theological meditation that presupposes the Valentinian myth without narrating it. Harold Attridge has analyzed the prose style as evidence of a highly educated author employing techniques of Hellenistic rhetoric.

The concept of Error (*plane*) as a personified force has attracted considerable scholarly attention. Christoph Markschies and Einar Thomassen have debated whether Error in the Gospel of Truth is a true hypostasis — an independent cosmic entity — or a personification of a condition (ignorance). The answer has implications for understanding the text's relationship to other Valentinian systems, in which Error's role is played by Sophia's fall or the Demiurge's ignorance. The chapter's concept of "deficiency" (*hysterema*) also connects to broader Valentinian technical vocabulary and has been analyzed in relation to Platonic concepts of privation and Middle Platonic theories of emanation.
