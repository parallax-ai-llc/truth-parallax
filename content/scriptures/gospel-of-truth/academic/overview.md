## Overview

The Gospel of Truth is among the most studied texts from the Nag Hammadi library, attracting scholarly attention from philologists, historians of religion, theologians, and literary critics. The text survives in two Coptic manuscripts (NHC I,3 and a fragmentary version in NHC XII,2), both translated from a lost Greek original. Academic investigation focuses on questions of authorship, date, literary structure, theological content, and the text's relationship to other Valentinian sources and to the emerging New Testament canon.

## The Authorship Debate

The central scholarly question is whether Valentinus himself composed the Gospel of Truth. Irenaeus mentions a Valentinian text by this name (*Against Heresies* 3.11.9), and the text's theology is consistent with what patristic sources attribute to Valentinus. Christoph Markschies has argued cautiously in favor of Valentinian authorship, while others note that the text could have been composed by a later member of the school. If genuinely by Valentinus, the Gospel of Truth would be one of the very few surviving texts by any named Gnostic teacher, making it an unparalleled primary source.

## Literary and Rhetorical Analysis

Scholars have noted the Gospel of Truth's distinctive literary quality. Unlike many Nag Hammadi texts, which present mythological narratives or dialogues, this text reads as a meditative homily or theological essay, employing sophisticated rhetorical techniques including chiasmus, metaphor, and paradox. Harold Attridge and George MacRae have analyzed its prose style as evidence of a highly educated author steeped in both Hellenistic rhetoric and Jewish-Christian scriptural traditions. The text's seamless integration of Johannine language, Pauline themes, and Platonic concepts makes it a rich case study in early Christian intellectual culture.
