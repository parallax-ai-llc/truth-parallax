---
title: "Yoga Kundalini Upanishad Chapter 3"
scripture: "yoga-kundalini-upanishad"
chapter: 3
---

**1** When the Kundalini reaches the Sahasrara and unites with Shiva, the Yogi experiences the state of Samadhi. In this state, the breath stops, the mind is dissolved, and only pure awareness remains.

**2** There are three types of Samadhi. The first is Savikalpa Samadhi, in which the consciousness of "I am meditating" remains. The second is Nirvikalpa Samadhi, in which all duality is dissolved. The third is Sahaja Samadhi, the natural and permanent state of absorption.

**3** In Sahaja Samadhi, the Yogi acts in the world but remains established in the state of union. He eats, he walks, he speaks, but his consciousness is ever fixed in Brahman. This is the highest attainment, the state of the Jivanmukta, the one who is liberated while living.

**4** The obstacles to the attainment of Samadhi are enumerated: disease, laziness, doubt, carelessness, sloth, worldliness, delusion, non-attainment of a stage, and instability. These nine obstacles must be overcome through persistent practice and detachment.

**5** The signs of the Kundalini's ascent are described: heat at the base of the spine, involuntary movements of the body, visions of light, hearing of inner sounds, feelings of bliss, and the spontaneous arising of knowledge.

**6** The Upanishad warns: "The practice of Kundalini Yoga should not be undertaken without the guidance of a qualified Guru. The premature or improper awakening of the Kundalini can cause physical and mental disturbance."

**7** The Khechari Mudra is described: "By turning the tongue backward and placing it in the cavity above the palate, the Yogi seals the flow of nectar from the Bindu and attains mastery over hunger, thirst, old age, and death."

**8** When the Prana enters the Sushumna and the mind is dissolved, the Yogi experiences the Anahata sounds. Ten sounds are heard in succession: the sound of a bell, a conch, a lute, a flute, a drum, thunder, a bee, a waterfall, a vina, and finally the sound of Om.

**9** The Yogi should focus on each sound as it arises, allowing the mind to merge with the sound. By this practice, the mind is absorbed in the sound, and the sound leads the mind to the silence beyond all sound.

**10** The Upanishad teaches the nature of the liberated soul: "The Jivanmukta is beyond the three Gunas — Sattva, Rajas, and Tamas. He is beyond pleasure and pain, beyond virtue and vice. He is established in the Self, which is beyond all duality."

**11** "For the Jivanmukta, the body continues to exist, sustained by the momentum of past karma, like a potter's wheel that continues to spin after the potter's hand is removed. But no new karma is created, for the Jivanmukta acts without ego."

**12** The Upanishad concludes: "This teaching of Kundalini Yoga is the secret of all secrets. It is the direct path to liberation. He who practices it with devotion, under the guidance of a Guru, shall attain the supreme state of Brahman. Om Tat Sat."
