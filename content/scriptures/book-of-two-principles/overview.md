## Overview

The *Liber de Duobus Principiis* (Book of Two Principles) is the most systematic theological treatise produced by the Cathar movement, attributed to the Italian Cathar theologian John of Lugio (Giovanni di Lugio) of the Desenzano community and composed around 1240–1260 CE. The text survives in a single Latin manuscript (Florence, Biblioteca Nazionale, Conventi Soppressi I.II.44) and was first published by Father Antoine Dondaine in 1939. It represents the "absolute dualist" position within Catharism — the view that two co-eternal, independent principles (good and evil) have existed from the beginning, in contrast to the "mitigated dualist" view that evil arose as a secondary rebellion within a single divine order.

The work is structured as a scholastic argument, employing logical reasoning and extensive biblical exegesis to demonstrate that the God of goodness and light cannot be the author of evil, suffering, and the material world. John of Lugio engages directly with Catholic theological objections and constructs a rigorous philosophical defense of absolute dualism, drawing on both testaments of the Christian Bible.

## Structure

| Chapter | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | On Free Will | Argues that true free will is incompatible with a single omnipotent creator, since an all-powerful good God would not create beings capable of choosing evil |
| 2 | On the Two Principles | Establishes the core thesis: two co-eternal principles — one of good, one of evil — are necessary to explain the existence of suffering and moral corruption |
| 3 | On the Evil Principle | Describes the nature, power, and domain of the evil principle and its role in creating the material world |
| 4 | On Signs and Arguments | Presents logical proofs and scriptural evidence for absolute dualism, engaging with Catholic counter-arguments |
| 5 | On Creation | Distinguishes between true creation (spiritual, from the good God) and fabrication (material, from the evil principle) |
| 6 | On the Soul and Its Fall | Explains how divine souls became trapped in material bodies through the machinations of the evil principle |
| 7 | On Salvation and the End | Describes the ultimate liberation of all trapped souls and the final separation of the two realms |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Cathar**: The foundational theological text of absolute dualist Catharism, providing the intellectual framework for the movement's most radical claims
- **Academic**: Essential primary source for the study of medieval heresy, Latin scholastic method outside the Catholic mainstream, and the intellectual history of dualism in the West
- **Comparative**: Invites comparison with Zoroastrian Zurvanism, Manichaean cosmology, and Gnostic demiurgical traditions
- **Catholic**: Read as the principal theological challenge that prompted inquisitorial and mendicant responses, including Thomas Aquinas's treatments of evil and the Albigensian Crusade
- **Gnostic**: Examined for its continuities and discontinuities with ancient Gnostic dualism, particularly Marcionite and Manichaean traditions
- **Interfaith**: Raises the universal theological problem of theodicy — how to reconcile divine goodness with the reality of suffering

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Absolute Dualism | Two co-eternal, independent principles (good and evil) govern reality |
| Theodicy | The existence of evil is explained by attributing it to a separate, uncreated principle rather than to divine permission |
| Anti-Materialism | The physical world is the creation of the evil principle and is inherently corrupt |
| Scriptural Argument | Extensive use of both Old and New Testaments to support dualist conclusions |
| Scholastic Method | Systematic logical argumentation modeled on contemporary Catholic scholasticism |
| Soteriology | Salvation is the liberation of divine souls from material imprisonment |
