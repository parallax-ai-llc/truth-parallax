## Overview

For Cathar readers, the *Liber de Duobus Principiis* is the definitive theological statement of absolute dualism — the conviction that two independent, co-eternal principles, one of perfect goodness and one of radical evil, have existed from the beginning and will exist forever. This position, championed by the Italian Cathar community of Desenzano under the theological leadership of John of Lugio, represents the most intellectually developed form of Cathar doctrine.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### The Impossibility of a Single Source

The central argument is logical: a perfectly good, omnipotent God cannot be the source of evil. If God is truly good, God would not create beings capable of choosing evil. If God is truly omnipotent, God could prevent evil. Since evil manifestly exists, either God is not good, not omnipotent, or not the sole principle of reality. The Cathar answer is the third option: evil has its own independent source.

### The Two Creations

The Cathar tradition distinguishes between true creation (the spiritual realm, created by the good God) and fabrication (the material world, produced by the evil principle). The visible world — with its suffering, decay, and death — bears the unmistakable signature of its malevolent author.

### Soteriological Implications

If the material world is the product of the evil principle, then salvation consists in the soul's liberation from matter. The consolamentum — the Cathar sacrament of spiritual baptism — is the ritual means by which this liberation is initiated, freeing the divine soul from its material prison.
