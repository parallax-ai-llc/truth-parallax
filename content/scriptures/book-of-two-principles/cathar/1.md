## Free Will and the Cathar Dilemma

The opening chapter of the *Liber de Duobus Principiis* addresses the problem of free will with a logical rigor that reflects John of Lugio's engagement with contemporary scholastic philosophy. The argument proceeds as follows: if God is omnipotent and perfectly good, then God could have created beings incapable of sin. If God chose to create beings capable of sin, then either God desired sin (which contradicts divine goodness) or God could not prevent sin (which contradicts divine omnipotence). In either case, the existence of free will as traditionally understood is incompatible with a single omnipotent, benevolent creator.

For Cathar readers, this argument was not merely philosophical but existential. The suffering they witnessed — poverty, disease, the violence of the Albigensian Crusade itself — demanded an explanation more satisfying than the Catholic appeal to mysterious divine providence. The Cathar answer was clear: free will as the source of evil is an insufficient explanation. Evil is too pervasive, too structural, too deeply embedded in the fabric of material existence to be the result of individual moral choices. It must have a cosmic source.

The chapter establishes the methodological foundation for everything that follows: rigorous logical argument, engagement with scriptural texts, and the systematic dismantling of the Catholic position. John of Lugio writes not as a mystic but as a theologian, meeting his Catholic opponents on their own intellectual ground.
