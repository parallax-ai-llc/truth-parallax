## Free Will and the Gnostic Perspective

The opening chapter's treatment of free will resonates with ancient Gnostic analyses of the human condition. Like the *Liber de Duobus Principiis*, many Gnostic texts argue that the misery of human existence cannot be explained by the misuse of divinely bestowed freedom but must be attributed to a deeper cosmological cause — the entrapment of divine sparks in a world created by an ignorant or malevolent being.

The Apocryphon of John describes the demiurge Yaldabaoth creating human beings and then trapping the divine spirit (breathed into Adam by Sophia) within material bodies. The Valentinian system describes the passions of Sophia generating the material world as a byproduct of her fall from the Pleroma. In both cases, the problem of evil is solved not by appeal to human freedom but by appeal to a cosmic drama that precedes and determines the human condition.

The *Liber de Duobus Principiis* shares this structural move while differing in important details. Where ancient Gnostic systems typically derive the evil creator from the divine fullness (as a fallen emanation), the Cathar system posits the evil principle as independently co-eternal with the good God. This represents a more radical dualism than most ancient Gnostic texts endorse, though it parallels the Manichaean system more closely.
