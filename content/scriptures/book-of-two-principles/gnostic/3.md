## The Evil Creator and Anti-Cosmic Theology

The third chapter's description of the evil principle as the creator of the material world participates in what Hans Jonas called "anti-cosmic theology" — the systematic devaluation of the created order that characterizes both ancient Gnosticism and medieval Catharism. In both traditions, the material world is not a fallen but fundamentally good creation; it is the product of a fundamentally hostile power.

The anti-cosmic impulse finds its most dramatic expression in the Gnostic texts that describe the material world as a prison, a tomb, or a place of drunkenness and forgetfulness. The Gospel of Thomas declares: "Whoever has come to know the world has found a corpse" (Logion 56). The Apocryphon of John describes the archons creating human bodies as "fetters of oblivion." The *Liber de Duobus Principiis* participates in this tradition, though it expresses it in philosophical rather than mythological language.

For readers versed in ancient Gnosticism, the *Liber* demonstrates the persistence of anti-cosmic theology across a millennium of Western religious history. Whether this persistence reflects historical transmission (through Manichaean, Paulician, and Bogomil channels) or the independent recurrence of anti-cosmic intuitions in response to similar existential conditions remains one of the most debated questions in the history of religions.
