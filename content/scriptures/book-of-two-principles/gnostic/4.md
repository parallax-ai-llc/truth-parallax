## Scriptural Exegesis: Gnostic and Cathar Methods Compared

The fourth chapter's use of scriptural texts to support dualist conclusions invites comparison with ancient Gnostic hermeneutical practices. Both traditions developed sophisticated methods for reading the Bible against its apparent intention — finding evidence of a divided godhead, a malevolent creator, and a hidden spiritual truth beneath the literal surface of the text.

Marcion's distinction between the wrathful God of the Old Testament and the loving Father revealed by Jesus provides the most obvious ancient parallel to the Cathar hermeneutic. Valentinian exegesis, as preserved in texts like the Gospel of Philip and the Tripartite Tractate, employed allegorical methods to discover the Pleroma, the fall of Sophia, and the demiurgical creation within the canonical scriptures. Sethian texts reinterpreted Genesis narratives, making the serpent a hero and the creator God a villain.

The *Liber de Duobus Principiis* employs a somewhat different method: rather than allegorizing, it takes the biblical text at face value and argues that the plain sense of certain passages supports dualism. This literalist approach to dualist exegesis is distinctive and may reflect the influence of scholastic hermeneutical training on Cathar intellectuals.
