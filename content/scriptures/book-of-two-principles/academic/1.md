## Free Will in Scholastic Context

The opening chapter's treatment of free will situates the *Liber de Duobus Principiis* within the broader context of thirteenth-century scholastic debate. The problem of reconciling divine omnipotence with creaturely freedom was not unique to Cathar theology; it occupied major Catholic thinkers including Boethius, Anselm, Peter Lombard, and (shortly after the composition of the *Liber*) Thomas Aquinas. John of Lugio's engagement with this problem demonstrates his familiarity with the scholastic method and his ability to deploy it against the Catholic position.

The chapter's logical structure — thesis, objection, counter-objection, resolution — mirrors the *quaestio* form standard in contemporary university theology. This has led scholars to hypothesize that John of Lugio (or his intellectual circle) had access to scholastic education, possibly through defection from Catholic religious orders or through the cosmopolitan intellectual culture of northern Italian city-states.

The historical significance of this chapter lies in what it reveals about Cathar intellectual life. The common image of Cathars as simple, uneducated folk preachers is contradicted by the sophistication of this text. The Cathar movement, at least in its Italian absolute dualist wing, produced theologians capable of engaging Catholic scholasticism on its own terms.
