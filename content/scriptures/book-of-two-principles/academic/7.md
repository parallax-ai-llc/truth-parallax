## Eschatology and the Final Separation

The concluding chapter's eschatological vision provides scholars with evidence of a distinctively Cathar understanding of the end times — one that differs fundamentally from Catholic eschatology. Where Catholic theology anticipates the resurrection of the body, the last judgment, and the renewal of creation (Romans 8:21, Revelation 21:1), the Cathar eschatology envisions a complete separation of the spiritual and material realms.

This eschatological difference reflects the deeper cosmological divide between the two traditions. If the material world is God's good creation, then its renewal and perfection is the logical culmination of salvation history. If the material world is the evil principle's fabrication, then its dissolution and the liberation of trapped souls is the only possible happy ending.

The chapter's treatment of Christ as a spiritual (rather than incarnate) being connects the *Liber* to the broader docetic tradition in Christianity — the view that Christ's body was an appearance rather than a reality. This christological position was condemned by the Catholic Church from the earliest centuries (1 John 4:2-3, the Johannine letters' insistence that Jesus came "in the flesh"), but it persisted in various forms among dualist movements throughout the medieval period.
