## Biblical Hermeneutics and Cathar Exegesis

The fourth chapter offers scholars a rare window into Cathar biblical interpretation. The *Liber de Duobus Principiis* demonstrates that Cathar communities maintained active engagement with the Christian Bible, interpreting it through a dualist lens that was both internally consistent and radically different from Catholic exegetical tradition.

The hermeneutical principles at work are revealing. The Cathar exegete reads the Old Testament as a document of the evil principle's self-revelation — the God of Genesis, Exodus, and the prophets is identified with the evil creator. The New Testament, particularly the Johannine and Pauline writings, is read as the revelation of the true, good God through Christ. This hermeneutical strategy has obvious parallels with Marcion's second-century distinction between the God of the Old Testament and the Father of Jesus, though the Cathar system is more consistently dualist.

The chapter's engagement with Catholic counter-arguments provides evidence of actual disputations between Cathar and Catholic theologians. The *Liber* responds to specific objections — for instance, the Catholic argument from Romans 9:19-21 (God as potter) — suggesting that the text was composed in dialogue with real opponents rather than as a purely theoretical exercise.
