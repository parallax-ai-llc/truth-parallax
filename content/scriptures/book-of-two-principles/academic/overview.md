## Overview

The *Liber de Duobus Principiis* is the most substantial theological treatise produced by the medieval Cathar movement and one of the few surviving texts written by Cathars themselves (as opposed to the far more numerous inquisitorial and polemical sources produced by their opponents). Its discovery and publication by Antoine Dondaine in 1939 transformed the study of Catharism by providing direct access to Cathar theological reasoning.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Manuscript and Attribution

The text survives in a single Latin manuscript (Florence, Biblioteca Nazionale, Conventi Soppressi I.II.44), bound together with the Latin Cathar Ritual and other anti-heretical materials. The attribution to John of Lugio of Desenzano is based on internal evidence and the testimony of Raynier Sacconi, a former Cathar turned Dominican inquisitor, who identifies John as the leading theologian of the absolute dualist faction.

### Absolute vs. Mitigated Dualism

The text represents the absolute dualist position within Catharism — the view that two co-eternal, independent principles govern reality. This stands in contrast to the mitigated dualism of other Cathar communities (particularly Concorezzo), which held that the evil principle was a fallen angel subordinate to the one true God. The internal debate between absolute and mitigated dualists is one of the most important features of Cathar theological development.

### Scholastic Method

Remarkably, the *Liber de Duobus Principiis* employs the scholastic method of argumentation standard in contemporary Catholic theology — positing theses, marshaling authorities, addressing objections, and drawing conclusions. This demonstrates that Cathar intellectuals were trained in the same educational milieu as their Catholic opponents.
