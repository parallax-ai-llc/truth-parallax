## Overview

From a comparative perspective, the Book of Two Principles represents one of the most systematic expressions of cosmic dualism in Western religious history. The conviction that reality is governed by two independent, opposing principles — one good, one evil — is a recurring response to the problem of suffering across cultures and centuries.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Zoroastrian Dualism

The most obvious comparison is with Zoroastrianism, which posits two primordial spirits: Spenta Mainyu (the beneficent spirit) and Angra Mainyu (the destructive spirit). In the Zurvanite variant, both spirits are offspring of Zurvan (Time), making them co-eval if not strictly co-eternal. The structural parallel with Cathar absolute dualism is striking, though direct historical influence remains debated.

### Manichaean Cosmology

Manichaeism, founded by Mani in third-century Persia, elaborated the most complex dualist system in religious history — a cosmic drama of Light and Darkness, with the material world as the battleground. The question of whether Catharism represents a historical continuation of Manichaeism (through Paulician and Bogomil intermediaries) or an independent reinvention of dualist themes remains one of the most debated questions in medieval religious history.

### The Problem of Evil

The *Liber de Duobus Principiis* addresses what is perhaps the most universal problem in theology: how to reconcile divine goodness with the reality of evil. Every theistic tradition must confront this question, and the solutions range from free-will theodicy (mainstream Christianity, Judaism, Islam) to karma (Hinduism, Buddhism) to dualism (Zoroastrianism, Manichaeism, Catharism).
