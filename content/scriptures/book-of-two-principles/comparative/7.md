## The End of All Things: Eschatological Visions

The concluding chapter's vision of the final separation of spirit and matter invites comparison with eschatological visions across the world's religions. Every major tradition offers some account of how the present order will end and what will replace it, and these accounts reveal fundamental differences in cosmological and soteriological orientation.

The biblical tradition of apocalyptic eschatology (Daniel, Revelation) envisions the destruction of the present order and its replacement by a renewed creation in which God's will is perfectly realized. The Hindu concept of *pralaya* (cosmic dissolution) describes the periodic dissolution and re-creation of the universe within infinite cycles of time. The Buddhist concept of *parinirvana* describes the complete liberation of all beings from the cycle of rebirth. The Norse *Ragnarok* envisions the destruction and rebirth of the world through cosmic battle.

The Cathar eschatology is distinctive in its finality: the separation of spirit and matter is permanent. There is no renewal of creation, no resurrection of the body, no reintegration of the two realms. The material world does not end and begin again; it ends absolutely, and the souls trapped within it return permanently to their spiritual home. This vision reflects the absolute character of Cathar dualism: if spirit and matter are products of opposed principles, their ultimate separation is the only possible resolution.
