## Scriptural Interpretation and Religious Authority

The fourth chapter's deployment of biblical texts to support dualist conclusions illustrates a phenomenon common across religious traditions: the capacity of sacred texts to support radically different, even contradictory, interpretations depending on the hermeneutical framework brought to bear.

In Islam, the Quran has been interpreted to support both Ash'ari determinism and Mu'tazili free will, both Sufi mysticism and Salafi literalism. In Hinduism, the same Upanishadic texts have been read to support both Shankara's non-dualism (Advaita) and Ramanuja's qualified non-dualism (Vishishtadvaita). In Buddhism, the sutras have been interpreted to support both the gradualist path of Theravada and the sudden awakening of certain Zen traditions.

The Cathar exegesis of the Bible thus illustrates a universal hermeneutical principle: texts do not interpret themselves. The meaning of a sacred text depends on the community that reads it, the questions it brings, and the theological framework within which it operates. The *Liber de Duobus Principiis* demonstrates that the Christian Bible, read through a dualist lens, yields dualist conclusions — just as, read through a Catholic lens, it yields Catholic ones.
