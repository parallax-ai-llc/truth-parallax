## Cosmic Dualism: A Cross-Cultural Pattern

The chapter's assertion of two co-eternal principles places it within one of the most persistent patterns in the history of religion: cosmic dualism. The conviction that reality is governed by two opposing forces — variously characterized as light and darkness, good and evil, order and chaos — appears across widely separated cultures, suggesting a deep structural feature of human religious imagination.

Zoroastrian dualism (Ahura Mazda vs. Angra Mainyu) provides the most systematic ancient parallel. Manichaean cosmology (the Realm of Light vs. the Realm of Darkness) offers the most elaborate mythological development. Chinese yin-yang philosophy presents a complementary rather than antagonistic dualism. Navajo cosmology's First Man and First Woman represent another form of primal duality.

The comparative question is why dualism recurs so persistently. One answer is experiential: human beings encounter the world as divided — light and dark, hot and cold, pleasure and pain, life and death — and religious cosmology reflects this experienced duality. Another answer is logical: dualism offers a cleaner solution to the problem of evil than monotheism, which must explain how a single good God permits suffering.
