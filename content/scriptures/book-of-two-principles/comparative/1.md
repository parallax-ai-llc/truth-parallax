## Free Will and Determinism Across Traditions

The opening chapter's engagement with the problem of free will places the *Liber de Duobus Principiis* within a cross-cultural philosophical debate that spans millennia. The tension between divine sovereignty and human freedom is not unique to Christianity but appears in virtually every theistic tradition, generating a spectrum of responses from hard determinism to radical libertarianism.

In Islamic theology, the Ash'ari and Mu'tazili schools debated divine predestination (*qadar*) and human responsibility with an intensity comparable to the Christian debates. In Hindu philosophy, the Bhagavad Gita's treatment of dharmic duty in the context of cosmic determinism raises parallel questions. In Buddhist philosophy, the doctrine of dependent origination (*pratityasamutpada*) offers a non-theistic alternative that dissolves the free will problem by rejecting both the sovereign creator and the autonomous agent.

The Cathar solution — attributing the problem to a dualized cosmos — is distinctive in its radicality but not in its concern. Every tradition that affirms both divine goodness and the reality of evil must somehow account for their coexistence, and the range of solutions reveals fundamental differences in theological anthropology and cosmology.
