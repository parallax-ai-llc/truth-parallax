## The Personification of Evil

The third chapter's description of the evil principle as an active, willful, creative force invites comparison with personifications of evil across world religions. The tendency to attribute evil to a personal cosmic agent — rather than to impersonal forces, human error, or divine inscrutability — appears in many traditions, though the agent's nature and status vary widely.

In Zoroastrianism, Angra Mainyu (later Ahriman) is a personal being with will, intention, and creative power. In Christianity, Satan is a fallen angel — powerful but subordinate to God. In Islam, Iblis is a jinn who refused God's command to bow to Adam. In Hindu mythology, the *asuras* (anti-gods) represent cosmic forces opposed to the *devas* (gods). In Buddhism, Mara is the personification of desire and death who tempted Siddhartha under the Bodhi tree.

The *Liber de Duobus Principiis*' evil principle is more radical than any of these: a being co-eternal with God, not subordinate or derived but fundamentally independent. This represents the extreme end of the spectrum of evil personification — a point at which the personification becomes a full-fledged cosmological principle.
