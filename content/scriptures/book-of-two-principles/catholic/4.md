## Scriptural Authority and Catholic Hermeneutics

The fourth chapter's use of biblical texts to support dualism exemplifies the hermeneutical challenge that Catharism posed to Catholic biblical interpretation. The passages cited by John of Lugio — "the whole world lies in the power of the evil one" (1 John 5:19), "the god of this world" (2 Corinthians 4:4), "my kingdom is not of this world" (John 18:36) — are part of the canonical New Testament, and their dualist potential cannot be dismissed.

The Catholic hermeneutical response proceeds on several fronts. First, Catholic exegesis insists on reading individual passages within the context of the whole canon — a principle known as the *analogia fidei* (analogy of faith). Passages that speak of "the god of this world" must be read alongside passages that affirm God's sovereignty over all creation (Psalm 24:1, Colossians 1:16). Second, Catholic interpretation distinguishes between the present, provisional "rule" of evil (which exists by divine permission) and the ultimate sovereignty of God, which will be fully manifested at the eschaton.

The *Liber de Duobus Principiis*' exegetical challenge thus forced Catholic theologians to develop more nuanced hermeneutical tools — a process that contributed to the maturation of medieval biblical scholarship and ultimately to the systematic hermeneutics of later Catholic theology.
