## Free Will and Catholic Anthropology

The opening chapter's argument against free will under a single omnipotent creator strikes at the heart of Catholic anthropology. The Catholic tradition, following Augustine, affirms that human beings are created with genuine freedom — the capacity to choose between good and evil — and that this freedom is a gift of God, not a defect in creation. The abuse of this freedom, not a cosmic evil principle, is the source of moral evil in the world.

Thomas Aquinas's treatment of free will in the *Summa Theologiae* (I-II, qq. 6-21) provides the most systematic Catholic response to the type of argument the *Liber* advances. For Aquinas, free will is not a burden imposed on humanity by an indifferent or incompetent God but the very condition of genuine love. Love that is compelled is not love; virtue that is forced is not virtue. The possibility of evil is the necessary condition for the reality of good.

The Catholic response also appeals to the mystery of divine providence: God permits evil not because God is powerless to prevent it but because God can bring good out of evil — a claim supremely exemplified in the crucifixion and resurrection of Christ. The *felix culpa* ("happy fault") of the Easter Vigil liturgy celebrates the paradox that the greatest evil (the death of God's Son) became the instrument of the greatest good (the salvation of the world).
