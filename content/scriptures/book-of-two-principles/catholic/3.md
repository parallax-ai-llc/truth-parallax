## Evil as Privation: The Augustinian Response

The third chapter's claim that the evil principle is a positive, creative force directly challenges the Augustinian theory of evil as privation (*privatio boni*) — the view that evil has no independent existence but is the absence or corruption of good, as darkness is the absence of light and disease is the corruption of health.

Augustine developed this theory in response to his own youthful adherence to Manichaeism — a dualist system with structural similarities to Catharism. His rejection of Manichaean dualism was both philosophical (evil cannot be a substance, because all being, as created by God, is good) and experiential (his own moral struggles were better explained by disordered will than by cosmic warfare).

For Catholic readers, the Augustinian theory has the advantage of preserving both divine sovereignty and the reality of evil without fragmenting the godhead. Evil is real — terribly real — but it is parasitic on the good, having no substance of its own. Cancer is real, but it exists only as a corruption of healthy tissue; lies are real, but they exist only as distortions of truth. This framework allows the Catholic tradition to take evil with full seriousness while maintaining that God is the sole ultimate principle of reality.
