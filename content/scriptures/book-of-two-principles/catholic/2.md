## The Unity of God and the Catholic Response

The second chapter's assertion of two co-eternal principles represents the theological claim that the Catholic tradition has most emphatically rejected. The Nicene Creed's opening affirmation — "I believe in one God, the Father Almighty, maker of heaven and earth, of all things visible and invisible" — directly contradicts every element of the Cathar position: the oneness of God, the divine omnipotence, and the attribution of all creation (visible and invisible) to a single divine source.

The Fourth Lateran Council (1215), convened in part to address the Cathar challenge, declared: "We firmly believe and simply confess that there is only one true God, eternal, immense, unchangeable, incomprehensible, omnipotent, and ineffable... one principle of all things, creator of all things visible and invisible." This conciliar definition represents the Catholic Church's direct doctrinal response to Cathar dualism.

The Catholic argument against two principles proceeds on both philosophical and scriptural grounds. Philosophically, the existence of two independent, co-eternal principles would undermine the intelligibility of reality: if reality has no single source, then there is no guarantee of cosmic order, no basis for natural law, and no ultimate ground of meaning. Scripturally, the unity of God is affirmed throughout both testaments (Deuteronomy 6:4, Isaiah 45:5-7, Mark 12:29).
