## The Fall, Original Sin, and Human Nature

The sixth chapter's narrative of the soul's imprisonment in matter presents an alternative to the Catholic doctrine of original sin — an alternative that the Catholic tradition ultimately rejected but that addresses some of the same existential concerns. Both traditions recognize that the human condition is marked by suffering, moral failure, and a sense of alienation from the divine. They differ fundamentally in their diagnosis.

For Catholic theology, the human condition results from original sin — the primal disobedience of Adam and Eve, which disordered human nature and transmitted a wounded condition to all subsequent generations. Original sin does not destroy human nature but weakens it, inclining the will toward evil while leaving intact the capacity for good (assisted by grace). The material world, though affected by the consequences of sin, remains God's good creation.

For the Cathar tradition, the human condition results from cosmic kidnapping — the evil principle's capture and imprisonment of divine souls in material bodies. The soul is not wounded but imprisoned; the body is not weakened but inherently hostile. Catholic theology thus preserves the possibility of material redemption (the sacraments, the resurrection of the body), while Cathar theology demands escape from matter entirely.
