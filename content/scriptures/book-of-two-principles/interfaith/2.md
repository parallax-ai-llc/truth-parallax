## Dualism and the Search for Meaning

The second chapter's presentation of two co-eternal principles represents one of humanity's oldest and most intuitive responses to the experience of a world divided between beauty and horror, kindness and cruelty, life and death. The dualist intuition — that reality is governed by opposing forces — surfaces wherever human beings reflect seriously on the nature of their experience.

The Zoroastrian concept of cosmic choice between *asha* (truth) and *druj* (falsehood) provides a moral framework for individual and collective life. The Chinese concepts of yin and yang describe a complementary rather than antagonistic duality. The Navajo concept of *hozho* (beauty/harmony) and its disruption by witchcraft and disorder presents a dynamic balance between order and chaos.

For interfaith dialogue, the *Liber de Duobus Principiis* raises the question of whether dualism is a valid theological response to human experience or a temptation to oversimplify a more complex reality. Most traditions have ultimately rejected strict cosmic dualism — affirming either a single divine source (monotheism) or a more nuanced understanding of multiplicity and unity. Yet the dualist impulse persists, precisely because the experience of radical evil continues to challenge every tradition's account of divine goodness.
