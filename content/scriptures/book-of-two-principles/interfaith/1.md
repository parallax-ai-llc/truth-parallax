## Freedom, Suffering, and the Human Condition

The opening chapter's interrogation of free will addresses a concern shared across all religious and philosophical traditions: how to account for human suffering in a world that many believe to be divinely ordered. The *Liber de Duobus Principiis*' argument — that free will cannot explain the depth and pervasiveness of evil — resonates with the lived experience of suffering that prompts religious questioning in every culture.

The Buddhist First Noble Truth — "life is suffering" (*dukkha*) — begins from a similar observation but reaches a radically different conclusion. Rather than attributing suffering to a cosmic evil principle, Buddhism locates its source in craving (*tanha*) and ignorance (*avidya*). The Hindu concept of *maya* (cosmic illusion) offers yet another framework: suffering arises from the mistaken identification of the self with the body and the world. In Islam, suffering is understood as a test (*ibtila*) from God, designed to purify and strengthen the believer's faith.

The interfaith value of the *Liber*'s argument lies not in the persuasiveness of its dualist solution but in the honesty of its starting point. The text refuses to minimize or explain away the reality of evil. Whatever one thinks of the two-principles doctrine, the chapter's insistence that evil demands a serious explanation — not a pious evasion — is a stance that every tradition must respect.
