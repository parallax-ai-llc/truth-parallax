## Naming Evil: How Traditions Confront Darkness

The third chapter's attempt to describe the nature of the evil principle illustrates a challenge faced by every religious tradition: how to name, understand, and respond to evil without either minimizing its reality or granting it undue cosmic status. The balance is delicate, and different traditions strike it differently.

Judaism's approach is notably varied: the *yetzer ha-ra* (evil inclination) is internal to human nature; Satan in Job is a member of the divine court, not an independent adversary; post-biblical Jewish thought develops more elaborate demonologies while maintaining God's ultimate sovereignty. Christianity attributes evil to the fallen angel Satan while insisting on God's sovereignty and the ultimate defeat of evil. Islam teaches that Iblis (Satan) acts only by God's permission and that evil serves God's testing purpose. Buddhism avoids ontologizing evil, treating it as the consequence of ignorance rather than a cosmic force.

The Cathar tradition's willingness to grant evil independent cosmic status represents the extreme end of a spectrum. Interfaith reflection on this spectrum can illuminate how each tradition's understanding of evil shapes its understanding of God, human nature, and the moral life.
