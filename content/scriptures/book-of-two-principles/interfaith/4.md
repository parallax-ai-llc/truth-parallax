## Sacred Texts and Contested Interpretations

The fourth chapter's use of biblical texts to support dualism offers a case study in a phenomenon common to all scriptural traditions: the capacity of sacred texts to yield fundamentally different — even contradictory — interpretations depending on the reader's presuppositions and community.

In Judaism, the same Torah has been interpreted to support both liberal and ultra-Orthodox practice. In Islam, the same Quran has been read to support both mystical union with God and strict legalistic observance. In Hinduism, the same Bhagavad Gita has been cited by both pacifists (Gandhi) and warriors (Tilak). The *Liber de Duobus Principiis*' heretical reading of the Christian Bible is thus not an anomaly but an illustration of a universal hermeneutical reality.

The interfaith lesson is humility: no tradition's interpretation of its sacred texts is self-evident. The meaning of scripture emerges through the dialogue between text and community, and communities can err — sometimes catastrophically — in their interpretations. The willingness to engage with heterodox readings, not to endorse them but to understand them, is a mark of mature religious thought.
