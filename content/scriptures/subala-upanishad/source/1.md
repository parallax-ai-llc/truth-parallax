---
title: "Subala Upanishad - Chapter 1"
scripture: "subala-upanishad"
chapter: 1
---

**1** Om. Subala approached Brahma and said: "Lord, teach me the knowledge of Brahman."

**2** Brahma said: "In the beginning, this universe was non-existent. From non-existence arose existence. That existence was one alone. That one thought, 'May I be many, may I grow forth.' It created fire. That fire thought, 'May I be many, may I grow forth.' It created water. Water thought, 'May I be many, may I grow forth.' It created food.

**3** "That Being, having created all this, entered into it. Having entered into it, it became both the sentient and the insentient, the defined and the undefined, the supported and the unsupported, the knowing and the unknowing, the real and the unreal.

**4** "The real became all this that exists. They call it the Real. On this there is this verse: In the beginning this was non-existent. From it was born what exists. That made itself by itself. Therefore it is called the self-made.

**5** "That which is the self-made is the essence. For truly, on obtaining the essence, one becomes blissful. Who indeed could breathe, who could live, if this bliss existed not in the space within the heart?

**6** "This bliss alone gives happiness. When one finds fearless support in that which is invisible, incorporeal, indefinable, and without any resting place, then he has reached fearlessness.

**7** "But when one makes even the slightest distinction in it, there is fear. That itself becomes fear for the learned one who does not reflect.

**8** "On this there is this verse: From fear of it the wind blows. From fear of it the sun rises. From fear of it fire, Indra, and death, the fifth, all run.

**9** "This is the supreme knowledge, and this is the supreme bliss. He who knows this bliss of Brahman does not fear anything at any time.

**10** "Such a one is not troubled by the thoughts: Why did I not do what is good? Why did I do what is evil? He who knows this frees himself from both these thoughts. He who knows this, verily, frees himself from both these thoughts."
