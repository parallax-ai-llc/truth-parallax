## Overview

Jewish engagement with Philippians centers on Paul's autobiographical passage in chapter 3, where he describes his former life "as to the law, a Pharisee" (3:5) and then declares all these credentials "rubbish" (*skybala*) compared to knowing Christ (3:8). This passage is crucial for understanding early Jewish-Christian separation and for assessing Paul's relationship to his Jewish heritage.

## Key Interpretive Concerns

### Paul's Pharisaic Identity
Paul's self-description as "circumcised on the eighth day, of the people of Israel, of the tribe of Benjamin, a Hebrew of Hebrews; as to the law, a Pharisee" (3:5) provides valuable evidence about Pharisaism in the Second Temple period. Jewish scholars debate how representative Paul's experience was.

### Revaluation of Torah
Paul's declaration that he counts his Jewish credentials as "loss" and "rubbish" (3:7-8) has been read both as a rejection of Judaism and, more recently, as a rhetorical strategy specific to the question of Gentile inclusion. The New Perspective on Paul argues that Paul rejected not Torah itself but Torah as a boundary marker excluding Gentiles.

### Heavenly Citizenship
The claim that "our citizenship is in heaven" (3:20) reflects a Hellenistic Jewish concept familiar from Philo of Alexandria, who spoke of the wise person's true homeland being the heavenly realm.

## Scholarly Engagement

Jewish scholars such as Alan Segal and Pamela Eisenbaum have explored Philippians 3 as evidence of Paul's ongoing — if transformed — Jewish identity rather than a simple rejection of Judaism.
