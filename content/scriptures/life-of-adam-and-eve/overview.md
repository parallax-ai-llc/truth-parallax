## Overview

The Life of Adam and Eve is a Jewish pseudepigraphical work that expands upon the Genesis narrative of humanity's first parents, recounting their lives after the expulsion from the Garden of Eden. The text exists in multiple versions — the Latin Vita Adae et Evae, the Greek Apocalypsis Mosis, the Armenian, Georgian, and Slavonic recensions — each preserving distinct and overlapping traditions about Adam and Eve's penitence, encounters with Satan, visions of the heavenly world, and eventual deaths.

The original composition likely dates to the first century CE, probably in a Semitic language, though the precise relationship between the various versions remains debated. The text reflects profound theological reflection on the consequences of the Fall, the nature of repentance, Satan's motivations for deceiving humanity, and the promise of eventual resurrection. Its retelling of Satan's refusal to worship Adam — based on his claim of prior creation — became one of the most influential narratives in the history of religions.

The Life of Adam and Eve profoundly influenced Jewish, Christian, and Islamic traditions about the first human beings. The narrative of Satan's fall through pride and jealousy shaped Christian demonology, while the story of Adam and Eve's penitence in the rivers provided a model for repentance traditions. The text's vision of paradise and its theology of death as both punishment and mercy have resonated across centuries of religious reflection.

## Structure

Life of Adam and Eve is structured as follows:

1. **Sections 1-2**: Expulsion and Penitence: Adam and Eve's departure from Eden and their penitential standing in rivers
2. **Sections 3-4**: Satan's Story: The devil's account of his fall through refusal to worship Adam, and his continued deception of Eve
3. **Sections 5-6**: Eve's Account: Eve's retelling of the temptation and fall, her vision of the heavenly court
4. **Sections 7-8**: Death and Promise: Adam's illness, death, and burial by angels; Eve's death; the promise of resurrection

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Academic**: Essential for understanding the development of Adam and Eve traditions, the origin of Satan mythology, and Second Temple Jewish anthropology
- **Comparative**: Offers rich parallels with Islamic Iblis traditions, Zoroastrian accounts of the first humans, and universal origin mythologies
- **Protestant**: Studied for its influence on Christian hamartiology, demonology, and the theological interpretation of Genesis 3
- **Catholic**: Valued for its contribution to the theology of original sin, the veneration of repentance, and the development of Marian typology (Eve as anti-type of Mary)
- **Eastern Orthodox**: Resonates with Orthodox theology of ancestral sin, the cosmic consequences of the Fall, and the hope of restoration
- **Interfaith**: A foundational text for Jewish-Christian-Muslim dialogue on human origins, the nature of evil, and the possibility of redemption

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| The Fall and Its Consequences | The expulsion from paradise and the transformation of human existence through labor, suffering, and mortality |
| Repentance | Adam and Eve's extended penitence in rivers as a model of turning back to God after sin |
| Satan's Origin | Satan's refusal to worship Adam and his fall from heaven through pride, providing an etiology of evil |
| Death and Mortality | The entrance of death into the world as both divine punishment and merciful limit on human suffering |
| Promise of Resurrection | God's assurance that death is not the final word and that humanity will be restored |
| Paradise Lost and Regained | The memory of Eden and the eschatological hope of return to paradise |
