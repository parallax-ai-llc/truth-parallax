## The Soul's Desire for God

Catholic mystical tradition reads Song of Solomon 1 as the beginning of the soul's journey toward divine union. The Bride's opening cry — "Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth" — is understood as the soul's deepest prayer: the desire for intimate communion with God.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth" (1:2)

St. Bernard of Clairvaux devoted his first sermons on the Song to this verse alone. He distinguished three kisses: the kiss of the feet (penitence), the kiss of the hand (gratitude), and the kiss of the mouth (contemplative union). Only the mature soul dares to ask for the kiss of the mouth.

Origen, whose commentary on the Song was the first and most influential, read the Bridegroom's "kisses" as the words of Scripture and the illuminations of the Holy Spirit.

### "I am dark but lovely" (1:5)

Catholic commentators read this christologically and ecclesially:

| Reader | Interpretation |
|--------|---------------|
| Origen | The Church, dark with sin but lovely through grace |
| St. Bernard | The soul, imperfect but beloved |
| St. Ambrose | The Gentile Church, dark in origin but beautiful in faith |

### "Draw me after you, let us run" (1:4)

The interplay of singular ("draw me") and plural ("let us run") indicates that the soul's personal response to divine love draws others along. Catholic spirituality has always understood that personal holiness radiates outward.

## Theological Significance

Song of Solomon 1 establishes the fundamental Catholic mystical insight: the spiritual life begins with desire. As the *Catechism* teaches, "The desire for God is written in the human heart" (CCC 27). The Song gives voice to that desire.
