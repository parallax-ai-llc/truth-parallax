## Overview

The Song of Solomon has no direct parallel in the Quran, and mainstream Islamic scholarship generally views it with caution, as its explicit love poetry does not conform to the expected style of prophetic scripture. However, the Sufi mystical tradition developed its own rich tradition of divine love poetry — from Rumi to Ibn Arabi — that shares remarkable thematic parallels with the Song's celebration of passionate, seeking love.

## Islamic Reception

### Mainstream View
Orthodox Islamic scholarship questions the Song's attribution to Solomon the prophet, noting that its explicit romantic content does not align with the expected character of revealed scripture. This is considered further evidence of *tahrif* in the biblical text.

### Sufi Parallels
Sufi poets, while not commenting directly on the Song, developed strikingly similar themes:
- The beloved as the divine
- The lover's search and longing
- Gardens, wine, and beauty as metaphors for spiritual experience
- Union with the beloved as the goal of the spiritual path

## Key Themes Shared with Islamic Tradition

- **Divine Love (*ishq*)**: The Sufi tradition of passionate love for God
- **Longing (*shawq*)**: The soul's yearning for its divine origin
- **Beauty (*jamal*)**: God as the Beautiful who loves beauty (hadith)
- **Union (*wisal*)**: The mystic's goal of intimacy with the Divine

## Sufi Literary Parallels

| Song of Solomon | Sufi Poetry |
|----------------|------------|
| "I am sick with love" (2:5) | Rumi: "Love is the cure for every pain" |
| "I sought him whom my soul loves" (3:1) | Ibn Arabi: "I follow the religion of love" |
| "Set me as a seal upon your heart" (8:6) | Hafiz: "I am branded by the seal of love" |
