## The Beloved in the Garden

Islamic engagement with Song of Solomon 2 evaluates the text through the lens of *tawhid* and prophetic authenticity, while noting thematic parallels with the Sufi tradition of divine love poetry.

## Islamic Assessment

### Textual Evaluation

Mainstream Islamic scholarship views the Song of Solomon as a text that has undergone *tahrif*. Its explicit love poetry, while not inherently objectionable, is not considered characteristic of the prophetic revelation (*wahy*) that Solomon would have received from God.

### Sufi Parallels

The Sufi tradition developed a rich vocabulary of divine love that shares thematic ground with the Song's imagery:

| Song Image | Sufi Parallel |
|-----------|--------------|
| Spring | *Ishq* (passionate divine love) |
| Beloved | Garden imagery in Persian mystical poetry |
| Banner Of Love | The mystic's search for the Beloved |
| Foxes | The symbolism of beauty as divine reflection |

## Quranic Framework

Islamic ethics affirms love within the bounds of *halal* (permissible) relationships. The Quran celebrates marital love (30:21) and divine love (5:54) while maintaining the transcendence and unity of God.

## Theological Significance

Song of Solomon 2 provides an occasion for Islamic reflection on the proper relationship between human love and divine love. Sufi mysticism bridges the gap: human love, purified and redirected, becomes a vehicle for experiencing divine love (*mahabba*).
