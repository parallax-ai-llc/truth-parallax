## The Song and Islamic Perspective

Islamic scholarship approaches Song of Solomon 1 with significant reservations. The book's explicit love poetry is not considered characteristic of prophetic revelation, and its attribution to Solomon the prophet is questioned. However, the Sufi mystical tradition developed parallel themes of divine love that share remarkable affinities with the Song's imagery.

## Islamic Theological Assessment

### The Question of Attribution

While Islam honors Solomon (*Sulayman*) as a wise prophet, mainstream Islamic scholarship does not accept the Song of Solomon as prophetic scripture. The book's content — frank love poetry without explicit theological instruction — does not conform to the expected character of prophetic revelation and is considered evidence of *tahrif*.

### Sufi Parallels

Despite the mainstream rejection of the text, Sufi poets developed strikingly similar themes:

| Song of Solomon | Sufi Tradition |
|----------------|---------------|
| "Let him kiss me" | Rumi: "I have lived on the lip of insanity, wanting to know reasons, knocking on a door. It opens. I've been knocking from the inside." |
| "I am dark but lovely" | The mystic's recognition of unworthiness before the Beloved |
| "Draw me after you" | The concept of *jadhb* (divine attraction) |
| Vineyard imagery | The garden as a recurring symbol in Persian poetry |

## Quranic Themes

While not commenting on the Song directly, the Quran addresses love and beauty:

- **Quran 3:14**: "Beautified for people is the love of that which they desire"
- **Hadith (Muslim)**: "God is beautiful and loves beauty"
- **Quran 30:21**: "And among His signs is that He created for you from yourselves mates, that you may find tranquility in them; and He placed between you affection and mercy"

## Theological Significance

The Song of Solomon illustrates, from an Islamic perspective, the divergence between biblical and Quranic understandings of revelation. While its themes of love find resonance in Sufi mysticism, its textual form does not meet the Islamic criteria for prophetic scripture.
