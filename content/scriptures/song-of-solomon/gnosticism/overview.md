## Overview

The Song of Solomon provided Gnostic interpreters with rich imagery for their theology of the *bridal chamber* — the sacramental reunion of the divine masculine and feminine that restores the fractured Pleroma. The Valentinian tradition in particular developed an elaborate bridal chamber theology that drew on the Song's imagery of lover and beloved seeking union.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### The Bridal Chamber
The Gospel of Philip (NHC II,3) presents the *bridal chamber* as the highest sacrament — the reunion of the soul with its angelic counterpart. The Song's language of seeking, finding, and union maps onto this mystical reunion.

### Syzygy (Divine Pairs)
Gnostic cosmology posits paired Aeons (male-female *syzygies*) within the Pleroma. The Song's two lovers become an icon of the original divine pairing, separated by the fall of Sophia and reunited through gnosis.

### Sophia's Return
The Bride seeking her Beloved in the Song mirrors Sophia's search for the divine light from which she fell. Her eventual reunion with her consort represents the redemption of the fallen divine element.

## Nag Hammadi Connections

- **The Gospel of Philip**: Extensive bridal chamber theology
- **The Exegesis on the Soul**: The soul as a fallen woman seeking reunion with her true husband
- **The Thunder, Perfect Mind**: Divine feminine speech echoing the Song's boldness

## Theological Significance

| Song Image | Gnostic Reading |
|-----------|----------------|
| The Bridegroom | The divine masculine (Christ/Logos) |
| The Bride | Sophia, the soul, or the pneumatic Church |
| The garden | The Pleroma |
| "My beloved is mine and I am his" | Syzygy — the restored divine pair |
| The watchmen who beat her (5:7) | The archons who assault the soul |
| "Set me as a seal" (8:6) | The pneumatic seal of gnosis |
