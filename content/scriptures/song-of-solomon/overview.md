## Overview

The Song of Solomon (*Shir HaShirim* in Hebrew, "Song of Songs" — meaning the greatest of songs) is a collection of lyric love poetry traditionally attributed to King Solomon. It is unique in the biblical canon for its exclusive focus on romantic love, its absence of explicit theological instruction, and its rich sensory imagery drawn from the natural world.

## Structure

The Song is structured as a series of dialogues and monologues between lovers:

1. **Chapter 1**: Mutual admiration and longing between the Bride and the Beloved
2. **Chapters 2–3**: Springtime love, the search by night, and Solomon's procession
3. **Chapters 4–5**: The beauty of the Bride, the garden imagery, and the Beloved's knock
4. **Chapters 6–7**: The Shulamite's beauty and the dance of Mahanaim
5. **Chapter 8**: The seal of love and the power of love over death

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Song occupies a unique interpretive position across traditions:

- **Judaism**: An allegory of God's love for Israel; read at Passover; Rabbi Akiva called it "the Holy of Holies"
- **Christianity**: Interpreted as Christ's love for the Church or the soul's mystical union with God
- **Islam**: Not accepted as prophetic scripture, but Sufi love poetry shares its mystical themes
- **Gnosticism**: The bridal imagery connects to the sacrament of the bridal chamber

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Love | Passionate, reciprocal, exclusive love between the lovers |
| Beauty | Elaborate descriptions of the beloved's physical beauty |
| Seeking | The lover's search for the beloved through the city and countryside |
| Garden | The enclosed garden as a symbol of intimacy and fruitfulness |
| Seal | "Set me as a seal upon your heart" — love as strong as death |
