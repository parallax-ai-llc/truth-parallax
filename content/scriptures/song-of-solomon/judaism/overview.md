## Overview

In Judaism, the Song of Solomon (*Shir HaShirim*, "Song of Songs") is one of the five *megillot* and is read during Passover (Pesach). Rabbi Akiva declared it "the Holy of Holies" of Scripture (Mishnah Yadayim 3:5), and the dominant Jewish interpretation reads it as an allegory of God's love for Israel, with the Exodus as the historical backdrop.

## Key Interpretive Traditions

### Allegorical (God and Israel)
The primary rabbinic reading: the Beloved is God, the Bride is Israel. The Song narrates the love story between God and His people from the Exodus through the messianic age.

### Midrashic
Shir HaShirim Rabbah provides extensive verse-by-verse commentary, relating every image to events in Israel's history or to Torah and mitzvot.

### Kabbalistic
The Zohar reads the Song as describing the union of the divine masculine and feminine aspects — *Tiferet* and *Shekhinah* — within the Godhead.

## Theological Emphases

- **Divine Love**: God's passionate, covenantal love for Israel
- **Exodus**: The historical backdrop of the Song
- **Torah**: The beloved's garden as Torah
- **Messianic Hope**: The Song anticipates ultimate redemption

## Liturgical Context

Shir HaShirim is read on the Shabbat of Chol HaMoed Pesach (or on the seventh day of Pesach in some communities), connecting the Song's love poetry to the liberation from Egypt — the beginning of God's "romance" with Israel.
