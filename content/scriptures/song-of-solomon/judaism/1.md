## The Love Between God and Israel

Jewish interpretation of Song of Solomon 1 reads the entire poem as an allegory of the love between God and Israel, with the Exodus as the foundational backdrop. Rabbi Akiva declared the Song "the Holy of Holies" of Scripture (Mishnah Yadayim 3:5), and this chapter opens the sacred love story.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth" (1:2)

Shir HaShirim Rabbah interprets the "kisses" as the giving of the Torah at Sinai — the moment of most intimate contact between God and Israel. The Midrash teaches that God's "mouth" represents the Torah, and Israel longs for the direct encounter she experienced at Sinai.

Rashi identifies the speaker as the community of Israel in exile, longing for the intimacy of the Temple era.

### "I am dark but lovely" (1:5)

Rashi: "I am dark through my sins but lovely through the merit of my ancestors." The Midrash also reads this as Israel's self-description: darkened by exile and suffering, yet beautiful in God's sight because of the covenant.

### "Tell me, you whom my soul loves" (1:7)

The Targum on Song of Songs translates this as Israel's prayer to God: "Tell me where You pasture Your flock" — where can Israel find God's presence in exile?

| Verse | Allegorical Reading |
|-------|-------------------|
| "Kisses of his mouth" | Torah given at Sinai |
| "Dark but lovely" | Israel in exile, beautiful in covenant |
| "My own vineyard" | Israel's spiritual life |
| "The king's chambers" | The Holy of Holies |

## Liturgical Context

Shir HaShirim is read on the Shabbat of Pesach (Passover), connecting the love story to the Exodus — the paradigmatic moment when God "chose" Israel as His beloved.

## Theological Significance

For Judaism, Song of Solomon 1 opens the most sacred love story in the Bible: God's choosing of Israel, Israel's response, and the ongoing passion that sustains the covenant despite exile and suffering.
