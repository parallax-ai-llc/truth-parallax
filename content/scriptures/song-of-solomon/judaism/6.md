## The Beauty of the Shulamite

Jewish interpretation of Song of Solomon 6 reads the poem allegorically as depicting the love between God and Israel, drawing on Midrash, Targum, and the medieval commentators to find in every image a reference to Israel's sacred history and covenant relationship.

## Key Themes

### Beauty and Shulamite

Shir HaShirim Rabbah interprets the imagery of beauty and Shulamite as references to specific moments in Israel's history: the Exodus, the giving of Torah, the building of the Temple, or the experience of exile and redemption.

### Garden

Rashi reads the theme of garden in relation to the covenant: every moment of distance between God and Israel is followed by reunion, and every moment of reunion anticipates future separation and redemption.

### Uniqueness and Awe

The imagery of uniqueness and awe is given specific historical referents by the Targum, which systematically translates the Song's love poetry into the language of Israel's national and spiritual history.

## Rabbinic Commentary

| Source | Approach |

## Liturgical Context

Shir HaShirim is read during Pesach, connecting the love poetry to Israel's liberation from Egypt — the foundational act of God's love for His people.

## Theological Significance

For Judaism, Song of Solomon 6 is the sacred testimony of God's love for Israel and Israel's response. Rabbi Akiva's declaration that it is the "Holy of Holies" means that this love relationship is the innermost truth of the entire Torah.
