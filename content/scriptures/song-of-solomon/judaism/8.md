## The Seal of Love: Love as Strong as Death

Song of Solomon 8 contains the poem's most profound theological declaration: "Set me as a seal upon your heart... for love is strong as death" (8:6). Jewish interpretation reads this as the culmination of the covenant love story between God and Israel.

## Key Verses and Rabbinic Interpretation

### "Set me as a seal upon your heart" (8:6)

Rashi interprets this as Israel's plea to God: "Place me as a seal upon Your heart, so that You will never forget me." The seal represents the permanence of the covenant — even in exile, Israel remains imprinted on God's heart.

The Zohar reads the seal as the *brit* (covenant) that binds the divine masculine (*Tiferet*) and feminine (*Shekhinah*) aspects of God.

### "Love is strong as death" (8:6)

Shir HaShirim Rabbah applies this to Israel's love for God: the love that sustained the martyrs — Rabbi Akiva, the Ten Martyrs — who chose death rather than abandoning Torah.

The Midrash teaches: "As death separates the soul from the body, so does Israel's love for God separate Israel from the worship of idols."

### *Shalhevet-yah* — "The flame of God" (8:6)

Jewish commentators note the divine name embedded in this word: love is literally a "flame of Yah." The Vilna Gaon reads this as indicating that true love between God and Israel partakes of divine fire — it is not merely human emotion but a divine quality.

| Verse | Rabbinic Interpretation |
|-------|----------------------|
| "Seal upon your heart" | The covenant's permanence |
| "Love strong as death" | Martyrdom for Torah |
| "Flame of God" | Love as divine attribute |
| "Many waters cannot quench" | Exile and persecution cannot destroy the covenant |

## Theological Significance

Song of Solomon 8 declares that the love between God and Israel is eternal, indestructible, and divine in nature. This is the theological foundation for Jewish hope: no exile, no persecution, no catastrophe can break the covenant bond.
