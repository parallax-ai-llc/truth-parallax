## The Beloved in the Garden

Song of Solomon 2 is one of the most beloved chapters in the poem, containing the famous "voice of my beloved" passage (2:8-14) and the declaration "My beloved is mine, and I am his" (2:16).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "I am a rose of Sharon, a lily of the valleys" (2:1)

Whether spoken by the Bride or the Beloved, this verse has inspired extensive commentary:

- **Allegorical**: Christ as the "rose of Sharon" or the Church's beauty in Him
- **Literal**: The Bride's modest self-description using common wildflowers
- **Typological**: The beauty of covenantal love reflected in natural imagery

### "His banner over me was love" (2:4)

Protestant interpreters from the Puritans onward have loved this verse. The "banner" (*degel*) suggests both protection and public declaration. Love is not hidden but proclaimed — as Christ's love is publicly declared on the cross.

### "Catch us the foxes, the little foxes" (2:15)

The "little foxes that spoil the vineyards" has become a Protestant proverb for the small sins and neglects that damage relationships:

| Interpretation | Application |
|---------------|------------|
| Marital | Small irritations that erode intimacy |
| Spiritual | Minor sins left unchecked |
| Pastoral | The need for vigilance in love |

### "My beloved is mine, and I am his" (2:16)

This declaration of mutual belonging is the heart of covenantal love. Protestant marriage theology finds here the paradigm of exclusive, reciprocal commitment.

## Theological Significance

Song of Solomon 2 celebrates the reciprocity of love — both human and divine. The beloved's approach in spring (2:10-13) images the coming of Christ, bringing new life where there was winter. The mutuality of "mine" and "his" reflects the covenant bond.
