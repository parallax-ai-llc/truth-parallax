## Overview

The Song of Solomon has been interpreted in Protestant tradition along both allegorical and literal lines. The Reformers inherited the medieval allegorical tradition (the Song as depicting Christ's love for the Church) but also began to recover the book's celebration of human romantic love as a good gift from God.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Allegorical (Christ and the Church)
The dominant Protestant reading from the Reformation through the 19th century: the Bridegroom is Christ, the Bride is the Church. This approach is found in commentaries by Matthew Henry, Charles Spurgeon, and many Puritan writers.

### Literal-Natural
Modern Protestant scholarship increasingly reads the Song as a celebration of human love and sexuality within the covenant of marriage. This approach honors the book's plain sense while affirming the goodness of marital intimacy.

### Typological
A middle position: the Song genuinely celebrates human love, but this love is a type or shadow of Christ's love for the Church (Ephesians 5:25-32).

## Theological Emphases

- **Marriage as Holy**: The Song affirms the goodness of marital love against ascetic denigration
- **Mutual Love**: The reciprocity of desire between the lovers models covenantal love
- **Beauty and Desire**: The body and its pleasures are gifts of God
- **Exclusivity**: "My beloved is mine and I am his" — the covenant bond
