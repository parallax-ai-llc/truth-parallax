## The Bride's Longing

Song of Solomon 1 introduces the two lovers and establishes the themes of mutual desire, beauty, and exclusivity that pervade the entire poem. Protestant interpretation has oscillated between allegorical (Christ and the Church) and literal (celebration of marital love) readings.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth" (1:2)

The bold opening has no parallel in biblical literature. Allegorical readers (Spurgeon, Matthew Henry) identify the "kisses" with the intimacy of communion with Christ — His word, His presence, His grace. Literal readers see the frank expression of marital desire as affirming the goodness of human sexuality.

### "I am dark but lovely" (1:5)

The Bride's self-description raises important interpretive questions:

| Reading | Interpretation |
|---------|---------------|
| Allegorical | The Church, though sinful, is made beautiful by grace |
| Literal | A sun-darkened vineyard worker, lovely despite social status |
| Typological | Human love, imperfect but genuine, reflecting divine love |

### "Tell me, you whom my soul loves" (1:7)

The language of "soul-love" (*she-ahavah nafshi*) elevates this beyond mere physical attraction. Protestant interpreters note that biblical love encompasses the whole person — body, soul, and spirit.

### The Vineyard (1:6)

"My own vineyard I have not kept" — the Bride's neglect of her own vineyard (herself) while tending her brothers' vineyards has been read as:
- The tension between duty and personal fulfillment
- The Church's failure to cultivate inner life while busy with external work
- A woman's sacrifice of self-care for family obligation

## Theological Significance

Song of Solomon 1 establishes that love — whether between human lovers or between Christ and His Church — is initiated by desire and sustained by mutual admiration. The Protestant tradition affirms the goodness of this desire as a gift from God.
