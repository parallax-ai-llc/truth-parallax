## The Seal of Love and Divine Eros

Song of Solomon 8 reaches the poem's theological summit: "Love is strong as death" (8:6). Orthodox theology reads this as a revelation of divine *eros* — the passionate love of God that overcomes even death.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Set me as a seal upon your heart" (8:6)

St. Gregory of Nyssa interprets the seal as the imprint of the divine image on the soul — the *eikon* of God restored through Christ. To be sealed on God's heart is to be eternally held in His love, a love that the baptismal seal confirms.

### "Love is strong as death" (8:6)

Orthodox theology, following Pseudo-Dionysius and St. Maximus, understands divine *eros* as the power that draws all creation toward God. Christ's descent into death and His resurrection demonstrate that love is indeed stronger than death — love conquers Hades.

The Paschal hymn proclaims: "Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life." This is the Song's declaration realized in history.

### *Shalhevet-yah* — "The flame of the Lord" (8:6)

Orthodox interpreters note that the divine name may be embedded in the word for "flame," suggesting that the love described here is not merely human but participates in the uncreated fire of God — the fire of Pentecost, the fire of the burning bush, the fire of the Transfiguration.

| Theme | Orthodox Teaching |
|-------|-----------------|
| The seal | The baptismal seal; the divine image |
| Love stronger than death | Christ's Pascha |
| Flame of the Lord | Uncreated divine energy |
| Many waters cannot quench | The Church's endurance through persecution |

## Theological Significance

Song of Solomon 8 reveals, in Orthodox reading, that love is the ultimate reality — because God Himself is love. The Song's concluding declaration points toward the Paschal mystery: love's victory over death through Christ's resurrection.
