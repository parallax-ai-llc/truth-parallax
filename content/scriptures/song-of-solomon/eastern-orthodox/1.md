## The Soul's Desire for the Divine

Orthodox interpretation of Song of Solomon 1 follows St. Gregory of Nyssa's homilies, which read the opening as the soul's awakening to desire for God — a desire that will grow infinitely through the process of *epektasis* (eternal progress).

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### "Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth" (1:2)

St. Gregory reads this as the soul's prayer for direct experience of God — not mediated through creation or even through the Law (which Gregory calls "the kisses of the prophets") but through intimate encounter with the divine Presence itself.

### "I am dark but lovely" (1:5)

The Orthodox reading emphasizes transformation: the soul is dark through sin but is being made lovely through divine grace. This reflects the Orthodox understanding of salvation as a process — the soul is being transfigured, not merely declared righteous.

### "Draw me, we will run after you" (1:4)

St. Gregory sees in this verse the principle of *epektasis*: the soul is drawn forward by God and runs after Him, never arriving at a final destination but always pressing deeper into the infinite mystery of divine love.

## Liturgical Context

Song of Solomon imagery pervades Orthodox hymnography, especially for Theotokos feasts. The Bride's beauty is applied to the Mother of God, the "garden enclosed" and "sealed fountain."

## Theological Significance

For the Orthodox, Song of Solomon 1 reveals that the spiritual life begins with desire — but a desire that is never fully satisfied, because the soul is drawn into the inexhaustible riches of God. This is not frustration but joy: the perpetual discovery of the Beloved.
