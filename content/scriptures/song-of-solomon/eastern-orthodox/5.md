## The Beloved Knocks

The Orthodox Church reads Song of Solomon 5 through the patristic tradition, especially St. Gregory of Nyssa's homilies, as an allegory of the soul's infinite progress (*epektasis*) into the inexhaustible mystery of divine love.

## Key Themes

### Beloved and Myrrh

The chapter's imagery of beloved and myrrh is read by the Fathers as expressing stages in the soul's growth toward *theosis*. The beloved's beauty reflects the beauty of Christ, and the lover's desire mirrors the soul's yearning for divine union.

### Search

St. Gregory of Nyssa interprets the theme of search within the framework of *epektasis*: the soul is perpetually drawn forward, never reaching a point of stasis, always discovering new depths in the Beloved's inexhaustible nature.

### Daughters Of Jerusalem and Description

The imagery of daughters of Jerusalem and description is read through the lens of Orthodox sacramental theology: physical beauty participates in divine beauty, and the material world is capable of mediating the divine presence.

## Patristic Commentary

| Father | Teaching |
|--------|---------|
| St. Gregory of Nyssa | The soul's infinite progress in God |
| Origen | The spiritual senses and divine contemplation |
| St. Maximus the Confessor | Divine eros and the cosmos |

## Liturgical Context

The Song's imagery pervades Orthodox hymnography for feasts of the Theotokos: "garden enclosed," "sealed fountain," and "altogether beautiful" are applied to the Mother of God, who embodies the perfection of the soul's response to divine love.

## Theological Significance

For the Orthodox, Song of Solomon 5 is not merely poetry but theology: the revelation that the deepest truth about God and humanity is love — a love that is eternal, transforming, and inexhaustible.
