## Overview

The Orthodox Church reads the Song of Solomon primarily as an allegory of God's love for Israel, Christ's love for the Church, and the soul's journey toward theosis. The patristic tradition — especially St. Gregory of Nyssa's homilies on the Song — established the Orthodox reading as a mystical ascent of unending desire for God.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Patristic Allegory
St. Gregory of Nyssa's 15 homilies on the Song interpret it as the soul's infinite progress toward God (*epektasis*). The Bride's constant seeking and never fully possessing the Beloved mirrors the soul's eternal growth into the divine life.

### Divine Eros
Orthodox theology, following St. Maximus the Confessor and Pseudo-Dionysius, distinguishes divine *eros* from mere human passion. The Song's passionate language is read as an icon of God's ecstatic love that draws creation toward itself.

### Liturgical
The Song's imagery pervades Orthodox hymnography. The Theotokos (Mother of God) is addressed with Song language: "garden enclosed," "sealed fountain."

## Theological Emphases

- **Epektasis**: The soul's infinite progress into God
- **Divine Eros**: God's passionate love for creation
- **Theosis**: Union with God as the goal of human existence
- **Bridal Ecclesiology**: The Church as the Bride of Christ
