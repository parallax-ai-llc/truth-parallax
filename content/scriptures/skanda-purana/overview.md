## Overview

The Skanda Purana is the largest of the eighteen Mahapuranas and one of the most expansive texts in all of world literature. Named after Skanda (also known as Kartikeya or Murugan), the warrior son of Shiva, the text in its various recensions encompasses an enormous range of material on mythology, pilgrimage, dharma, and devotional theology.

The Skanda Purana exists in multiple versions that differ dramatically in content and extent, making it one of the most textually complex Puranas. Scholars recognize that the text as traditionally constituted is a massive compilation drawn from many originally independent texts, composed over a period spanning from the 6th to the 15th centuries CE. Its primary distinction is its extraordinary attention to sacred geography (tirtha-mahatmya), providing detailed accounts of virtually every major pilgrimage site in India.

## Structure

The text is traditionally organized into seven Khandas (sections):

1. **Mahesvara Khanda — Shiva Section**: Mythology of Shiva, the marriage of Shiva and Parvati, and the birth of Skanda
2. **Vaishnava Khanda — Vishnu Section**: Narratives of Vishnu's avatars, Vaishnava theology, and sacred sites associated with Vishnu
3. **Brahma Khanda — Brahma Section**: Cosmogony, genealogies, and sacred sites associated with Brahma
4. **Kashi Khanda — Varanasi Section**: The greatness of Kashi (Varanasi), its temples, and its supreme holiness
5. **Avantya Khanda — Avanti Section**: Sacred sites of Avanti (Ujjain), the Mahakaleshwar temple, and related legends
6. **Nagara Khanda — City Section**: Sacred cities and their legends, with focus on sites in Gujarat
7. **Prabhasa Khanda — Prabhasa Section**: The sacred site of Prabhasa, its significance, and associated narratives

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Skanda Purana holds significance across multiple Hindu traditions:

- **Vaishnavism**: Valued for the Vaishnava Khanda and its extensive treatment of Vishnu's sacred sites
- **Shaivism**: A major Shaiva text, especially the Mahesvara and Kashi Khandas, celebrating Shiva's supremacy
- **Advaita Vedanta**: Cited for passages in the Kashi Khanda on the nature of Brahman and liberation through knowledge
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: Engaged selectively, with emphasis on passages consistent with Vaishnava theology
- **Academic**: The most important single source for studying Indian sacred geography and pilgrimage traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Sacred Geography | Comprehensive accounts of pilgrimage sites across the Indian subcontinent |
| Skanda-Kartikeya | The mythology and worship of the divine warrior son of Shiva |
| Kashi | The supreme holiness of Varanasi as the city of liberation |
| Shiva Mythology | Extensive narratives of Shiva's cosmic deeds and divine relationships |
| Tirtha-Mahatmya | The spiritual merit and transformative power of pilgrimage |
