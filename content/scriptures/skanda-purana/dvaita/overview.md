## Overview

Dvaita Vedanta, founded by Madhvacharya (1238-1317 CE), engages with the Skanda Purana by carefully distinguishing passages that support Vishnu's supremacy from those that require reinterpretation within the Dvaita framework. Apparent Shaiva supremacy claims are reinterpreted through the Dvaita principle that Shiva, while exalted, remains eternally subordinate to Vishnu.

Madhvacharya's hermeneutical method involves reading all scriptures, including the Puranas, as ultimately pointing to Vishnu's supremacy. The five-fold eternal distinction (pancha-bheda) between God and souls, God and matter, soul and matter, one soul and another, and one material entity and another provides the interpretive framework for this reading.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Literal Supremacy of Vishnu
Dvaitins read declarations of divine supremacy as literal theological truths. When a text declares the supreme deity as the cause of all, this is taken as an ontological claim about the real hierarchy of being, with Vishnu at the apex.

### Pancha-bheda (Five-fold Difference)
The Dvaita reading emphasizes the five eternal distinctions: between God and souls, God and matter, soul and matter, one soul and another, and one material entity and another. These distinctions are permanent and never dissolve.

### Tattvavada (Realism)
Dvaitins insist on the reality of the world described in the Puranas. Cosmological accounts describe real structures of a real universe created and sustained by a real personal God.

## Theological Emphases

- **Svatantrya**: Vishnu's absolute independence — he depends on nothing else for his existence or actions
- **Taratamya**: The graded hierarchy of beings, from Brahma and Vayu down to insentient matter
- **Aparoksha Jnana**: Direct, unmediated knowledge of Vishnu as the goal of spiritual practice
- **Eternal Servitude**: The liberated soul remains eternally distinct from and subordinate to Vishnu
- **Bhimakaya**: The supreme strength and power of Vishnu as demonstrated through his cosmic acts
