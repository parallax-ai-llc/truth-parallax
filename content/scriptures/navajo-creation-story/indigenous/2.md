The Second World, the Blue World or Ni'hodootl'izh, represents the next stage of emergence. After being expelled from the Black World due to conflict and moral failings, the beings entered a realm populated by blue-feathered beings including swallow people. This world introduced new forms of social interaction and conflict, as the newcomers had to negotiate their place among existing inhabitants.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Blue World (Ni'hodootl'izh) carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
