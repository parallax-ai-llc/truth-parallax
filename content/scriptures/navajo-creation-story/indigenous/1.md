The First World, known as the Black World or Ni'hodilqil, represents the initial stage of creation in the Diné Bahaneʼ. In this primordial realm of darkness, the first beings — insect people and air-spirit beings — came into existence. The Black World was a place of conflict and spiritual immaturity, where the inhabitants struggled with basic moral and social challenges.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Black World (Ni'hodilqil) carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
