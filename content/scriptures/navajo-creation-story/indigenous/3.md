The Third World, the Yellow World or Ni'haltsidoh, marks a significant development in the emergence narrative. This world contained rivers, mountains, and more complex landscapes. The beings encountered grasshopper people and other creatures. It was in this world that many important social structures began to take shape, and the beings started developing more sophisticated relationships with the natural world.

From an Indigenous perspective, The Yellow World (Ni'haltsidoh) carries living significance for the community's ongoing spiritual and cultural practice. This is not a text to be analyzed from the outside but a sacred narrative that continues to inform daily life, ceremonial practice, and the community's relationship with the land and with each other. The knowledge contained in this section is maintained by specific custodians and transmitted through established protocols that ensure its integrity across generations.

Indigenous engagement emphasizes the inseparability of this narrative from the broader web of cultural practice, language, and identity. The themes addressed here are not abstract philosophical concepts but practical guides for living in right relationship with the community, the natural world, and the spiritual forces that sustain existence.
