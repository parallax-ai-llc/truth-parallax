The Third World, the Yellow World or Ni'haltsidoh, marks a significant development in the emergence narrative. This world contained rivers, mountains, and more complex landscapes. The beings encountered grasshopper people and other creatures. It was in this world that many important social structures began to take shape, and the beings started developing more sophisticated relationships with the natural world.

Academic analysis of The Yellow World (Ni'haltsidoh) draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
