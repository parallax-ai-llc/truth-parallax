The Second World, the Blue World or Ni'hodootl'izh, represents the next stage of emergence. After being expelled from the Black World due to conflict and moral failings, the beings entered a realm populated by blue-feathered beings including swallow people. This world introduced new forms of social interaction and conflict, as the newcomers had to negotiate their place among existing inhabitants.

Academic analysis of The Blue World (Ni'hodootl'izh) draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
