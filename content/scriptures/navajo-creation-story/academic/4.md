The Fourth World, the White World or Ni'hodisxos, is where First Man (Altse Hastiin) and First Woman (Altse Asdzaa) emerged as central figures. This world saw the development of more complex social and spiritual structures, but also faced a great flood that forced the beings to seek passage to the next world. The reed through which they climbed upward became a central symbol of emergence and transformation.

Academic analysis of The White World (Ni'hodisxos) draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
