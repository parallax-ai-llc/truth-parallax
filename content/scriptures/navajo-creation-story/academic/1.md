The First World, known as the Black World or Ni'hodilqil, represents the initial stage of creation in the Diné Bahaneʼ. In this primordial realm of darkness, the first beings — insect people and air-spirit beings — came into existence. The Black World was a place of conflict and spiritual immaturity, where the inhabitants struggled with basic moral and social challenges.

Academic analysis of The Black World (Ni'hodilqil) draws on multiple disciplines including religious studies, anthropology, linguistics, and comparative literature. Scholars have examined this section's historical context, its literary and narrative structure, its relationship to other versions and parallel traditions, and its philosophical implications. Textual analysis reveals complex layers of meaning that reward careful study while resisting reduction to any single interpretive framework.

Critical scholarship has increasingly emphasized the importance of engaging with Indigenous perspectives and interpretive traditions rather than imposing external analytical categories. This section's themes intersect with contemporary academic debates about Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition, and the limitations of Western scholarly methodologies when applied to non-Western sacred narratives.
