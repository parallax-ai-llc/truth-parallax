The Fourth World, the White World or Ni'hodisxos, is where First Man (Altse Hastiin) and First Woman (Altse Asdzaa) emerged as central figures. This world saw the development of more complex social and spiritual structures, but also faced a great flood that forced the beings to seek passage to the next world. The reed through which they climbed upward became a central symbol of emergence and transformation.

Anthropological study of The White World (Ni'hodisxos) examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
