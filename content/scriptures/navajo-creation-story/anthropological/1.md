The First World, known as the Black World or Ni'hodilqil, represents the initial stage of creation in the Diné Bahaneʼ. In this primordial realm of darkness, the first beings — insect people and air-spirit beings — came into existence. The Black World was a place of conflict and spiritual immaturity, where the inhabitants struggled with basic moral and social challenges.

Anthropological study of The Black World (Ni'hodilqil) examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
