The Second World, the Blue World or Ni'hodootl'izh, represents the next stage of emergence. After being expelled from the Black World due to conflict and moral failings, the beings entered a realm populated by blue-feathered beings including swallow people. This world introduced new forms of social interaction and conflict, as the newcomers had to negotiate their place among existing inhabitants.

Anthropological study of The Blue World (Ni'hodootl'izh) examines its role within the broader social, cultural, and ecological context of the community that maintains it. This section's themes are not merely narrative elements but reflect and reinforce specific patterns of social organization, kinship, territorial claims, ceremonial practice, and ecological knowledge.

Anthropological analysis reveals how this section functions within the community's lived experience — how it shapes and is shaped by social relationships, how it encodes practical knowledge about the environment, and how it provides the framework for ritual and ceremonial practice. Understanding these social dimensions is essential for appreciating the full significance of the narrative beyond its surface-level storyline.
