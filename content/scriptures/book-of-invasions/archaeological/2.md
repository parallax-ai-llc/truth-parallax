The Cessair narrative, describing the first settlement of Ireland by a woman-led group before the biblical Flood, finds no direct archaeological correlate — and this absence is itself instructive. Ireland's earliest confirmed human presence dates to the Mesolithic period, with the site at Mount Sandel, County Derry (c. 7900 BCE), excavated by Peter Woodman in the 1970s, yielding post-built structures, microliths, and evidence of salmon and eel exploitation. The gap between this evidence and the text's pre-diluvian framework reveals the Lebor Gabála's synthetic method: mapping Ireland's prehistory onto a Eusebian-Orosian universal chronology.

The Cessair section's emphasis on a maritime arrival resonates with current archaeological models of Ireland's initial colonization. Isotopic and genetic studies, including ancient DNA work by Lara Cassidy and Daniel Bradley at Trinity College Dublin, have confirmed that Ireland's first settlers arrived by sea from continental Europe, likely via the western seaways connecting Iberia, Brittany, and the Atlantic coast. The logistics of Mesolithic seafaring — attested through obsidian distribution patterns in the Mediterranean and the colonization of offshore islands — suggest that the text's image of boat-borne settlers, though mythologized, encodes a structural truth about Ireland's insular settlement history.

The account of Cessair's company perishing in the Flood, leaving only Fintan mac Bóchra as survivor, parallels the archaeological phenomenon of apparent population discontinuities in Ireland's prehistoric record. Pollen analysis from sites such as Lough Gur, County Limerick — excavated by Seán P. Ó Ríordáin in the 1930s and 1940s — reveals episodes of forest regeneration that suggest periods of reduced human activity, which could be mythologized as catastrophic depopulations. The concept of a "failed" first settlement may preserve folk memory of early colonization episodes that left minimal archaeological traces.

Landscape features associated with the Cessair tradition, particularly Cul Cessrach in County Roscommon and Cessair's supposed landing place at Dún na mBarc in County Cork, have not yielded pre-Neolithic material. However, the place-name evidence (dindshenchas) embedded in the narrative connects to a broader pattern of mythological onomastics that Barry Raftery and other landscape archaeologists have used to identify sites of prolonged ritual or settlement significance, even when excavation evidence remains elusive.
