The Lebor Gabála Érenn's creation narrative and antediluvian framework invites comparison with the earliest phases of human habitation in Ireland, attested through Mesolithic sites such as Mount Sandel (c. 7900 BCE) in County Derry, one of the oldest known settlements on the island. The text's cosmogonic vision, situating Ireland within a biblical chronology from the Flood onward, reflects the medieval Irish scholarly project of integrating native tradition with Augustinian historiography — a synthesis whose material correlates lie not in antediluvian deposits but in the stratigraphic record of post-glacial colonization.

Pollen core data from sites such as Lough Sheeauns and Cashelkeelty demonstrate significant environmental changes during the early Holocene, including the spread of hazel, oak, and elm forests across Ireland. These paleoenvironmental findings illuminate the kind of landscape the text's mythic settlers would have encountered, though the narrative itself collapses geological time into genealogical sequence. The absence of Paleolithic material culture in Ireland — unlike Britain, where sites such as Paviland Cave preserve Upper Paleolithic evidence — is itself archaeologically significant, as it means the island's human story begins precisely in the period the text associates with post-diluvian recolonization.

The megalithic tradition of the Boyne Valley, particularly the passage tombs of Newgrange (Brú na Bóinne, c. 3200 BCE), Knowth, and Dowth, provides the most striking material context for the Lebor Gabála's mythological landscape. These monuments, predating the pyramids of Giza, employ sophisticated astronomical alignments — Newgrange's winter solstice illumination of the inner chamber being the most famous — that suggest cosmological knowledge among Neolithic communities. Archaeologist Michael J. O'Kelly's excavation of Newgrange (1962–1975) revealed the roof-box mechanism and confirmed the solstice alignment, demonstrating that these structures encoded celestial observations within monumental architecture. The Lebor Gabála's association of the Boyne Valley with supernatural beings (the Tuatha Dé Danann) may preserve a memory of these monuments as places of numinous significance long after their original builders had vanished from cultural memory.

Ceramic evidence from the Irish Neolithic — including Carrowkeel ware and later Grooved Ware pottery found at sites such as Knowth and the Céide Fields — provides a material typology for tracking cultural continuity and change across the periods the Lebor Gabála compresses into successive invasion narratives. George Eogan's decades of excavation at Knowth have revealed stratified occupation from the Neolithic through the Early Medieval period, offering precisely the kind of long-duration site history that parallels the text's vision of Ireland as a palimpsest of successive peoples.
