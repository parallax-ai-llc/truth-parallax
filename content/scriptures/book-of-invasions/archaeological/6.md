The Tuatha Dé Danann — the "peoples of the goddess Danu" — arrive in Ireland possessing four talismanic objects: the Stone of Fál, the Spear of Lugh, the Sword of Nuada, and the Cauldron of the Dagda. Archaeological approaches to this mythological inventory have focused on the material culture of prestige objects in Late Bronze Age and Iron Age Ireland, where hoards of gold, bronze weaponry, and ritual vessels provide tangible analogues to the text's supernatural treasures.

The Stone of Fál (Lia Fáil), identified with the standing stone at Tara, belongs to a class of monumental stones that served as inauguration sites across Celtic Europe. The pillar stone still standing on the Hill of Tara has been associated with royal inauguration ceremonies from the Iron Age onward, and comparable stones at Dunadd in Scotland and Cashel in Tipperary demonstrate a widespread insular tradition. George Petrie's nineteenth-century survey of Tara identified this stone, though its current location may differ from its original position within the ceremonial complex.

The Tuatha Dé Danann's association with supernatural craftsmanship — Goibniu the smith, Luchta the wright, Crédne the brazier — finds rich material context in Ireland's Bronze Age metalworking tradition. The Dowris Phase (c. 900–600 BCE), named after the massive hoard discovered at Dowris, County Offaly, represents the apogee of Irish Bronze Age metallurgy, producing buckets, horns (crotals), and socketed axes of extraordinary technical sophistication. Mary Cahill's analysis of the Gleninsheen gorget and the Mooghaun hoard — the largest gold hoard found in western or northern Europe — demonstrates the wealth and technical mastery of late Bronze Age Irish communities, providing a material substrate for the Lebor Gabála's vision of a supernaturally skilled people.

The text's claim that the Tuatha Dé Danann came from the "northern islands of the world" where they learned druidry and magic has been interpreted by scholars such as John Carey as a mythological displacement of indigenous pre-Christian Irish religious traditions onto a safely distant origin point. Archaeological evidence of ritual practice in later prehistoric Ireland — including votive depositions in bogs and lakes, such as the Broighter gold hoard from County Derry with its miniature gold boat — suggests that the religious culture attributed to the Tuatha Dé Danann draws on actual pre-Christian practices that survived in cultural memory through the medieval period when the Lebor Gabála was compiled.
