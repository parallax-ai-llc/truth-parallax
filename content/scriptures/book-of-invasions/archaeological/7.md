The Second Battle of Mag Tuired (Cath Maige Tuired), in which the Tuatha Dé Danann defeat the Fomorians, is one of the central mythological events in Irish tradition. The battle is traditionally situated in the Moytura area near Lough Arrow in County Sligo, a landscape dense with megalithic monuments including stone circles, cairns, and standing stones that William Wilde surveyed in the nineteenth century and that have since been documented by the Archaeological Survey of Ireland.

The Moytura region contains one of the highest concentrations of prehistoric monuments in western Ireland. The stone circles and cairn complexes on the limestone plateau between Lough Arrow and Lough Key have been investigated by Etienne Rynne and others, revealing Bronze Age funerary and ritual use. While no battlefield archaeology has been definitively connected to the mythological narrative, the sheer density of monumental remains in this landscape — including the cairn at Heapstown (one of the largest in Ireland) — suggests a region of sustained ritual significance that could generate or attract mythological narratives of cosmic conflict.

The battle narrative's detailed account of weapons and warfare — spears, swords, slings, and the devastating eye of Balor — invites comparison with the archaeological evidence for Late Bronze Age and Iron Age weaponry in Ireland. Leaf-shaped swords, spearheads, and shields from the Dowris Phase and later La Tène period have been recovered in significant numbers, often from watery contexts suggesting ritual deposition. Barry Raftery's comprehensive study of Iron Age Ireland documented the distribution of these finds, noting concentrations in regions associated with mythological significance.

The figure of Lugh, the master craftsman and warrior who leads the Tuatha Dé Danann to victory, has been linked through comparative Celtic studies to the widespread deity Lugus, attested epigraphically at Lugdunum (Lyon), Luguvalium (Carlisle), and other continental sites. The archaeological evidence for Lugus worship across the Celtic world — including the Chalon-sur-Saône pillar inscription and possible representations on the Gundestrup cauldron — provides a material framework for understanding Lugh not as a purely literary invention but as a figure rooted in pan-Celtic religious practice whose traditions were preserved and elaborated in the Irish literary tradition.
