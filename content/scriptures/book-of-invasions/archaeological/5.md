The Fir Bolg section describes a people who divided Ireland into five provinces (cúigí), established kingship at Tara, and were ultimately defeated by the Tuatha Dé Danann at the First Battle of Mag Tuired. Archaeological investigation of the political geography attributed to the Fir Bolg — particularly the fivefold provincial division — reveals that this territorial framework, while projected mythologically into remote antiquity, reflects an Iron Age and early medieval political reality whose material traces are best attested through royal site archaeology.

The Hill of Tara (Teamhair), which the text associates with Fir Bolg sovereignty, is one of the most intensively investigated archaeological complexes in Ireland. Conor Newman's aerial and geophysical surveys, together with earlier excavations by Seán P. Ó Ríordáin and Ruaidhrí de Valéra, have revealed a palimpsest of monuments spanning from the Neolithic (the Mound of the Hostages passage tomb, c. 3200 BCE) through the Iron Age (the Ráith na Ríg enclosure) to the early medieval period. The Mound of the Hostages, excavated in the 1950s, contained cremation burials and a notable Bronze Age inhumation with a necklace of jet, amber, and faience beads indicating far-flung exchange networks — evidence that Tara's prominence long predates the political kingship the Lebor Gabála associates with the Fir Bolg.

The text's account of the Fir Bolg as bearers of a specific material culture — they are etymologically associated with bags (bolg) of earth — has been read by T.F. O'Rahilly and others as a mythological encoding of a pre-Celtic substrate population. Archaeological approaches to this question have shifted since the DNA revolution: ancient genomic studies by Cassidy et al. (2020) demonstrate a major genetic transition in Ireland during the Bronze Age, with the arrival of populations carrying steppe-derived ancestry. This evidence complicates older models of "invasions" but confirms that significant population changes did occur, even if the Fir Bolg cannot be straightforwardly mapped onto any specific archaeological culture.

The First Battle of Mag Tuired, in which the Fir Bolg king Eochaid mac Eirc is slain, is traditionally located near Cong in County Mayo. While no definitively identified battlefield archaeology has been recovered from this location, the region is rich in Bronze Age material, including stone circles, standing stones, and fulachta fiadh (burnt mound sites). John Waddell's survey of Connacht's prehistoric monuments has documented the density of ritual and habitation sites in this landscape, providing a material context for the mythological tradition of a decisive territorial confrontation in western Ireland.
