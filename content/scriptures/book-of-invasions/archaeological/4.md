The Nemed narrative recounts the third settlement of Ireland, characterized by conflict with the Fomorians — maritime oppressors who exact tribute from Conaing's Tower on Tory Island. This account of a people struggling against sea-borne raiders while building forts and clearing land corresponds broadly to the middle and late Neolithic period (c. 3500–2500 BCE), when Ireland's monumental landscape was being actively constructed and when evidence of inter-community conflict begins to appear in the archaeological record.

Tory Island itself, off the Donegal coast, has yielded archaeological material spanning from the Neolithic to the early medieval period. Excavations by Michael Herity and others have identified stone structures and lithic assemblages consistent with prolonged occupation. The island's prominent position in the Lebor Gabála — as the seat of Fomorian power — may reflect a genuine tradition of its importance as a maritime waypoint or defensive stronghold, comparable to the role of offshore islands in other Atlantic European coastal settlement systems documented by Barry Cunliffe in his work on the Atlantic zone.

The Nemed section describes the construction of two royal forts and the clearing of twelve plains, situating this settlement phase within a narrative of territorial expansion and monumental building. Archaeologically, the late Neolithic saw an intensification of passage tomb construction in Ireland, particularly in the Boyne Valley, Carrowmore, and Carrowkeel complexes. The Carrowkeel passage tombs in the Bricklieve Mountains of County Sligo, surveyed by R.A.S. Macalister and others in the early twentieth century, demonstrate the kind of organized labor and territorial marking that the text attributes to Nemed's people.

The account of Nemed's descendants ultimately splitting into three groups — one going to Greece (to become the Fir Bolg), one to the northern islands (to become the Tuatha Dé Danann), and one to Britain — reads as a mythological charter for cultural diversification. Archaeological evidence of population movement and cultural exchange during the late Neolithic and Chalcolithic transition includes the appearance of Beaker pottery in Ireland (c. 2500–2200 BCE), associated with new funerary practices, metalworking technology, and possibly new populations. Neil Carlin's analysis of Beaker-period burials in Ireland has demonstrated that this was not a simple replacement but a complex process of cultural negotiation, providing material nuance to the text's stark narrative of departure and dispersal.
