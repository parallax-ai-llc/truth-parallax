The Partholón narrative describes a second wave of settlement that cleared plains, established lakes, and introduced agriculture and crafts to Ireland — a mythological sequence that maps suggestively onto the archaeological transition from Mesolithic to Neolithic. The arrival of farming in Ireland (c. 4000–3800 BCE), attested through cereal pollen, cattle bones, and rectangular timber houses at sites such as Céide Fields in County Mayo, the Thornhill complex in County Derry, and Ballynagilly in County Tyrone, represents precisely the kind of transformative cultural episode that the Lebor Gabála mythologizes as a new "invasion."

The Céide Fields, excavated by Seamas Caulfield, preserve a Neolithic field system of approximately 1,000 hectares beneath blanket bog, revealing stone-walled enclosures for cattle-raising that constitute the oldest known enclosed farmland in Europe. Partholón's association with the clearing of plains and the creation of agricultural land finds a striking material parallel in this evidence of systematic landscape transformation. Palynological data from the Céide region show a sharp decline in elm and a corresponding rise in grass and cereal pollen around 3700 BCE — the "Elm Decline" event documented across northwest Europe — marking the moment when farming communities actively reshaped Ireland's forest ecology.

The text attributes to Partholón's time the creation of several lakes and the clearing of four plains, a mythological motif that scholars such as John Carey have connected to etiological traditions explaining landscape features. Archaeologically, post-glacial lake formation in Ireland is well documented through geomorphological studies, and the draining or modification of wetlands for agriculture is attested at sites such as the trackways of Corlea, County Longford, where Barry Raftery excavated a massive oak trackway dated to 148 BCE, demonstrating long traditions of wetland management.

Partholón's people are said to have perished of plague on the plain of Sen Mag (Old Plain), leaving Ireland empty again. This narrative of catastrophic depopulation may encode memory of genuine demographic crises visible in the archaeological record. Gabriel Cooney and Eoin Grogan have documented evidence of Neolithic settlement contraction in certain regions, while isotopic analysis of human remains from portal tombs and court tombs suggests nutritional stress and disease in some Neolithic communities. The mass burial tradition — evident at sites such as the court tombs of Creevykeel, County Sligo, excavated by the Harvard Archaeological Expedition in the 1930s — provides a material context for narratives of communal death.
