The Milesian invasion, the Lebor Gabála's final and climactic settlement narrative, describes the arrival of the Sons of Míl from Spain, their defeat of the Tuatha Dé Danann, and their establishment of the Gaelic-speaking dynasties that would dominate medieval Irish political life. This narrative has long been interpreted as a mythological charter for the ruling aristocracies of early medieval Ireland, and its claim of Iberian origin has attracted sustained archaeological attention, particularly in light of genetic and isotopic evidence for prehistoric connections between Ireland and the Iberian Peninsula.

Ancient DNA studies have confirmed significant genetic links between Bronze Age Irish populations and Iberian communities. The Rathlin Island genomes (c. 2000 BCE), analyzed by Cassidy et al. and Lara Cassidy's team at Trinity College Dublin, demonstrate that the Bronze Age population of Ireland carried substantial steppe-derived ancestry that arrived via continental Europe. Earlier Neolithic populations, however, show closer affinities to Iberian farming communities — a finding that complicates the Lebor Gabála's chronology but confirms that the text's insistence on a southern maritime origin for Ireland's people reflects genuine prehistoric demographic realities, albeit ones the medieval compilers could not have known in their archaeological specifics.

The Milesian arrival at the Kenmare River and their progression to Tara follows a route that traverses some of Ireland's most archaeologically significant landscapes. The entrance of the Milesians through Munster connects to the dense concentration of Bronze Age and Iron Age monuments in counties Kerry and Cork, including the stone circles of Drombeg and Kenmare, the copper mines of Ross Island (excavated by William O'Brien, revealing some of the earliest metalworking in Ireland and Britain, c. 2400 BCE), and the hillforts of counties Limerick and Tipperary.

The text's account of the Milesians negotiating with the three goddesses of sovereignty — Ériu, Banba, and Fódla — at Uisneach, Slieve Felim, and Slieve Mis respectively, links the invasion narrative to a set of sacred landscape features that served as ritual centers in later prehistory and the early medieval period. Uisneach in County Westmeath, identified as the omphalos or navel of Ireland, has yielded evidence of large-scale assemblies and ritual burning from excavations led by Roseanne Schot, who documented extensive deposits of animal bone, charcoal, and artifacts from the Iron Age and early medieval periods. The mythological encounter between conquerors and land-goddesses thus plays out across a landscape whose archaeological record confirms centuries of ritual use as a site where political legitimacy was enacted through ceremonial practice.
