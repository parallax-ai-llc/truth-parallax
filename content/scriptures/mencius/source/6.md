---
title: "Mencius - Chapter 6"
scripture: "mencius"
chapter: 6
---

**1** The philosopher Kao said: "Man's nature is like the willow tree, and righteousness is like a cup or a bowl. The fashioning of benevolence and righteousness out of man's nature is like the making of cups and bowls from the willow tree."

**2** Mencius said: "Can you, leaving untouched the nature of the willow, make cups and bowls with it? You must do violence and injury to the willow before you can make cups and bowls with it."

**3** "If you must do violence and injury to the willow in order to make cups and bowls with it, on your principles you must in the same way do violence and injury to humanity in order to fashion from it benevolence and righteousness!"

**4** "Your words, alas! would certainly lead all men on to reckon benevolence and righteousness to be calamities."

**5** Kao said: "Man's nature is like water whirling round in a corner. Open a passage for it to the east, and it will flow to the east; open a passage for it to the west, and it will flow to the west."

**6** "Man's nature is indifferent to good and evil, just as the water is indifferent to the east and west."

**7** Mencius replied: "Water indeed will flow indifferently to the east or west, but will it flow indifferently up or down? The tendency of man's nature to good is like the tendency of water to flow downwards."

**8** "There are none but have this tendency to good, just as all water flows downward."

**9** "Now by striking water and causing it to leap up, you may make it go over your forehead, and, by damming and leading it, you may force it up a hill — but are such movements according to the nature of water?"

**10** "It is the force applied which causes them. When men are made to do what is not good, their nature is dealt with in this way."

**11** Mencius said: "From the feelings proper to it, human nature is constituted for the practice of what is good. This is what I mean in saying that the nature is good."

**12** "If men do what is not good, the blame cannot be imputed to their natural powers."

**13** "The feeling of commiseration belongs to all men; so does that of shame and dislike; and that of reverence and respect; and that of approving and disapproving."

**14** "The feeling of commiseration implies the principle of benevolence; that of shame and dislike, the principle of righteousness; that of reverence and respect, the principle of propriety; and that of approving and disapproving, the principle of knowledge."

**15** "Benevolence, righteousness, propriety, and knowledge are not infused into us from without. We are certainly furnished with them."

**16** "And a different view is simply owing to a want of reflection. Hence it is said: 'Seek and you will find them. Neglect and you will lose them.'"

**17** "Men differ from one another in regard to them — some as much again as others, some five times as much, and some to an incalculable amount — it is because they cannot carry out fully their natural powers."

**18** "In good years the children of the people are most of them good, while in bad years the most of them abandon themselves to evil. It is not owing to any difference of their natural powers conferred by Heaven that they are thus different."

**19** "The abandonment is owing to the circumstances through which they allow their minds to be ensnared and drowned in evil."

**20** "There now is barley. Let it be sown and covered up; the ground being the same, and the time of sowing likewise the same, it grows rapidly up, and, when the full time is come, it is all found to be ripe. Although there may be inequalities of produce, that is owing to the difference of the soil, as rich or poor, to the unequal nourishment afforded by the rains and dews, and to the different ways in which man has performed his business in reference to it."
