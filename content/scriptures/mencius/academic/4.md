Academic study of Mencius 4 examines the chapter's political philosophy and its relationship to the broader tradition of Chinese political thought. The concept of the Way of the former kings has been analyzed as a legitimating strategy that grounds contemporary reform proposals in the authority of the idealized past. Scholars debate whether Mencius genuinely believed in the historical reality of the sage-kings' institutions or used them as rhetorical devices to advance his own political program.

The chapter's teaching on the ruler's moral responsibility has been examined in relation to Western theories of political obligation and the social contract tradition. Some scholars argue that Mencius anticipates aspects of social contract theory by grounding the ruler's authority in his fulfillment of obligations to the people, while others emphasize the fundamental differences between the Confucian model of moral rulership and the contractualist model of consensual governance.
