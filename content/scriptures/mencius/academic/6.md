Academic study of Mencius 6 has generated extensive philosophical scholarship on the nature of Mencius's moral psychology and its implications for ethical theory. The debate with Gaozi has been analyzed as one of the most sophisticated philosophical exchanges in ancient Chinese literature, with scholars examining the logical structure of each argument and evaluating their relative strength. Recent scholarship has emphasized the complexity of Gaozi's position, arguing that earlier interpreters may have oversimplified his views under the influence of Mencius's own presentation.

The doctrine of the innate goodness of human nature has been examined in relation to modern scientific research on moral cognition, including studies of infant moral perception and the neuroscience of empathy. Some scholars argue that contemporary empirical findings support Mencius's basic intuition that human beings possess innate moral capacities, while others caution against reading modern concepts into ancient texts. This interdisciplinary engagement has made the Mencius a central text in the emerging field of empirically informed moral philosophy.
