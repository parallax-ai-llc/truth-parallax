Academic scholarship on Mencius 1 examines the chapter's dialogues with Kings Hui and Xuan as evidence for the political conditions and intellectual debates of the Warring States period. Historians analyze the historical accuracy of these encounters, with some scholars arguing that the dialogues preserve genuine memories of Mencius's political consultations while others view them as literary constructions designed to illustrate philosophical principles. The chapter provides invaluable evidence for the practice of itinerant political advising that characterized the period.

The distinction between profit and righteousness has been analyzed by moral philosophers as an early formulation of the deontological intuition that moral rightness is independent of consequences. Comparative scholars have drawn parallels with Kant's distinction between acting from duty and acting from inclination, while also noting the significant differences between Mencius's virtue-based approach and Kantian formalism. The chapter's political philosophy has been examined in relation to contemporary debates about the moral foundations of governance.
