## Overview

The Mencius (Mengzi) records the teachings and dialogues of Mencius (372–289 BCE), the most prominent early successor of Confucius. The text presents a vigorous defense and extension of Confucian philosophy, most famously arguing that human nature is inherently good. Through conversations with rulers, rival philosophers, and disciples, Mencius articulates a vision of benevolent governance, moral psychology, and ethical cultivation that became foundational to the Confucian tradition.

## Structure

The seven chapters of the Mencius are each divided into two parts (A and B) and contain extended dialogues and argumentative essays:

1. **Chapter 1 — King Hui of Liang**: Dialogues on benevolent governance and the distinction between profit and righteousness
2. **Chapter 2 — Gongsun Chou**: The "flood-like qi," moral courage, and the four sprouts of virtue
3. **Chapter 3 — Teng Wen Gong**: Defense of Confucianism against Mohism and Yangism, economic theory
4. **Chapter 4 — Li Lou**: The Way of the former kings, moral responsibility of rulers
5. **Chapter 5 — Wan Zhang**: Historical exemplars, Heaven's mandate, and the sage in context
6. **Chapter 6 — Gaozi**: The celebrated debate on human nature and the innate moral sense
7. **Chapter 7 — Jin Xin**: Exhausting the mind, knowing Heaven, and the culmination of moral teaching

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Mencius occupies a central place in East Asian intellectual history:

- **Confucian**: Mencius is honored as the "Second Sage" and his text is a pillar of Confucian orthodoxy
- **Neo-Confucian**: Zhu Xi elevated the Mencius to the Four Books, making it essential for civil examination study
- **Daoist**: Daoist thinkers engaged Mencius's arguments on nature, sometimes critiquing his interventionist ethics
- **Academic**: The Mencius is a key source for understanding ancient Chinese moral philosophy and political thought

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Xing Shan (性善) | The innate goodness of human nature |
| Si Duan (四端) | The four moral sprouts: compassion, shame, deference, judgment |
| Ren Zheng (仁政) | Benevolent government as the ruler's primary obligation |
| Hao Ran Zhi Qi (浩然之氣) | The flood-like qi cultivated through moral integrity |
| Tian Ming (天命) | Heaven's mandate as the moral foundation of political authority |
