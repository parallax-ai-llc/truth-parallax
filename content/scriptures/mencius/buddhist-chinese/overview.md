## Overview

Chinese Buddhist interpreters have found in the Mencius a striking parallel to the Buddhist doctrine of buddha-nature (foxing). Mencius's argument that all human beings possess innate moral sprouts that can develop into full virtue resonates with the Mahayana teaching that all sentient beings possess the seed of buddhahood. This parallel facilitated the integration of Buddhist and Confucian moral thought in Chinese intellectual history.

## Interpretive Approach

Buddhist readers highlight Mencius's famous thought experiment of the child at the well's edge (2A.6), in which spontaneous compassion arises before any calculation of advantage, as evidence of a natural moral capacity analogous to the compassionate mind cultivated in Buddhist practice. The Mencius's teaching that the loss of the original good heart through neglect mirrors the Buddhist understanding of how ignorance and attachment obscure the inherently luminous mind. Both traditions thus share a fundamentally optimistic moral anthropology.

## Harmonization Projects

Song and Ming dynasty thinkers who sought to harmonize the Three Teachings found the Mencius particularly amenable to Buddhist-Confucian synthesis. The text's emphasis on introspection, its claim that seeking virtue is a matter of recovering what is already within, and its teaching on the exhaustion of the heart-mind (jin xin) all provided bridges between Confucian self-cultivation and Buddhist meditative practice.
