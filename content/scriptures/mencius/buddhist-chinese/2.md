Chinese Buddhist reading of Mencius 2 draws a striking parallel between the four moral sprouts and the Buddhist concept of buddha-nature (foxing). Just as Mencius argues that every human being possesses innate moral dispositions that can develop into full virtue, Mahayana Buddhism teaches that every sentient being possesses the seed of awakening that can develop into full buddhahood. Both doctrines ground an optimistic view of human moral potential in an innate capacity rather than in external grace or instruction.

The concept of the flood-like qi, cultivated through accumulated righteous action, invites Buddhist comparison with the accumulation of merit (punya) through virtuous practice. Both traditions teach that sustained moral effort generates a form of spiritual power or energy that transforms the practitioner and radiates outward to benefit others. The bodhisattva's accumulated merit, like Mencius's flood-like qi, is not seized through individual acts but grows naturally through consistent moral practice.
