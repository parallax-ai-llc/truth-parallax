Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Mencius 3 reads the polemics against Mohism and Yangism through the Buddhist lens of the Middle Way. Just as the Buddha rejected both extreme asceticism and sensual indulgence, Mencius rejects both the Mohist extreme of undifferentiated universal love and the Yangist extreme of self-interested withdrawal. The Confucian mean of graded love, beginning with family and extending outward, parallels the Buddhist teaching that compassion naturally begins with those closest to us and gradually expands to encompass all beings.

The chapter's economic teachings resonate with the Buddhist understanding that material deprivation creates obstacles to spiritual development. The Buddha taught that the Middle Way requires adequate provision for bodily needs, and Buddhist rulers throughout history have been admonished to ensure the economic welfare of their subjects as a precondition for the flourishing of the dharma. Mencius's linking of economic justice to moral cultivation reflects a parallel insight.
