Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Mencius 5 engages with the concept of Heaven's mandate through the Buddhist concept of karma. While the mechanisms differ, both traditions hold that political authority ultimately rests on moral foundations. In the Buddhist understanding, a ruler's position reflects the accumulated merit of past lives, and the loss of authority reflects the exhaustion of that merit. Mencius's teaching that Heaven's mandate is expressed through the people's acceptance provides a this-worldly mechanism that parallels the Buddhist karmic explanation.

The discussions of sage exemplars invite Buddhist comparison with the jataka tales of the Buddha's previous lives, in which the bodhisattva demonstrates exemplary moral conduct under challenging circumstances. Shun's maintenance of filial piety toward his unworthy parents parallels the bodhisattva's maintenance of compassion toward beings who cause him harm, illustrating the unconditional nature of the highest moral achievement in both traditions.
