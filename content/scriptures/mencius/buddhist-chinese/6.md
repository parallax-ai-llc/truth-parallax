Chinese Buddhist reading of Mencius 6, the debate on human nature, provides the most important point of contact between Confucian and Buddhist thought. Mencius's doctrine that human nature is inherently good directly parallels the Mahayana teaching that the mind is inherently luminous (prabhasvara) and that defilements are adventitious rather than intrinsic. Both traditions affirm that the moral or spiritual task is not to create goodness from nothing but to remove the obstructions that prevent the natural goodness of the mind from manifesting.

The thought experiment of the child at the well provides Buddhist interpreters with evidence for the universality of compassion as an innate mental quality. The spontaneous arising of alarm and concern, prior to any calculation, parallels the Buddhist understanding of the compassionate response as a natural function of the unobstructed mind. Chinese Buddhists cited this passage as evidence that Confucian moral philosophy, at its deepest level, converges with the Buddhist teaching on the inherent purity of mind.
