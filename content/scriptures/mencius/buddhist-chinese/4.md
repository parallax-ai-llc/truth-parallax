Chinese Buddhist reading of Mencius 4 finds in the teaching on moral influence a parallel to the Buddhist concept of the bodhisattva's spontaneous beneficial activity. Just as the sage's moral influence transforms others without coercion, the advanced bodhisattva benefits beings through the natural radiance of their realization rather than through deliberate intervention. Both traditions affirm that the highest form of moral influence operates through being rather than doing.

The chapter's emphasis on the ruler's responsibility for the people's welfare resonates with the Buddhist concept of the dharma-king whose primary duty is the spiritual and material welfare of his subjects. Chinese Buddhist political theory drew on both Confucian and Buddhist sources to develop a model of governance that integrates Mencius's benevolent governance with the Buddhist ruler's obligation to protect and promote the dharma.
