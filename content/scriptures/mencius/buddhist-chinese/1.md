Chinese Buddhist interpretation of Mencius 1 finds in the distinction between profit and righteousness a parallel to the Buddhist distinction between worldly (lokiya) and transcendent (lokuttara) motivations. Just as Mencius argues that governance should be grounded in benevolence rather than calculations of advantage, Buddhism teaches that actions performed with the motivation of liberation and compassion produce merit, while those performed for worldly gain perpetuate the cycle of suffering. Both traditions insist that the quality of motivation determines the moral value of action.

The chapter's appeal to the ruler's spontaneous compassion resonates with the Buddhist teaching that compassion (karuna) is an innate capacity of the mind that surfaces naturally when the obstructions of greed, hatred, and delusion are temporarily suspended. King Xuan's moment of compassion for the ox parallels the Buddhist understanding that all beings possess buddha-nature, which manifests spontaneously when conditions allow.
