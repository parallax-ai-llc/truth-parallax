Daoist reading of Mencius 1 critiques the Confucian distinction between profit and righteousness as itself a form of moral dualism that obscures the unity of the Way. From a Daoist perspective, the very act of categorizing actions as benevolent or self-interested introduces the moral discriminations that the Dao transcends. The Zhuangzi suggests that the sage acts without the consciousness of choosing between profit and righteousness, allowing the natural course of things to determine appropriate action without the imposition of moral categories.

However, Daoist interpreters note with interest Mencius's appeal to the ruler's spontaneous compassion for the ox being led to sacrifice. This moment of unreflective moral response, arising before any deliberation, approaches the Daoist ideal of spontaneous action (ziran). The problem, from a Daoist perspective, is that Mencius then seeks to systematize and cultivate this spontaneity, turning what should be natural into a deliberate moral program.
