Daoist reading of Mencius 3 views the polemics against Mohism and Yangism with detached amusement. From a Daoist perspective, all three positions—Confucian graded love, Mohist universal love, and Yangist self-preservation—represent partial perspectives trapped within the same framework of moral calculation. The Daoist sage transcends this entire debate by abandoning the attempt to determine the correct scope and distribution of moral concern, allowing compassion to flow naturally without predetermined boundaries.

The chapter's economic teachings on the well-field system are viewed by Daoists as another example of the Confucian desire to impose rational order on processes that function best when left to their natural course. The Daodejing's teaching that "the more prohibitions and rules, the poorer the people become" suggests that top-down economic planning, however benevolently motivated, interferes with the natural productivity of a society at ease with itself.
