Daoist interpretation of Mencius 2 engages with the concepts of the flood-like qi and the four sprouts from the perspective of naturalness and non-action. The flood-like qi, cultivated through accumulated righteous action, resonates partially with the Daoist understanding of de (virtue/power) as the natural energy that flows through one who is aligned with the Dao. However, Daoists object to Mencius's insistence that this qi must be cultivated through deliberate moral effort, arguing that genuine vital energy flows most powerfully when it is not directed by moral intention.

Mencius's parable of the farmer of Song who pulled up his sprouts to help them grow (2A.2) is read by Daoists as an unwitting endorsement of wu wei. By warning against forcing moral growth, Mencius inadvertently affirms the Daoist principle that the most profound transformations occur when human interference is minimized. Daoists suggest that Mencius should have extended this insight more broadly, recognizing that all moral cultivation is a form of "pulling up the sprouts" that impedes natural development.
