Daoist interpretation of Mencius 4 critiques the appeal to the Way of the former kings as an attachment to historical authority that prevents direct apprehension of the ever-present Dao. The Way does not reside in the practices and institutions of ancient rulers but is available at every moment to anyone who releases the burden of cultural tradition and moral ambition. The Daoist sage does not look backward to the sage-kings for guidance but inward to the spontaneous movement of the Dao in the present moment.

The chapter's teaching on the transformative power of moral example receives qualified Daoist appreciation. The image of virtue as wind that bends the grass suggests a form of influence that operates without coercion, approaching the wu wei ideal. However, Daoists would insist that genuine transformative influence arises not from cultivated moral virtue but from the natural power (de) of one who has released all moral pretensions and simply embodies the Dao.
