## Overview

Daoist engagement with the Mencius centers on a fundamental disagreement about human nature and moral cultivation. While Mencius argues that human nature is inherently good and requires deliberate cultivation, Daoist thinkers emphasize that true virtue arises spontaneously when artificial moral categories are abandoned. Yet certain elements of Mencius's thought—particularly the flood-like qi and the emphasis on not forcing growth—have found resonance in Daoist readings.

## Interpretive Approach

Daoist interpreters are drawn to Mencius's parable of the farmer of Song who pulled up his sprouts to help them grow (2A.2), reading it as an unwitting endorsement of the Daoist principle that moral development cannot be forced. The concept of the flood-like qi, cultivated through accumulated righteous action rather than seized through individual acts, parallels Daoist teachings on the gradual accumulation of de (virtue/power). However, Daoists critique Mencius's insistence on distinguishing benevolence from righteousness, profit from duty, as introducing the very moral dichotomies that obscure the natural Way.

## Creative Tension

The Daoist reading of the Mencius illustrates how philosophical traditions can learn from their disagreements. Mencius's arguments against the Yangist position—which Daoists sometimes claimed as their own—forced both traditions to refine their understanding of the relationship between individual flourishing and social responsibility.
