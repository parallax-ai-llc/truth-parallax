Daoist reading of Mencius 5 engages with the concept of Heaven's mandate from a naturalistic perspective. While Mencius interprets tian ming as a moral judgment expressed through popular consent, Daoists understand tian as the impersonal natural order that operates without moral intention. The rise and fall of rulers reflects the natural transformations of things rather than moral evaluations by a cosmic judge. This naturalistic reading liberates political change from the burden of moral interpretation that Confucianism imposes upon it.

Mencius's discussions of Shun's unconditional filial piety toward his unworthy parents are viewed by Daoists as an example of the moral rigidity that results from Confucian role-based ethics. The Daoist sage responds to each situation spontaneously, without the predetermined obligation to maintain filial devotion regardless of circumstances. When relationships become genuinely toxic, the natural response may be withdrawal rather than the heroic perseverance that Confucianism demands.
