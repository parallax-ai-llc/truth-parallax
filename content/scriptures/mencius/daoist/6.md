Daoist interpretation of Mencius 6, the debate on human nature, represents a fundamental point of disagreement. While Mencius argues that human nature is inherently good and requires cultivation, the Daoist position, articulated most fully in the Zhuangzi, holds that human nature is neither good nor bad but simply natural. The very categories of good and evil are human constructions that the Dao transcends. By labeling human nature as "good," Mencius inadvertently introduces the concept of "evil" as its opposite, creating the moral dualism that Daoists seek to overcome.

The thought experiment of the child at the well's edge, which Mencius uses to demonstrate innate moral feeling, is reinterpreted by Daoists as illustrating spontaneous natural response rather than innate moral goodness. The alarm one feels is simply the body's natural reaction to perceived danger, not evidence of a specifically moral disposition. By labeling this reaction as "moral," Mencius appropriates a natural phenomenon for his moral theory, imposing categories where none are needed.
