Confucian interpretation of Mencius 1 reads the dialogues with King Hui of Liang and King Xuan of Qi as foundational statements of the Confucian distinction between governance based on benevolence (ren zheng) and governance based on profit (li). Mencius's opening rebuke—"Why must Your Majesty speak of profit? There is only benevolence and righteousness"—establishes the moral framework for all subsequent political discussion in the Confucian tradition.

The chapter demonstrates Mencius's characteristic method of moral persuasion, drawing rulers toward benevolent governance through vivid analogies and appeals to their better instincts. His famous exchange with King Xuan about the ox being led to sacrifice reveals the Confucian conviction that even rulers possess innate moral feelings that, if properly cultivated, can serve as the foundation for just governance. The chapter establishes that the Confucian political project begins with the ruler's own moral transformation.
