Confucian interpretation of Mencius 3 reads the chapter's polemics against Mohism and Yangism as a vigorous defense of the Confucian mean between universal impartialism and selfish individualism. Mencius argues that the Mohist doctrine of universal love, which makes no distinction between one's own parents and strangers, undermines the natural affections that serve as the root of all virtue. Equally, the Yangist doctrine of self-preservation, which refuses any sacrifice for the public good, abandons the social responsibility that defines human moral life.

The chapter's economic teachings on the "well-field system" (jing tian) establish the Confucian principle that benevolent governance requires ensuring the material welfare of the people. Mencius argues that a ruler who fails to provide for the basic economic needs of his subjects cannot expect them to develop moral virtue, a teaching that connects economic justice to moral cultivation in a way that remains central to Confucian social thought.
