Confucian reading of Mencius 2 presents the crucial doctrines of the "flood-like qi" (haoran zhi qi) and the four moral sprouts (si duan). Mencius teaches that through the sustained accumulation of righteous action, one cultivates an expansive moral energy that fills the space between heaven and earth. This teaching has been foundational for Confucian moral psychology, establishing that virtue is not merely cognitive or behavioral but involves the transformation of one's vital energy.

The chapter's presentation of the four sprouts—compassion, shame, deference, and the sense of right and wrong—as innate moral dispositions present in every human being provides the cornerstone of the Confucian argument for the goodness of human nature. Confucian interpreters emphasize that these sprouts require cultivation to develop into full virtues, just as a seedling requires care to become a tree, establishing the essential role of education and moral effort in the Confucian project.
