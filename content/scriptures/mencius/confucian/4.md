Confucian reading of Mencius 4 develops the theme of the ruler's moral responsibility through the concept of the Way of the former kings. Mencius argues that the sage-kings of antiquity established principles of governance that remain valid for all time, and that contemporary rulers who deviate from these principles bring suffering upon their people. The chapter emphasizes that rulership is a moral trust, not a personal privilege, and that rulers who fail in their moral obligations forfeit their right to govern.

The chapter's teachings on moral exemplarity establish that the influence of the virtuous person extends far beyond direct instruction. Mencius compares the moral influence of the sage to the wind that bends the grass, illustrating the Confucian conviction that genuine virtue possesses a natural transformative power that operates through example rather than coercion.
