Confucian reading of Mencius 6 presents the celebrated debate with Gaozi on the nature of human moral disposition. Mencius refutes Gaozi's argument that human nature is morally neutral (like water that can flow east or west) by insisting that human nature has an inherent moral orientation, just as water naturally flows downward. This chapter is the locus classicus for the Confucian doctrine that human nature is inherently good (xing shan).

The chapter's thought experiments—including the spontaneous alarm one feels upon seeing a child about to fall into a well—provide the experiential evidence for Mencius's argument. Confucian interpreters emphasize that this reaction arises before any calculation of personal advantage, demonstrating that moral feeling is natural and immediate rather than learned or calculated. This innate moral sense provides the foundation upon which all Confucian moral education builds.
