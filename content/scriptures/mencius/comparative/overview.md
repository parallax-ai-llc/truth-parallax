## Overview

Comparative interpretation of the Mencius brings its arguments into dialogue with global philosophical traditions. Mencius's claim that human nature is inherently good, his grounding of morality in natural sentiment, and his insistence that rulers govern for the benefit of the people have invited comparisons with Western natural law theory, moral sentimentalism, and modern human rights discourse.

## Interpretive Approach

Comparative scholars examine parallels between Mencius's four sprouts and the moral sense theory of Shaftesbury and Hutcheson, between his concept of Heaven's mandate and Western natural law, and between his doctrine that the people may reject unjust rulers and modern theories of political legitimacy. These comparisons reveal both convergences—such as the shared conviction that morality is grounded in human nature rather than mere convention—and divergences, such as differing conceptions of individual autonomy and communal obligation.

## Global Relevance

The Mencius offers distinctive resources for contemporary global ethics, including its integration of moral psychology and political philosophy, its argument that economic justice is a precondition for moral development, and its vision of governance as fundamentally a moral rather than merely technical enterprise. These contributions make the Mencius an essential text for any genuinely global philosophical conversation.
