Comparative interpretation of Mencius 2 brings the doctrines of the flood-like qi and the four sprouts into dialogue with other traditions' accounts of moral motivation and innate moral capacity. The four sprouts closely parallel the "moral sense" theory of Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, and Hume, which similarly grounds morality in innate sentiments rather than reason or divine command. This parallel has generated extensive comparative scholarship and raised important questions about the relationship between Chinese and Western moral sentimentalism.

The concept of the flood-like qi invites comparison with the Greek concept of thumos (spiritedness), Aristotle's account of moral habituation, and the Christian concept of grace as a transformative power that enables virtuous action beyond natural capacity. All these traditions recognize that moral knowledge alone is insufficient for moral action and that some additional motivational resource—whether called qi, thumos, habitus, or grace—is needed to bridge the gap between knowing the good and doing it.
