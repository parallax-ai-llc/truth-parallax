Comparative interpretation of Mencius 4 brings the concept of moral exemplarity into dialogue with similar ideas in other traditions. The teaching that the sage transforms others through the power of moral example parallels the Christian concept of the saint as a living embodiment of divine grace, the Buddhist ideal of the bodhisattva whose mere presence benefits beings, and the Sufi concept of the wali (friend of God) whose spiritual state radiates transformative blessing (baraka). All these traditions affirm that the highest form of moral influence operates through being rather than through command.

The chapter's insistence on the ruler's moral responsibility invites comparison with the Western natural law tradition, which similarly grounds political authority in moral obligation, and with the Hindu concept of raja-dharma, the king's sacred duty to govern justly. These parallels reveal a widespread conviction that political power is a moral trust rather than a personal possession.
