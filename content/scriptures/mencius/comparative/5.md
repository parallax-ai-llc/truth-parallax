Comparative reading of Mencius 5 places the concept of Heaven's mandate in dialogue with other traditions' theories of political legitimacy. The teaching that rulers govern with Heaven's approval, which is expressed through popular acceptance, invites comparison with the Western social contract tradition (Locke, Rousseau), the Islamic concept of bay'ah (pledge of allegiance), and the Hindu-Buddhist concept of the chakravartin whose authority reflects accumulated merit. All these traditions seek to ground political authority in something beyond mere power.

The discussions of filial piety under extreme circumstances invite comparison with the Christian teaching on honoring parents, the Hindu concept of pitri-dharma (duty to ancestors), and the psychoanalytic exploration of family dynamics. Mencius's insistence on unconditional filial devotion raises questions about the limits of familial obligation that every tradition must address, revealing both shared moral intuitions and significant cultural differences in the way these intuitions are resolved.
