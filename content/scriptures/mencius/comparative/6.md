Comparative interpretation of Mencius 6 places the debate on human nature in dialogue with parallel discussions across world philosophy. Mencius's doctrine of innate goodness parallels Rousseau's concept of natural goodness corrupted by society, the Christian Pelagian tradition's emphasis on human moral capacity, and the Buddhist teaching on inherent buddha-nature. Gaozi's position that human nature is morally neutral resembles the Lockean tabula rasa and certain Aristotelian interpretations that view the soul as initially a potentiality for either virtue or vice.

The cross-cultural debate on human nature remains one of the most consequential questions in philosophy, with implications for education, governance, criminal justice, and social policy. Mencius's optimistic assessment supports educational approaches based on drawing out innate capacities (comparable to the etymological meaning of "education" from Latin educere, "to draw out"), while more pessimistic views support approaches based on external discipline and constraint. The Mencian position thus has practical implications that extend far beyond academic philosophy.
