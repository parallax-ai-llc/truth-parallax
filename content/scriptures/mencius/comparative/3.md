Comparative reading of Mencius 3 places the Confucian position on graded love between the Mohist and Yangist extremes in dialogue with similar debates in other traditions. The tension between universal love and particular attachment appears in the Christian tradition's negotiation between agape (universal love) and the special obligations of family and friendship, in the utilitarian-communitarian debate in Western ethics, and in the Buddhist navigation between universal compassion and the practical necessities of monastic community.

The chapter's economic teachings invite comparison with Aristotle's discussion of property and justice in the Politics, the Hebrew Bible's sabbatical and jubilee legislation, Islamic economic principles (zakat, prohibition of usury), and modern theories of distributive justice. Mencius's insistence that economic security is a prerequisite for moral development parallels Maslow's hierarchy of needs and challenges libertarian theories that treat economic distribution as morally neutral.
