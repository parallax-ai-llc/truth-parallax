Neo-Confucian reading of Mencius 2 finds in the doctrine of the flood-like qi and the four sprouts the experiential basis for the metaphysics of principle and vital energy. Zhu Xi interprets the flood-like qi as the condition in which vital energy (qi) is completely governed by and expressive of heavenly principle (li), producing a state of moral power and spiritual fullness. This integration of li and qi in the person mirrors the integration of principle and material force in the cosmos.

The four sprouts receive detailed Neo-Confucian analysis as manifestations of the four primary virtues—benevolence, righteousness, propriety, and wisdom—which are themselves expressions of heavenly principle in the human heart-mind. The Cheng-Zhu school interprets the sprouts as evidence that the original nature (benxing) endowed by Heaven is pure principle, while the need for cultivation reflects the distorting effects of turbid qi on the expression of this nature.
