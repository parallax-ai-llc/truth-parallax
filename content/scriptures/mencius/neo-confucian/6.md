Neo-Confucian reading of Mencius 6 is perhaps the most philosophically important chapter for the tradition, as it provides the textual foundation for the Neo-Confucian theory of human nature. Zhu Xi interprets Mencius's argument that human nature is good as meaning that the original nature (benxing), which is identical with heavenly principle, is perfectly good. The apparent evil in human conduct results not from the nature itself but from the distorting influence of turbid qi on the expression of principle.

This interpretive framework resolves the apparent contradiction between the goodness of human nature and the reality of human evil by distinguishing between the nature of principle (yili zhi xing) and the nature of the material constitution (qizhi zhi xing). The former is universally good; the latter varies according to the clarity or turbidity of an individual's qi. The purpose of moral cultivation is to purify one's qi so that the original goodness of principle can express itself without obstruction.
