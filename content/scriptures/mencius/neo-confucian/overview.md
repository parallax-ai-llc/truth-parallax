## Overview

Neo-Confucian interpretation elevated the Mencius to canonical status as one of the Four Books, making it essential reading for all educated persons in East Asia. Zhu Xi's commentary interpreted Mencius's teaching on the goodness of human nature through the lens of principle (li) and vital energy (qi), arguing that the original nature endowed by Heaven is pure principle, while moral failings result from the turbidity of qi. Wang Yangming drew on Mencius's doctrine of the innate moral sense (liangzhi) to develop his philosophy of innate knowing.

## Interpretive Approach

Neo-Confucian readers find in the Mencius the philosophical foundations for their metaphysical system. The four sprouts become evidence of heavenly principle embedded in the human heart-mind. Mencius's concept of the flood-like qi is interpreted as the vital energy that fills the moral person when knowledge and action are unified. The debates with Gaozi on human nature in chapter 6 become the locus classicus for Neo-Confucian discussions of the relationship between nature, mind, and moral effort.

## Cross-Regional Impact

The Neo-Confucian reading of the Mencius spread throughout East Asia, shaping Korean debates between the Yeongnam and Giho schools on the relationship between the four sprouts and the seven emotions, and influencing Japanese Confucian thinkers such as Nakae Tōju and Kumazawa Banzan in their development of Wang Yangming's thought.
