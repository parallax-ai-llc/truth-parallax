Neo-Confucian interpretation of Mencius 1 reads the distinction between profit and righteousness through the metaphysical framework of heavenly principle (tianli) versus selfish human desire (renyu). Zhu Xi interprets Mencius's rebuke of King Hui as the fundamental choice between following the public principle of Heaven, which manifests as benevolence and righteousness, and pursuing private advantage, which reflects the obscuration of principle by selfish desire. This interpretive move transforms a political teaching into a metaphysical doctrine about the structure of moral reality.

The chapter's demonstrations of the rulers' innate moral feelings are read by Neo-Confucian commentators as evidence that heavenly principle is present in every human heart-mind, even when obscured by the turbidity of qi and the accumulation of selfish habits. King Xuan's compassion for the ox reveals the principle of ren operating spontaneously, before the calculating mind intervenes to suppress it.
