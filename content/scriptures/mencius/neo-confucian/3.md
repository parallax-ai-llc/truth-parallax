Neo-Confucian reading of Mencius 3 treats Mencius's defense of Confucianism against Mohism and Yangism as the paradigmatic act of protecting orthodox teaching from heterodox challenge. Zhu Xi explicitly models his own critique of Buddhism and Daoism on Mencius's example, arguing that just as Mencius defended the Way of the sage-kings against rival teachings, Neo-Confucian scholars must defend Confucian truth against the errors of Buddhist emptiness and Daoist quietism. The chapter thus provides the scriptural warrant for the Neo-Confucian project of doctrinal clarification.

The chapter's distinction between Mohist universal love and Confucian graded love receives metaphysical grounding in Neo-Confucian thought. The Cheng brothers argue that differentiated moral relationships reflect the natural pattern of heavenly principle, which manifests distinctly in each relational context. Principle is one but its manifestations are many (li yi fen shu), and the attempt to erase these distinctions violates the inherent structure of moral reality. Mencius's defense of the division of labor is similarly read as recognition that the ordered differentiation of social roles reflects the principle-pattern of Heaven and Earth rather than arbitrary human convention.
