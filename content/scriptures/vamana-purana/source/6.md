---
title: "Vamana Purana Section 6"
scripture: "vamana-purana"
chapter: 6
---

**1** The concluding section of the Vamana Purana describes the nature of liberation and the ultimate purpose of human life.

**2** The sage said: "The human birth is the rarest and most precious of all births. Even the gods desire a human birth, for only in the human form can liberation be attained. To waste this life in the pursuit of sensory pleasures is the greatest tragedy."

**3** Four goals of human life are described: Dharma, righteous conduct; Artha, the acquisition of wealth through just means; Kama, the fulfillment of legitimate desires; and Moksha, liberation from the cycle of birth and death. Of these four, Moksha is the supreme goal.

**4** The Purana teaches: "Dharma, Artha, and Kama are not to be rejected, but they must be pursued in subordination to Moksha. He who pursues wealth and pleasure without regard for dharma falls into suffering. He who pursues dharma without aspiring to Moksha attains heaven but must return."

**5** The nature of liberation is described: "Liberation is not a place to go to. It is the removal of ignorance. When the veil of Maya is lifted, the soul realizes its eternal identity with Brahman. This realization is Moksha."

**6** "The liberated one sees the world as before, but sees it differently. He sees the divine in every being, in every object, in every event. He acts in the world but is not bound by his actions. He lives but does not die, for he knows himself to be immortal."

**7** The Purana describes three types of Moksha: Salokya, dwelling in the same world as the Lord; Samipya, being in the proximity of the Lord; Sarshti, possessing the same powers as the Lord; and Sayujya, merging into the being of the Lord.

**8** The Purana teaches: "Of these four, the devotee does not seek any. He seeks only the privilege of serving the Lord. The Gopis of Vrindavan did not desire liberation. They desired only to be with Krishna. This desireless devotion is the highest form of Moksha."

**9** The final teaching of the Purana: "The Lord is not distant. He is the nearest of the near — nearer than one's own breath, closer than one's own thought. He dwells in the heart of every being, waiting to be recognized."

**10** "To recognize him, one need not journey to distant lands, master complex philosophies, or perform extreme austerities. One need only turn inward, silence the mind, and listen. In the silence, the Lord speaks. In the stillness, the Lord is revealed."

**11** "This Vamana Purana, which has described the three steps of the Lord that measured the universe, concludes with the teaching that the same Lord who strides across the cosmos dwells as a tiny flame in the heart of every creature. The infinite resides in the infinitesimal."

**12** The sage concluded: "He who hears this Purana with faith, he who recites it with devotion, he who meditates upon its teachings — he shall be freed from all sins, all sorrows, and all bondage. He shall attain the lotus feet of Vishnu. Om Namo Narayanaya."
