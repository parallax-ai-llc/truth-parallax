## Overview

Vaishnava interpreters face a distinctive challenge with the Svetasvatara Upanishad, as the text explicitly names Rudra-Shiva as the supreme deity. Vaishnava commentators respond by reading "Rudra" as a name for Vishnu-Narayana, citing Vedic passages where Rudra is used as an epithet for the supreme being regardless of sectarian identity. The Upanishad's emphatic theism — its teaching of a personal God who creates, sustains, and liberates through grace — aligns perfectly with Vaishnava theology, even if the name requires reinterpretation.

## Theological Emphases

The Svetasvatara's teaching on divine grace (*prasada*, 6.21) is central to Vaishnava soteriology. Liberation does not come through self-effort alone but through the Lord's favor, which responds to the devotee's surrender. The parable of the two birds on one tree (4.6-7) — one eating the fruit, the other watching — is read as the individual soul and the Supreme Lord (Vishnu), eternally related but never identical. The concept of *maya* as God's creative power is understood through the Vaishnava framework as Vishnu's *yogamaya*, by which He projects the world while remaining untouched.
