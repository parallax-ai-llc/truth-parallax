Chapter 3 makes the most explicit theistic declaration in the Upanishads: "There is one Rudra only" (*eko hi rudro*, 3.2) who rules all worlds by His sovereign power. Vaishnava interpreters read "Rudra" as a name for Vishnu-Narayana, citing Vedic passages where Rudra is used as an epithet for the supreme being without sectarian specificity. The chapter's description of God as the cosmic person (*purusha*) who pervades all creation, has a thousand heads and a thousand eyes (3.14-16, echoing the Purusha Sukta), is identified with Vishnu's universal form.

The teaching that this one God "creates the universe, maintains it, and at the end of time rolls it up" (3.2) describes Vishnu's three cosmic functions as creator, preserver, and destroyer. The chapter's emphasis on divine sovereignty — God ruling by His innate power (*ishana*) — supports the Vaishnava understanding of Vishnu as the supreme independent Lord (*svatantra*) upon whom all other beings depend.
