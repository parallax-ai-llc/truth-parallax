The opening philosophical inquiry — what is the first cause? — is answered definitively by the Shaiva tradition: Shiva, the supreme consciousness. The chapter's systematic rejection of impersonal candidates (time, nature, chance) and its affirmation that a conscious being rules over all cosmic principles directly supports the Shaiva understanding of Paramashiva as the ultimate reality. The question is not merely academic but existential — what is the ground of our being?

The instruction to practice yoga and meditation to discover this ground (1.3) aligns with the Shaiva emphasis on direct experiential recognition (*pratyabhijna*) rather than mere intellectual speculation. The teaching that God is "hidden in all beings" resonates with the Shaiva concept of Shiva as *gupta* (hidden) within the field of experience, concealed by His own creative power (*maya-shakti*). The chapter establishes the framework that the remaining five chapters will develop: the one God who is both the cause and the substance of all that exists.
