Chapter 4 is crucial for Shaiva theology, introducing *maya* as Shiva's creative power and presenting the two-birds parable as an image of the soul's relationship to the Lord. Maya is not illusion in the Advaita sense but Shiva's *shakti* — the dynamic creative energy through which consciousness manifests as the material world. The teaching that the great Lord wields *maya* (4.10) places Shiva as the master of the creative process, not its victim.

The two-birds parable (4.6-7) is read through the Shaiva recognition (*pratyabhijna*) doctrine. The eating bird — the individual self caught in karmic experience — and the watching bird — the witnessing Lord — are ultimately one consciousness appearing as two. The moment when the eating bird "sees the glory of the Lord" is the moment of recognition: the individual realizes that its consciousness is not separate from the witnessing consciousness. Liberation is not a change of position but a change of understanding — seeing that one has always been the watching bird.
