Chapter 2's yoga instructions are central to Shaiva practice. The detailed account of meditation posture, breath control (*pranayama*), and sense withdrawal (*pratyahara*) corresponds to the Shaiva yoga taught in texts like the Shiva Sutras and the Vijnana Bhairava Tantra. The teaching that the practitioner should find a quiet, clean, level place and sit with the body erect and the mind focused (2.8-10) establishes the physical foundations of Shaiva contemplative practice.

The vision that arises from this practice — seeing God as "smoke, fire, lightning, crystal, and the moon" (2.11) — describes the progressive experiences (*anubhava*) that arise as Shiva-consciousness reveals itself within meditation. These are not external phenomena but manifestations of the inner light of consciousness as it sheds successive layers of concealment. The culminating vision of God as the light behind all light (2.14-16) is the recognition of Shiva as *prakasha* — the self-luminous consciousness that illuminates all experience.
