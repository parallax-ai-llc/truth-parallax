The two-birds parable — one eating, one watching — is one of the most evocative images in Indian philosophy and finds resonances worldwide. The Christian concept of the soul's "inner witness" (the indwelling Holy Spirit who observes without being tainted by sin), the Sufi distinction between the *nafs* (ego-self) caught in desire and the *ruh* (spirit) that witnesses from above, and the Buddhist concept of mindfulness (awareness observing experience without attachment) all share the parable's fundamental insight: within each person there is both a participant in experience and a witness of experience.

The concept of *maya* as divine creative power resonates with the Kabbalistic *tzimtzum* (God's self-contraction to make space for creation), the Neoplatonic *proodos* (emanation from the One), and the Christian doctrine of *creatio ex nihilo* through the divine Word. Each tradition grapples with the question of how an infinite, perfect God gives rise to a finite, imperfect world — and each posits a creative power or act that mediates between the Absolute and the relative.
