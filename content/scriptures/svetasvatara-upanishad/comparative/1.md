The Svetasvatara's opening inquiry — what is the cause of existence: time, nature, necessity, chance, or God? — anticipates the cosmological debates of medieval Western philosophy with remarkable precision. Thomas Aquinas's Five Ways, the Islamic *kalam* cosmological argument, and Maimonides' *Guide for the Perplexed* all engage with the same set of candidates and arrive at similar theistic conclusions. The convergence suggests that the search for a first cause generates a limited set of logical possibilities.

The chapter's rejection of impersonal principles in favor of a personal God parallels the Aristotelian argument for an Unmoved Mover, the Islamic philosophers' argument for a *Wajib al-Wujud* (Necessary Being), and the Chinese Neo-Confucian debates about whether *li* (principle) or *qi* (material force) is ultimate. Each tradition confronts the question of whether the ground of existence is conscious or unconscious, personal or impersonal — and the Svetasvatara's answer places it firmly in the theistic camp.
