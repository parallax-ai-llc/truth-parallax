The Svetasvatara's declaration "There is one Rudra only" represents a moment of monotheistic crystallization comparable to the Shema in Judaism ("Hear, O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One"), the Islamic *Shahada* ("There is no god but God"), and Akhenaten's hymn to the Aten in ancient Egypt. Each represents a dramatic assertion of divine unity against a polytheistic background, and each became foundational for subsequent theological development.

The description of the one God as creating, maintaining, and destroying the universe parallels the Christian Trinity's appropriation of creation (Father), redemption (Son), and sanctification (Spirit), the Hindu *Trimurti* (Brahma, Vishnu, Shiva), and the Kabbalistic three pillars of the *sefirot*. The threefold cosmic function appears to be a recurrent pattern in how theistic traditions conceptualize divine activity — perhaps reflecting the logical necessity of accounting for origin, continuation, and transformation.
