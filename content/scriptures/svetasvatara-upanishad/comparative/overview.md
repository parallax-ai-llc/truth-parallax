## Overview

The Svetasvatara Upanishad's synthesis of impersonal absolute and personal God represents a philosophical achievement with parallels in many traditions. Its vision of a deity who creates through *maya*, sustains through providence, and liberates through grace resonates with Islamic theology (Allah's creation through divine command), Christian Trinitarian theology (the Father creating through the Logos), and Sikh teaching (Waheguru as both *nirguna* and *saguna*).

## Comparative Themes

The two-birds parable (4.6-7) — the individual soul and the Lord sharing one body like two birds on one tree — invites comparison with Plato's allegory of the charioteer and two horses, the Christian doctrine of the indwelling Holy Spirit, and the Sufi concept of *qalb* (the heart as the meeting place of human and divine). The Svetasvatara's teaching on divine grace as necessary for liberation parallels the Christian Protestant doctrine of *sola gratia*, Ramanuja's *prapatti*, and Shinran's *tariki* (other-power) in Jodo Shinshu Buddhism. The concept of *maya* as divine creative power compares with the Kabbalistic *tzimtzum* and the Neoplatonic *proodos* (procession).
