Chapter 5's exploration of the relationship between individual and supreme selves resonates with the problem of the soul's relationship to God in every theistic tradition. The Christian debate between monergism (God alone acts in salvation) and synergism (human will cooperates with grace) parallels the Svetasvatara's question of whether the soul liberates itself through knowledge or is liberated by divine grace. Islam's tension between *qadr* (divine decree) and *ikhtiyar* (human choice) addresses the same fundamental question.

The description of the soul as "of the size of a thumb" finds a parallel in the Egyptian *ba* (soul-bird) depicted in funerary art as a small entity residing within or near the body, and in the Platonic concept of the soul as a distinct, bounded entity inhabiting the body. The question of the soul's "size" — whether it is finite or infinite, localized or ubiquitous — recurs across traditions and reveals fundamental assumptions about the nature of selfhood and its relationship to the divine.
