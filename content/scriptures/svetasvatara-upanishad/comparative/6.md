The Svetasvatara's culminating vision — a supreme God who is the cause of all causes, known through devotion and accessible through grace — presents a theology remarkably similar to developed theisms across the world. The declaration that God is self-existent, without cause or creator, parallels the Islamic *asma al-husna* (the ninety-nine names of Allah), the Christian doctrine of divine *aseity* (self-existence), and the Jewish understanding of God as the one who "I AM THAT I AM" (Exodus 3:14).

The teaching on grace (6.21) — that liberation comes through God's freely given favor, not through human effort alone — parallels the Christian *sola gratia* doctrine, the Islamic concept of *rahma* (divine mercy), the Pure Land Buddhist reliance on Amitabha's compassionate vow, and the Sikh teaching on *nadar* (divine glance of grace). The convergence of these independent traditions on the concept of divine grace suggests that the encounter with one's own insufficiency — and the corresponding recognition of dependence on a power greater than oneself — is a fundamental dynamic of the religious life across cultures.
