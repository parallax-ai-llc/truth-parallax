## Overview

The Svetasvatara Upanishad belongs to the Krishna Yajur Veda and is named after the sage Svetasvatara who teaches it. It is unique among the principal Upanishads for its explicit theism — it names Rudra-Shiva as the supreme deity and introduces concepts of divine grace (*prasada*) and *maya* (creative illusion) that profoundly shaped later Hindu theology. The text bridges the impersonal Brahman of the earlier Upanishads with the personal God of the devotional traditions.

## Structure

The six chapters develop a theistic philosophy progressively:

1. **Chapter 1**: The philosophical problem — what is the cause of existence? — and the method of meditation
2. **Chapter 2**: Yoga and meditation as means to realize God
3. **Chapter 3**: The one God (Rudra) who creates, sustains, and withdraws the universe
4. **Chapter 4**: Maya, the divine creative power, and God's relationship to the world
5. **Chapter 5**: The two selves — the individual soul and the supreme self — and their relationship
6. **Chapter 6**: The culminating vision of God as the cause of all causes, attainable by grace

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Vaishnavism**: The text's theism is claimed for Vishnu; Rudra is read as a name for Narayana
- **Shaivism**: The primary Upanishadic authority for Shiva's supremacy and the foundation of Shaiva Siddhanta
- **Advaita Vedanta**: Shankara reads the theistic language as provisional, pointing ultimately to impersonal Brahman
- **Dvaita Vedanta**: A powerful source for the teaching of a personal God distinct from individual souls
- **Academic**: Key evidence for the emergence of sectarian theism within the Vedic tradition
- **Comparative**: The doctrine of grace invites comparison with Christian, Islamic, and Pure Land Buddhist soteriology

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Personal God | Rudra-Shiva as the supreme, conscious cause of the universe |
| Maya | The creative power by which God projects the phenomenal world |
| Divine Grace | Liberation depends on God's *prasada*, not human effort alone |
| Two Birds | The parable of two birds on one tree — the individual soul and the Lord |
| Yoga | Meditation and self-discipline as the means to divine realization |
