Shankara reads Chapter 3's theistic declarations as provisional teachings (*upadhi-vishishta Brahman*) — descriptions of Brahman conditioned by *maya* for the sake of the devotee. The one Rudra who creates, maintains, and destroys the universe is Brahman in Its role as *Ishvara* (the Lord), a functional description rather than an ultimate characterization. From the absolute standpoint, Brahman has no attributes and performs no actions — It simply is.

The identification of Rudra with the cosmic *purusha* is treated as a meditation (*upasana*) rather than a literal theological claim. Shankara argues that the Upanishad uses the name "Rudra" not to advocate a sectarian position but as a designation for the supreme principle, just as other Upanishads use Brahman, Atman, or Prajapati. The key phrase "the inner self of all beings" (3.11) points beyond the theistic framework to the Advaita truth: the one reality is the self of all, and all apparent multiplicity is *maya*.
