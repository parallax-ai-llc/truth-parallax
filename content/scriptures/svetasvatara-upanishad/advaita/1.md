Shankara reads Chapter 1's philosophical inquiry as establishing the need for a single, non-dual cause of the universe. The systematic rejection of partial explanations — time, nature, elements, chance — demonstrates that no finite principle can serve as the ultimate ground. The chapter points toward Brahman, which Shankara identifies not with the personal deity described in later chapters (a provisional teaching) but with the attributeless Absolute that transcends all categories.

The instruction to practice yoga and meditation (1.3) is read as a preliminary discipline that purifies the mind for the reception of Brahman-knowledge. The theistic language — God ruling over cosmic principles, hidden in all beings — describes Brahman from the empirical standpoint (*vyavaharika*), where the distinction between God and world appears real. From the absolute standpoint (*paramarthika*), there is no ruler and no ruled, no hidden and no manifest — there is only Brahman, the one without a second.
