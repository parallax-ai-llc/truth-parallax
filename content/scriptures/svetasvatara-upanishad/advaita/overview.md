## Overview

Shankara's interpretation of the Svetasvatara Upanishad reads its theistic language as a concession to the devotee's level of understanding (*adhikari-bheda*), while its ultimate teaching points to the impersonal, attributeless Brahman. The name "Rudra" refers not to a sectarian deity but to Brahman in Its capacity as the cosmic principle. The Upanishad's explicit theism is treated as preparatory — it leads the aspirant from devotion to a personal God toward the higher realization of non-dual Brahman.

## Theological Emphases

The Svetasvatara's concept of *maya* is foundational for Advaita. Shankara reads passages like 4.9-10 as establishing that the phenomenal world is a projection of Brahman's *maya-shakti*, which has no independent reality. The two-birds parable (4.6-7) is interpreted not as two eternally distinct beings but as the one self in two aspects — the empirical self caught in karma and the witnessing self that is Brahman. When the lower bird "sees the Lord and His glory" and realizes its identity, the duality is dissolved. The teaching on grace (6.21) is read as the self's own nature revealing itself when ignorance is removed.
