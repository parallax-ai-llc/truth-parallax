Shankara reads Chapter 5's discussion of the individual and supreme selves as a teaching that must be understood in light of the two levels of truth (*paramarthika* and *vyavaharika*). From the empirical standpoint, the individual self appears as a distinct entity bound by the *gunas* and dwelling in the body. From the absolute standpoint, there is only one self — Brahman — and the appearance of individuality is the product of *maya*.

The teaching that the individual self is "of the size of a thumb" (5.8) is treated by Shankara as a meditation (*upasana*) aid — a way of focusing attention on the self's presence within the heart — rather than a literal description of the self's size. Brahman is infinite and cannot have a size. The Samkhya terminology of *gunas* and *prakriti* is accepted provisionally: from the empirical standpoint, the self appears bound by the *gunas*, but from the absolute standpoint, the self was never bound. Knowledge of this truth is liberation.
