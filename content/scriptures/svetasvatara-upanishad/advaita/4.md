Chapter 4's introduction of *maya* is foundational for Advaita Vedanta. Shankara reads the teaching that *prakriti* is *maya* and the Lord (*mahesvara*) is its wielder (4.10) as establishing that the phenomenal world is a projection of Brahman's creative power — real from the empirical standpoint but ultimately without independent existence. Maya is neither real (since it is transcended in liberation) nor unreal (since it produces genuine experience) but *anirvachaniya* (indescribable in terms of being or non-being).

The two-birds parable (4.6-7) is Shankara's key image for the relationship between the empirical self (*jiva*) and the absolute self (*Brahman*). The eating bird is the self identified with body and mind, experiencing karma's fruits. The watching bird is the witness-consciousness, ever-free and uninvolved. When the eating bird "sees the Lord" — that is, when the *jiva* realizes its identity with the witness — it is freed from sorrow. The two birds are not really two selves but one self appearing as two due to the superimposition of *maya*.
