Shankara reads Chapter 6's culminating vision of God as a description of *saguna Brahman* (Brahman with attributes) — the highest object of devotion and meditation, but not the ultimate reality. The enumeration of divine attributes (omniscience, omnipotence, self-sufficiency) belongs to the empirical level of truth, where the distinction between God and world appears real. From the absolute standpoint, Brahman has no attributes, no activity, and no relationship to any world.

The verse on grace (6.21) is interpreted by Shankara not as the intervention of an external deity but as the self's own nature revealing itself when the obstacles of ignorance are removed. "Grace" is the spontaneous self-luminosity of Brahman, which shines forth when *avidya* is destroyed by knowledge. The "bridge to immortality" (6.20) is not a personal God carrying souls across but Brahman-knowledge itself, which bridges the apparent gap between the individual and the Absolute. The chapter's theistic language is a concession to the devotee's level of understanding, pointing toward but not yet expressing the ultimate non-dual truth.
