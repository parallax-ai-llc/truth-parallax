Shankara reads Chapter 2's yoga instructions as preparatory practices (*sadhana*) that purify the mind and prepare it for the dawn of Brahman-knowledge. The physical and mental disciplines described — posture, breath control, concentration — are not the direct means to liberation but remove the obstacles (*avarana* and *vikshepa*) that prevent the self's recognition of its own nature. The mind must be made still and pure before it can serve as a mirror reflecting Brahman.

The visions described in 2.11 — smoke, fire, lightning — are treated by Shankara as preliminary experiences that arise during meditation but are not the goal. They belong to the realm of phenomenal experience and must be transcended. The true realization is beyond all visions: it is the self's recognition of its identity with Brahman, which is not an experience (something that happens to a subject) but the cessation of the illusion that the subject is separate from the Absolute.
