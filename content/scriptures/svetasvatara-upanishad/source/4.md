---
title: "Svetasvatara Upanishad - Chapter 4"
scripture: "svetasvatara-upanishad"
chapter: 4
---

**1** He who is one, without any colour, by the manifold application of his power distributes many colours according to his hidden purpose, and into whom at the beginning and end the universe is gathered — may He endow us with good thoughts.

**2** That itself is the fire, that is the sun, that is the air, that is the moon; that also is the seed, Brahman, the waters, Prajapati.

**3** Thou art woman, thou art man; thou art the youth and the maiden; thou, as an old man, totterest along on a staff; thou art born with thy face turned everywhere.

**4** Thou art the dark blue bird, thou art the green parrot with red eyes. Thou art the thundercloud, the seasons, the seas. Thou art without beginning, and beyond all time and space. Thou art He from whom all worlds are born.

**5** There is one unborn female being, red, white, and black, producing many offspring like herself. There is one unborn male who loves her and stays by her side; there is another unborn male who leaves her after he has had his enjoyment.

**6** Two birds of beautiful plumage, inseparable friends, dwell upon one and the same tree. Of these two, one eats the sweet fruit, and the other looks on without eating.

**7** Dwelling on the same tree, the individual soul becomes entangled and feels misery, being deluded on account of his not being the Lord. But when he beholds the other, the Lord, whom all worship, and His glory, then his grief passes away.

**8** He who does not know that indestructible being of the Rig-Veda, that highest ether-like Self wherein all the gods reside — of what use is the Rig-Veda to him? Those who know it rest satisfied.

**9** That from which the maker sends forth all this — the sacred verses, the offerings, the sacrifices, the ceremonies, all that was, all that shall be, and all that the Vedas declare — in that the other is bound up through that illusion.

**10** Know then that nature is illusion, and that the great Lord is the illusion-maker. This whole world is pervaded with beings that are parts of Him.

**11** By meditating on Him, by uniting with Him, and by becoming one with Him, there is cessation of all illusion in the end.

**12** By knowing God there is a falling off of all fetters; when the sufferings are destroyed, there is cessation of birth and death. By meditating on Him, there is the third state; on the dissolution of the body, there is universal lordship. Being alone, his desire is fulfilled.

**13** That which is to be known, the eternal, truly exists. That beyond which there is nothing — that which is the innermost Self of all, the distinguisher of size and number — when one knows Him, one is freed from fetters. Thus spoke the teaching.

**14** There is nothing higher than or different from Him; nothing greater or more minute than He. Like a tree He stands, rooted in heaven. By Him, the Person, is this whole universe filled.

**15** That which is beyond this world is without form and without suffering. Those who know it become immortal, but others go only to sorrow.

**16** He, the Lord of all, the omniscient, the inner controller, the source of all, the creator, sustainer, and destroyer of all beings — He dwells in the heart.

**17** That which has hands and feet everywhere, that which has eyes, heads, and mouths everywhere, that which has hearing everywhere — He exists in the world enveloping all.

**18** Appearing as though having the qualities of all the senses, yet devoid of all senses, He is the ruler of all, the lord of all, the great refuge of all.

**19** The embodied one, within the city of nine gates, flies forth outward into the whole world, the controller of the whole world, of all that is stationary and all that moves.

**20** Without hands and feet He grasps and moves. He sees without eyes, He hears without ears. He knows all that is to be known, but no one knows Him. They call Him the first, the great Person.

**21** Subtler than the subtlest, greater than the greatest, the Self is set in the heart of every creature. The one who is free from desire, with mind and senses composed, beholds the majesty of the Self and becomes free from sorrow.

**22** I know this undecaying, ancient one, the Self of all things, which exists everywhere being infinite. Of whom they declare there is stoppage of birth, for the theologians proclaim Him to be eternal.
