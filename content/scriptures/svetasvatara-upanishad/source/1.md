---
title: "Svetasvatara Upanishad - Chapter 1"
scripture: "svetasvatara-upanishad"
chapter: 1
---

**1** What is the cause of this universe? Is it Brahman? Whence do we come? By what do we live? On what are we established? O knowers of Brahman, governed by whom do we live in pleasure and in pain, each in our respective situation?

**2** Should time, or nature, or necessity, or chance, or the elements be considered as the cause? Or he who is called the Purusha, the Supreme Spirit?

**3** The sages, absorbed in meditation through one-pointedness of mind, discovered the creative power, belonging to the Lord Himself and hidden in its own qualities. That one Lord rules over all those causes — time, the self, and the rest.

**4** Him we regard as a wheel with one rim, with three tires, sixteen ends, fifty spokes, twenty counter-spokes, six sets of eight, whose one rope is manifold, which has three different paths, and whose illusion arises from two causes.

**5** We meditate on Him as a river of five streams, from five sources, fierce and crooked, whose waves are the five vital breaths, whose original source is the fivefold perception, with five whirlpools, an impetuous flood of fivefold misery, divided into fifty kinds and with five branches.

**6** In this vast wheel of Brahman, which enlivens all things, in which all rest, the soul flutters about thinking that the self in him and the Mover are different. Then when blessed by Him, he gains immortality.

**7** This has been declared: the Supreme Brahman is in the Vedas, the imperishable. The beings who dwell therein as the knowers of Brahman, being absorbed in it, are freed from birth.

**8** The Lord supports this universe, which consists of a combination of the perishable and the imperishable, the manifest and the unmanifest. As long as the self does not know the Lord, it is attached to worldly pleasures and is bound; but when it knows Him, all fetters fall away.

**9** There are two, the one knowing and the other not-knowing, both unborn, the one strong and the other weak. There is the unborn one through whom each man receives the recompense of his works. And there is the infinite Self, appearing in all forms, but Himself inactive. When a man finds out these three, that is Brahman.

**10** What is perishable is the Pradhana. What is immortal and imperishable is Hara. The one God rules over the perishable and the soul. By meditating on Him, by union with Him, and by entering into His being more and more, there is finally cessation from every illusion.

**11** When God is known, all fetters fall off; sufferings are destroyed, and birth and death cease. By meditating on Him there is a third state: on the dissolution of the body, universal lordship. Being alone, his desire is fulfilled.

**12** That Eternal which rests in the self should be known. Truly there is nothing beyond this to be known. By knowing the enjoyer, the enjoyed, and the ruler, everything has been said. This is the threefold Brahman.

**13** As the form of fire while it exists in the under-wood is not seen, and yet its seed is not destroyed, but has to be seized again and again by means of the stick and the under-wood, so it is in both cases, and the Self has to be seized in the body by means of the syllable Om.

**14** By making one's body the under-wood and the syllable Om the upper-wood, by practicing the friction of meditation, one may see the God who is hidden, as it were.

**15** As oil in sesame seeds, as butter in cream, as water in springs, as fire in fire-sticks, so is the Self seized in one's own soul, if one looks for Him with truthfulness and austerity.

**16** The Self, which pervades all things as butter is contained in cream, which is rooted in self-knowledge and austerity — this is Brahman, the highest teaching. This is Brahman, the highest teaching.
