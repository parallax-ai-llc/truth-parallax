Madhva finds in Chapter 1 powerful support for the Dvaita doctrine of a personal, sovereign God. The systematic elimination of impersonal principles — time, nature, necessity, chance, elements — as ultimate causes demonstrates that only a conscious, willful being can account for creation. This being is Vishnu (whom Madhva identifies behind the name Rudra used later in the text), the supreme Lord who rules over all cosmic principles by His sovereign will.

The teaching that God is "hidden in all beings" (1.3) describes Vishnu as the inner controller who governs creation from within while remaining distinct from it. The chapter's emphasis on seeking God through meditation and yoga is read as an instruction for devotional practice — the disciplined focusing of the mind on the Lord's nature and attributes. The philosophical rigor of the opening inquiry demonstrates that theism is not naive faith but the conclusion of careful rational analysis.
