Madhva reads Chapter 4's teaching on *maya* as describing God's real creative power — not illusion but the material energy through which Vishnu constructs the real world. The declaration that the Lord wields *maya* (4.10) proves that God is the master of material nature, not identical with it. Maya is subordinate to God, a tool He uses, not a power that conceals His nature.

The two-birds parable (4.6-7) is a cornerstone of Dvaita theology. The two birds are genuinely two — the individual soul and the Supreme Lord — sitting on the same tree (the body). The eating bird (the soul) experiences karma while the watching bird (Vishnu) observes without being affected. Liberation occurs when the soul "sees the Lord" — recognizes Vishnu's presence and surrenders to Him — but this does not make the soul identical with God. The two birds remain two; what changes is the lower bird's relationship to the higher: from ignorance and independence to knowledge and loving dependence.
