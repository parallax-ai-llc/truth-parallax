## Overview

Madhva's Dvaita reading of the Svetasvatara Upanishad embraces its theistic vision while identifying the supreme God not as Shiva but as Vishnu under the name Rudra. The Upanishad's emphatic insistence on a personal God who creates by will, rules by sovereignty, and liberates by grace provides strong support for the Dvaita position against Advaita's impersonal Brahman. The soul's eternal difference from God is demonstrated by the two-birds parable, where one bird eats while the other watches — two distinct beings sharing one tree.

## Theological Emphases

The Svetasvatara's concept of *maya* is read by Madhva not as cosmic illusion but as God's real creative power — the material energy (*prakriti*) through which Vishnu produces the universe. The world is real, not an illusory superimposition. The teaching on grace (6.21) confirms the Dvaita principle that liberation depends entirely on God's will and cannot be achieved by knowledge alone. The soul who "sees the Lord" in the two-birds parable does not become identical with God but enters into a relationship of eternal devotion and service, experiencing God's bliss while remaining a distinct individual.
