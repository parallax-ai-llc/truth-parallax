Chapter 5's detailed account of the individual self's relationship to the supreme self directly supports the Dvaita position. The individual self is described as distinct from, subordinate to, and dependent upon the supreme self. The metaphor of the self being "of the size of a thumb" dwelling in the heart demonstrates that the soul is a real, finite entity — not an infinite Brahman under illusion. The soul's finitude is its essential nature, not a defect to be overcome.

The binding of the soul by the three *gunas* (5.7) is a real condition, not an illusion, and liberation from this binding requires the grace of the supreme Lord, not merely the soul's intellectual recognition of its supposed identity with Brahman. The chapter's Samkhya-like framework is absorbed into the Dvaita system: *prakriti* and the *gunas* are real, the soul is real, and God is real — three eternally distinct categories united only by the soul's devotion to and God's sovereignty over both soul and nature.
