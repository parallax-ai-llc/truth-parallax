Madhva reads Chapter 2 as teaching the devotional practice of focused meditation on Vishnu. The yoga instructions — posture, breath control, concentration — are means of directing the mind toward God with single-pointed attention. Unlike Advaita, which treats meditation as preparatory to impersonal knowledge, Dvaita holds that meditation *is* the practice of devotion — the disciplined focusing of the soul's love and attention on the Lord.

The visions described in 2.11 (smoke, fire, lightning) are real experiences granted by Vishnu's grace to the devoted practitioner. They are signs of progress, not illusions to be dismissed. The teaching that God is seen "as oil in sesame seeds, as butter in cream" (2.15) demonstrates that God is always present within creation but requires the effort of devotional practice — like churning cream — to be recognized. The chapter establishes that the knowledge of God is experiential, not merely intellectual.
