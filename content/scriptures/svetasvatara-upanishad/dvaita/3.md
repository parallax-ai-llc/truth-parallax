Chapter 3's declaration "There is one Rudra only" provides powerful support for Dvaita's insistence on a single, supreme, personal God. Madhva reads "Rudra" as a designation for Vishnu, who alone possesses the power to create, maintain, and destroy the universe. The chapter's enumeration of divine attributes — omniscience, omnipresence, sovereignty, self-luminosity — describes the God of Dvaita theology: a being of infinite perfections who is eternally distinct from the souls and the world He governs.

The cosmic *purusha* description (3.14-16) is identified with Vishnu's universal form (*vishvarupa*), demonstrating that God pervades all of creation while remaining its sovereign Lord. The teaching that this God is "the inner self of all beings" (3.11) is read not as identity (the Advaita interpretation) but as indwelling: Vishnu is present within all beings as their inner controller, sustaining and governing them while remaining a distinct, supreme person.
