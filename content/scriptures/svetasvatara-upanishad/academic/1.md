Chapter 1 presents one of the most sophisticated philosophical discussions in the Upanishads, systematically examining and rejecting candidate first causes before arriving at a theistic conclusion. Scholars have noted the remarkable resemblance to early Samkhya categories in the list of candidates (time, self-nature, necessity) and have debated whether the Svetasvatara is engaging with an early form of Samkhya philosophy or drawing from a common pool of philosophical terminology.

The chapter's conclusion — that a personal God, rather than an impersonal principle, is the first cause — represents a significant development in Upanishadic thought. Earlier Upanishads tend toward impersonal formulations of the Absolute (Brahman, Atman, Sat), while the Svetasvatara explicitly argues for a conscious, personal deity. This transition has been analyzed as reflecting broader developments in Indian religious culture during the 4th-3rd century BCE, including the emergence of devotional theism that would later flower in the Bhakti movement.
