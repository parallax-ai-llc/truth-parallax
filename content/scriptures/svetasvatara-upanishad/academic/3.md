Chapter 3 contains the Svetasvatara's most explicitly theistic material, with its repeated identification of the supreme being as Rudra. Scholars have debated whether this represents a genuinely sectarian Shaiva position or a broader Vedic theism that employs Rudra as a traditional name for the supreme deity. The chapter's heavy reliance on Purusha Sukta (RV 10.90) material — the cosmic person with a thousand heads, a thousand eyes — suggests an attempt to claim Rig Vedic authority for the emerging Rudra-Shiva theology.

The language and style of Chapter 3 differ from the philosophical inquiry of Chapter 1, suggesting possible composite authorship or layered composition. The declaration *eko hi rudro* has been analyzed as a polemic against polytheistic interpretations of the Vedas, asserting that the apparently multiple gods are manifestations of one supreme deity. This monotheistic or "monolatrous" move parallels similar developments in Israelite religion (the Deuteronomic reform) and in Greek philosophy (Xenophanes' critique of anthropomorphic polytheism).
