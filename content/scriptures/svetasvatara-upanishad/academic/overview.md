## Overview

The Svetasvatara Upanishad is typically dated to the 4th-3rd century BCE, making it one of the later principal Upanishads. It is remarkable for its synthesis of multiple philosophical currents — Upanishadic monism, proto-Samkhya dualism, Yoga practice, and Shaiva devotional theism — into a coherent theological vision. The text represents a crucial transitional moment when the abstract Brahman of the earlier Upanishads was being identified with a personal, named deity.

## Research Themes

Scholarly debate centers on the text's relationship to the development of Hindu sectarianism. Some scholars (Oberlies, Bronkhorst) see it as the earliest evidence of a distinct Shaiva theological tradition, while others argue its theism is more broadly Vedic than specifically sectarian. The Upanishad's Samkhya-like terminology (*purusha*, *prakriti*, *gunas*) has raised questions about its relationship to the classical Samkhya system — whether it represents a proto-Samkhya that was still theistic or an independent synthesis. The concept of *maya* here differs significantly from Shankara's later usage and appears closer to "creative power" than "cosmic illusion."
