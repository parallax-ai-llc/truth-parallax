Chapter 4 introduces two concepts that profoundly shaped later Hindu philosophy: *maya* as divine creative power and the two-birds parable. The concept of *maya* here (4.9-10) differs significantly from its later Advaita usage. In the Svetasvatara, *maya* appears closer to "creative power" or "artistic skill" than to "cosmic illusion" — it is the means by which God creates, not a veil that conceals reality. The later transformation of *maya* from creative power to illusion represents one of the most significant conceptual shifts in Indian philosophy.

The two-birds parable (4.6-7) appears also in the Rig Veda (1.164.20) and the Mundaka Upanishad (3.1.1-2). Its original meaning is debated: was it initially a Samkhya-like dualism (consciousness and nature as eternally distinct), a proto-Advaita teaching (the two birds as one self in two modes), or a theistic teaching (individual soul and supreme Lord)? The Svetasvatara's context strongly favors the theistic reading, but the image's polyvalence has allowed all three interpretations to claim it.
