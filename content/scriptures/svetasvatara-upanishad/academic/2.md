Chapter 2 contains one of the earliest systematic descriptions of yoga practice in Indian literature, predating Patanjali's Yoga Sutras by several centuries. The instructions on posture, breath control, and mental concentration (2.8-13) provide important evidence for the development of yoga as a formal spiritual discipline. Scholars debate whether these instructions describe an independent Upanishadic yoga or reflect practices already associated with Shaiva or proto-Samkhya traditions.

The phenomenological descriptions of meditative experience — visions of smoke, fire, lightning, crystal, and the moon (2.11) — are notable for their specificity and suggest that the author had direct experience of meditation. These descriptions have been compared with similar accounts in Buddhist meditation manuals (the *nimitta* or meditation signs described in the *Visuddhimagga*) and in Daoist inner alchemy texts. The chapter's integration of practical yoga instruction with theological claims about a personal God is distinctive and represents an important stage in the development of Indian theistic spirituality.
