Section 1's declaration that the mind causes both bondage and liberation represents an important moment in the development of Indian philosophy of mind. This teaching — which later becomes central to both Yoga and Vedanta — locates the problem of suffering in psychological rather than cosmological or ritual terms. The shift from ritual impurity to mental impurity as the fundamental obstacle to liberation marks a decisive turn in Indian religious thought.

Scholars note the text's economy: in a few verses, it establishes both a diagnosis (the mind's attachment causes suffering) and the beginning of a cure (freeing the mind from attachment). This conciseness suggests a teaching designed for memorization and repeated contemplation.
