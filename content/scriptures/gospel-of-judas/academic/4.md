## The Betrayal Scene: Translation Controversies

The final section of the Gospel of Judas (Codex Tchacos pp. 55–58) contains the most contested passage in the entire text. Jesus's statement to Judas — variously translated as "you will exceed all of them" (National Geographic) or "you will do worse than all of them" (DeConick) — hinges on the Coptic verb and its context. The phrase that follows, "for you will sacrifice the man that clothes me," is grammatically clear but theologically ambiguous: is Judas performing a salvific service or committing the ultimate sacrilege?

The debate between the "positive Judas" and "negative Judas" readings has become one of the most visible scholarly controversies in recent biblical studies. Marvin Meyer and Elaine Pagels read the text as presenting Judas as a Gnostic hero who assists Jesus's liberation from the body. April DeConick and Louis Painchaud argue that Judas is a tragic or demonic figure who serves the archons, not the true God. Lance Jenott's 2011 critical edition attempts a mediating position, emphasizing the text's literary complexity and resistance to simple hero/villain categories.

The text ends abruptly with Judas handing Jesus over to the high priests. The absence of a crucifixion narrative, resurrection account, or post-Easter commission distinguishes the Gospel of Judas from canonical and most non-canonical Gospel literature. This abrupt closure has been interpreted as either a deliberate literary choice (the liberation is accomplished at the moment of handover) or a consequence of the text's fragmentary state.
