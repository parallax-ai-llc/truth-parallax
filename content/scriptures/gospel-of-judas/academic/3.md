## The Cosmogonic Section: Sources and Parallels

The cosmogonic revelation (Codex Tchacos pp. 47–53) has attracted the most intense scholarly attention, both for its theological content and for the severe damage to the manuscript at crucial points. The passage presents a Sethian cosmogony featuring the Great Invisible Spirit, the Barbelo aeon, the Self-Generated (Autogenes), four luminaries, Adamas, Seth, and the generation of seventy-two luminaries and 360 firmaments.

John Turner's systematic comparison with other Sethian cosmogonies (the Apocryphon of John, the Gospel of the Egyptians, Zostrianos, the Three Steles of Seth) has established the Gospel of Judas as belonging to the "classic" phase of Sethian thought, before the movement's engagement with Neoplatonism produced the more philosophically oriented texts (Allogenes, Zostrianos, Marsanes). The cosmogonic section's vocabulary and structure most closely parallel the shorter version of the Apocryphon of John (NHC III,1 and BG 8502,2).

The figures of Saklas and Nebro as co-creators of the material world represent a distinctive variation within Sethian cosmogony. In the Apocryphon of John, Yaldabaoth serves as the primary demiurge; the Gospel of Judas distributes demiurgical activity between two figures, possibly reflecting a different stage or branch of Sethian theological development.
