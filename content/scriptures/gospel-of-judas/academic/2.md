## The Temple Vision: Literary and Historical Analysis

The Temple vision sequence (Codex Tchacos pp. 38–41) presents significant textual and interpretive challenges. Several lines are fragmentary, and the precise details of the disciples' vision depend on scholarly reconstruction. The identification of twelve priests at the altar with the twelve disciples is explicit in the text, but the specifics of their transgressions are partially lost to lacunae.

Form-critically, the passage belongs to the genre of visionary interpretation — a form well attested in Jewish apocalyptic literature (Daniel 2, 4 Ezra, 2 Baruch) and employed elsewhere in Gnostic texts (Pistis Sophia, the Apocalypse of Peter [NHC VII,3]). The vision-interpretation structure creates a hermeneutical hierarchy in which Jesus possesses the authoritative key to symbolic meaning and the disciples require instruction.

The passage's critique of sacrifice has been compared to other early Christian anti-sacrificial rhetoric (Hebrews, the Epistle of Barnabas), but scholars note that the Gospel of Judas goes further than any canonical or sub-apostolic text in rejecting the sacrificial framework altogether. Rather than spiritualizing sacrifice (as Hebrews does), the text identifies it with demiurgical worship. This position aligns the Gospel of Judas with the most radical forms of second-century anti-Judaism and anti-institutional Christianity.
