## Codex Tchacos and the Opening Scene

The opening section of the Gospel of Judas (Codex Tchacos pp. 33–36) introduces the text's dramatic setting: Jesus appears among his disciples during "the three days before he celebrated Passover." The temporal frame places the revelation in the final days before the passion, creating a compressed narrative urgency. The incipit — "The secret account of the revelation that Jesus spoke in conversation with Judas Iscariot during a week three days before he celebrated Passover" — identifies the text as belonging to the genre of secret revelation dialogues well attested in the Nag Hammadi library.

The eucharistic scene that follows raises important questions for scholars of early Christian liturgy. Jesus laughs at the disciples' prayer of thanksgiving (*eucharistia*) over bread, implying that their ritual is misdirected. Scholars debate whether this reflects a genuine critique of second-century eucharistic practice or a literary device serving the text's anti-ecclesiastical agenda. The scene's relationship to the eucharistic traditions reflected in the Didache, 1 Corinthians 11, and Justin Martyr's *First Apology* remains a productive area of investigation.

The challenge to produce the "perfect human" and Judas's partial response (he can stand before Jesus but must turn his face away) have been interpreted differently by scholars. The National Geographic team read Judas's response positively; DeConick argues that his inability to meet Jesus's gaze signals spiritual inadequacy rather than humility.
