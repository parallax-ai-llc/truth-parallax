## Temple Critique and the Rejection of Sacrifice

The Gospel of Judas's wholesale rejection of sacrificial worship invites comparison with anti-sacrificial movements across religious traditions. The Hebrew prophets (Isaiah, Amos, Micah) critiqued empty sacrifice while affirming the covenantal relationship with God. The Buddhist rejection of Vedic animal sacrifice (ahimsa) reframed spiritual practice around meditation and ethical conduct. Guru Nanak's critique of Brahmanical ritual purity in Sikhism redirected devotion toward the divine Name (*Naam*).

In each case, the critique of external sacrifice points toward an interiorization of worship — a move from outward ritual to inward transformation. The Gospel of Judas participates in this cross-cultural pattern, though it goes further than most by identifying the God who receives sacrifice as an inferior, ignorant deity. This additional cosmological dimension distinguishes Gnostic anti-sacrificial rhetoric from prophetic critique, which remains internal to the relationship with a single God.

The vision of the Temple with its corrupt priests also resonates with the motif of institutional corruption found across traditions — the critique of the Pharisees in canonical Christianity, the Buddhist concept of *mappo* (the degenerate age of the dharma), the Sufi distinction between the scholar of the letter (*alim*) and the knower of the heart (*arif*).
