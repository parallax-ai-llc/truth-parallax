## Cosmogonic Myths: Creating and Explaining the World

The Sethian cosmogony revealed to Judas belongs to a vast cross-cultural tradition of origin narratives that explain how the world came to be and why it contains suffering. The text's distinctive feature — the claim that the material world is the product of an ignorant or malevolent creator — finds parallels in several traditions.

Zoroastrian cosmology posits two primordial spirits, Spenta Mainyu (beneficent) and Angra Mainyu (destructive), whose cosmic conflict shapes the material world. Manichaean cosmogony describes the invasion of the Realm of Light by the forces of Darkness, resulting in the trapping of divine light-particles in matter. Hindu cosmology in the Puranas describes cycles of creation and dissolution in which the material world emerges from divine play (*lila*) but also involves the descent of souls into *samsara* (the cycle of rebirth).

The Gospel of Judas shares with these traditions the conviction that the present condition of the world requires explanation — that existence as we find it cannot be the direct and unmediated expression of the highest divine will. Where the traditions diverge is in their assessment of the material world's ultimate value and the possibility of its redemption.
