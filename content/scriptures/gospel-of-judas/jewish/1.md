## The Eucharistic Scene and Jewish Table Fellowship

The opening scene of the Gospel of Judas depicts Jesus laughing at his disciples' prayer of thanksgiving over bread — a scene that, from a Jewish perspective, invokes and distorts the traditions of *berakhah* (blessing) and communal table fellowship central to Jewish religious life. The *birkat ha-mazon* (grace after meals) and the Shabbat *kiddush* represent forms of thanksgiving over bread and wine that long predate Christian eucharistic practice.

The text's critique of the disciples' prayer raises questions about the relationship between Jewish and Christian ritual forms in the second century. Scholars such as Andrew McGowan and Paul Bradshaw have demonstrated that early Christian meal practices were diverse and that the standardization of the eucharist as a distinct sacrament was a gradual process. The Gospel of Judas's hostility toward eucharistic worship may reflect intra-Christian debates about the proper form of communal meals — debates that inevitably involved the renegotiation of Jewish heritage.

For Jewish readers, the scene illustrates how Jewish liturgical forms were simultaneously adopted and alienated by emerging Christianity — a process visible across the spectrum of early Christian literature, from the Didache's prayer forms (clearly modeled on Jewish *berakhot*) to the Gospel of John's progressive distancing from "the Jews." The Gospel of Judas represents an extreme case: Jewish-derived liturgical practice is not merely transformed but condemned as worship of a false god.
