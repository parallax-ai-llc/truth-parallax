## Temple Imagery and Prophetic Tradition

The Temple vision in the Gospel of Judas draws on imagery deeply rooted in Jewish tradition while deploying it for anti-Jewish purposes. The twelve priests at the altar recall the Levitical priesthood, and the sacrificial activity described — including the shocking mention of child sacrifice — echoes the prophetic denunciations of Molech worship (Leviticus 18:21, 2 Kings 23:10, Jeremiah 32:35) and the broader prophetic critique of cultic corruption.

Jewish readers will note both the continuities and the distortions. The Hebrew prophets critiqued sacrificial practice from within the covenant relationship — calling Israel back to justice, mercy, and genuine devotion rather than rejecting sacrifice per se. Isaiah's "I do not delight in the blood of bulls" (1:11) is followed by "learn to do good; seek justice, rescue the oppressed" (1:17). The prophetic critique presupposes the validity of the covenantal framework.

The Gospel of Judas, by contrast, rejects the entire covenantal framework by identifying the God who established the Temple cult with the ignorant archon Saklas. This move transforms prophetic critique into cosmological rejection — a shift that represents one of the most radical forms of anti-Jewish theology in ancient literature. For Jewish scholars, the text illustrates how Gnostic movements appropriated prophetic rhetoric while fundamentally betraying its covenantal logic.
