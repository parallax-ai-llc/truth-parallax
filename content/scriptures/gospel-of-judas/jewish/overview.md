## Overview

From a Jewish perspective, the Gospel of Judas is significant for its extensive use and radical transformation of Jewish scriptural traditions. The text's cosmogony draws heavily on Genesis, its angelology borrows from Jewish apocalyptic literature, and its critique of Temple sacrifice echoes — while fundamentally distorting — prophetic critiques found within the Hebrew Bible itself. The text illustrates how second-century Gnostic movements appropriated Jewish materials while often inverting their meaning.

## Key Interpretive Principles

### Transformation of Genesis

The figures of Adam (Adamas) and Seth are central to the text's cosmogony, but they are transformed into heavenly archetypes rather than earthly ancestors. The Sethian appropriation of Jewish genealogical tradition creates a spiritual lineage that bypasses biological descent — a move that simultaneously depends on and subverts the Jewish emphasis on covenant and peoplehood.

### The Creator as Fool

The text identifies the creator of the material world as Saklas — an Aramaic word meaning "fool." This radical devaluation of the creator God represents a direct inversion of Jewish theology, in which creation is declared "very good" (Genesis 1:31) and the Creator is worthy of praise. For Jewish readers, this identification of the God of Israel with an ignorant archon is one of the text's most provocative claims.

### Temple Sacrifice

The disciples' vision of the Temple and Jesus's interpretation of its priests as misleading sacrificers echoes prophetic critiques (Isaiah 1:11-17, Amos 5:21-24) but extends them into a wholesale rejection of sacrificial worship — going far beyond anything in the Hebrew prophetic tradition.

## Theological Emphases

- **Jewish materials in non-Jewish contexts**: The text demonstrates how Jewish scriptures were read against their original intention by Gnostic interpreters
- **Angelology**: The archons and luminaries of the text draw on Jewish angelological traditions (1 Enoch, 2 Enoch, Jubilees) while radically reframing them
- **Anti-Judaism or internal critique?**: The text's attitude toward the God of Israel remains a subject of scholarly debate
