## Jewish Angelology and Gnostic Transformation

The cosmogonic section of the Gospel of Judas makes extensive use of Jewish angelological traditions, transforming them into a Sethian cosmological system. The luminaries (Harmozel, Oroiael, Daveithai, Eleleth), the figure of Adamas as a heavenly archetype, and the role of Seth as progenitor of a spiritual race all derive from Jewish interpretive traditions surrounding Genesis and the early chapters of 1 Enoch.

The *hekhalot* and *merkabah* literature of late antique Judaism describes elaborate heavenly hierarchies, angelic beings with specific names and functions, and the soul's ascent through celestial palaces — themes that resonate with the Gospel of Judas's cosmic architecture. Scholars such as Gedaliahu Stroumsa and Nathaniel Deutsch have traced the channels through which Jewish mystical traditions flowed into Gnostic systems, arguing that Gnosticism cannot be understood without reference to its Jewish matrix.

The figure of Saklas is particularly significant for Jewish readers. The use of an Aramaic word ("fool") to name the creator of the material world suggests a milieu in which Aramaic (or at least Aramaic theological vocabulary) was still current. The demotion of the creator God to an ignorant archon represents the most radical possible inversion of Jewish theology — an inversion that nonetheless depends on Jewish materials (divine names, angelic hierarchies, Genesis narratives) for its construction.
