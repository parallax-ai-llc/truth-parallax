## Overview

The Gospel of Judas is a Gnostic text surviving in a single Coptic manuscript (Codex Tchacos), carbon-dated to approximately 280 CE, though the original Greek composition is generally placed in the mid-second century. The text was known to Irenaeus of Lyon, who denounced it around 180 CE in *Adversus Haereses* (1.31.1). Lost for nearly seventeen centuries, the codex resurfaced on the antiquities market in the 1970s and was finally published by the National Geographic Society in 2006 after extensive conservation work.

Unlike the canonical Gospels and most other early Christian literature, the Gospel of Judas presents Judas Iscariot not as a traitor but as the only disciple who truly understands Jesus's teaching. Jesus singles Judas out from the other disciples, reveals to him the mysteries of the cosmos, and instructs him to facilitate the crucifixion — not as an act of betrayal but as a necessary liberation of the divine spirit from its material prison. The other eleven disciples are depicted as ignorant, worshipping the wrong God (the demiurge), and leading the emerging church astray.

## Structure

The text is divided into four sections based on its dramatic and thematic movements:

| Section | Title | Content |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 1 | The Laughing Jesus | Jesus appears among the disciples, laughs at their eucharistic prayer, and challenges their understanding of God |
| 2 | The Vision of the Twelve | The disciples recount a disturbing vision of the Temple; Jesus interprets it as a critique of their own religious practice |
| 3 | Sethian Cosmogony | Jesus reveals the structure of the divine realm — Barbelo, the Self-Generated, the luminaries, Adamas, Seth, and the creation of the material world by Saklas and Nebro |
| 4 | Judas's Ascent and the Betrayal | Judas receives his own star, is told he will "exceed all of them," and hands Jesus over to the authorities |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Gospel of Judas occupies a provocative position across multiple interpretive traditions:

- **Gnostic**: Read as a Sethian revelation dialogue that redefines salvation as liberation from the material cosmos created by an ignorant demiurge
- **Academic**: Central to debates about the diversity of early Christianity, the construction of orthodoxy and heresy, and the ethics of antiquities trafficking
- **Comparative**: Raises universal questions about the nature of loyalty, obedience, and the willingness to bear moral stigma for a higher purpose
- **Christian**: Challenges traditional interpretations of Judas's role in salvation history and the meaning of the crucifixion
- **Jewish**: Examined for its use of Genesis traditions (Adamas, Seth), its relationship to Jewish apocalypticism, and its depiction of Temple sacrifice
- **Interfaith**: Prompts reflection on how different traditions construct categories of betrayal, sacrifice, and redemptive suffering

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Inverted Betrayal | Judas's act is reframed as the highest form of obedience and spiritual understanding |
| Sethian Cosmology | An elaborate hierarchy of divine beings (aeons, luminaries, angels) structures the universe |
| Demiurgical Critique | The creator god of Genesis is identified as the ignorant angel Saklas ("fool") |
| Laughter and Irony | Jesus repeatedly laughs at the disciples' misunderstanding, subverting conventional piety |
| Liberation through Death | The crucifixion frees the divine spirit from the "garment" of the body |
| Anti-Ecclesiastical Polemic | The twelve disciples represent — and are criticized as — the emerging proto-orthodox church |
