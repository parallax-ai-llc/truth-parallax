## Judas's Star and the Liberating Betrayal

The final section brings the text's Gnostic theology to its narrative climax. Jesus tells Judas: "You will exceed all of them. For you will sacrifice the man that clothes me." This statement encapsulates the entire Gnostic soteriology of the text in a single sentence. The "man that clothes me" is the material body — the garment fashioned by Saklas that temporarily houses Jesus's divine spirit. Judas's act of handing Jesus over to the authorities is not betrayal but divestiture: he enables the removal of the bodily garment so that the spirit can return to the realm of Barbelo.

Judas is told that his "star" — his spiritual destiny — leads the way. In Sethian astral theology, each pneumatic individual has a star in the divine realm, a luminous counterpart that marks their origin and destination. Judas's star "has passed by" the others, signaling his superiority. He will be cursed by the other generations but will ultimately "rule over them" — a reversal that would have resonated deeply with Gnostic communities who understood themselves as persecuted bearers of superior truth.

The text's abrupt ending — Judas approaches the authorities and hands Jesus over — omits the crucifixion, resurrection, and all post-Easter narrative. For the Gnostic reader, these events are unnecessary. The salvific act is not the crucifixion itself but the liberation it enables. The story ends at the moment of release, not at the cross.
