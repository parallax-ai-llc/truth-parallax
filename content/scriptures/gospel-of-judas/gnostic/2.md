## The Temple Vision and Anti-Sacrificial Polemic

The disciples recount a shared vision of a great house (the Temple) with an altar, twelve priests, and crowds bringing offerings. They observe the priests committing sins — some sacrificing their own children, others engaging in various transgressions — all "in the name of Jesus." When they ask Jesus to interpret the vision, his response is devastating: the twelve priests are the twelve disciples themselves, and the sacrifices are the people they are leading astray.

For Gnostic interpreters, this section constitutes the text's most direct assault on emerging Catholic Christianity. The Temple represents the institutional church; the twelve priests are the apostles and their episcopal successors; the sacrifices are the faithful whom the proto-orthodox church is offering to the demiurge under the mistaken belief that they are serving the true God. The violence at the altar — including child sacrifice — allegorically depicts the spiritual harm inflicted by a church that teaches salvation through sacrificial atonement rather than gnosis.

The passage draws on Jewish prophetic critiques of empty ritual (cf. Isaiah 1, Amos 5) but extends them far beyond their original scope. In the Gnostic reading, the problem is not that sacrifice is performed improperly but that the entire sacrificial system serves the wrong deity. The God of the Temple — whether the Jerusalem Temple or the Christian altar — is Saklas, not the transcendent Father revealed by Jesus to Judas alone.
