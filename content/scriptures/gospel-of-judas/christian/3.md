## Sethian Cosmogony and the Doctrine of Creation

The cosmogonic section of the Gospel of Judas presents the most fundamental theological challenge to Christian orthodoxy: the identification of the creator God with an ignorant, rebellious archon. The Nicene Creed's affirmation of "one God, the Father Almighty, maker of heaven and earth" stands in direct contradiction to the Sethian system, in which the highest God has no direct involvement in material creation.

Christian theology has consistently affirmed the goodness of creation (Genesis 1:31, "God saw everything that he had made, and indeed, it was very good") and rejected any system that attributes the material world to a lesser or evil deity. The anti-Gnostic writings of Irenaeus, Tertullian, and Hippolytus devote extensive argument to defending creation's goodness and the identity of the creator God with the Father of Jesus Christ.

Yet the problem the Gnostics attempted to solve — the existence of suffering, evil, and death in a world supposedly created by a good God — remains the most persistent challenge in Christian theology. The doctrine of the Fall, the Augustinian theory of original sin, and the various theodicies proposed by Christian thinkers from Irenaeus to Plantinga all represent attempts to hold together divine goodness and the reality of evil without recourse to the Gnostic solution of a divided godhead.
