## The Apostolic Church Under Critique

The Temple vision's identification of the twelve priests with the twelve disciples constitutes a direct attack on apostolic authority — the foundation of ecclesiology in Catholic, Orthodox, and many Protestant traditions. If the apostles themselves are misleading the faithful, then the churches that claim continuity with their teaching and authority are built on a corrupt foundation.

Christian readers must engage seriously with this critique, even while rejecting its theological premises. The history of the church includes genuine instances of institutional corruption, clerical abuse, and the persecution of dissenters — realities that lend the Gospel of Judas's polemic a disturbing resonance. The text's critique of the apostles as false leaders who "sacrifice their children" (metaphorically, their congregations) anticipates later reformers' critiques of institutional Christianity, from the Waldensians to the Protestant Reformers to liberation theologians.

At the same time, Christian tradition has consistently maintained that the church's authority, while exercised by fallible human beings, derives ultimately from Christ and the Holy Spirit rather than from the personal holiness of its leaders. The Donatist controversy of the fourth and fifth centuries established the principle that the validity of sacraments does not depend on the worthiness of the minister — a principle that directly addresses the kind of critique the Gospel of Judas levels.
