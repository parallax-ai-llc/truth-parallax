## The Betrayal Reframed: Implications for Soteriology

The Gospel of Judas's reinterpretation of the betrayal as a liberating act strikes at the heart of Christian soteriology. If the crucifixion is not a sacrifice for sin but a liberation of the divine spirit from material bondage, then the entire edifice of atonement theology — from Paul's "Christ died for our sins" (1 Corinthians 15:3) to Anselm's satisfaction theory to contemporary penal substitution — collapses.

Christian readers have generally responded to this challenge in two ways. First, by reasserting the canonical narrative's theological coherence: the crucifixion is salvific precisely because it involves genuine suffering, genuine death, and genuine resurrection — not the shedding of an illusory bodily garment. The incarnation means that God enters fully into the human condition, including its mortality, and transforms it from within. Second, by acknowledging the Gospel of Judas as evidence of the theological diversity of early Christianity while maintaining that the canonical tradition preserves the authentic apostolic witness.

The text's abrupt ending — without crucifixion or resurrection — is theologically significant from a Christian perspective. The resurrection is the cornerstone of Christian faith (1 Corinthians 15:14, "if Christ has not been raised, then our proclamation has been in vain"). A Gospel that omits the resurrection is, from the orthodox standpoint, a Gospel that misses the entire point.
