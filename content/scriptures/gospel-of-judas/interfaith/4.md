## The Scapegoat and the Sacred: Judas Across Traditions

The Gospel of Judas's rehabilitation of its title character invites interfaith reflection on the figure of the scapegoat — the individual who bears the community's guilt, violence, or shadow and is expelled or destroyed so that the community can maintain its cohesion and self-understanding.

René Girard's mimetic theory identifies scapegoating as a foundational mechanism of human culture. The Hebrew Bible's ritual of the scapegoat (Leviticus 16) — in which a goat is laden with the community's sins and driven into the wilderness — ritualizes this mechanism. The passion narratives of the canonical Gospels can be read through a Girardian lens as the exposure and subversion of scapegoating: Jesus, the innocent victim, reveals the mechanism by refusing to participate in it.

The Gospel of Judas adds another layer: Judas himself becomes a scapegoat within Christian tradition, bearing the guilt of deicide and serving as the archetype of treachery. By giving Judas a voice and a motivation, the Gospel of Judas performs a function analogous to post-colonial literature that recovers the perspectives of those whom imperial narratives have silenced. Whether one accepts the text's theology or not, its insistence that the condemned figure has a story worth telling resonates with interfaith commitments to justice, compassion, and the dignity of the marginalized.
