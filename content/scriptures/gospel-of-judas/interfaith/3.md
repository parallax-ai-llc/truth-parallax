## The Problem of Evil: Cosmological Responses

The cosmogonic section of the Gospel of Judas offers a distinctive answer to what is perhaps the most universal question in religious thought: why does suffering exist? The Sethian answer — that the material world was created by an ignorant deity, not by the true God — is one of the most radical solutions ever proposed to the problem of theodicy.

Different traditions have approached this question with different conceptual tools. Classical theism (Jewish, Christian, Islamic) maintains divine omnipotence and goodness while explaining evil through free will, soul-making, or mystery. Zoroastrianism posits a cosmic dualism in which good and evil are primordial forces in conflict. Buddhism locates the origin of suffering (*dukkha*) not in a cosmic agent but in the three poisons of ignorance, attachment, and aversion. Hinduism offers multiple frameworks — *karma*, *maya* (illusion), *lila* (divine play) — for understanding the coexistence of the divine and the imperfect.

The Gospel of Judas's solution has the virtue of clarity: evil exists because an evil (or ignorant) being created the material world. It has the cost of fragmenting the divine and rendering the material world unredeemable. Interfaith dialogue on the problem of evil benefits from comparing these solutions, not to rank them but to understand the trade-offs each tradition makes in its attempt to hold together divine goodness and human suffering.
