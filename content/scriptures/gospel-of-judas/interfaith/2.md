## Institutional Religion and Its Discontents

The Temple vision and its interpretation — the twelve priests as the twelve apostles leading the faithful astray — give voice to a critique of institutional religion that recurs across traditions and centuries. Every major religious tradition has generated internal movements of dissent that accuse the establishment of betraying the founder's original message.

The Protestant Reformation charged the Catholic Church with corrupting the gospel through indulgences, clerical excess, and works-righteousness. The Theravada-Mahayana split in Buddhism involved competing claims about authentic transmission of the Buddha's teaching. Karaite Judaism challenged the authority of rabbinic oral tradition. Kharijite Islam rejected both Sunni and Shia claims to legitimate succession. In each case, the reformers claimed direct access to the original revelation, bypassing the accumulated institutional structures of the mainstream.

The Gospel of Judas represents an extreme form of this pattern — a text that rejects not merely the institutional church's practices but its fundamental theology, its God, and its apostolic foundation. Interfaith dialogue benefits from recognizing that every tradition contains such radical dissent and that the dissenters' critiques, however extreme, often identify genuine tensions within the tradition they challenge.
