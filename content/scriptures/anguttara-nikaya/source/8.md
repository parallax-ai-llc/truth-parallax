---
title: "Anguttara Nikaya - Chapter 8: Atthakanipata (The Book of the Eights)"
scripture: "anguttara-nikaya"
chapter: 8
---

**1** "Monks, there are these eight worldly conditions that keep the world turning, and the world turns around these eight worldly conditions. What eight? Gain and loss, fame and disrepute, praise and blame, pleasure and pain."

**2** "Monks, for the uninstructed worldling, gain arises, and he thinks: 'I have gain.' Loss arises, and he thinks: 'I have loss.' His mind is obsessed by gain and loss. But for the instructed noble disciple, when gain arises he understands: 'This gain has arisen for me. It is impermanent, conditioned, dependently arisen.' He understands the same for loss."

**3** "Monks, there are these eight qualities of a great person. What eight? Here a monk has faith, has virtue, is learned, is a Dhamma-speaker, frequents assemblies, teaches the Dhamma confidently, has attained the four jhanas, and by the destruction of the taints has realized the taintless liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom."

**4** "Monks, there are these eight causes, these eight conditions for the obtaining of the wisdom that is fundamental to the holy life, and if already obtained, for its increase, expansion, development, and fulfillment. What eight? Here a monk lives in dependence on a teacher or a respected companion from whom he learns conscience and shame, love and reverence."

**5** "From time to time he approaches and asks questions: 'How is this, venerable sir? What is the meaning of this?' Those venerable ones disclose what has not been disclosed to him, clear up what is not clear, and remove his doubts about numerous perplexing points."

**6** "Having heard that Dhamma, he achieves two kinds of seclusion: seclusion of body and seclusion of mind. He is virtuous. He has learned much. He has aroused energy. He is mindful. He is wise."

**7** "Monks, there are these eight persons worthy of gifts. What eight? The stream-enterer, the one practicing for the realization of the fruit of stream-entry, the once-returner, the one practicing for the realization of the fruit of once-returning, the non-returner, the one practicing for the realization of the fruit of non-returning, the Arahat, and the one practicing for Arahatship."

**8** "Monks, there are these eight assemblies. What eight? The assembly of nobles, of brahmins, of householders, of ascetics, of the devas of the Four Great Kings, of the Thirty-Three, of Mara's retinue, and of Brahmas. I recall having approached hundreds of assemblies of each kind, and in each I sat and conversed with them and addressed them."

**9** "Monks, there are these eight liberations. What eight? Possessing form, one sees forms — the first liberation. Not perceiving form internally, one sees forms externally — the second. One is resolved only upon the beautiful — the third. By completely surmounting perceptions of form, one enters the sphere of infinite space — the fourth. One enters the sphere of infinite consciousness — the fifth. One enters the sphere of nothingness — the sixth. One enters the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception — the seventh. One enters the cessation of perception and feeling — the eighth."

**10** "Monks, there are these eight thoughts of a great person. What eight? This Dhamma is for one of few wishes, not for one of many wishes. For one who is content, not for one who is discontent. For one who is secluded, not for one who delights in company. For one who is energetic, not for one who is lazy. For one who has established mindfulness, not for one who is unmindful. For one who is concentrated, not for one who is unconcentrated. For one who is wise, not for one who is unwise. For one who delights in non-proliferation, not for one who delights in proliferation."
