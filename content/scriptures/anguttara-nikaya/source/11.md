---
title: "Anguttara Nikaya - Chapter 11: Ekadasakanipata (The Book of the Elevens)"
scripture: "anguttara-nikaya"
chapter: 11
---

**1** "Monks, there are these eleven advantages of loving-kindness. What eleven? One sleeps well. One wakes well. One does not see bad dreams. One is dear to human beings. One is dear to non-human beings. The devas guard one. Fire, poison, and weapons do not affect one. One's mind quickly becomes concentrated. One's facial complexion is serene. One dies unconfused. If one penetrates no higher, one is reborn in the Brahma world."

**2** "Monks, if a monk cultivates the liberation of mind by loving-kindness, develops it, makes much of it, makes it his vehicle, makes it his foundation, stabilizes it, consolidates it, and properly undertakes it, these eleven advantages may be expected."

**3** "Monks, whatever a monk frequently thinks upon and ponders, that becomes the inclination of his mind. If he frequently thinks upon and ponders thoughts of loving-kindness, he has abandoned thoughts of ill will; his mind inclines to thoughts of loving-kindness."

**4** "Monks, a monk who, with a mind of loving-kindness, pervades one direction, then the second, then the third, then the fourth, then above, below, around, and everywhere, the entire world—with a mind of loving-kindness, abundant, exalted, immeasurable, without hostility, without ill will—he pervades the entire world."

**5** "Monks, there are these eleven doors to the deathless. What eleven? Here, a monk dwells contemplating the body in the body, ardent, clearly comprehending, mindful, having removed covetousness and grief in regard to the world. As he dwells thus, contemplating the body in the body, either a foundation of delight arises, or equanimity is established."

**6** "He dwells contemplating feelings in feelings, ardent, clearly comprehending, mindful. As he dwells thus, either a foundation of delight arises or equanimity is established."

**7** "He dwells contemplating mind in mind, ardent, clearly comprehending, mindful. As he dwells thus, either a foundation of delight arises or equanimity is established."

**8** "He dwells contemplating phenomena in phenomena, ardent, clearly comprehending, mindful. As he dwells thus, either a foundation of delight arises or equanimity is established."

**9** "Furthermore, a monk gives a Dhamma talk to others in detail as he has heard it and learned it. Furthermore, a monk recites the Dhamma in detail as he has heard it and learned it. Furthermore, a monk thinks about and ponders the Dhamma as he has heard it and learned it, examining it with the mind."

**10** "Furthermore, a monk has well grasped a certain meditation sign, well attended to it, well sustained it, well penetrated it with wisdom. In each of these eleven cases, either a foundation of delight arises or equanimity is established."

**11** "If a foundation of delight arises, the mind is liberated. If equanimity is established, the mind is liberated. That monk should be confident: 'Birth is destroyed, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is nothing further beyond this.' These are the eleven doors to the deathless."

**12** "Monks, suppose a hen has eight, ten, or twelve eggs, and she has properly sat on them, properly incubated them, properly maintained them. Even though such a wish as this would not arise in her: 'Oh, that my chicks might pierce their shells with the points of their claws and beaks and hatch out safely!'—yet the chicks are capable of piercing their shells and hatching out safely. So too, for a monk who dwells thus diligent, ardent, and resolute, even though such a wish would not arise: 'Oh, that my mind might be liberated!'—yet his mind is liberated from the taints by non-clinging."
