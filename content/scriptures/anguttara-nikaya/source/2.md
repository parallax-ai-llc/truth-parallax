---
title: "Anguttara Nikaya - Chapter 2: Dukanipata (The Book of the Twos)"
scripture: "anguttara-nikaya"
chapter: 2
---

**1** "Monks, there are two kinds of happiness. What two? The happiness of the householder and the happiness of one gone forth. These are the two kinds of happiness. The higher of these is the happiness of one gone forth."

**2** "Monks, there are two kinds of happiness. What two? Sensual happiness and the happiness of renunciation. The higher of these is the happiness of renunciation."

**3** "Monks, there are two things that partake of knowledge. What two? Calm and insight. When calm is developed, the mind is developed. When the mind is developed, lust is abandoned. When insight is developed, wisdom is developed. When wisdom is developed, ignorance is abandoned."

**4** "Monks, there are two conditions for the arising of right view. What two? The voice of another and careful attention. These are the two conditions for the arising of right view."

**5** "Monks, there are these two kinds of fools. What two? One who takes up a burden that has not fallen to him, and one who does not take up a burden that has fallen to him."

**6** "Monks, there are these two kinds of wise persons. What two? One who does not take up a burden that has not fallen to him, and one who takes up a burden that has fallen to him."

**7** "Monks, there are these two things that cause remorse. What two? Here, one has done what is evil and has not done what is good. These are the two things that cause remorse."

**8** "Monks, there are these two things that do not cause remorse. What two? Here, one has done what is good and has not done what is evil. These are the two things that do not cause remorse."

**9** "Monks, there are two persons who slander the Tathagata. What two? One who explains a discourse whose meaning requires interpretation as one whose meaning is explicit, and one who explains a discourse whose meaning is explicit as one whose meaning requires interpretation."

**10** "Monks, there are two persons who do not slander the Tathagata. What two? One who explains a discourse whose meaning requires interpretation as one whose meaning requires interpretation, and one who explains a discourse whose meaning is explicit as one whose meaning is explicit."

**11** "Monks, there are two assemblies. What two? The superficial assembly and the deep assembly. And what is the superficial assembly? Here, in a certain assembly the monks are restless, puffed up, vain, garrulous, rambling in their talk, with muddled mindfulness, without clear comprehension, unconcentrated, their minds astray, their sense faculties uncontrolled. This is the superficial assembly."

**12** "And what is the deep assembly? Here, in a certain assembly the monks are not restless, not puffed up, not vain, not garrulous, not rambling in their talk, with mindfulness established, with clear comprehension, concentrated, their minds unified, their sense faculties controlled. This is the deep assembly."

**13** "Monks, there are two kinds of giving. What two? Giving material things and giving the Dhamma. The higher of these is the giving of the Dhamma."

**14** "Monks, there are two powers. What two? The power of reflection and the power of development. What is the power of reflection? Here one reflects: 'If I do an evil deed by body, speech, or mind, I shall be reproached by myself and by the wise.' This is the power of reflection. What is the power of development? Here a monk develops the enlightenment factor of mindfulness, of investigation, of energy, of rapture, of calm, of concentration, of equanimity. This is the power of development."

**15** "Monks, there are two things that protect the world. What two? Shame and dread of wrongdoing. If these two things did not protect the world, there would be no recognition of mother, mother's sister, uncle's wife, teacher's wife, or the wives of honourable men. The world would fall into confusion, as with goats and sheep, fowl and pigs, dogs and jackals. But because these two things protect the world, there is recognition of mother, of father, of elder, and of teacher."

**16** "Monks, these two bright qualities protect the world. What two? Shame and dread of wrongdoing. These two bright qualities protect the world."
