## Overview

The Hidden Words (Kalimát-i-Maknúnih) is one of the most beloved and widely read works of Bahá'u'lláh, the founder of the Bahá'í Faith. Composed around 1858 during Bahá'u'lláh's period in Baghdad, the text consists of brief ethical and mystical aphorisms presented as the distilled essence of divine revelation across the ages. Bahá'u'lláh describes the work as that which "hath descended from the realm of glory, uttered by the tongue of power and might, and revealed unto the Prophets of old."

## Structure

The Hidden Words is divided into two parts, here grouped into fifteen thematic sections:

1. **Arabic Hidden Words** (71 passages, Sections 1–8): Terse, luminous aphorisms on the soul's relationship with God, justice, detachment, death, steadfastness, and the inner life
2. **Persian Hidden Words** (82 passages, Sections 9–15): More expansive, poetic meditations on mystical themes including the garden of the heart, true riches, knowledge, humility, purification, love, and the eternal covenant

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Bahá'í**: A central devotional text, memorized and recited in daily prayer and meditation by Bahá'ís worldwide
- **Academic**: A rich example of mystical aphoristic literature, studied for its literary form and its relationship to Sufi and biblical wisdom traditions
- **Comparative**: Invites comparison with the Beatitudes, Sufi poetry, Proverbs, the Tao Te Ching, and other wisdom literature
- **Islamic**: Draws deeply on the tradition of hadith qudsi and Sufi literary forms, while transforming them within a new prophetic claim
- **Christian**: Resonates with the ethical and mystical teachings of Jesus, particularly the Sermon on the Mount and the Johannine tradition
- **Interfaith**: A text of extraordinary accessibility and universal appeal, widely used in interfaith settings

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Divine Love | The intimate, personal relationship between the Creator and the human soul |
| Detachment | Liberation from attachment to the material world as the path to spiritual freedom |
| Justice | The preeminent virtue, called "the best beloved of all things" in God's sight |
| The Heart | The human heart as the throne of God, to be purified and made a mirror of divine attributes |
| Mortality and Eternity | The transience of earthly life and the imperishable nature of the soul |
