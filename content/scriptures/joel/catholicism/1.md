Joel 1 describes a locust plague of unprecedented severity, calling the entire community — from elderly to infant — to mourning and fasting. Catholic liturgical practice draws heavily on this chapter's model of communal penance. The call to "consecrate a fast, call a solemn assembly" (1:14) provides the biblical template for the Church's penitential seasons, particularly Lent.

The four kinds of locusts (1:4) were interpreted by the Church Fathers as successive waves of spiritual destruction. Jerome identified them with the empires that oppressed Israel — Babylonian, Persian, Greek, and Roman — while other patristic interpreters saw them as stages of sin's progressive devastation of the soul: temptation, consent, habit, and spiritual death.

The chapter's agricultural imagery — the vine dried up, the fig tree withered, the grain offering and drink offering cut off from the house of the LORD (1:9-12) — connects material devastation to liturgical deprivation. Catholic theology sees in this the sacramental dimension of creation: when the earth is barren, the Eucharistic offering is diminished, revealing the intimate connection between the natural order and the life of worship.
