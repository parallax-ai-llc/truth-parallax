## Overview

Catholic interpretation of Joel is deeply interwoven with the Church's liturgical life. Joel's call to fasting, weeping, and mourning (1:13-14; 2:12-17) has shaped Catholic penitential practice, and Joel 2:12-18 serves as the first reading on Ash Wednesday in the Roman Lectionary — making it one of the most publicly heard prophetic texts in Catholic worship.

## Patristic and Liturgical Heritage

The Church Fathers read Joel through a Christological and ecclesiological lens. **Augustine** interpreted the locust plague as symbolic of heretical movements devouring the faith. **Jerome** in his commentary connected the four types of locusts (1:4) to successive waves of spiritual enemies. The Ash Wednesday liturgical context places Joel's call to repentance at the very threshold of Lent, emphasizing that conversion of heart (*metanoia*) is the essential response to God's merciful initiative.

## Theological Emphases

Catholic teaching draws on Joel 2:28-29 to affirm the charismatic dimension of the Church alongside its institutional structure — the Spirit is poured out on all, not only on clergy or hierarchs. Pope John XXIII invoked Joel's prophecy when convening Vatican II, praying for a "new Pentecost." The Catholic Charismatic Renewal movement, emerging in the late 1960s, found in Joel both scriptural warrant and prophetic encouragement. The eschatological dimension — the valley of Jehoshaphat and cosmic judgment — informs Catholic eschatology's teaching on the general judgment and the restoration of all things.
