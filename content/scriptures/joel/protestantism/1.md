Joel 1 opens with a vivid description of a locust plague that has devastated the land of Judah, stripping it of grain, wine, and oil. Protestant interpreters have debated whether the four kinds of locusts mentioned in 1:4 represent successive waves of a single invasion, four stages of locust development, or an allegorical depiction of invading armies. Most evangelical scholars accept a literal locust plague that Joel then uses as a springboard for eschatological reflection.

The prophet calls for communal mourning and fasting (1:13-14), summoning priests to lead the people in lamentation. Protestant preaching has drawn from this chapter the principle that natural disasters are occasions for spiritual examination — not that God mechanically punishes sin through nature, but that calamity should prompt reflection on the community's relationship with God.

The chapter's closing verses (1:15-20) introduce the "Day of the LORD" — a concept that will dominate the rest of the book. Protestant eschatology varies widely in its interpretation of this phrase, from a specific future tribulation period (dispensationalism) to a recurring pattern of divine judgment that finds its ultimate expression in the return of Christ (Reformed theology).
