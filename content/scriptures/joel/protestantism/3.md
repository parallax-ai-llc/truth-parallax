Joel 3 depicts the final judgment of the nations in the Valley of Jehoshaphat (meaning "the LORD judges"). Protestant eschatology reads this chapter as describing events associated with Christ's return — the gathering of the nations, the cosmic harvest, and the establishment of God's eternal kingdom. Dispensational interpreters identify this with a specific future battle, while amillennialists read it as symbolic of the final judgment.

The charges against the nations — selling God's people into slavery, dividing the land, casting lots for captives (3:2-3) — articulate principles of divine justice that transcend the specific historical context. Protestant theology draws from this the principle that God holds all nations accountable for their treatment of the vulnerable, regardless of whether they acknowledge the covenant.

The chapter's concluding vision of abundance — "the mountains shall drip sweet wine, and the hills shall flow with milk, and a fountain shall come forth from the house of the LORD" (3:18) — presents the eschatological hope in terms of material restoration. Protestant interpreters connect this to the new creation promised in Revelation 21-22, where the curse is reversed and creation is renewed. The final declaration — "the LORD dwells in Zion" (3:21) — points to the ultimate goal of salvation: God dwelling with his people in unbroken communion.
