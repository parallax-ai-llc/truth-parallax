Joel 2 is the theological heart of the book, moving from terrifying judgment to extraordinary promise. The chapter opens with a trumpet blast warning of an approaching army — whether literal locusts described in military terms or an apocalyptic army prefigured by the plague remains debated among Protestant scholars. The cosmic imagery — darkened sun, trembling earth — pushes the language beyond any merely natural event.

The pivotal call to repentance in 2:12-17 has shaped Protestant worship. "Rend your hearts and not your garments, and return to the LORD your God, for he is gracious and merciful" (2:13) epitomizes the Protestant emphasis on inward, authentic faith. The verse's balance of divine sovereignty ("who knows whether he will not turn and relent?") and genuine human agency (the call to repent) illustrates the theological tension that Protestant traditions navigate differently.

Joel 2:28-32, the promise of the Spirit poured out on "all flesh," is the most consequential passage in the book for Protestant theology. Peter's citation at Pentecost (Acts 2:17-21) establishes this as the foundational text for the Church's charismatic identity. Pentecostal and charismatic Protestants read it as warrant for ongoing miraculous gifts, while cessationists argue for a unique apostolic fulfillment. The democratization of prophecy — sons, daughters, old men, young men, servants — has also fueled egalitarian movements within Protestantism.
