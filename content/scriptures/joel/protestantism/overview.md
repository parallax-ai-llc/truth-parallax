## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Joel centers on two theological pillars: the call to genuine repentance and the promise of the Holy Spirit's outpouring. The book holds a unique place in Protestant theology because Joel 2:28-32 is the text Peter quotes at Pentecost (Acts 2:17-21), making it foundational for Pentecostal and charismatic movements as well as for Reformed and Lutheran understandings of the Spirit's work.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

Protestants have debated whether the locust plague in Joel 1 is literal, allegorical (representing invading armies), or apocalyptic. **Calvin** favored a literal plague that Joel uses as a springboard for eschatological teaching. Many dispensationalist interpreters read Joel's "Day of the LORD" as a future tribulation period, while Reformed scholars tend to see it as a recurring pattern fulfilled in stages — at Pentecost, throughout church history, and at Christ's return.

## Theological Emphases

Joel 2:13 ("Rend your hearts, not your garments") epitomizes the Protestant emphasis on inward, heartfelt faith over outward religious performance. The promise that God will "restore the years the locusts have eaten" (2:25) is widely preached as a promise of spiritual renewal. The democratization of the Spirit — "your sons and daughters will prophesy" — has been particularly influential in egalitarian and Pentecostal Protestant traditions, supporting arguments for broader participation in ministry.
