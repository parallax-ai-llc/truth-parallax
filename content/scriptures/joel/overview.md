## Overview

Joel is one of the shortest books among the Minor Prophets, yet its theological influence is immense. The prophet Joel, son of Pethuel, speaks in response to a devastating locust plague that has ravaged the land of Judah. From this immediate crisis, Joel draws out far-reaching eschatological themes — the Day of the LORD, the outpouring of the Spirit, and the final judgment of the nations.

## Structure

1. **The Locust Plague and Call to Repentance** (Chapter 1): A vivid description of agricultural devastation and a summons to communal mourning and fasting
2. **The Day of the LORD and Divine Response** (Chapter 2): The locust army as harbinger of the Day of the LORD; God's promise to restore and pour out the Spirit "on all flesh"
3. **Judgment of the Nations and Restoration** (Chapter 3): The valley of Jehoshaphat, cosmic signs, and the final vindication of God's people

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Judaism**: Joel is read on the Shabbat between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur in some traditions; Joel 2:13 ("Rend your hearts and not your garments") is a key text for *teshuvah* (repentance)
- **Christianity**: Joel 2:28-32 is quoted by Peter at Pentecost (Acts 2:17-21) as fulfilled in the outpouring of the Holy Spirit
- **Islam**: The eschatological themes — cosmic upheaval, divine judgment, and restoration — parallel Quranic descriptions of the Day of Judgment (*Yawm al-Qiyamah*)
- **Gnosticism**: The "Day of the LORD" language can be read as the Demiurge's wrathful control, while the Spirit outpouring hints at gnosis available to all

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Day of the LORD | A day of both judgment and salvation, cosmic in scope |
| Repentance | The call to genuine, heartfelt turning to God |
| Spirit Outpouring | The promise that God's Spirit will be poured out on all flesh |
| Agricultural Imagery | The locust plague as both literal disaster and apocalyptic symbol |
| Restoration | God's promise to restore "the years the locusts have eaten" |
