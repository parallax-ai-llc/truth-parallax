## Overview

Gnostic interpretation of Joel applies the characteristic inversion of prophetic literature. The "Day of the LORD" — which Joel presents as God's terrible judgment — can be reread in Gnostic terms as the Demiurge's wrathful assertion of control over the material world. The locust plague represents the archons' destructive power over material existence, demonstrating the fundamentally hostile nature of the created order.

## The Spirit Outpouring Reread

Joel 2:28-29, the promise of the Spirit poured out on all flesh, holds particular interest for Gnostic readers. Rather than a future eschatological event, Gnostics could interpret this as the awakening of *gnosis* — the divine spark within each pneumatic individual recognizing its true origin in the Pleroma. The democratization of prophecy ("your sons and daughters shall prophesy, your old men shall dream dreams") aligns with the Gnostic rejection of hierarchical mediation: true knowledge comes directly to each individual, not through priestly institutions or temple systems.

## Cosmic Dissolution and Liberation

Joel's apocalyptic imagery — the sun darkened, the moon turned to blood, the heavens and earth shaking — is read not as divine punishment but as the dissolution of the Demiurge's false creation. In Gnostic eschatology, the end of the material world is liberation rather than catastrophe. The "valley of decision" (3:14) becomes the moment when pneumatic souls choose gnosis over ignorance. Joel's concluding vision of abundant springs flowing from the LORD's house (3:18) can be read as the living waters of knowledge emanating from the true God beyond the Demiurge.
