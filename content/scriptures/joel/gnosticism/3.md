Joel 3 presents the Demiurge's final assertion of sovereign power — the judgment of the nations in his valley, by his standards, for his purposes. The Gnostic reader recognizes this as the archontic endgame: the Demiurge positioning himself as the ultimate arbiter of cosmic justice, demanding that all creation submit to his verdict. The "Valley of Jehoshaphat" is the courtroom of the archontic system, where the material world's accounts are settled according to the Demiurge's ledger.

The cosmic harvest imagery — "Put in the sickle, for the harvest is ripe... the winepress is full" (3:13) — describes the Demiurge's final extraction of value from his creation. The material world has served its purpose, and now the archontic forces move to collect what they have sown. But the Gnostic reading sees a double meaning: the "harvest" might also be the gathering of pneumatic sparks from their material husks, the liberation of divine light from the prisons of flesh.

The concluding vision of the fountain flowing from the LORD's house (3:18) is the most promising image for Gnostic interpretation. Water, in many Gnostic texts, symbolizes the living knowledge that flows from the Pleroma. If this fountain originates beyond the Demiurge's house — from the true divine source — then Joel's concluding vision, despite its archontic framing, points toward the ultimate reality: the inexhaustible flow of gnosis that will eventually dissolve the barriers between the material world and the realm of pure light.
