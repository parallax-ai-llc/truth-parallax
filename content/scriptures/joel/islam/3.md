Joel 3 depicts the final judgment of the nations, a theme that occupies a central place in Islamic theology. The gathering of all nations for judgment in the Valley of Jehoshaphat parallels the Quranic descriptions of the *mahshar* (the great gathering) on the Day of Resurrection: "And the earth will shine with the light of its Lord, and the record of deeds will be placed, and the prophets and the witnesses will be brought" (39:69).

The charges against the nations — enslaving God's people, dividing their land, selling children for wine and prostitutes (3:3) — articulate principles of justice that Islamic law (*shari'ah*) also upholds. The exploitation of the vulnerable, the trafficking of human beings, and the seizure of others' lands are among the gravest sins in Islamic jurisprudence. Joel's God holds all nations to these standards, regardless of their relationship to the covenant — a universalism that Islam shares.

The concluding vision of abundance and divine presence (3:17-21) resonates with the Quranic descriptions of paradise: "Gardens beneath which rivers flow" (2:25) and "Whatever you wish, you will have therein" (41:31). While Joel's vision is focused on earthly Jerusalem, the Islamic reading extends the promise to the eternal realm, where the righteous dwell in the presence of God forever. The fountains of Joel's restored Jerusalem prefigure the rivers of *jannah* that flow with water, milk, wine, and honey.
