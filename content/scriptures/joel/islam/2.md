Joel 2 combines apocalyptic warning with the promise of renewal, themes central to Islamic eschatology. The cosmic imagery — the sun darkened, the earth trembling, the heavens shaking (2:10) — closely parallels the Quranic descriptions of the Day of Judgment: "When the sky breaks apart, and when the stars fall, scattering, and when the seas are erupted" (82:1-3). Both Joel and the Quran use cosmic dissolution as the language of ultimate divine reckoning.

The call to repentance (2:12-17) aligns with the Quranic emphasis on *tawbah* as the appropriate human response to divine warning. The Quran promises: "But indeed, I am the Perpetual Forgiver of whoever repents and believes and does righteousness and then continues in guidance" (20:82). Joel's assurance that God is "gracious and merciful, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love" (2:13) corresponds to the Islamic invocation of God as *al-Rahman* (the Most Merciful) and *al-Rahim* (the Especially Merciful).

The Spirit-outpouring passage (2:28-32) raises theological questions for Islamic interpretation. While the Quran affirms that God sends his spirit (*ruh*) to whom he wills, the concept of a universal Spirit-outpouring "on all flesh" does not fit the Islamic model of specific prophetic revelation. Muslim scholars would read this either as a promise specific to the Israelite prophetic tradition or as evidence of textual elaboration, while affirming the broader truth that God communicates with humanity through chosen means.
