## Overview

Eastern Orthodox interpretation of Joel is profoundly shaped by the feast of Pentecost, one of the great feasts of the liturgical year. Joel 2:28-32 is read as the Old Testament prophecy fulfilled when the Holy Spirit descended upon the apostles, constituting the Church as the Spirit-filled body of Christ. The Orthodox reading integrates Joel into a comprehensive theology of the Spirit's ongoing work of sanctification and cosmic renewal.

## Patristic Heritage

**Cyril of Alexandria** interpreted Joel's locust plague as both a historical judgment and a type of the spiritual devastation caused by sin. **Gregory of Nazianzus** drew on Joel's Spirit-outpouring prophecy in his theological orations on the Holy Spirit, arguing that the progressive revelation of the Trinity reaches its climax at Pentecost. The Fathers consistently read Joel's promises of restoration through the lens of *theosis* — humanity's participation in the divine nature through the Spirit.

## Liturgical and Theological Significance

The Pentecost liturgy in the Orthodox Church features Joel prominently. The "kneeling prayers" at Pentecost Vespers invoke Joel's vision of universal Spirit-outpouring as now realized in the Church. Joel's call to repentance (2:12-13) resonates with the Orthodox understanding of the spiritual life as continual *metanoia*. The cosmic imagery of chapter 3 — the sun darkened, the moon turned to blood — is understood not as the annihilation of creation but as its transfiguration, consistent with the Orthodox hope for the renewal rather than destruction of the material world.
