Joel 1 describes a devastating locust plague that the Greek Fathers read as both historical event and spiritual type. Cyril of Alexandria interpreted the locusts as demonic powers that strip the soul of its spiritual fruits — faith, hope, love, and the virtues. The progressive devastation described in 1:4, where successive waves of locusts consume what the previous wave left, mirrors the progressive nature of spiritual decline when the soul is left unguarded.

The prophet's call to communal lamentation and fasting (1:13-14) resonates deeply with Orthodox liturgical practice. The Great Canon of St. Andrew of Crete, chanted during Great Lent, embodies the same spirit of communal repentance that Joel prescribes. The summoning of the entire community — elders, children, nursing infants, even the bridal couple (cf. 2:16) — reflects the Orthodox understanding that repentance is a corporate as well as individual act.

The disruption of grain and drink offerings (1:9, 13) carries particular weight in Orthodox theology, where the Eucharist (the offering of bread and wine) is the center of the Church's life. When the material elements of worship are destroyed, the sacramental connection between heaven and earth is threatened. Joel's crisis is ultimately a liturgical crisis — the breakdown of the cosmic liturgy that sustains creation.
