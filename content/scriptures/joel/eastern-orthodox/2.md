Joel 2 moves from the terror of divine judgment to the promise of the Spirit's outpouring. Orthodox theology reads the chapter's progression — from darkness to light, from judgment to renewal — as a pattern of the paschal mystery. The terrible "Day of the LORD" corresponds to Great and Holy Friday, while the promise of restoration and Spirit-outpouring corresponds to the Resurrection and Pentecost.

The call to repentance (2:12-17) is central to Orthodox spiritual life. The emphasis on the heart — "rend your hearts and not your garments" — aligns with the hesychast tradition's focus on the prayer of the heart. True *metanoia* is not an external performance but a transformation of the *nous* (the spiritual intellect) — a turning of the whole person toward God that the Jesus Prayer and the practice of watchfulness (*nepsis*) are designed to cultivate.

Joel 2:28-32, the Spirit-outpouring prophecy, is read at the Pentecost Vespers with the kneeling prayers, one of the most dramatic liturgical moments in the Orthodox year. The prophecy's fulfillment at Pentecost constitutes the Church as a charismatic community in which the Spirit is given not to a priestly elite but to all the faithful. Orthodox theology insists that charisms are given for the building up of the body, not for individual exaltation, and that they must be exercised within the ecclesial community under proper discernment.
