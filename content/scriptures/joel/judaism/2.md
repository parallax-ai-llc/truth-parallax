Joel 2 is the theological center of the book, moving from apocalyptic terror to the promise of restoration and Spirit-outpouring. The opening alarm — "Blow the trumpet in Zion!" (2:1) — evokes the shofar of Rosh Hashanah and the warning system that signaled danger to the covenant community. Jewish liturgy draws on this verse to connect Joel's message to the annual cycle of repentance.

The pivotal call to repentance (2:12-17) is among the most important penitential texts in Jewish tradition. "Rend your hearts and not your garments" (2:13) epitomizes the prophetic insistence on interior transformation over external display — a theme that resonates through the Yom Kippur liturgy. The priestly prayer of 2:17 — "Spare your people, O LORD" — models the intercessory role that the rabbis inherited from the Temple priesthood.

The Spirit-outpouring passage (2:28-29) is interpreted in Jewish tradition differently from the Christian reading. The Targum Jonathan translates "I will pour out my Spirit" as "I will pour out my spirit of prophecy," limiting the gift to prophetic inspiration rather than a universal charismatic endowment. Jewish messianic expectation includes the renewal of prophecy, but within the framework of Torah and the rabbinic tradition rather than as an independent, unmediated spiritual experience.
