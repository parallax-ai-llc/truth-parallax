## Overview

In Jewish tradition, Joel (*Yoel*) is part of the Twelve Minor Prophets (*Trei Asar*), counted as a single book. Joel's vivid call to communal repentance and his vision of divine restoration have made the book an important text for Jewish liturgy and theology, particularly in connection with the Days of Awe and the theme of *teshuvah* (repentance).

## Rabbinic Interpretation

The Talmud and Midrash engage Joel primarily through its themes of repentance and eschatology. The rabbis noted Joel's emphasis on tearing the heart rather than garments (2:13) as an exemplary statement of the prophetic insistence on inward transformation. **Rashi** interpreted the locust plague literally but saw it as a divine warning calling Israel to return to God. **Ibn Ezra** debated the dating of Joel and read the four species of locusts (1:4) as stages of a single devastating swarm rather than successive invasions.

## Liturgical Usage

Joel 2:15-17 provides the template for communal fast-day liturgies in Jewish practice: "Blow the trumpet in Zion, consecrate a fast, call a solemn assembly." The Targum Jonathan translates Joel with characteristic expansions, rendering the Spirit-outpouring passage (2:28-29) in terms of prophetic inspiration rather than a universal charismatic gift. In the Jewish reading, Joel's eschatological vision points to the messianic age when God will dwell among the people and vindicate Israel against its oppressors in the valley of Jehoshaphat — understood as the valley of divine judgment.
