Joel 1 describes a locust plague of extraordinary severity, calling for the kind of communal fasting and lamentation prescribed in the Torah for times of national crisis. Rashi reads the four types of locusts (1:4) as four successive swarms of a single species, each devouring what the previous left behind. Ibn Ezra, by contrast, suggests they represent different species of crop-destroying insects, reflecting his interest in natural history and precise linguistic analysis.

The call to the elders to testify whether such devastation has been seen before (1:2-3) connects the crisis to communal memory — a characteristic concern of Jewish tradition. The Mishnah (Ta'anit 1-3) codifies the practice of communal fasting in response to drought and plague, drawing on the model Joel describes. The shofar blast of 1:14-15 signals the urgency of the moment, connecting Joel's crisis to the liturgical rhythm of Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur.

The disruption of the *minhah* (grain offering) and *nesekh* (drink offering) in 1:9 strikes at the heart of Temple worship. For Jewish readers, this is not merely agricultural loss but liturgical catastrophe — the inability to fulfill the daily *tamid* offering. The Talmud records that the cessation of the daily sacrifice was among the most devastating consequences of the siege of Jerusalem.
