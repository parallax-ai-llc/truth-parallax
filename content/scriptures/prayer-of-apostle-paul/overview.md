## Overview

The Prayer of the Apostle Paul (NHC I,1) is a short Valentinian prayer inscribed on the front flyleaf of Nag Hammadi Codex I (the Jung Codex). Written in a different hand from the rest of the codex, it may have served as a kind of invocation or protective text for the collection. The prayer is attributed to Paul and invokes Christ under several Valentinian titles: "my Redeemer," "my Psychagogue" (guide of souls), and the one who leads the soul to the Pleroma. The text asks for the gifts of the First-born, knowledge of the Father, and the fullness of grace. Despite its brevity, the prayer is theologically rich, combining Pauline language with Valentinian concepts and demonstrating that Valentinian Christianity expressed itself not only in speculative theology but in devotional prayer. A colophon reads "In peace. Christ is holy."

## Structure

## Cross-Traditional Significance

- **Gnostic**: A rare example of Valentinian devotional prayer, revealing the personal piety underlying Gnostic theology
- **Academic**: Studied for its Valentinian theological vocabulary, its relationship to Pauline letters, and its physical placement in the codex
- **Comparative**: Offers parallels to prayers of invocation and petition across religious traditions
- **Christian**: Demonstrates the continuity between Valentinian and orthodox Christian prayer practices
- **Jewish**: Parallels Jewish prayer forms of invocation and petition, particularly the language of divine names and attributes
- **Interfaith**: Illustrates the universal practice of prayer as a means of seeking divine aid and transformation

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Psychagogue | Christ is invoked as the "guide of souls" who leads the faithful to the divine realm |
| Valentinian Devotion | The prayer reveals the personal, devotional dimension of Valentinian Christianity |
| Pauline Attribution | The attribution to Paul connects the prayer to the Valentinian tradition of claiming Paul as their apostle |
| Pleroma and Redemption | The prayer seeks complete redemption and full participation in the divine fullness (Pleroma) |
