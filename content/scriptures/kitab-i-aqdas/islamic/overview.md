## Overview

From an Islamic perspective, the Kitab-i-Aqdas raises fundamental theological questions about the finality of prophethood (*khatm an-nubuwwa*) and the authority of the Quran as God's final revelation. Mainstream Islamic theology holds that Muhammad is the Seal of the Prophets and the Quran is the final, uncorrupted divine scripture. The Baha'i claim that Baha'u'llah brings a new revelation is therefore deeply contested.

## Key Interpretive Issues

### Finality of Prophethood
The Quran (33:40) designates Muhammad as *khatam an-nabiyyin* (Seal of the Prophets). Mainstream Sunni and Shia scholarship interprets this as precluding any new prophet or messenger after Muhammad. Baha'is interpret "seal" differently, understanding it as the culmination of a prophetic cycle rather than the absolute end of divine communication.

### Relationship to Sharia
Scholars of Islamic law note that the Aqdas both preserves and modifies elements of Islamic practice. Obligatory prayer, fasting, and pilgrimage continue in altered forms, while other provisions — permission of music, abolition of holy war, changes to inheritance law — represent direct departures from established *fiqh*.

### Historical Context
The Baha'i movement emerged from Shia Islam in nineteenth-century Iran. Understanding the Aqdas requires knowledge of Twelver Shia messianic expectations, the Bab's prior revelatory claims, and the specific theological environment of Qajar-era Persia.

## Theological Emphases

- **Khatm an-nubuwwa**: The question of prophetic finality
- **Abrogation (naskh)**: Whether post-Quranic abrogation is theologically possible
- **Bid'a (innovation)**: The Aqdas's departures from Sharia viewed as impermissible innovation
- **Dialogue**: Some contemporary Muslim scholars engage the Aqdas in interfaith conversation
