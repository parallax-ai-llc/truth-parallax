## Overview

The Kitab-i-Aqdas (Arabic: الكتاب الأقدس, "The Most Holy Book") is the central book of the Baha'i Faith, revealed by Baha'u'llah in Arabic around 1873 while he was a prisoner in Akka (present-day Acre, Israel). It is regarded by Baha'is as the charter of the future world civilization and the repository of the laws and ordinances of God for this dispensation.

## Structure

The Kitab-i-Aqdas does not follow a systematic legal code format but weaves together laws, exhortations, prophecies, and theological reflections in a flowing, revelatory style:

1. **Laws of Prayer and Fasting**: Obligatory prayer, fasting periods, and devotional ordinances
2. **Personal Status Laws**: Marriage, divorce, inheritance, and burial
3. **Miscellaneous Laws**: Prohibitions, permissions, and social regulations
4. **Admonitions and Exhortations**: Moral guidance and spiritual counsel
5. **Prophecies and Promises**: Declarations about the future of humanity and the Baha'i Faith

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Kitab-i-Aqdas holds distinct significance across multiple interpretive frameworks:

- **Baha'i**: The supreme legislative authority for Baha'i life, complemented by Baha'u'llah's other writings and authoritative interpretations
- **Islamic Studies**: Examined as a post-Islamic revelation that both continues and abrogates aspects of Sharia
- **Christian Studies**: Compared with biblical law and New Testament ethics
- **Academic**: Studied as a modern scripture emerging from nineteenth-century Middle Eastern religious ferment
- **Comparative**: Analyzed alongside other legal-revelatory texts across traditions
- **Interfaith**: Read as a contribution to universal ethical discourse

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Progressive Revelation | God's truth unfolds through successive messengers |
| World Order | The vision of a unified global civilization |
| Individual and Community | Laws balancing personal spiritual growth with social responsibility |
| Abrogation and Renewal | This dispensation supersedes previous religious laws |
| Justice | The foundation of human society and divine governance |
