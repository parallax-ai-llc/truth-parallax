## Overview

Tobit is not a recognized text in Islam, and the book has no direct parallel in the Quran or Hadith literature. However, Islamic tradition engages with its themes indirectly through the category of Isra'iliyyat, narratives from Jewish and Christian sources that entered Islamic scholarly discourse. The book's central themes of angelic guidance, patient endurance, and trust in God resonate with Islamic values.

## Parallels and Resonances

The figure of Raphael, an angel sent by God to guide and heal, finds resonance in the Islamic understanding of angels as agents of divine will. While Islam names four chief angels (Jibril, Mika'il, Israfil, and Azra'il), the concept of angelic assistance to believers is well established. Tobit's patient endurance of suffering parallels the Quranic portrayal of Ayyub (Job), who likewise remained faithful through affliction and was ultimately healed.

## Islamic Assessment

Muslim scholars would evaluate Tobit's narrative through the lens of tawhid and prophetic integrity. The story's monotheistic framework and emphasis on prayer and charity are consistent with Islamic principles. However, elements such as the use of fish organs to drive away demons would be regarded with caution, as Islamic theology attributes power over jinn and demons to God alone, not to material remedies.
