Catholic interpretation of Tobit 2 emphasizes the mystery of innocent suffering and the virtue of patience under trial. Tobit's blinding after performing an act of mercy, burying a dead Israelite, illustrates the Catholic teaching that righteous suffering is not evidence of divine disfavor but may serve God's inscrutable purposes. This connects to the broader Catholic theology of redemptive suffering, developed most fully in John Paul II's apostolic letter Salvifici Doloris.

The exchange between Tobit and his wife Anna, in which she challenges his piety in the face of suffering, mirrors the dialogue between Job and his wife. Catholic commentators note that Tobit's patient endurance, while not perfect, reflects the growth in virtue that suffering can produce. The Church Fathers read Tobit's blindness allegorically as well: the physical darkness representing a stage of spiritual purification through which the soul must pass before receiving the light of God's fuller revelation.
