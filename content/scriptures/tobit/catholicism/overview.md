## Overview

The Catholic Church regards Tobit as fully canonical scripture, affirmed at the Councils of Hippo (393), Carthage (397), Florence (1442), and definitively at the Council of Trent (1546). Catholic interpretation reads Tobit as divinely inspired narrative that teaches about prayer, marriage, almsgiving, and the role of angels in God's providential care.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

Catholic exegesis highlights Tobit as a rich source for sacramental theology. The marriage of Tobias and Sarah, preceded by prayer and blessed by the angel, is read as a model for the sacrament of matrimony. The Rite of Marriage in the Catholic liturgy draws on Tobit's imagery. Raphael's instruction that "prayer with fasting and almsgiving is better than treasure" (12:8) supports Catholic teaching on the meritorious nature of good works and the spiritual disciplines of Lent.

## Patristic and Magisterial Reception

The Church Fathers drew extensively on Tobit. Ambrose cited the book in his treatise on Tobias to warn against usury. Augustine referenced Tobit's blindness and healing as an allegory of spiritual illumination. The book appears in the Lectionary and is read at wedding liturgies, ensuring its ongoing presence in Catholic worship and devotion.
