Protestant readers encounter Tobit 1 as the opening of a compelling diaspora narrative. Tobit introduces himself as a faithful Israelite in Nineveh who observes the Law despite living under Assyrian rule, performing acts of charity and burying the dead at personal risk. While Protestants do not regard this chapter as canonical scripture, they recognize its value as a portrait of pious Jewish life in exile that illustrates themes found throughout the canonical Old Testament.

The chapter's emphasis on almsgiving and righteous living resonates with Protestant ethics, though Protestant theology would ground these virtues in canonical commands rather than in Tobit's authority. Tobit's faithfulness amid a hostile environment mirrors the experience of Daniel and his companions, providing a useful devotional parallel. The narrative sets up a story of undeserved suffering that Protestant readers can engage with as edifying literature, even while maintaining that doctrinal authority rests solely with the canonical books.
