Tobit 2 describes Tobit's blinding after bird droppings fall into his eyes while he sleeps in a courtyard, having just buried a dead Israelite. Protestant interpreters note the strong parallels with the Book of Job: a righteous man suffers inexplicably, his wife challenges his piety, and the reader is left to grapple with the mystery of undeserved suffering. This chapter provides Protestants with a useful intertext for understanding the broader biblical wisdom tradition.

While the specific details of Tobit's blinding strike some Protestant readers as folkloristic, the theological question the chapter raises is profoundly biblical: why do the righteous suffer? Protestant theology addresses this through the canonical wisdom literature and ultimately through the cross of Christ, but Tobit 2 offers a poignant narrative exploration of the same question that many Protestant commentators find genuinely moving.
