Tobit 6 narrates the encounter with a large fish in the Tigris River and Raphael's instruction that its heart, liver, and gall can be used to drive away demons and cure blindness. Protestant readers have historically found this chapter among the most difficult in the book, as the use of material remedies against spiritual beings appears to border on magical practice.

Protestant theology emphasizes God's direct sovereignty over demonic forces and is cautious about attributing spiritual power to physical objects. However, some Protestant scholars note that the fish organs function within the narrative as instruments of divine will rather than as magical objects in themselves. The chapter also advances Raphael's plan to unite Tobias with Sarah, setting up the marriage that will resolve both families' crises. Protestant commentators often treat this chapter as illustrative of the book's folkloristic literary genre rather than as a model for spiritual practice.
