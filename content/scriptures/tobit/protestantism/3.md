Tobit 3 presents the simultaneous prayers of Tobit in Nineveh and Sarah in Ecbatana, both asking God for death in their despair. Sarah has been tormented by the demon Asmodeus, who has killed her seven successive husbands on their wedding nights. The chapter concludes by revealing that God heard both prayers simultaneously and dispatched the angel Raphael to answer them.

Protestant readers appreciate the chapter's theology of prayer, which parallels canonical teaching on God hearing the cries of the afflicted (Psalm 34:17). However, the role of Asmodeus as a named demon and Raphael as an angelic agent sent in response raises questions for Protestant theology, which tends to be more restrained about angelology and demonology than Catholic or Orthodox traditions. The literary artistry of the parallel prayers is widely admired, and the chapter's message that God works behind the scenes even when sufferers cannot perceive it resonates deeply with Reformed notions of providence.
