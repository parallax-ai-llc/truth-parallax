Tobit 5 introduces Raphael, who appears in disguise as a fellow Israelite named Azariah and offers to accompany Tobias on his journey to Media. The dramatic irony of the chapter is central to its literary effect: the reader knows Raphael's true identity, while the characters do not. Protestant interpreters appreciate this as skilled storytelling, even as they note that the angel's role is more prominent than in most canonical narratives.

The chapter raises the question of divine providence operating through hidden means, a theme that Protestants find theologically congenial. Calvin's doctrine of providence, in which God directs all events through secondary causes that may be invisible to those involved, finds a narrative illustration in Raphael's hidden guidance. However, Protestant theology would emphasize that such angelic mediation is not normative for the believer's experience and should not form the basis for a developed angelology.
