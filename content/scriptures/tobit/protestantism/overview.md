## Overview

Protestant tradition does not include Tobit in the biblical canon. Following the lead of Jerome, who distinguished between canonical and ecclesiastical books, and the later decisions of the Reformation, Protestants classify Tobit as apocryphal. Martin Luther included it in his 1534 Bible translation in a separate section, noting these books were "not held equal to the Sacred Scriptures" but were "useful and good for reading."

## Reasons for Exclusion

The book was not part of the Hebrew Bible as received in rabbinic Judaism, and Protestant reformers adopted the shorter Hebrew canon. Additionally, certain elements raised concerns: the angel Raphael's role appeared to elevate angelic mediation beyond what Protestant theology affirms, and the use of fish organs as a remedy against demons struck some reformers as superstitious. The Westminster Confession (1646) explicitly states that the apocryphal books have "no authority in the Church of God."

## Engagement with the Content

Despite its non-canonical status, many Protestant scholars appreciate Tobit as a window into Second Temple Jewish piety. The book's emphasis on prayer, almsgiving, and trust in divine providence resonates with Protestant values, even if Protestants derive these teachings from canonical texts. The narrative structure, with its dramatic irony and providential plot, is widely admired as a masterpiece of Jewish storytelling.
