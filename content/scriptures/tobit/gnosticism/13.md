A Gnostic reading of Tobit 13 would reinterpret the hymn's vision of a glorified Jerusalem as a description of the Pleroma itself, the transcendent realm of light from which all divine sparks originate and to which they seek to return. The precious stones with which the city is rebuilt represent the luminous Aeons of the Pleroma, each one a facet of the divine fullness. The vision of a city shining with supernatural radiance corresponds to the Gnostic descriptions of the realm of light.

The hymn's call for repentance and return would be read as a call to awakening, to turn from the material world's deceptions and remember one's true origin in the divine realm. The universalism of the hymn, calling all nations to praise God, reflects the Gnostic conviction that divine sparks are scattered throughout the cosmos and that the work of salvation extends to all who possess the pneumatic seed, regardless of their earthly nation or condition.
