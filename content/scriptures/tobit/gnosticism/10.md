Gnostic interpretation of Tobit 10 would read the anxiety of Tobit and Hannah as an illustration of the condition of those who remain in ignorance while the work of salvation proceeds elsewhere. The parents, still in Nineveh and still blind (both physically and spiritually in Tobit's case), represent souls who have not yet received gnosis and who therefore experience the world as a place of fear, uncertainty, and loss.

The separation between the parents and their son mirrors the Gnostic separation between those who remain trapped in the material world and those who are journeying toward liberation. Hannah's grief, though genuine, arises from her inability to perceive the larger divine plan unfolding beyond her sight. The chapter serves as a reminder that the work of salvation is often invisible to those who have not yet received the illuminating knowledge.
