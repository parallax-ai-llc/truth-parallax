Gnostic reading of Tobit 14 would interpret Tobit's death as the soul's final departure from the material world, the pneumatic's return to the Pleroma after completing the earthly sojourn. His prophecy of Nineveh's destruction represents the Gnostic expectation that the material world, the Demiurge's flawed creation, will ultimately be dissolved. The destruction of the worldly city symbolizes the end of archonic rule.

Tobias's departure from Nineveh before its destruction, acting on his father's prophetic warning, represents the pneumatic who heeds the revealed knowledge and escapes the material world before its final collapse. The Gnostic eschatological vision does not look forward to the renovation of the material world but to its dissolution and the return of all divine sparks to the Pleroma. Tobit's peaceful death at a great age, having seen his grandchildren and received the vindication of his faith, models the Gnostic ideal of the illuminated soul departing in knowledge and peace.
