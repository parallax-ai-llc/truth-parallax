Jewish readers find in Tobit 4 a compendium of ethical instruction closely aligned with Torah values. Tobit's emphasis on tzedakah (charitable giving), honoring parents, fair wages, and endogamous marriage resonates with the ethical teachings found in Deuteronomy and the wisdom literature. The concept of almsgiving as a 'treasure stored up' anticipates the rabbinic teaching that mitzvot (commandments) create merit that accompanies a person to the world to come.

The chapter's instruction on marriage within the ancestral family reflects the strong preference for endogamy found in the Torah narratives of the patriarchs and maintained in varying degrees throughout Jewish history. Jewish scholars note that Tobit's ethical teachings, while not authoritative as canon, provide important evidence for the moral worldview of Second Temple Judaism and its continuity with both earlier biblical and later rabbinic ethics.
