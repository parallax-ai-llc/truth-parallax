Jewish scholars read Tobit 7 with attention to its portrayal of marriage customs in the Second Temple period. The writing of a marriage contract mirrors the development of the ketubah in Jewish law, which would become a standard requirement in rabbinic marriage. The emphasis on kinship and tribal identity in the marriage arrangement reflects the biblical concern with endogamy found in the patriarchal narratives.

The chapter provides historical evidence for Jewish marriage practices in the diaspora, including the role of the father in arranging marriages, the exchange of blessings, and the formal documentation of the union. These practices, while not identical to later rabbinic requirements, show continuity with the tradition that would produce the detailed marriage laws of the Mishnah and Talmud.
