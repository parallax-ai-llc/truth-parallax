Jewish scholars read Tobit 13 as a Second Temple psalm that reflects the theological concerns of Jews living in exile. The hymn's call to praise God even in exile, its confession of national sin as the cause of dispersion, and its vision of a restored Jerusalem align with themes found in the canonical prophets and the exilic psalms (Psalms 137, 126).

The vision of Jerusalem rebuilt with precious stones (13:16-17) draws on Isaiah 54:11-12 and anticipates later Jewish eschatological expectations of a glorified Holy City. This tradition would continue to develop in rabbinic literature and Jewish liturgy, where the prayer for the rebuilding of Jerusalem remains central to daily worship. While Tobit's hymn was not included in the canonical Psalter, it provides important evidence for the development of Jewish hymnody and eschatological hope in the Second Temple period.
