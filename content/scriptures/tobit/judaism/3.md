Jewish scholarly interest in Tobit 3 centers on the figure of Asmodeus (Ashmedai), who would later appear prominently in rabbinic literature. The Talmud (Gittin 68a-b) contains an elaborate narrative about King Solomon and Ashmedai, suggesting that the demon figure from Tobit entered the broader Jewish literary tradition. The chapter's simultaneous prayers of Tobit and Sarah reflect the Jewish belief in the power of communal prayer, even when the pray-ers are geographically separated.

The theology of Tobit 3, in which God hears the prayers of the afflicted and responds through angelic mediation, is consistent with the Jewish understanding found in the canonical Psalms and prophetic literature. The chapter demonstrates the Second Temple Jewish belief in an active angelic hierarchy, a belief that would be variously affirmed and moderated in subsequent rabbinic tradition.
