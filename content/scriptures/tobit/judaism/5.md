Jewish scholarly analysis of Tobit 5 examines the chapter's angelology within the broader context of Second Temple Jewish beliefs about angels. The appearance of Raphael as a named angel with a specific divine mission reflects the developed angelology found in texts like 1 Enoch and the Dead Sea Scrolls, where named angels serve distinct functions in God's administration of the cosmos.

The chapter's dramatic irony, in which Raphael conceals his identity behind a human persona, raises interesting questions about the relationship between the human and angelic realms in Second Temple thought. Rabbinic Judaism would later moderate some of the more elaborate angelological speculations of the Second Temple period, but the belief in angelic messengers remained part of Jewish tradition, as evidenced by the Talmudic discussions of angels and the development of angelic imagery in Jewish liturgy and mysticism.
