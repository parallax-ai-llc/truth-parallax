Jewish readers of Tobit 2 encounter a narrative that closely parallels the biblical Job: a righteous man suffers a devastating affliction immediately after performing a good deed. The chapter raises the theodicy question that is central to Jewish philosophical and theological inquiry, from the Talmudic discussions of 'tzaddik v'ra lo' (why the righteous suffer) to the medieval philosophical treatments of Saadia Gaon and Maimonides.

The relationship between Tobit and his wife Anna in this chapter reflects the domestic tensions that suffering creates, a theme explored with great sensitivity in rabbinic literature. While Tobit was not incorporated into the canon, its exploration of innocent suffering within a framework of covenant faithfulness represents a significant contribution to the broader Jewish conversation about divine justice and human endurance.
