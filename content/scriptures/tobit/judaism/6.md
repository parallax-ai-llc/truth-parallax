Jewish scholars approach Tobit 6 with particular interest in its intersection of folk medicine, demonology, and angelology. The use of fish organs as remedies against demons and blindness reflects a broader tradition of Jewish folk healing attested in the Dead Sea Scrolls, the Sefer ha-Razim, and later in the Talmud. These practices existed alongside official halakhic discourse and provide evidence for popular Jewish beliefs in the Second Temple period.

The chapter's instructions for using material elements against demonic forces raise questions about the boundaries between religion and magic in ancient Judaism, a topic extensively discussed in modern scholarship. Jewish historians note that while rabbinic literature would later seek to regulate and limit such practices, the Second Temple period shows a more fluid relationship between official theology and popular belief, and Tobit provides a valuable window into this world.
