Jewish scholarly engagement with Tobit 1 focuses on the chapter's detailed portrait of diaspora Jewish observance. Tobit describes his adherence to tithing, pilgrimage to Jerusalem, dietary laws, and endogamous marriage, all practices that align with halakhic requirements found in the Torah and developed in rabbinic literature. The discovery of Aramaic and Hebrew fragments of Tobit at Qumran (4Q196-200) confirms that the book circulated among Jews in the Second Temple period.

The chapter's emphasis on burying the dead, even at personal risk, reflects a value deeply embedded in Jewish law. The mitzvah of met mitzvah (an unattended corpse that anyone must bury) is discussed in the Talmud (Megillah 3b) and resonates with Tobit's practice. While the book was not included in the rabbinic canon, its depiction of diaspora piety provides valuable historical evidence for how Jewish communities maintained their identity and observance far from the Land of Israel.
