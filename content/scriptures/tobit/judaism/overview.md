## Overview

Tobit is not part of the Jewish biblical canon (Tanakh), but it holds significant historical importance as a product of Second Temple Judaism. Fragments of Tobit in both Aramaic and Hebrew were discovered among the Dead Sea Scrolls at Qumran (4Q196-200), demonstrating that the book was read and copied by Jewish communities in the late Second Temple period.

## Historical Significance

The book reflects diaspora Jewish life with remarkable detail: observance of dietary laws, tithing, endogamous marriage, burial of the dead, and almsgiving. These practices align closely with halakhic concerns found in rabbinic literature, even though the rabbis did not ultimately include Tobit in the canon. The book provides valuable evidence for how Jews in the eastern diaspora understood their religious obligations and maintained their identity under foreign rule.

## Legacy in Jewish Tradition

Although excluded from the Tanakh, Tobit's influence persisted in Jewish culture. Some elements of the story, particularly the demon Asmodeus (Ashmedai), appear in later rabbinic literature, notably in the Talmud (Gittin 68a-b). The book's emphasis on honoring parents, charitable giving, and faithful marriage reflects values central to Jewish ethics across all periods.
