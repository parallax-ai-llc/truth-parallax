## Theravada Perspective

This section of the Avatamsaka Sutra presents the Buddha's initial awakening display, in which infinite worlds are adorned with wondrous phenomena as the Tathagata sits beneath the Bodhi tree, his enlightenment simultaneously pervading every realm of existence. From a Theravada standpoint, this passage is studied as a major Mahayana elaboration that moves well beyond the scope of the Pali Canon, while nonetheless building upon principles found in early Buddhist teaching.

Theravada scholars recognize that the Avatamsaka's cosmic vision extends the early Buddhist teaching of dependent origination (*paticcasamuppada*) into a domain far beyond what the Pali suttas envision. Where the Pali tradition describes dependent origination as the causal chain underlying individual suffering and liberation, the Avatamsaka expands this into a metaphysical principle governing the entire cosmos — every phenomenon arising in dependence upon every other phenomenon.

While the devotional and cosmological elements of this section have no parallel in the Theravada canon, the underlying insight that all things are conditioned and interdependent represents a creative and philosophically significant development of early Buddhist thought.
