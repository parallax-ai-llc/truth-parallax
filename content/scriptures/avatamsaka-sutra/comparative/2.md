## Comparative Perspective

This section of the Avatamsaka presents the cosmic Buddha Vairocana and the lotus treasury world-system (*Padmagarbha-lokadhatu*), an infinite ocean of worlds arranged in a vast lotus formation, each world containing its own Buddha and assembly. Viewed comparatively, this passage engages with some of the deepest and most persistent questions in human thought: the relationship of part to whole, the one and the many, the nature of consciousness, and the structure of ultimate reality.

The Avatamsaka's vision of interpenetration finds resonance in mystical traditions across religions. Meister Eckhart's teaching that "God is in all things, and all things are in God" articulates a similar intuition from within the Christian mystical tradition. The Sufi concept of *wahdat al-wujud* (unity of existence) as developed by Ibn Arabi describes a reality in which the divine is fully present in every created thing. The Kabbalistic notion of divine sparks (*nitzotzot*) scattered throughout creation parallels the Avatamsaka's teaching that every particle contains the Buddha.

In the modern context, the Avatamsaka's imagery of Indra's Net has been embraced by systems theorists, ecologists, and network scientists as an ancient anticipation of interconnected, non-hierarchical models of reality. The sutra's vision challenges the atomistic, reductionist assumptions of much Western thought and offers an alternative framework in which relationship and interconnection are primary.
