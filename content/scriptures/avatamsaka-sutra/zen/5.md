## Zen Reading

This section of the Avatamsaka presents the youth Sudhana's pilgrimage to fifty-three spiritual teachers (*kalyanamitra*) of extraordinary diversity, culminating in his entry into the tower of Maitreya and his final realization of the dharmadhatu in the presence of Samantabhadra. For Zen practitioners, the Avatamsaka's vision of interpenetration and totality provides the philosophical ground for the central Zen experience: the realization that the ordinary world, just as it is, is the Buddha-realm.

The Chan master Qingliang Chengguan (738–839), who served as the fourth Huayan patriarch and deeply influenced Chan Buddhism, taught that the Avatamsaka's vision is realized not through philosophical analysis but through direct seeing. When a Zen practitioner drinks tea, the entire dharmadhatu is present in that act. When she sweeps the garden, Indra's Net shimmers in every motion of the broom. This is not metaphor but direct description of awakened experience.

Dongshan Liangjie's Five Ranks — the mutual interpenetration of the absolute and the relative — draw directly on the Avatamsaka's philosophical framework and translate it into the language of Zen practice. The practitioner does not escape the relative to reach the absolute but realizes their perfect non-duality in each moment of ordinary activity.
