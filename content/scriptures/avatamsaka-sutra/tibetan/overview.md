## Overview

In Tibetan Buddhism, the Avatamsaka Sutra is known as *mDo phal po che* ("The Great Extensive Sutra") and is included in the Kangyur. While the Avatamsaka does not serve as the basis for a distinct Tibetan school as it does in China and Japan, its key sections — especially the Dashabhumika and the Gandavyuha — are studied extensively in relation to the bodhisattva path and the stages of realization.

## The Ten Stages in Tibetan Scholasticism

The Dashabhumika section of the Avatamsaka is one of the primary sources for the Tibetan understanding of the bodhisattva *bhumis* (stages). Tibetan commentators such as Tsongkhapa integrate the ten stages with the five paths (*panchamarga*), the six perfections (*paramita*), and the Madhyamaka understanding of emptiness to produce a comprehensive map of the path to Buddhahood.

## Yogacara and Madhyamaka Readings

Tibetan scholars read the Avatamsaka through both Yogacara and Madhyamaka lenses. The sutra's emphasis on mind (*chitta*) as the basis of all experience has Yogacara resonances, while its teaching on the emptiness and interpenetration of all dharmas is interpreted through Madhyamaka analysis. The synthesis of these philosophical systems, characteristic of Tibetan scholasticism, finds rich support in the Avatamsaka's multivalent text.

## Sudhana in Tibetan Art

The Gandavyuha narrative of Sudhana's pilgrimage is depicted in Tibetan thangka paintings and temple murals, particularly in the Nyingma and Kagyu traditions. Sudhana's encounters with diverse teachers — male and female, monastic and lay, human and non-human — illustrate the Tibetan emphasis on the guru-disciple relationship (*bla ma'i rnal 'byor*) and the importance of relying on qualified spiritual friends.
