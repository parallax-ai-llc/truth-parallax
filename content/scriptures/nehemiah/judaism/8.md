## The Great Torah Reading: Foundation of Synagogue Liturgy

Jewish interpretation of Nehemiah 8 reads it as the foundational scene for synagogue worship. The Talmud (Bava Kamma 82a) credits Ezra with instituting public Torah reading, and this chapter depicts the practice in its most dramatic form.

## Key Themes

### Public Torah Reading

The assembly's request that Ezra bring the Torah establishes the principle that Torah reading is a communal act, not a private study. The Talmud prescribes Torah reading on Mondays, Thursdays, Shabbat, and festivals — a pattern attributed to Ezra's institution.

### The Meturgeman

"They gave the sense, so that the people understood" (8:8). Jewish tradition identifies this as the origin of the *targum* — the Aramaic translation/interpretation that accompanied public Torah reading for communities no longer fluent in Hebrew. The role of the *meturgeman* (translator) became a fixed feature of synagogue worship.

### Sukkot Rediscovered

The recovery of the Feast of Tabernacles (8:14-17) illustrates how Torah study leads to the recovery of forgotten practices. The statement that Sukkot had not been properly observed "since the days of Joshua" reflects the Talmud's recognition that certain observances were lost during periods of spiritual decline.

## Theological Significance

- **Public Torah reading**: The foundation of synagogue worship
- **Translation and interpretation**: The Torah must be understood, not merely recited
- **Recovery of practice**: Torah study restores forgotten observances
- **Communal joy**: Understanding the Torah produces celebration
