## Overview

In the Jewish canon, Nehemiah forms a single book with Ezra in the Ketuvim (Writings). While Ezra represents the priestly-scribal tradition, Nehemiah embodies lay leadership — a governor who combines political skill with deep piety. The Talmud discusses Nehemiah alongside Ezra but assigns him a distinct role in the restoration of Jewish communal life.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

### Talmudic
The Talmud (Sanhedrin 93b) identifies Nehemiah ben Hacaliah with Zerubbabel, though this identification is debated. His "Remember me" prayers are discussed as models of petition. The communal covenant of chapter 10 is analyzed for its halakhic implications.

### Medieval
Rashi and Ibn Ezra comment on Nehemiah's historical narrative, clarifying geographical references, genealogies, and the chronological relationship to Ezra's mission.

### Modern
Contemporary Jewish scholars examine Nehemiah's governance within the context of Persian imperial administration, exploring how Jewish self-governance functioned under foreign rule.

## Theological Emphases

- **Lay leadership**: Nehemiah models effective governance without priestly credentials
- **Jerusalem's centrality**: Rebuilding the walls affirms Zion's enduring importance
- **Covenant community**: The people voluntarily bind themselves to Torah observance
- **Shabbat observance**: Nehemiah's Sabbath reforms establish communal practice
