## Economic Justice

Nehemiah 5 addresses an internal crisis: wealthy Jews are exploiting their poorer brethren through usurious lending, forcing families into debt slavery. Nehemiah's response is one of the most powerful models of social justice leadership in Scripture.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The Cry of the Poor (5:1-5)

The poor are mortgaging fields, vineyards, and even their children to survive. Protestant social ethics sees this as a violation of covenant community: the rich are profiting from the misery created by the very crisis the community faces together.

### Nehemiah's Anger (5:6-7)

"I was very angry." Protestant interpreters note that righteous anger at injustice is not only permitted but required of godly leaders. This is not personal offense but moral outrage at covenant violation.

### The Public Confrontation (5:7-11)

Nehemiah confronts the nobles publicly and demands restitution. Protestant ethics of accountability requires that leaders who exploit the vulnerable be challenged openly, not merely counseled privately.

### Nehemiah's Personal Example (5:14-19)

Nehemiah refuses the governor's food allowance, personally bearing the cost of hospitality. Protestant leadership teaching emphasizes that leaders must model the standards they demand of others.

## Theological Significance

- **Social justice**: God demands economic fairness within the covenant community
- **Righteous anger**: Moral outrage at exploitation is appropriate
- **Public accountability**: Leaders must confront injustice openly
- **Leading by example**: Personal sacrifice validates public demands
