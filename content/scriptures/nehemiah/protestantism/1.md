## Nehemiah's Prayer

Nehemiah 1 opens with one of the great prayers of the Old Testament. Upon hearing that Jerusalem's walls lie in ruins, Nehemiah — cupbearer to King Artaxerxes — responds with fasting, weeping, and a carefully structured prayer that becomes a model for Protestant intercession.

## Key Verses and Interpretations

### The News from Jerusalem (1:1-3)

Nehemiah inquires about the returned exiles and receives devastating news: "The remnant there in the province who survived the exile is in great trouble and shame. The wall of Jerusalem is broken down." Protestant interpreters note that Nehemiah's concern begins with a question — godly leaders seek information before acting.

### The Prayer Structure (1:4-11)

Nehemiah's prayer follows a pattern that Protestant theology identifies as exemplary:

| Element | Verses | Application |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Adoration | 1:5 | God's greatness and covenant faithfulness |
| Confession | 1:6-7 | Corporate sin acknowledged |
| Supplication | 1:8-9 | Appeal to God's promises |
| Specific petition | 1:11 | Favor before the king |

### The Arrow Prayer (1:11)

"Give success to your servant today, and grant him mercy in the sight of this man." Protestant commentators note the phrase "this man" — Nehemiah regards the mighty Artaxerxes merely as "this man" in comparison with the God he addresses.

## Theological Significance

- **Prayer before action**: Nehemiah prays for four months before acting
- **Corporate confession**: He identifies with his people's sins
- **Covenantal appeal**: Prayer is grounded in God's promises
- **Lay calling**: A secular official takes spiritual initiative
