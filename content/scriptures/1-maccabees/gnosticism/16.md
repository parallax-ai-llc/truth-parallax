Gnostic interpreters read the murder of Simon in chapter 16 as a final illustration of the treachery inherent in the material world, where even the bonds of family and loyalty are corrupted by archontic influence. Ptolemy's betrayal represents the Demiurge's ultimate weapon — the subversion of trust and love, the most intimate human connections turned into instruments of destruction.

The book's abrupt and inconclusive ending resonates with the Gnostic understanding that material history has no satisfying conclusion within its own terms. The narrative simply breaks off, gesturing toward a fulfillment that cannot be found within the material realm. For Gnostic readers, this incompleteness points toward the transcendent reality that lies beyond all earthly narratives — the Pleroma, the fullness of the divine, which alone can provide the resolution that history perpetually promises but never delivers.
