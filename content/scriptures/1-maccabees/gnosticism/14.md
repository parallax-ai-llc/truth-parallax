Gnostic interpreters read chapter 14's celebration of Simon's reign as a depiction of the best that material existence can offer — peace, prosperity, and the freedom to worship. Yet even this idealized portrait falls short of the Gnostic vision of true fulfillment, which transcends all earthly conditions. The description of each person sitting under their vine and fig tree represents, at most, the psychic contentment of those who have not yet awakened to the pneumatic reality beyond the material world.

The institutionalization of Simon's authority through official decrees and inscriptions illustrates the Gnostic critique of religion becoming codified and externalized. True spiritual authority, in Gnostic understanding, cannot be inscribed on bronze tablets but must be directly transmitted from spirit to spirit through the living experience of gnosis.
