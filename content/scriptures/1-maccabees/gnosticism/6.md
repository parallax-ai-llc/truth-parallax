Gnostic interpreters read the death of Antiochus IV in chapter 6 as the dissolution of an archontic power whose time has expired. The Demiurge's agents, though seemingly invincible, are ultimately subject to the cycles of creation and destruction that govern the material world. Antiochus's deathbed acknowledgment of a higher power illustrates the Gnostic teaching that even the archons dimly perceive the existence of the true God beyond their ken.

The siege and political turmoil that follow represent the instability of the material order, which can never achieve the permanence and peace that belong only to the Pleroma. Gnostic readers find in this chapter confirmation that the kingdoms of this world are inherently transient and that attachment to them — whether as rulers or subjects — is a form of spiritual bondage.
