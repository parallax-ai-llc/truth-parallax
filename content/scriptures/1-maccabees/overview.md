## Overview

1 Maccabees is a historical narrative covering the period from approximately 175 to 134 BCE, recounting the Jewish revolt against the Seleucid Empire and the establishment of the Hasmonean dynasty. Originally composed in Hebrew (now lost), the book survives in its Greek translation within the Septuagint. It is regarded as one of the most historically reliable accounts of the Maccabean period.

## Structure

1 Maccabees is organized around the careers of three brothers from the priestly family of Mattathias:

1. **Background and Revolt** (Chapters 1–2): Antiochus IV's persecution and Mattathias's uprising
2. **Judas Maccabeus** (Chapters 3–9): Military campaigns, temple rededication, and death in battle
3. **Jonathan** (Chapters 9–12): Diplomacy, alliances with Rome and Sparta, and political maneuvering
4. **Simon** (Chapters 13–16): Independence achieved, the Hasmonean high priesthood established

## Cross-Traditional Significance

1 Maccabees occupies a complex position across religious traditions:

- **Catholicism and Eastern Orthodoxy**: Canonical Scripture, included in the Old Testament
- **Protestantism**: Regarded as apocryphal; valued historically but not doctrinally authoritative
- **Judaism**: Not part of the Tanakh, but the events it describes are commemorated in the festival of Hanukkah
- **Islam**: Not a recognized text, though the broader theme of resisting religious oppression resonates with Islamic values

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|

| Covenant Faithfulness | Loyalty to Torah as the defining mark of Jewish identity |

| Temple | The centrality of the Jerusalem Temple to Jewish worship |
| Providence | God's hidden hand guiding Israel's deliverance |
