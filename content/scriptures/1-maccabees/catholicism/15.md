Catholic commentators read chapter 15's account of Antiochus VII's threats against Simon as a reminder that the peace of God's people in this world is always provisional. The Seleucid demand for the return of captured territories and payment of tribute illustrates the perennial tension between the claims of secular empire and the rights of the faith community — a tension the Catholic Church has navigated throughout its history.

Simon's refusal to surrender Jewish territory, grounded in the argument that the land belongs to Israel by ancestral right, resonates with Catholic natural law teaching about the rights of peoples to their ancestral homeland. The chapter's diplomatic and military preparations illustrate the Catholic principle of legitimate defense while maintaining the priority of negotiation and peaceful resolution.
