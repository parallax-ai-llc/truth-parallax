The tragic murder of Simon and his sons in chapter 16 is read by Catholic interpreters as a sobering conclusion to the Maccabean narrative, illustrating the perennial reality of human sinfulness even within communities dedicated to God's service. The treachery of Ptolemy, Simon's own son-in-law, demonstrates that external enemies are not the only threat to the faithful — betrayal from within is equally dangerous.

The survival of John Hyrcanus and the continuation of Hasmonean rule point, in Catholic reading, to the resilience of God's purposes despite human failure. Catholic interpreters see in the book's conclusion a foreshadowing of the Church's own experience: betrayed by some of her own children yet sustained by divine providence through every crisis. The incomplete ending of the narrative gestures toward the greater fulfillment that awaits in the coming of Christ.
