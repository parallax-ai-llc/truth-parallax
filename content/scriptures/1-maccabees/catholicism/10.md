Catholic interpreters read Jonathan's acceptance of the high priesthood in chapter 10 as a complex development in salvation history. While acknowledging the irregularity of his non-Zadokite priestly lineage, Catholic exegesis emphasizes that God can work through imperfect human instruments to accomplish divine purposes. The Hasmonean high priesthood, though canonically irregular, preserved Jewish worship and identity during a critical period.

The chapter's account of rival Seleucid kings competing for Jonathan's allegiance illustrates the Catholic understanding of the Church's relationship to political power — maintaining independence while engaging pragmatically with temporal authorities. Catholic social teaching draws on such historical examples to articulate the principle of the legitimate autonomy of temporal affairs alongside the Church's right and duty to speak on matters of faith and morals.
