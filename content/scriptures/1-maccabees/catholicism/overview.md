## Overview

The Catholic Church recognizes 1 Maccabees as deuterocanonical Scripture, affirmed at the Councils of Hippo (393), Carthage (397), and definitively at the Council of Trent (1546). Catholic interpretation reads the book as inspired sacred history that reveals God's providential care for his covenant people during a period of severe persecution. The Maccabean narrative is understood within the broader arc of salvation history leading to the coming of Christ.

## Interpretive Approach

Catholic exegesis applies the four senses of Scripture to 1 Maccabees. At the literal level, it is valued as a historical account of God's deliverance of Israel. Allegorically, the purification and rededication of the Temple prefigures Christ's cleansing of the Temple and the Church's ongoing liturgical renewal. Morally, the courage of Mattathias and his sons models faithful resistance to compromise with the world. The Magisterium draws on 1 Maccabees to support the principles of just war and legitimate defense of religious freedom.

## Key Themes in Catholic Reading

Catholic interpreters give particular attention to the Temple rededication in chapter 4, seeing in it a type of the Eucharistic liturgy and the consecration of sacred spaces. The Maccabean zeal for Torah observance resonates with Catholic teaching on the harmony of faith and works. The book's emphasis on priestly leadership aligns with Catholic ecclesiology, and the Hasmonean combination of priestly and political authority is read as a foreshadowing of the Church's role in temporal affairs under the guidance of divine law.
