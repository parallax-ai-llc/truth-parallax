Catholic interpreters read the conflict with Alcimus and Nicanor in chapter 7 as a cautionary tale about the corruption of religious office. Alcimus, though holding the legitimate high priestly title, acts as an instrument of pagan power — a situation the Catholic tradition addresses through its distinction between the validity and the worthiness of a minister. The sacramental principle *ex opere operato* does not excuse personal unworthiness, and Alcimus's betrayal of the Hasideans illustrates the scandal of unfaithful clergy.

The defeat of Nicanor and the celebration of his downfall demonstrate what Catholic theology calls the vindication of divine justice. The establishment of "Nicanor's Day" as a commemorative feast reflects the Catholic practice of liturgically remembering God's interventions in history, a practice that connects the Maccabean period to the broader Catholic calendar of saints' days and holy days.
