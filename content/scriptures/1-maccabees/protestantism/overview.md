## Overview

Protestant tradition classifies 1 Maccabees among the Apocrypha — books included in the Septuagint but not the Hebrew canon. Following the lead of Martin Luther, who placed these books in a separate section between the Old and New Testaments, Protestants regard 1 Maccabees as historically valuable and edifying but not authoritative for establishing doctrine. The book is nevertheless appreciated for illuminating the intertestamental period and providing background for the New Testament world.

## Interpretive Approach

Protestant scholars generally read 1 Maccabees as a reliable historical source for understanding the political and religious conditions that shaped Second Temple Judaism. The book's restrained theological style — it avoids direct mention of God's name — is seen as evidence of its historical sobriety rather than theological deficiency. Reformed commentators have noted parallels between the Maccabean struggle for religious liberty and the Protestant fight against ecclesiastical tyranny.

## Key Themes in Protestant Reading

Protestant interpreters emphasize the themes of covenant faithfulness and resistance to idolatry, seeing in the Maccabean revolt a precedent for standing firm against religious compromise. The narrative of Mattathias refusing to offer pagan sacrifice resonates with Protestant convictions about the primacy of conscience before God. However, the Hasmonean combination of priestly and political authority is viewed with some ambivalence, as it foreshadows the kind of institutional power Protestantism has historically critiqued.
