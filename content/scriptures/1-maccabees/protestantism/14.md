Chapter 14's celebration of Simon's achievements, including peace, prosperity, and the formal recognition of his authority by the Jewish people, is read by Protestant interpreters as the high point of the Hasmonean achievement. The decree honoring Simon, inscribed on bronze tablets and placed in the Temple, represents the institutionalization of the revolutionary movement.

Protestant readers observe both the admirable aspects of Simon's rule — the restoration of religious practice and national dignity — and its troubling implications. The combination of high priesthood, military command, and political authority in one person creates the kind of concentrated power that Protestant political thought has historically regarded with suspicion.
