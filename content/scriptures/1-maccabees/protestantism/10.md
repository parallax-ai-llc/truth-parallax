Protestant interpreters read chapter 10's account of Jonathan's political maneuvering between rival Seleucid claimants as evidence of the complex realities facing religious communities in hostile political environments. Jonathan's acceptance of the high priesthood from Alexander Balas raises significant questions for Protestant readers, who note that he was not of the legitimate Zadokite priestly line.

The chapter illustrates the tension between pragmatic political engagement and religious principle — a tension that Protestant political theology has grappled with throughout its history. The Maccabean willingness to accept honors from pagan rulers in exchange for military support foreshadows the later compromises that would ultimately undermine the Hasmonean legacy.
