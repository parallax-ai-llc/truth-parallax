The death of Antiochus IV, narrated in chapter 6, is read by Protestant interpreters as an instance of divine judgment against tyrants. The king's reported deathbed repentance — acknowledging that his suffering stems from his treatment of Jerusalem — serves as a cautionary tale about the consequences of persecuting God's people. Protestant readers note the parallel with the biblical pattern of divine retribution against oppressors.

The siege of Beth-zur and the political crisis following Antiochus's death illustrate the precarious nature of the Maccabean position, dependent on both military prowess and shifting imperial politics. Protestant commentators observe how the narrative maintains a realistic perspective on the costs and uncertainties of the struggle for religious freedom.
