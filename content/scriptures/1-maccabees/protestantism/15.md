Protestant commentators read chapter 15's account of the growing threat from Antiochus VII Sidetes as a reminder that political independence remains fragile and contingent. The Seleucid demand for the return of captured territories and the imposition of tribute challenges the autonomy that Simon had secured, illustrating the precarious position of a small nation between great powers.

The chapter's diplomatic exchanges and military preparations are read by Protestant interpreters through the lens of the doctrine of lesser magistrates — the principle that subordinate authorities have the right and duty to resist unjust demands from superior powers. This Maccabean precedent was cited by some Reformation-era thinkers in justifying resistance to tyrannical rulers.
