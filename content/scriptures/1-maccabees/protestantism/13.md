Simon's assumption of leadership following Jonathan's capture is read by Protestant interpreters as a continuation of God's providential preservation of the Jewish community. The chapter's account of Simon's military and diplomatic achievements — including the capture of Gazara and the expulsion of its pagan inhabitants — illustrates the ongoing struggle for religious and political independence.

Protestant commentators note Simon's fortification of the Temple mount as both a practical military measure and a symbolic assertion of the centrality of worship in national life. The tension between spiritual and military priorities that runs through the Maccabean narrative continues to provide material for Protestant reflection on the proper relationship between church and state.
