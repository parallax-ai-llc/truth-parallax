Protestant readers note the significance of Jonathan's diplomatic outreach to Rome and Sparta in chapter 12, including the remarkable claim of kinship between Jews and Spartans. The chapter demonstrates the Maccabean effort to establish legitimacy through international alliances, a strategy that Protestant political theorists have analyzed in terms of natural law and the rights of nations.

Jonathan's capture by Trypho at the chapter's end serves as a dramatic reversal, reminding readers that political alliances offer no ultimate security. Protestant interpreters find here a reinforcement of the Reformation principle that trust must ultimately rest in God rather than in human powers and institutions.
