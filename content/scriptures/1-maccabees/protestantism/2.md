Protestant commentators view Mattathias's refusal to offer pagan sacrifice as a powerful example of courageous faith. His cry, "Let every one who is zealous for the law and supports the covenant come out with me" (2:27), resonates with Protestant convictions about individual conscience and the duty to resist religious compromise. The chapter establishes the theological foundation for the revolt: fidelity to Torah above all other loyalties.

The deathbed speech of Mattathias, recounting the faithfulness of Israel's ancestors, is read by Protestant interpreters as a reminder that God's covenant promises remain operative even in the darkest periods of persecution. The emphasis on ancestral example rather than institutional authority aligns with Protestant sensibilities about the primacy of scriptural models over ecclesiastical tradition.
