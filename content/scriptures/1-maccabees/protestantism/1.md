Protestant interpreters read 1 Maccabees 1 as a sobering account of religious persecution under Antiochus IV Epiphanes, who desecrated the Jerusalem Temple and outlawed Jewish religious practice. While Protestants do not regard this text as canonical Scripture, they recognize it as a historically reliable source for understanding the crisis that provoked the Maccabean revolt and the intertestamental context that shaped the world into which Jesus was born.

The chapter's catalog of Antiochus's outrages — the plundering of the Temple, the erection of the "abomination of desolation," and the forced adoption of pagan customs — serves as a case study in the dangers of state-imposed religion. Protestant readers have drawn parallels between these events and the persecution of Reformers by ecclesiastical authorities, finding in the Maccabean period a precedent for the principle that obedience to God takes priority over submission to human rulers.
