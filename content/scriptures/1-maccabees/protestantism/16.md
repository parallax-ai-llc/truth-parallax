The murder of Simon and two of his sons by his son-in-law Ptolemy, narrated in chapter 16, brings 1 Maccabees to a tragic and abrupt conclusion. Protestant readers see in this act of treachery the inevitable corruption that follows when spiritual movements become political dynasties. The violence within the Hasmonean family itself illustrates the biblical warning that "those who live by the sword will die by the sword."

The survival of John Hyrcanus and the brief notice of his subsequent reign point forward to the continuation of Hasmonean rule, which Protestant interpreters evaluate as a mixed legacy — preserving Jewish identity and worship while increasingly succumbing to the temptations of worldly power. The book's ending reinforces the Protestant conviction that no human institution can serve as an adequate substitute for direct reliance on God.
