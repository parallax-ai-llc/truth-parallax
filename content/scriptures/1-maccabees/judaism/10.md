Jewish interpreters read chapter 10's account of Jonathan's political maneuvering with particular attention to his acceptance of the high priesthood from Alexander Balas. This appointment was controversial because the Hasmoneans were not of the Zadokite line that had traditionally held the high priesthood, and many scholars see in this usurpation one of the seeds of the sectarian divisions — including the emergence of the Qumran community — that characterized late Second Temple Judaism.

The chapter illustrates the transformation of the Maccabean movement from a religious revolt into a political dynasty, a development that Jewish thought has evaluated with ambivalence. While acknowledging the Hasmonean preservation of Jewish independence and religious practice, rabbinic tradition was wary of the concentration of priestly and political power in a single family, viewing it as a departure from the proper ordering of Jewish communal life.
