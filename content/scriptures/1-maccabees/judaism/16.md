The murder of Simon in chapter 16 brings the Maccabean narrative to a tragic and ambiguous conclusion. Jewish interpreters note the bitter irony that Simon, who achieved the most complete independence of any Hasmonean leader, was murdered not by a foreign enemy but by his own son-in-law — a fate that seems to validate the rabbinic ambivalence toward the Hasmonean dynasty.

The survival of John Hyrcanus and the continuation of Hasmonean rule point to a future that Jewish readers know would ultimately end in the Roman conquest and the destruction of the Temple. The book's abrupt ending, without theological summation or moral conclusion, leaves the evaluation of the Maccabean achievement to the reader — a judgment that Jewish thought has continued to debate across the centuries.
