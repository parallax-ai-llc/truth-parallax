Jewish scholars read chapter 11 as evidence of Jonathan's growing political sophistication and the increasingly secular character of the Hasmonean enterprise. The complex negotiations with competing Seleucid rulers demonstrate the realities of power politics in the Hellenistic world, in which Jewish leaders were compelled to engage with foreign powers on their own terms.

The chapter's account of Jonathan's military interventions in Seleucid internal affairs raises questions about the limits of permissible entanglement with gentile politics — a concern that would become central to rabbinic political thought. The rabbinic principle of dina d'malkhuta dina (the law of the kingdom is the law) represents a later attempt to define the proper boundary between Jewish communal autonomy and submission to gentile sovereignty.
