Jewish readers appreciate chapter 3's portrait of Judas Maccabeus as a warrior-leader in the tradition of the biblical judges — a charismatic figure raised up by God to deliver Israel from oppression. Judas's prayer before battle, invoking the precedent of David against Goliath, connects the Maccabean revolt to the central biblical narrative of divine deliverance through unlikely human instruments.

Modern Jewish historians have debated the accuracy and ideological bias of 1 Maccabees' account of Judas's campaigns, noting the author's tendency to portray the conflict in terms drawn from earlier biblical narratives. Nevertheless, Judas remains one of the most celebrated figures in Jewish history, and his military achievements are remembered as evidence that Jewish survival has sometimes required the willingness to fight as well as to pray.
