Jewish interpreters read 1 Maccabees 1 as a historical account of one of the most severe crises in Jewish history — the attempt by Antiochus IV Epiphanes to eradicate Jewish religious practice entirely. The chapter's catalog of prohibitions — circumcision, Sabbath observance, Torah study, and sacrifice — strikes at the core of Jewish covenantal identity and provokes reflection on what constitutes the irreducible essence of Jewish faith and practice.

Modern Jewish scholarship situates this chapter within the broader context of the Hellenistic cultural encounter, noting that the crisis was not simply one of external persecution but also of internal division between Jews who embraced Hellenization and those who resisted it. This tension between acculturation and fidelity to tradition remains a defining challenge for Jewish communities in the modern world, making 1 Maccabees 1 perennially relevant to Jewish self-understanding.
