Jewish interpreters view Mattathias's revolt as a foundational act of religious resistance whose legacy extends far beyond its immediate historical context. His refusal to offer pagan sacrifice and his rallying cry to the faithful have served as inspiration for Jewish communities facing persecution throughout the centuries, from the Roman period through the medieval pogroms to the modern era.

The rabbinic tradition's somewhat ambivalent treatment of Mattathias reflects a broader tension within Judaism between the valorization of militant resistance and the preference for spiritual heroism. The Talmudic reinterpretation of Hanukkah — shifting emphasis from military victory to the miracle of the oil — represents a deliberate rabbinic decision to privilege the sacred over the martial. Modern Zionist thinkers reversed this emphasis, reclaiming the Maccabean warriors as models of Jewish national courage.
