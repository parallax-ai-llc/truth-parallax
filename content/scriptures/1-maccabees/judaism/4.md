The rededication of the Temple described in chapter 4 is the historical foundation of Hanukkah, one of the most widely observed Jewish festivals. Jewish interpreters note that while 1 Maccabees describes the event in primarily military and political terms — emphasizing the purification of the sanctuary and the establishment of an annual commemoration — the rabbinic tradition added the supernatural element of the oil that burned for eight days, shifting the emphasis from human achievement to divine miracle.

This interpretive move reflects a fundamental principle of rabbinic hermeneutics: the insistence that historical events must be read through a theological lens that highlights God's action rather than human heroism. Contemporary Jewish thought continues to negotiate between these emphases, with some communities stressing the national and military dimensions of Hanukkah and others emphasizing its spiritual and miraculous aspects.
