Jewish scholars read chapter 15's account of Antiochus VII's demands as a reminder that Jewish political independence in the ancient world was always contingent on the balance of power among surrounding empires. Simon's refusal to surrender territory, grounded in the claim of ancestral inheritance, articulates a theology of land that remains central to Jewish self-understanding.

The argument that the land of Israel belongs to the Jewish people by divine promise and historical right, not merely by conquest, connects the Maccabean narrative to the broader biblical theology of the land. This claim has been invoked throughout Jewish history in various contexts, and continues to inform contemporary discussions about Jewish identity and the relationship between people, land, and covenant.
