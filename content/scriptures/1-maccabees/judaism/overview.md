## Overview

Although 1 Maccabees is not included in the Tanakh (Hebrew Bible), the events it narrates are foundational to the Jewish festival of Hanukkah and to Jewish self-understanding regarding religious persecution and resistance. The book was known to the rabbis of the Talmudic period, but its exclusion from the canon — likely finalized at Yavneh around the late first century CE — may reflect ambivalence toward the Hasmonean dynasty, whose later corruption was well remembered.

## Interpretive Approach

Jewish engagement with 1 Maccabees has shifted across eras. The Talmudic rabbis largely downplayed the military aspects of the Maccabean revolt, preferring to emphasize the miracle of the oil that burned for eight days — a tradition not found in 1 Maccabees itself but in the Babylonian Talmud (Shabbat 21b). Medieval Jewish thinkers like Maimonides referenced the Hasmonean period in the context of Hanukkah law. In the modern era, Zionist thinkers reclaimed 1 Maccabees as a narrative of Jewish national courage and self-determination.

## Key Themes in Jewish Reading

Jewish interpreters have drawn from 1 Maccabees themes of kiddush Hashem (sanctification of God's name through martyrdom), the obligation to resist forced assimilation, and the importance of Torah fidelity. The tension between the book's militaristic nationalism and the rabbinic preference for spiritual heroism continues to generate productive debate within Jewish thought. The Hasmonean legacy also raises questions about the proper relationship between religious authority and political power.
