Islamic interpreters read the death of Antiochus IV in chapter 6 as an instance of divine retribution against an oppressive tyrant — a theme that recurs throughout the Quran in the accounts of Pharaoh, the people of 'Ad, and other nations destroyed for their rejection of God's messengers. The Quranic principle that "the wrongdoers will come to know by what a great reverse they will be overturned" (26:227) finds illustration in the downfall of the Seleucid persecutor.

Antiochus's reported deathbed acknowledgment of his crimes resonates with the Quranic account of Pharaoh's last-minute declaration of faith as the sea closed over him (10:90-91) — a repentance that came too late to avert divine judgment. Muslim readers draw from this narrative the moral lesson that tyranny and oppression are ultimately self-defeating, as God's justice will prevail in this world or the next.
