Islamic interpreters find in Mattathias's revolt a model of legitimate resistance against religious oppression. The Quranic permission to fight in self-defense — "Permission is given to those who are fought against because they have been wronged" (22:39) — provides a theological framework for evaluating the Maccabean uprising. Mattathias's refusal to worship false gods parallels the Quranic account of Ibrahim smashing the idols of his people.

The emphasis on zeal for God's law and willingness to sacrifice worldly comfort for the sake of faith resonates with the Islamic concept of jihad in its original sense — the struggle to establish and maintain conditions in which God can be worshipped freely. Muslim readers note that the Maccabean revolt was motivated not by territorial ambition but by the desire to preserve the right to worship the one true God, a motivation that Islamic jurisprudence regards as the most legitimate cause for armed resistance.
