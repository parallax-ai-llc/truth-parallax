## Overview

1 Maccabees is not part of the Islamic scriptural canon, and the Quran makes no direct reference to the Maccabean revolt or its protagonists. However, Islamic scholarship has engaged with the text as part of the broader Isra'iliyyat tradition — narratives from Jewish and Christian sources that illuminate the history of earlier prophetic communities. The book's themes of resistance to religious oppression and steadfast monotheism resonate with core Quranic values.

## Interpretive Approach

Muslim scholars who have examined 1 Maccabees tend to read it through the lens of the Quranic principle that God supports those who struggle (jihad) against injustice and the imposition of false worship. The Maccabean refusal to worship idols parallels the Quranic accounts of Ibrahim (Abraham) smashing idols and the steadfastness of earlier prophets against polytheistic persecution. While the specific historical details are treated with scholarly caution, the underlying moral framework is seen as consistent with Islamic teaching on the duty to preserve pure monotheism.

## Key Themes in Islamic Reading

Islamic interpreters find points of resonance in the Maccabean commitment to resisting forced religious assimilation, which echoes the Quranic injunction that "there is no compulsion in religion" (2:256). The theme of a small, faithful community prevailing against a powerful empire through divine assistance mirrors narratives in the Quran about the believers at Badr and other pivotal moments. The destruction and desecration of the Temple by Antiochus IV also connects with Islamic traditions about the sanctity of holy places and the gravity of their violation.
