Islamic scholars reading chapter 10 note Jonathan's political maneuvering with interest, as the Islamic tradition has its own rich history of engaging with competing political powers. Jonathan's acceptance of honors from rival Seleucid claimants raises questions about the proper balance between religious principle and political pragmatism — a tension that Muslim political thought has addressed through the concept of maslaha (public interest).

The chapter's depiction of a religious leader assuming both spiritual and political authority resonates with the Islamic model of leadership in which religious and temporal governance are ideally unified under a single authority guided by divine law. Muslim readers note both the strengths and dangers of this model as illustrated in Jonathan's career.
