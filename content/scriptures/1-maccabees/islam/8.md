Islamic interpreters approach the Roman alliance in chapter 8 through the lens of Islamic jurisprudence on treaties with non-Muslim powers. The principle of mu'ahada (mutual pact) permits such alliances when they serve the interests of the Muslim community and do not compromise its religious obligations. Judas's treaty with Rome is evaluated as a pragmatic measure taken to secure religious freedom for his people.

The chapter's description of Roman governance, including its avoidance of monarchy and its system of collective leadership, resonates with the Islamic concept of shura (consultation) as a principle of governance. Some Muslim political theorists have noted parallels between the Roman republican model described here and the consultative governance practiced by the early caliphs before the establishment of hereditary dynasties.
