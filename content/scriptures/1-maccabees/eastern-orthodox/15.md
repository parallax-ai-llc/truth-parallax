Orthodox commentators read chapter 15's account of renewed Seleucid threats as a reminder that the peace of the faithful in this world is always fragile and provisional. The demand of Antiochus VII for the return of territories and tribute illustrates the perpetual tension between the claims of earthly empire and the freedom of the people of God — a tension the Orthodox Church has experienced throughout its history under various political regimes.

Simon's refusal to capitulate is read as an act of confessional courage, defending not merely political territory but the sacred space of worship and communal life. Orthodox interpreters, drawing on their own tradition of resistance to imperial encroachment on ecclesiastical affairs, find in Simon's stance a model for the Church's assertion of its spiritual independence against the pretensions of secular power.
