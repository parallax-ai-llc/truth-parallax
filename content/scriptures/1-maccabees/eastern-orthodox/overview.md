## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox Church includes 1 Maccabees in its Old Testament canon, following the broader Septuagint tradition. Orthodox interpretation reads the book through the lens of the patristic Fathers and the liturgical tradition, in which the Maccabean heroes are venerated as holy martyrs and confessors of the faith. The feast of the Holy Maccabean Martyrs (August 1) reflects the deep integration of this text into Orthodox devotional life.

## Interpretive Approach

Orthodox exegesis situates 1 Maccabees within the typological framework of salvation history. The Maccabean resistance to Hellenistic paganism is understood as a prefiguration of the Church's struggle against heresy and persecution. The Fathers — particularly St. Gregory Nazianzen and St. John Chrysostom — praised the Maccabees as exemplars of faith, drawing moral lessons from their courage and dedication to ancestral tradition. The book's narrative of purifying and restoring the Temple is read as anticipating the sanctification of the Church through the indwelling of the Holy Spirit.

## Key Themes in Orthodox Reading

Orthodox interpreters emphasize the symphonic relationship between divine providence and human action in the Maccabean narrative. The victories of Judas Maccabeus are understood not as merely military achievements but as manifestations of God's power working through faithful servants. The emphasis on liturgical worship and the centrality of the Temple resonates deeply with Orthodox sacramental theology, in which the church building and its liturgy are understood as earthly icons of heavenly realities.
