The death of Judas Maccabeus in chapter 9 is read by Orthodox interpreters as a martyrdom — the willing sacrifice of life in defense of the faith. Orthodox hagiography regularly draws on such narratives to construct models of Christian heroism, in which military valor is transfigured by spiritual purpose. The lament over Judas echoes the psalmic tradition of mourning that is central to Orthodox funeral and memorial services.

Jonathan's emergence as successor illustrates the Orthodox principle of continuity in leadership — the conviction that God always provides shepherds for his people even in the darkest times. The difficult period following Judas's death is understood as a time of testing and purification, analogous to the periods of persecution that have repeatedly challenged and refined the Orthodox Church throughout its history.
