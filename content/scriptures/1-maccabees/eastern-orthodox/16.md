The murder of Simon in chapter 16 is read by Orthodox interpreters as a sobering reminder of human fallenness and the ever-present danger of betrayal from within the community of faith. The Orthodox tradition of reading the Passion narratives through the lens of Judas's betrayal finds resonance in Ptolemy's treachery against his own father-in-law — an act that violates both the bonds of kinship and the sacred trust of communal solidarity.

The survival of John Hyrcanus and the continuation of the Hasmonean line point, in Orthodox reading, to the resilience of God's economy of salvation despite human sin. The book's abrupt ending, without resolution or theological summation, is understood as gesturing toward the fulfillment that lies beyond the text — the coming of Christ, the true High Priest and King, whose kingdom will have no end.
