The fountain for cleansing (13:1) is reinterpreted in Gnostic thought as the outpouring of *gnosis* that purifies the pneumatic from the contamination of material existence. The "sin and uncleanness" that the fountain addresses are not moral failings but the ontological contamination of living in the Demiurge's world — the spiritual residue of material entanglement. The sacramental rites of Gnostic communities, particularly baptism and anointing as described in Valentinian texts, enacted this purification through ritual and teaching.

The removal of false prophets (13:2-6) aligns with the Gnostic critique of institutional religious authority. The false prophet who denies his prophetic identity (13:5) represents, in Gnostic reading, the psychic teacher who recognizes the inadequacy of his demiurgic religion but lacks the courage to speak the truth. The wounds on his body (13:6) — received "in the house of my friends" — are the marks of institutional conformity: the scars that come from submitting to the Demiurge's religious system rather than embracing the liberating truth of *gnosis*.

The smitten shepherd (13:7) and the refining of the remnant connect to the Valentinian doctrine of the three types of humanity. The two-thirds who perish represent the *hylics* and *psychics* who cannot survive the dissolution of the material order. The one-third who are refined through fire represent the *pneumatics* whose divine spark, tested and purified through the experience of material existence, emerges ready for reunion with the Pleroma.
