Zechariah 14's climactic vision of cosmic transformation provides the Gnostic reader with a prophetic anticipation of the Pleroma's restoration. The splitting of the Mount of Olives (14:4) represents the breaking open of the material cosmos — the Demiurge's creation fracturing under the pressure of the true God's intervention. The valley created between the divided mountain is the passageway through which the imprisoned pneumatics escape the material world, the cosmic birth canal through which the divine sparks return to their origin.

The living waters flowing from Jerusalem (14:8) — half to the east and half to the west — are read as the outpouring of *gnosis* in both directions (toward the spiritual and toward the material), dissolving the barriers between the Pleroma and the cosmos. The "Dead Sea" and the "Mediterranean" become symbols of the stagnant material realm and the restless sea of becoming, both of which are vivified and transformed by the influx of divine knowledge.

The final vision — all nations celebrating Sukkot, holiness inscribed everywhere, every pot sacred, no more traders in the temple (14:20-21) — represents the ultimate Gnostic hope: the complete dissolution of the distinction between sacred and profane because the material order itself has been transcended. When all divine sparks have returned to the Pleroma, there will be no more need for temples, rituals, or mediators. The trader is expelled because the economy of exchange — the Demiurge's system of debt, obligation, and compensation — has been replaced by the economy of fullness (*pleroma*), where all is freely given and freely received in the boundless light of the true God.
