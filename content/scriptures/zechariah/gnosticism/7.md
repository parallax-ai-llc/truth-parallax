The fasting dispute in Zechariah 7 provides ammunition for the Gnostic critique of empty ritualism. The prophet's dismissal of fasting that serves the self rather than God (7:5-6) aligns with the Gnostic suspicion of all material religious practices. Fasting, sacrifice, pilgrimage — all the physical observances of the Demiurge's religion — are meaningless unless they serve the awakening of *gnosis*. Some Gnostic groups practiced severe asceticism as a way of mortifying the material body, while others rejected all physical discipline as irrelevant to the spiritual work of knowledge.

The ethical imperatives of 7:9-10 — justice, mercy, compassion for the vulnerable — present a more complex case for Gnostic interpretation. While Gnostic theology tends to de-emphasize ethical behavior in favor of spiritual knowledge, the Valentinian tradition in particular maintained that the pneumatic, having received *gnosis*, would naturally live ethically because their actions flow from their divine nature rather than from external commandment. The prophetic call to justice is thus fulfilled not through obedience to law but through the spontaneous expression of the divine nature within the knower.

The warning about the ancestors' stubbornness (7:11-14) is read as describing the *hylic* and *psychic* types who cannot receive *gnosis* because their nature does not contain the divine spark. Their "stopping of ears" is not merely moral failure but ontological limitation — they are creatures of the Demiurge without the pneumatic capacity for higher knowledge. The exile that results from their disobedience is the natural consequence of remaining within the Demiurge's system of rewards and punishments.
