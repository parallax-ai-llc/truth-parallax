The shepherd allegory of Zechariah 11, with its themes of rejection and betrayal, is fertile ground for Gnostic reinterpretation. The shepherd who tends the flock "doomed to slaughter" (11:4) represents the Gnostic revealer who enters the Demiurge's realm knowing that the mission will be met with rejection by the majority who lack the capacity for *gnosis*. The breaking of the staffs Favor and Union represents not God's abandonment of His people but the revealer's recognition that the demiurgic covenant and the unity of the material community are illusions that must be shattered for true liberation to begin.

The thirty pieces of silver (11:12-13) — the contemptuous valuation of the divine shepherd — is read as illustrating the material world's inability to perceive the true value of *gnosis*. The divine revealer is assessed by the standards of the Demiurge's economy (silver, commerce, exchange) and found to be worth nothing more than a slave. The instruction to "throw it to the potter" is reinterpreted: the potter is the Demiurge himself, the cosmic craftsman who fashions the material world, and the silver is returned to the system that produced it.

The foolish shepherd (11:15-17) who abandons the flock represents the Demiurge in his true character — a ruler who exploits rather than serves, who devours the flesh and tears the hooves of his subjects. The curse upon this shepherd is the prophetic anticipation of the Demiurge's eventual exposure and defeat, when the material cosmos he governs will be dissolved and the pneumatics he imprisoned will be freed.
