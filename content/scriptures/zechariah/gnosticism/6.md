The four chariots (6:1-8) are interpreted in Gnostic cosmology as the four elements (earth, water, air, fire) or the four cardinal directions that define the Demiurge's material cosmos. The chariots emerge from between mountains of bronze — the impenetrable boundaries of the material creation — and patrol the earth, enforcing the Demiurge's order. The Gnostic reader sees in this vision not providential governance but cosmic surveillance and control.

The crowning of Joshua (6:9-15) and the prophecy of the Branch who is "priest on his throne" presents an interesting challenge for Gnostic interpretation. The combination of priestly and royal authority in a single figure could be read positively as describing the Gnostic Christ who transcends the separated functions of the Demiurge's system, or negatively as describing the Demiurge himself who claims total authority over his creation. Valentinian Gnostics would likely read this as an unwitting prophecy of the true Christ who comes not to serve the Demiurge's Temple but to reveal the true temple of the Pleroma.

The promise that the Branch will "build the temple of the LORD" (6:12) is reinterpreted as the Gnostic revealer constructing the spiritual temple of *gnosis* — the community of the enlightened who recognize their divine origin. The true temple is not a building of stone but the assembled pneumatics who together constitute the dwelling place of the true God, invisible to the Demiurge and his archons.
