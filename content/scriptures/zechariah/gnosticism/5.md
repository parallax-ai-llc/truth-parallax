The flying scroll of judgment (5:1-4) is read in Gnostic thought as the Demiurge's instrument of control — the written law that brings condemnation rather than liberation. The Gnostic critique of law, influenced by Paul's letters (especially Galatians), sees the written code as a tool of the archons who maintain their power over humanity through commandments, transgressions, and punishments. The scroll that "enters the house" to curse the thief and the perjurer represents the pervasive surveillance of the Demiurge's legal system, from which only *gnosis* can provide escape.

The woman named Wickedness (5:5-11) sealed in the ephah provides a Gnostic allegory for the entrapment of Sophia in the material world. In the Valentinian myth, Sophia's desire (often personified as Achamoth, the lower Sophia) is trapped in the material realm, unable to return to the Pleroma until the Gnostic Christ rescues her. The ephah — a measuring basket associated with commerce and materiality — represents the material container that imprisons the divine feminine principle.

The removal of the ephah to Shinar (5:11), where a "house" will be built for it, parallels the Gnostic account of the material cosmos as a dwelling place constructed for the trapped divine element. Shinar (Babylon), as the archetypal land of confusion and exile, represents the material world itself — the place where the divine spark is housed in alien territory, awaiting liberation through the knowledge brought by the revealer.
