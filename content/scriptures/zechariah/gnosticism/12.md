Zechariah 12's siege narrative — Jerusalem attacked by all nations yet divinely defended — is read in Gnostic terms as the cosmic assault on the community of knowers. The pneumatic community, possessing *gnosis*, is under perpetual threat from the archonic powers and their earthly representatives (institutional religion, political authority, social conformity). Yet the community is divinely protected — not by the Demiurge but by the true God whose power transcends the material order.

The pierced one (12:10) presents a fascinating case for Gnostic Christology. Docetic Gnostics, who denied that the divine Christ truly suffered, would read this passage as describing the appearance of suffering rather than its reality. The Gnostic Gospel of Peter and the *Second Treatise of the Great Seth* both present the crucifixion as something that happened to the material body while the true Christ laughed above the cross. The mourning, then, is the grief of those who do not understand that the divine cannot truly be harmed by material violence.

Other Gnostic schools, particularly the Valentinians, maintained that the *psychic* Christ did indeed suffer while the *pneumatic* Christ remained impassible. This sophisticated Christology allows for genuine mourning (for the psychic element that suffered) while maintaining the impassibility of the divine (the pneumatic element that cannot be pierced). The text's ambiguity — "they will look on me... and mourn for him" — could be read as reflecting precisely this distinction between the divine speaker and the suffering figure.
