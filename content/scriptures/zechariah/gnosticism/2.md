The measuring line vision (2:1-5) — where Jerusalem's expansion exceeds all measurement — is given a distinctive Gnostic interpretation. The immeasurability of the true divine realm (Pleroma) cannot be captured in earthly dimensions. The measuring angel's futile attempt to quantify Jerusalem symbolizes, for Gnostic readers, the impossibility of containing the infinite within the finite. The promise that Jerusalem will be "without walls" suggests a transcendence of the material boundaries that define and confine the Demiurge's creation.

The divine fire surrounding the city (2:5) resonates with Gnostic light theology. The Pleroma is characterized by luminosity, and the divine sparks within humanity are fragments of that original light. The fire that protects Jerusalem from a Gnostic perspective is not the Demiurge's protective flame but the Pleromatic light that envelops and preserves the pneumatic community — those who possess *gnosis* — within the hostile material cosmos.

The call to flee from "the land of the north" (2:6) is allegorized as the pneumatic's departure from the realm of material entanglement. Babylon, the land of exile, represents the material world itself — the domain of the Demiurge from which the divine spark must escape. The Gnostic salvation narrative is fundamentally a journey of return: from the foreign land of matter back to the homeland of the Pleroma.
