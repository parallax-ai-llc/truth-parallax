The delegation from Bethel asking about fasting (7:2-3) raises a question that became central to Jewish practice after the destruction of the Second Temple: which commemorative fasts should be maintained, and what is their proper significance? The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 18b) discusses the four fasts — the 17th of Tammuz, the 9th of Av (Tisha B'Av), the Fast of Gedaliah, and the 10th of Tevet — connecting them to events surrounding the destruction of both Temples. Zechariah's response, prioritizing justice over ritual, shaped the rabbinic understanding that fasting is a means to repentance, not an end in itself.

The prophetic emphasis on justice, mercy, and compassion (7:9-10) became the ethical standard against which all religious observance is measured in Jewish tradition. The Talmud (Sukkah 49b) teaches that *gemilut chasadim* (acts of lovingkindness) is greater than *tzedakah* (charity) because it can be done with one's person as well as one's money, for the living and the dead, for rich and poor alike. Zechariah's ethical demands are thus understood as establishing priorities within the religious life, not as opposing ritual to ethics.

The warning about ancestral stubbornness (7:11-14) serves in Jewish tradition as a paradigm for understanding the destruction of the First Temple and the exile. The rabbinic principle that "the sins of the fathers are a sign for the children" (*ma'aseh avot siman le-vanim*) turns these historical warnings into perennial lessons: each generation must learn from the past to avoid repeating its failures.
