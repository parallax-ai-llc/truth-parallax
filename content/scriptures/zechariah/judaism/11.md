The shepherd allegory of Zechariah 11 presents significant interpretive challenges in Jewish tradition. The prophet's enactment of the shepherd's role, the breaking of the two staffs, and the thirty pieces of silver are read in the context of the failure of post-exilic leadership. Rashi interprets the shepherd as God himself, tending a flock doomed by its own sins. The breaking of staff Favor represents the withdrawal of divine protection, and the breaking of Union represents the division between Judah and the remaining tribes.

The thirty pieces of silver (11:12-13) are interpreted by Jewish commentators without reference to the Christian Passion narrative. Rashi reads the "lordly price" ironically — the people value their divine shepherd at the price of a slave, demonstrating their contempt for God's guidance. Radak connects the silver to the wages of prophetic service, suggesting that Israel valued its prophets cheaply. The instruction to "throw it to the potter" is understood as a gesture of contempt, casting the paltry sum into the Temple treasury (where potters apparently worked).

The foolish shepherd (11:15-17) is interpreted in Jewish eschatological tradition as a figure who will arise in the last days to mislead Israel. Some commentators connect this to the concept of *Mashiach ben Yosef* (Messiah son of Joseph) who, according to certain traditions, will die in battle before the coming of *Mashiach ben David*. Others see the foolish shepherd as a purely negative figure — a false leader whose punishment demonstrates God's intolerance of those who exploit His people.
