## Overview

In Jewish tradition, Zechariah (Zekharyah) is among the most eschatologically significant of the Twelve Minor Prophets. His night visions inaugurated a form of prophetic literature — mediated through angelic interpreters, rich in symbolism, oriented toward cosmic transformation — that would deeply influence later apocalyptic works such as Daniel and the Enochic literature. The book's Haftarah readings connect it to Shabbat Chanukah (2:14-4:7) and the first day of Sukkot (14:1-21), embedding its theology in the liturgical cycle.

## Rabbinic Interpretation

The Talmud and Midrash engage extensively with Zechariah's imagery. The menorah vision (chapter 4) is connected to the Chanukah miracle and to the Temple's sacred lampstand. The messianic figure on a donkey (9:9) is interpreted in the Talmud (Sanhedrin 98a) alongside Daniel 7:13 — if Israel is worthy, the Messiah comes on clouds of heaven; if not, humble and riding on a donkey. The thirty pieces of silver (11:12-13) and the pierced one (12:10) are given non-Christological readings in rabbinic tradition.

## Liturgical Significance

Zechariah 14 is the Haftarah for the first day of Sukkot, connecting the eschatological vision of all nations celebrating the Feast of Booths to the contemporary observance. The prophecy "the LORD shall be King over all the earth; on that day the LORD shall be one and His name one" (14:9) is incorporated into the daily liturgy as the concluding verse of the Aleinu prayer.
