The vision of the measuring line (2:1-5) — measuring Jerusalem's dimensions only to be told that the city will overflow all boundaries — has been read in Jewish tradition as a prophecy of Jerusalem's eschatological expansion. The Talmud (Bava Batra 75b) envisions a Jerusalem that will extend throughout the land of Israel and ultimately encompass the world. This expansive vision sustained Jewish hope during centuries of exile when physical Jerusalem was small, damaged, or inaccessible.

The call to flee from "the land of the north" (2:6) was applied throughout Jewish history to successive diasporas. Each wave of persecution — from the Crusader massacres to the expulsions from Spain and England to the pogroms of Eastern Europe — was read against prophetic texts like this that simultaneously described the danger of exile and promised ultimate deliverance. The Zionist movement drew on such texts as part of its appeal for the return to Eretz Yisrael.

The promise that "many nations will join themselves to the LORD" (2:11) is interpreted in Jewish tradition as the eschatological recognition of God's sovereignty by all peoples. This does not necessarily mean conversion to Judaism but rather the acknowledgment of the one God that the Noahide laws anticipate. Maimonides (Laws of Kings 8:11) codifies the expectation that righteous Gentiles who observe the seven Noahide laws have a share in the world to come.
