Zechariah 14 is the Haftarah for the first day of Sukkot, establishing a liturgical connection between the eschatological vision and the annual festival. The prophecy that "all nations will come up year after year to worship the King, the LORD of hosts, and to celebrate the Feast of Booths" (14:16) transforms Sukkot from a national agricultural festival into a universal eschatological celebration. The water-drawing ceremony (*Simchat Beit Ha-Shoevah*) that was a highlight of the Second Temple Sukkot observance connects to the "living waters" flowing from Jerusalem (14:8).

The declaration "The LORD will be King over all the earth; on that day the LORD will be one and His name one" (14:9) is incorporated into the daily liturgy as the concluding verse of the Aleinu prayer, making it one of the most frequently recited prophetic texts in Jewish worship. The statement affirms absolute monotheism in its most comprehensive form: not only will all peoples recognize the one God, but the unity of the divine name — currently fragmented by diverse and competing theologies — will be fully recognized.

The final verses describing the sanctification of everything in Jerusalem — from horses' bells to cooking pots, all inscribed "Holy to the LORD" (14:20-21) — present the ultimate vision of Jewish messianic hope: the complete permeation of daily life by holiness. The distinction between *kodesh* (holy) and *chol* (profane) that structures halakhic life will be transcended when all of existence becomes sacred. The closing statement — "there shall no longer be a trader in the house of the LORD" — is interpreted by Rashi as meaning there will no longer be unworthy people in the Temple, and by others as prophesying the end of commerce in the sacred precincts. Either way, the vision is of a world wholly devoted to God.
