Zechariah 9:9 — the king riding on a donkey — is one of the most significant messianic texts in Jewish tradition, though its interpretation diverges sharply from the Christian reading. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 98a) presents a famous resolution of the apparent contradiction between Daniel 7:13 (the Messiah coming on clouds of heaven) and Zechariah 9:9 (the Messiah riding on a donkey): if Israel is worthy, the Messiah comes in glory; if not, in humility. This dual possibility reflects the rabbinic conviction that the mode of redemption depends, in part, on Israel's spiritual state.

The judgment oracles against Damascus, Tyre, Sidon, and the Philistine cities (9:1-8) are read in Jewish tradition as prophecies of divine judgment against Israel's historical enemies. Some medieval commentators, including Ibn Ezra, connected these oracles to the campaigns of Alexander the Great, reading the prophecy as partially fulfilled in the Hellenistic conquest of the Near East. The Midrash emphasizes that God's judgment of the nations serves to vindicate Israel and prepare the way for the messianic king.

The "prisoners of hope" (9:12) became a poignant phrase in Jewish experience. Throughout centuries of persecution, dispersion, and marginalization, the Jewish community maintained hope in divine deliverance. This hope was not passive resignation but active expectation, expressed in daily prayer, in the preservation of identity and practice, and in the orientation of life toward Jerusalem and the promised redemption.
