Zechariah 12's vision of Jerusalem besieged by all nations yet miraculously delivered is central to Jewish eschatological expectation. The image of Jerusalem as "a cup of staggering" and "an immovable rock" (12:2-3) for the nations expresses the conviction that Jerusalem's destiny is supernaturally guaranteed. The Midrash teaches that Jerusalem is the "navel of the world" (*tabbur ha-aretz*), the point from which creation began and to which all things will return.

The verse "they will look upon me, the one they have pierced" (12:10) is interpreted very differently in Jewish tradition than in Christianity. Rashi reads "the one they have pierced" as referring to the Messiah son of Joseph who falls in battle (based on the Talmudic discussion in Sukkah 52a), while others interpret it as referring to those among Israel who were killed by the nations. The Targum Jonathan paraphrases the passage to avoid any implication that God himself is pierced. The mourning that follows is understood as national mourning for the fallen — not repentance for having caused the death, but grief over the loss.

The detailed mourning by individual families (12:12-14) — the house of David apart, the house of Nathan apart, the house of Levi apart — reflects the Jewish mourning custom of family-specific grief. The separation of mourners echoes the laws of *avelut* (mourning), where each family bears its grief distinctly while participating in communal lament. This passage reinforces the Jewish understanding that eschatological events will involve both national and deeply personal dimensions.
