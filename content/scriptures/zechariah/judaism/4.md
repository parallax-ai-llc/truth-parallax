The golden menorah vision (4:1-14) holds special significance in Jewish tradition because of its connection to the Temple menorah and, by extension, to the Chanukah celebration. The Haftarah for Shabbat Chanukah is drawn from Zechariah 2:14-4:7, and the menorah vision is read as prophetically anticipating the rededication of the Temple under the Maccabees. The seven-branched menorah became the quintessential symbol of Jewish identity, appearing on the Arch of Titus, on ancient coins, and as the emblem of the modern State of Israel.

The declaration "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit" (4:6) has been interpreted in various Jewish contexts. During the Chanukah celebration, it serves as a counterpoint to the military narrative: the Maccabean victory was ultimately a spiritual triumph, not merely a military one. In the modern period, this verse has been invoked by both religious Zionists (who emphasize divine providence in the establishment of the State) and anti-Zionists (who argue that the State should not be established by force).

The two olive trees (4:11-14), identified as "the two anointed ones" (*shnei vnei ha-yitzhar*), are understood in Jewish tradition as representing the dual leadership of Zerubbabel (the Davidic heir) and Joshua (the high priest). This dual-leadership model influenced the Dead Sea Scrolls community's expectation of two messiahs — a royal messiah of David and a priestly messiah of Aaron — a concept attested in the Damascus Document and the Rule of the Community.
