The four chariots (6:1-8) patrolling the earth are interpreted in Jewish tradition as agents of divine governance over the nations. The Midrash connects these chariots to the four kingdoms of Daniel's visions (Babylonian, Persian, Greek, Roman), with each chariot executing God's judgment upon the successive empires that oppressed Israel. The two bronze mountains from which the chariots emerge are identified by some commentators as Mount Zion and the Mount of Olives, symbolizing Jerusalem's role as the center of divine governance.

The crowning of Joshua (6:9-15) with the proclamation of the Branch presents a complex interpretive challenge in Jewish tradition, since it seems to combine priestly and kingly functions in a single figure — something the Torah carefully separates. Rabbinic commentators note that the crown is placed on Joshua's head but the prophecy speaks of a future Branch who will build the Temple. Radak interprets this as a symbolic act pointing to the messianic future when the offices of priest and king will be harmoniously united.

The prophecy that "those who are far off shall come and help to build the temple of the LORD" (6:15) has been applied in Jewish tradition to the contributions of diaspora Jews to the support and rebuilding of Jerusalem. Throughout history, Jewish communities worldwide sent financial support to the Jewish community in the Land of Israel, fulfilling in practical terms this prophetic vision of those "far off" participating in the holy work.
