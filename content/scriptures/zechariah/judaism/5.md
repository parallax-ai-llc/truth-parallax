The flying scroll (5:1-4) representing the curse against theft and false swearing resonates with Jewish legal tradition's emphasis on honest dealing and truthful testimony. The Talmud devotes extensive discussion to the prohibitions against theft (*genevah* and *gezelah*) and false oaths (*shevuat sheker*), and Zechariah's vision of a supernatural curse pursuing violators underscores the gravity of these offenses. The scroll's enormous dimensions — thirty by fifteen cubits, matching the porch of Solomon's Temple — symbolically connect the curse to the Temple itself: those who violate the covenant desecrate the sacred space.

The woman named Wickedness (*ha-Rishah*) contained in the ephah and transported to Shinar (5:5-11) has been interpreted in various ways in Jewish tradition. Rashi reads the ephah as representing the measure of Israel's sins, now full and ready for removal. Radak sees the woman as the embodiment of idolatry being exiled to Babylon, its natural home. The Midrash connects this to the removal of the *yetzer hara* for idolatry, which the Talmud (Yoma 69b) teaches was neutralized during the early Second Temple period.

The destination of Shinar (Babylon) carries deep symbolic weight in Jewish consciousness: it is the land of exile, the site of the Tower of Babel, and the location of the first diaspora community. The removal of wickedness to Shinar creates a symbolic reversal — what began in Babylon returns to Babylon, leaving the holy land purified.
