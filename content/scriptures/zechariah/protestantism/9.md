Zechariah 9 marks the transition to "Second Zechariah" and introduces the messianic king in one of the most directly fulfilled prophecies in Scripture. After oracles of judgment against surrounding nations (9:1-8), the prophet proclaims: "Rejoice greatly, O daughter of Zion! Shout aloud, O daughter of Jerusalem! Behold, your king is coming to you; righteous and having salvation is he, humble and mounted on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey" (9:9). All four Gospels identify Christ's triumphal entry into Jerusalem as the fulfillment of this prophecy.

Protestant interpreters emphasize the paradox of this messianic vision: the king comes not on a war horse but on a donkey, not in military might but in humble salvation. This inverts every expectation of political messiahship and establishes the pattern of the kingdom of God: power through weakness, victory through surrender, glory through humility. The subsequent promise that "he shall speak peace to the nations" (9:10) extends the king's reign beyond Israel to universal scope.

The chapter closes with the beautiful image of the "prisoners of hope" (9:12) — those who, even in confinement, maintain hope in God's deliverance. Protestant pastoral theology draws on this phrase to encourage believers who suffer: hope itself is a form of faithfulness, and God promises to "restore double" to those who endure.
