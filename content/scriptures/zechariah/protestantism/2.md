The second vision presents a man with a measuring line going out to measure Jerusalem, only to be told that the city will be "inhabited as villages without walls, because of the multitude of people and livestock in it" (2:4). Protestant interpreters read this as a promise that Jerusalem's future glory will overflow any physical boundaries. The LORD himself will be "a wall of fire all around her, and I will be the glory in her midst" (2:5).

This vision challenges the returned exiles' modest expectations. They are rebuilding walls and measuring boundaries, but God's vision for Jerusalem is far larger — an open, overflowing city protected not by stone walls but by divine presence. Protestant preachers apply this to the Church: God's people are not confined to institutional structures but are an expansive, Spirit-protected community that transcends human boundaries.

The chapter closes with a summons to flee from Babylon (2:6-7) and a remarkable promise: "Many nations shall join themselves to the LORD in that day, and shall be my people" (2:11). This Gentile inclusion theme is central to Protestant missiology, anticipating the Great Commission and the ingathering of the nations into the people of God through faith in Christ.
