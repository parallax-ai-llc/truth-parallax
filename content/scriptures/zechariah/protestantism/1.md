Zechariah 1 opens with a call to repentance that echoes the pre-exilic prophets: "Return to me, says the LORD of hosts, and I will return to you" (1:3). Protestant interpreters note the conditional structure — God's return is linked to the people's repentance — while simultaneously affirming that the ability to repent is itself a gift of grace. The chapter then introduces the first of Zechariah's eight night visions: a man among myrtle trees, with horses of red, sorrel, and white patrolling the earth.

The interpreting angel (*mal'akh*) who mediates the visions is a distinctive feature of Zechariah's prophecy, marking a transition from the direct divine speech of earlier prophets to the mediated revelation that characterizes later apocalyptic literature. Protestant commentators see this angelic mediation not as diminishing divine communication but as preparing for the ultimate mediator, Christ, who reveals God fully.

The vision concludes with a powerful assurance: the LORD is "exceedingly jealous for Jerusalem" (1:14) and angry with the nations that added to Judah's affliction. The promise that Jerusalem will be rebuilt and the Temple completed (1:16) provides immediate encouragement to the post-exilic community while pointing toward the ultimate restoration that Christ will accomplish.
