Zechariah 7 addresses a delegation from Bethel that asks whether the community should continue the fasts commemorating Jerusalem's destruction. The prophet's response, delivered in the LORD's name, cuts through ritual formalism to demand ethical substance: "When you fasted and mourned... was it for me that you fasted? And when you eat and when you drink, do you not eat for yourselves and drink for yourselves?" (7:5-6). Protestant interpreters emphasize the prophetic priority of justice and mercy over ritual observance.

The chapter recapitulates the pre-exilic prophetic message: "Render true judgments, show kindness and mercy to one another, do not oppress the widow, the fatherless, the sojourner, or the poor, and let none of you devise evil against another in your heart" (7:9-10). Protestant social ethics draws heavily on this passage, insisting that genuine religion is demonstrated in just and compassionate treatment of the vulnerable, not in the multiplication of religious observances.

The warning that the ancestors "refused to pay attention and turned a stubborn shoulder and stopped their ears" (7:11) serves as a sober reminder that the capacity for spiritual deafness is perennial. Protestant preaching tradition uses such passages to challenge comfortable religion that substitutes ceremony for obedience and ritual for righteousness.
