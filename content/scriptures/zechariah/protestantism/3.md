The vision of Joshua the high priest standing before the angel of the LORD, with Satan at his right hand to accuse him, is one of the most theologically rich passages in the Minor Prophets. Joshua stands in filthy garments — representing the guilt of the priesthood and the nation — while Satan serves as prosecutor. The angel commands: "Remove the filthy garments from him," and declares, "I have taken your iniquity away from you, and I will clothe you with pure vestments" (3:4).

Protestant interpreters read this as a powerful image of justification by grace. Joshua cannot cleanse himself; the filthy garments of sin are removed by divine action, and clean garments of righteousness are provided as a gift. Luther and Calvin both saw in this vision the essential gospel dynamic: the sinner stands accused, but God himself provides the righteousness that silences the accuser. The parallel to Pauline theology — "clothe yourselves with the Lord Jesus Christ" (Romans 13:14) — is unmistakable.

The chapter introduces "my servant the Branch" (3:8) — a messianic title that recurs in 6:12 and connects to Isaiah 11:1 and Jeremiah 23:5. Protestant Christology identifies the Branch with Jesus Christ, who fulfills both the priestly (cleansing from sin) and royal (Davidic descendant) dimensions of this prophecy. The "stone with seven eyes" set before Joshua (3:9) is read as pointing to Christ, the cornerstone of the Church.
