The vision of the golden lampstand flanked by two olive trees (4:1-14) contains one of the most beloved verses in Protestant devotion: "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit, says the LORD of hosts" (4:6). This declaration, addressed to Zerubbabel who faces the seemingly impossible task of completing the Temple, encapsulates the Protestant conviction that God's work is accomplished through spiritual power rather than human resources.

The two olive trees, identified as "the two anointed ones who stand by the Lord of the whole earth" (4:14), are generally understood as Zerubbabel (the royal figure) and Joshua (the priestly figure). Protestant interpreters see in their pairing a foreshadowing of Christ who unites the offices of king and priest. The Reformers also applied this text against the medieval papacy's claim to temporal power: the true work of God advances not through political might but through the Spirit's working through the Word.

The mountain that becomes a plain before Zerubbabel (4:7) and the promise that his hands will complete the Temple (4:9) demonstrate God's faithfulness in finishing what He begins. Protestant theology of perseverance draws encouragement from this passage: the God who begins a good work will bring it to completion (Philippians 1:6).
