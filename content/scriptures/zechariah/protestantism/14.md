Zechariah 14 presents the climactic vision of the Day of the LORD in language that has profoundly shaped Protestant eschatology. The chapter describes a final siege of Jerusalem, the LORD's intervention on the Mount of Olives (which splits in two), living waters flowing from Jerusalem, and the establishment of God's universal kingship: "The LORD will be king over all the earth. On that day the LORD will be one and his name one" (14:9).

Protestant eschatological interpretation diverges significantly on this chapter. Premillennialists (particularly dispensationalists) read it as a literal description of events surrounding Christ's second coming to Jerusalem. Amillennialists read the imagery symbolically, seeing it as a representation of Christ's ultimate victory over evil and the consummation of the Kingdom. Postmillennialists view it as describing the progressive triumph of the gospel throughout history. All agree, however, that the chapter points to the ultimate triumph of God's purposes.

The chapter's final vision — all nations coming to Jerusalem to celebrate the Feast of Booths (Sukkot), with even the bells on horses inscribed "Holy to the LORD" and every cooking pot in Jerusalem becoming sacred (14:20-21) — portrays a world where the sacred-secular distinction has been abolished. Protestant interpreters see this as the eschatological fulfillment of the Reformation's doctrine of the priesthood of all believers: in the age to come, all of life will be worship, and holiness will pervade every dimension of existence.
