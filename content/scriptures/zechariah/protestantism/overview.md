## Overview

Protestant interpretation of Zechariah emphasizes the book's remarkable Christological content. More than any other Minor Prophet, Zechariah provides specific prophetic images that the New Testament applies directly to Jesus: the humble king riding on a donkey (9:9), the thirty pieces of silver (11:12-13), the pierced one whom they will mourn (12:10), and the smitten shepherd whose flock is scattered (13:7). Reformed and evangelical commentators read these passages as demonstrating the divine inspiration and predictive power of Old Testament prophecy.

## Key Interpretive Approaches

The eight night visions (chapters 1-6) are read as a progressive revelation of God's plan for restored Jerusalem and the coming Messiah. The Branch (3:8, 6:12) who combines priestly and kingly offices finds fulfillment in Christ, the ultimate priest-king. The eschatological chapters (9-14) are debated among Protestant interpreters: premillennialists, amillennialists, and postmillennialists all find material for their respective systems in Zechariah's complex imagery.

## Theological Emphases

The foundational principle "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit, says the LORD of hosts" (4:6) is one of the most quoted verses in Protestant devotion, encapsulating the Reformed conviction that God accomplishes His purposes through spiritual power rather than human effort.
