Zechariah 12 begins a section sometimes called the "little apocalypse" of Zechariah (chapters 12-14). Jerusalem is besieged by "all the nations of the earth" (12:3), but God makes the city "an immovable rock" that injures all who try to lift it. Protestant interpreters debate whether this refers to a literal future siege of Jerusalem or symbolizes the spiritual assault against the Church. Premillennial interpreters tend toward the former; amillennial and postmillennial interpreters toward the latter.

The most theologically significant verse is 12:10: "They will look on me, the one they have pierced, and they will mourn for him as one mourns for an only child." The identification of the speaker as God himself ("they will look on me") while the mourning is for a distinct figure ("him") creates a theological tension that Protestant Christology resolves in the doctrine of the Incarnation: God in Christ was pierced on the cross. John 19:37 quotes this verse directly in reference to the crucifixion, and Revelation 1:7 applies it to Christ's second coming.

The mourning described in 12:10-14 — every family mourning separately, including the house of David and the house of Nathan — is read by Protestant interpreters as a picture of genuine, personal repentance that comes when people truly see what their sin has done to Christ. This is not performative public grief but deep, individual conviction of sin that leads to salvation.
