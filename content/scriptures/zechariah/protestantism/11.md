Zechariah 11 is one of the most enigmatic and disturbing chapters in the prophetic literature. The prophet is commanded to act out the role of a shepherd over a flock "doomed to slaughter" (11:4), tended by shepherds who exploit them without pity. The shepherd breaks two staffs named "Favor" (or "Grace") and "Union," symbolizing the breaking of God's covenant and the dissolution of the brotherhood between Judah and Israel.

The passage that has drawn the most attention is the shepherd's wages: "They weighed out as my wages thirty pieces of silver" — a paltry sum, the price of a gored slave (Exodus 21:32). The LORD commands: "Throw it to the potter — the lordly price at which I was priced by them" (11:13). Matthew 27:3-10 applies this directly to Judas's betrayal of Jesus for thirty pieces of silver and the subsequent purchase of the potter's field. Protestant interpreters see this as one of the most striking specific fulfillments in messianic prophecy.

The chapter closes with an oracle against a "foolish shepherd" who abandons the flock (11:15-17). Protestant commentators identify this figure variously with the corrupt leadership of Israel in the first century, the Antichrist, or a general type of false leadership that contrasts with the Good Shepherd of John 10. The curse pronounced on this worthless shepherd — "his arm shall be utterly withered, and his right eye shall be utterly blinded" — signals divine judgment on all who exploit rather than serve God's people.
