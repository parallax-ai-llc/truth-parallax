The fifth and sixth visions — the flying scroll (5:1-4) and the woman in the ephah (5:5-11) — address the purging of sin from the restored community. The enormous scroll, thirty feet by fifteen feet, represents God's curse against theft and false swearing, entering the houses of the guilty to consume them. Protestant interpreters emphasize the comprehensive nature of divine judgment: sin cannot be hidden from a holy God.

The woman named "Wickedness" sealed in a measuring basket and carried to Shinar (Babylon) represents the removal of sin from the holy land. The vision is rich in symbolic reversals: wickedness, personified and contained, is exiled to the very land from which Israel has returned. Protestant commentators read this as an assurance that God is actively at work to purify His people, a process that continues through the sanctifying work of the Spirit in the believer's life.

The two visions together present a theology of holiness that is both negative (judgment against sin) and positive (removal of wickedness). Protestant sanctification theology holds these in tension: the believer is simultaneously justified (declared righteous) and being sanctified (made righteous), with sin progressively removed as the Spirit's work unfolds.
