The vision of Joshua the high priest (3:1-10) engages Islamic theology at several points. The figure of Satan (*Shaytan*) as accuser has a direct parallel in the Quran, where Iblis (Satan) is described as an adversary who seeks to mislead humanity. However, in Islamic theology, Shaytan has no power to accuse before God's tribunal — his role is limited to temptation. The Quranic Satan whispers suggestions (114:4-5) but cannot bring formal charges; God alone is the Judge.

The cleansing of Joshua — the removal of filthy garments and the bestowal of pure vestments — resonates with the Islamic concept of *tawbah* (repentance) and divine forgiveness. The Quran teaches that God accepts repentance and purifies the penitent: "Indeed, Allah loves those who are constantly repentant and loves those who purify themselves" (2:222). The prophetic vision of a priest cleansed by divine initiative parallels the Islamic understanding that God's mercy precedes and enables human repentance.

The "Branch" prophecy (3:8) is of limited direct significance in Islamic theology, as the specifically Davidic messianic expectations do not form part of the Islamic framework. However, the concept of a divinely appointed leader who will establish justice and purify worship resonates with the Islamic expectation of the Mahdi — a guided leader who will appear in the last days to fill the earth with justice.
