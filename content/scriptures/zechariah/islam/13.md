The fountain for cleansing (13:1) resonates with the Islamic emphasis on purification — both ritual (*wudu*, *ghusl*) and spiritual (*tazkiyah*). The Quran describes God as purifying the hearts of believers: "Allah wishes to purify you and to complete His favor upon you" (5:6). The prophetic vision of a fountain opened specifically for sin and uncleanness corresponds to the Islamic understanding that God provides the means of purification for those who seek it sincerely.

The removal of idols and false prophets (13:2-6) aligns with the central Islamic narrative of Muhammad's destruction of the idols in the Kaaba. The Prophet entered Mecca and systematically removed the 360 idols from the sacred precinct, reciting: "Truth has come and falsehood has departed. Indeed, falsehood is ever bound to depart" (17:81). Zechariah's prophecy of a day when idols are removed and false prophets shamed finds its closest historical parallel, from an Islamic perspective, in this event.

The smitten shepherd (13:7) and the refining of the remnant (13:8-9) connect to the Islamic concept of divine testing (*ibtila'*). The Quran teaches that God tests the faithful through hardship to distinguish the sincere from the hypocritical: "Do people think they will be left alone because they say 'We believe' and not be tested? We certainly tested those before them" (29:2-3). The image of refinement through fire — where two-thirds perish and one-third is purified — represents the severe but purposeful testing that produces authentic faith.
