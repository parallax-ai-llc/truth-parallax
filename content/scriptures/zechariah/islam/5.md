The flying scroll of judgment (5:1-4) targeting theft and false swearing resonates with Islamic jurisprudence, which treats both offenses with great severity. Theft (*sariqah*) is a *hadd* crime in Islamic law with prescribed penalties (5:38), and false oaths (*yamin al-ghamus*) are considered among the gravest sins. The prophetic vision of a divine curse pursuing violators into their homes reinforces the Islamic teaching that no sin is hidden from God: "Does he not know that Allah sees?" (96:14).

The removal of Wickedness to Shinar (5:5-11) parallels the Quranic theme of evil being contained and ultimately destroyed. The Quran describes how God confines and punishes the forces of evil: the *jinn* who disobey are pursued by "flaming missiles" (72:8-9), and Iblis and his followers are destined for Jahannam (Hell). The prophetic vision of wickedness personified, contained in a vessel, and exiled to a distant land provides a vivid image of evil's ultimate banishment.

The two women with wings (5:9) have no direct Quranic parallel, but the Islamic tradition of angelic beings who execute divine commands provides a framework for understanding them. Angels in Islam are servants of God who carry out various functions — from conveying revelation (Jibril) to recording deeds (Kiraman Katibin) to executing punishment (Malik). Zechariah's vision of winged agents removing evil fits within this Islamic angelological framework.
