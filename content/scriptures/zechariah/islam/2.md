The expansive vision of Jerusalem (2:1-5) — overflowing its walls, protected by divine fire — connects to the Islamic reverence for *al-Quds* and the broader theology of sacred cities. The Quran designates Mecca as the primary sanctuary (*haram*), and the Prophet Muhammad initially directed prayer toward Jerusalem before the *qiblah* was changed to Mecca (2:142-150). This history gives Jerusalem a foundational role in Islamic sacred geography.

The promise that "many nations shall join themselves to the LORD" (2:11) resonates with the Islamic vision of a universal *ummah*. Islam presents itself as the universal religion to which all peoples are called: "We have not sent you except as a mercy to all the worlds" (21:107). The prophetic vision of nations joining in the worship of the one God anticipates, from an Islamic perspective, the spread of Islam across every continent and culture.

The call to "flee from the land of the north" (2:6) connects to the broader prophetic theme of *hijrah* (migration) — the departure from a place of spiritual danger to a place of safety and divine worship. The Prophet Muhammad's migration from Mecca to Medina is the paradigmatic *hijrah* in Islamic history, and the concept of leaving behind spiritual corruption resonates with Zechariah's prophetic summons.
