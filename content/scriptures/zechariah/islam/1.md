Islamic engagement with Zechariah 1 draws on the thematic parallel between prophetic visions and the Quranic accounts of divine revelation. The night visions received by the biblical prophet — mediated through angelic interpreters — parallel the Islamic understanding of *wahy* (revelation) as transmitted through Jibril (Gabriel). The Quran describes the process of revelation in various modes: direct divine speech, angelic mediation, and inspiration behind a veil (42:51). Zechariah's visionary experience fits the second category.

The horsemen patrolling the earth (1:8-11) resonate with the Quranic concept of angelic beings who record deeds and oversee cosmic order. The Quran describes angels who guard and record: "He sends guardians over you" (6:61) and "two receivers receive, seated on the right and on the left" (50:17). The Islamic cosmology of angelic governance finds prophetic support in visions like Zechariah's, where divine agents traverse the earth carrying out God's purposes.

The divine jealousy for Jerusalem (1:14) connects to the Islamic recognition of Jerusalem (*al-Quds*) as the third holiest city in Islam, the site of the Prophet Muhammad's Night Journey (*Isra'*) and Ascension (*Mi'raj*). While Islamic theology does not share the specifically covenantal relationship with Jerusalem that characterizes Judaism and Christianity, the city holds deep spiritual significance as a place where God's presence has been manifested through successive prophets.
