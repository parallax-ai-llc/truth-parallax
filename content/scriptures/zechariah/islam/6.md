The four chariots (6:1-8) executing divine judgment across the earth align with the Islamic understanding of God's governance of the nations. The Quran affirms God's sovereignty over all peoples and their histories: "You give sovereignty to whom You will and take sovereignty away from whom You will" (3:26). The rise and fall of empires — a central theme in Islamic historiography from Ibn Khaldun to modern scholarship — reflects the same providential pattern that Zechariah's chariots symbolize.

The crowning of Joshua (6:9-15) and the prophecy of the Branch who will "build the temple of the LORD" have limited direct application in Islamic theology, which does not share the specifically Temple-centered eschatology of Judaism and Christianity. However, the broader theme of a divinely appointed leader who unites religious and political authority resonates with the Islamic concept of the *khalifah* (caliph) — a deputy of God who governs with divine guidance and maintains the community's spiritual and temporal welfare.

The promise that "those who are far off shall come and help to build the temple" (6:15) connects to the Islamic emphasis on the universal *ummah* contributing to the maintenance of sacred spaces. The rebuilding and maintenance of mosques is considered a meritorious act in Islam: "The mosques of Allah are only to be maintained by those who believe in Allah and the Last Day" (9:18). The prophetic vision of distant peoples contributing to sacred construction resonates with this principle.
