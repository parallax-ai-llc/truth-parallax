The question about fasting in Zechariah 7 connects directly to Islamic practice, as fasting (*sawm*) is one of the five pillars of Islam. Zechariah's critique of fasting divorced from justice and mercy echoes the Quranic teaching that Ramadan fasting is intended to cultivate *taqwa* (God-consciousness), not merely to endure hunger: "O you who believe, fasting is prescribed for you as it was prescribed for those before you, that you may become righteous" (2:183). The prophetic insistence that fasting must be accompanied by ethical behavior is shared across the Abrahamic traditions.

The social justice imperatives of 7:9-10 — true judgments, kindness, mercy, care for widows, orphans, and the poor — align precisely with the ethical priorities of the Quran. The concept of *'adl* (justice) is central to Islamic theology and governance: "O you who believe, be persistently standing firm in justice, witnesses for Allah" (4:135). The orphan, the widow, and the traveler are specifically named in the Quran as recipients of *zakat* and charitable giving (2:177).

The prophetic warning about the ancestors' refusal to listen (7:11-14) parallels the Quranic narratives of peoples who rejected their prophets and suffered destruction. The stories of 'Ad, Thamud, the people of Lot, and others serve the same function in the Quran as the exile serves in the prophetic literature: a historical demonstration that divine warnings are not empty threats but consequential communications that demand response.
