The golden lampstand (4:2) is a central symbol in Orthodox liturgical theology. The lampstand in the Temple corresponds to the many-canopied candelabra that adorn Orthodox churches, representing the light of Christ that illumines the world through the Church. The seven lamps are traditionally connected to the seven gifts of the Holy Spirit (Isaiah 11:2), and their continuous burning symbolizes the unceasing prayer and worship that the Church offers.

"Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit" (4:6) is foundational for the Orthodox theology of *synergeia* — the cooperation between divine grace and human effort. Orthodox theology insists that while human effort is necessary, it is the Holy Spirit who accomplishes the transformative work of salvation. Zechariah's declaration prevents any triumphalism or self-reliance in the spiritual life: every genuine achievement is ultimately the Spirit's work through willing human instruments.

The two olive trees (4:11-14) representing the royal and priestly anointed ones are connected in Orthodox thought to the *symphonia* between Church and state that characterized the Byzantine ideal. More profoundly, they point to Christ in whom the priestly and royal dignities are perfectly united — a unity celebrated in the Orthodox liturgy where Christ is hymned as "King of Kings and Lord of Lords, the only sovereign" and simultaneously as "the great High Priest" who offers the Eucharistic sacrifice.
