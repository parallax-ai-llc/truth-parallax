The fountain for cleansing (13:1) is connected in Orthodox sacramental theology to both baptism and the blood of Christ shed on the cross. The blessing of waters at Theophany (January 6), when the priest immerses the cross in water and sanctifies it, liturgically enacts Zechariah's vision: the waters of creation are transformed into a fountain of healing through the power of the cross. Every Orthodox baptismal font participates in this prophetic fountain.

The removal of false prophets and idols (13:2-6) is read as the Church's ongoing vocation of discerning true from false teaching. The Orthodox tradition's emphasis on the consensus of the Fathers (*consensus patrum*) and the conciliar authority of the Church serves this prophetic function: tested through centuries of debate and prayer, Orthodox doctrine represents the community's collective discernment of truth from error.

The smitten shepherd (13:7) and the scattering and refining of the flock (13:8-9) are central to Orthodox Paschal theology. The Great and Holy Saturday liturgy traces the shepherd's journey through death and into Hades, where He gathers the scattered and liberates the captive. The promise that the refined remnant will say "The LORD is my God" and God will answer "They are my people" (13:9) is the covenant restored — the consummation of the entire prophetic tradition in the resurrection of Christ.
