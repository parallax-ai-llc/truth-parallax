Zechariah 14 presents the ultimate eschatological vision in terms that profoundly shaped Orthodox theology of the last things. The LORD standing on the Mount of Olives (14:4) is connected to the Ascension, where Christ departed from this same mountain (Acts 1:12), and to the promise of His return. The Orthodox celebration of the Ascension includes the reading of the angelic promise: "This same Jesus will come in the same way as you have seen Him go into heaven" — a return that Zechariah describes in cosmic terms.

The living waters flowing from Jerusalem (14:8) are given a sacramental and eschatological interpretation in Orthodox theology. The waters represent the Holy Spirit flowing from the Church into all creation, sanctifying and vivifying the world. The Orthodox blessing of waters at Theophany, where rivers and seas are blessed, enacts this prophetic vision on a cosmic scale — the entire creation participating in the sanctifying flow of divine grace.

The final vision — all nations celebrating Sukkot, holiness inscribed on the horses' bells, every pot in Jerusalem sacred (14:20-21) — presents the Orthodox eschatological hope in its fullest expression. This is the *apokatastasis* — the restoration of all things — that the Fathers spoke of with hope, when the distinction between sacred and profane will be overcome and God will be "all in all" (1 Corinthians 15:28). The final word of the book — "there shall no longer be a trader in the house of the LORD" — envisions a world where commercial exchange gives way to pure gift, reflecting the inner life of the Trinity where all is freely given and received in love.
