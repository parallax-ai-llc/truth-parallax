The vision of Joshua's cleansing is among the most liturgically significant passages in Zechariah for the Orthodox tradition. The removal of filthy garments and the investiture with pure vestments is reenacted sacramentally in every Orthodox baptism, where the newly baptized is stripped, immersed, and then clothed in a white garment signifying the new life in Christ. The patristic connection between Joshua's re-clothing and baptismal regeneration is found in Cyril of Jerusalem's *Mystagogical Catecheses*.

Satan's role as accuser (3:1) informs the Orthodox understanding of spiritual warfare. The faithful are engaged in an ongoing struggle against the "accuser of the brethren" (Revelation 12:10), and the Church's prayers of exorcism — both in baptism and in the various blessings of the liturgical tradition — invoke divine power against the adversary. Gregory of Nyssa and John Chrysostom both emphasized that the baptismal rite includes a formal renunciation of Satan, corresponding to the angelic rebuke in this vision.

The Branch (3:8) is identified in Orthodox Christology with the divine Logos who "branches out" from the root of Jesse (Isaiah 11:1) and from the bosom of the Father (John 1:18). The union of priestly and messianic imagery in this passage anticipates the Orthodox theology of Christ as the eternal High Priest who enters the heavenly sanctuary (Hebrews 9:11-12) and offers Himself as the once-for-all sacrifice.
