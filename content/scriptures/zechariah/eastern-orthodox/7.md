The question about fasting in Zechariah 7 resonates deeply with Orthodox liturgical practice, which includes the most extensive fasting discipline in Christianity. The Orthodox Church prescribes fasting on Wednesdays and Fridays throughout the year, during Great Lent, Apostles' Fast, Dormition Fast, and Nativity Fast. Zechariah's critique of fasting divorced from justice and mercy is taken seriously in Orthodox pastoral theology: the Fathers consistently taught that fasting without charity is empty, and that the purpose of fasting is to free resources for the poor.

The prophetic imperatives of 7:9-10 — true judgments, kindness, mercy, and care for the vulnerable — are integrated into Orthodox social teaching. While Orthodox theology tends to emphasize the transformation of the person through prayer, fasting, and sacramental participation, it also insists that personal holiness must bear fruit in just and compassionate action. The monastic tradition of hospitality (*philoxenia*) and care for the poor embodies this prophetic demand.

The ancestral failure to listen (7:11-14) and its catastrophic consequences serve as a warning within the Orthodox reading of salvation history. The Fathers used such passages to emphasize the reality of human freedom and its consequences: God does not compel obedience, but disobedience has real and devastating effects, both individual and communal. The exile is a type of the spiritual exile from God that results from persistent rejection of divine call.
