Zechariah 11's shepherd allegory, with its breaking of the staffs Favor and Union, is read in Orthodox theology as describing the consequences of Israel's rejection of the Messiah. The breaking of Favor represents the setting aside of the old covenant's protective grace; the breaking of Union represents the rupture between the followers of Christ and those who refused Him. Orthodox Holy Week services trace this theme of rejection and its cosmic consequences with profound liturgical solemnity.

The thirty pieces of silver (11:12-13) are commemorated in the Orthodox liturgy of Holy Wednesday, which focuses on the betrayal of Judas. The liturgical texts draw explicit connections between Zechariah's prophecy and the Passion narrative, presenting Judas's treachery as a fulfillment of prophetic warning. The "lordly price" — dripping with divine irony — reveals the human capacity to place a contemptible value on what is infinitely precious.

The foolish shepherd (11:15-17) is interpreted in patristic literature as a type of the Antichrist or of false teachers who exploit the flock. The Orthodox eschatological tradition, while less systematized than Western apocalypticism, maintains a clear expectation of anti-Christian powers that will arise in the last days. The curse upon the worthless shepherd who abandons the flock serves as a perpetual warning to church leaders of the gravity of their calling.
