The prophecy of the humble king on a donkey (9:9) is celebrated in the Orthodox Feast of the Entry into Jerusalem, one of the twelve great feasts. The liturgical texts for this feast draw extensively on Zechariah, and the faithful process with palms and branches, reenacting Christ's triumphal entry. The paradox of the vision — a victorious king on a beast of burden — encapsulates the Orthodox theology of kenosis: God's power is revealed through self-emptying humility.

The abolition of weapons and the proclamation of peace "to the nations" (9:10) connects to the Orthodox vision of Christ's universal kingship. The icon of "Christ Enthroned" depicts a king whose sovereignty extends over all creation — not through military conquest but through the cross, which Orthodox theology understands as the true throne from which Christ rules. The cosmic peace (*eirene*) that Christ establishes is not merely the absence of conflict but the harmonious ordering of all things in God.

The "prisoners of hope" (9:12) resonate with the Orthodox theology of the faithful who endured centuries of captivity and persecution — under Ottoman rule, under communist regimes — sustained by the hope of divine deliverance. The prophetic promise of "double" restoration (9:12) is read not merely as material compensation but as the superabundance of grace that follows suffering, a pattern perfectly realized in the Paschal mystery of death and resurrection.
