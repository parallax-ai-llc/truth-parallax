The promise that Jerusalem will be "inhabited as villages without walls" (2:4) is read in Orthodox ecclesiology as a prophecy of the Church's borderless expansion. The divine fire that surrounds the city (2:5) is connected to the experience of divine light in Orthodox mystical theology — the uncreated energies of God that both protect and illuminate the faithful. The Hesychast tradition, with its emphasis on the vision of divine light, finds a prophetic anticipation in this image of God as a wall of fire and indwelling glory.

The summons to "flee from the land of the north" (2:6) is given an ascetic interpretation in Orthodox monastic tradition: the spiritual life requires departure from the "Babylon" of worldly attachment and a journey toward the "Jerusalem" of divine communion. The monastic vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience enact this prophetic call in concrete, embodied practice.

The inclusion of "many nations" who will become the LORD's people (2:11) is central to the Orthodox theology of mission. The Church's universality is not an imperial project but a fulfillment of prophetic promise — God's love overflowing the boundaries of any single nation to embrace all peoples. The missionary saints of the Orthodox tradition — Cyril and Methodius, Herman of Alaska, Nicholas of Japan — embodied this prophetic vision in their work among diverse peoples.
