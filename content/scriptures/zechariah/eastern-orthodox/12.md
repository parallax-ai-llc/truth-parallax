Zechariah 12's vision of Jerusalem besieged by all nations, yet divinely defended, speaks powerfully to the Orthodox experience of the Church as a community under constant assault yet preserved by God. The image of Jerusalem as an "immovable rock" (12:3) is connected in Orthodox ecclesiology to the Church founded on the rock of apostolic faith, against which the gates of hell shall not prevail.

The prophecy "they will look on me, the one they have pierced" (12:10) is central to Orthodox Good Friday worship. The Orthodox tradition of *Epitaphios* — the solemn burial procession on Good Friday evening — invites the faithful to "look on" the crucified Lord with mourning and love. The shift from "me" (the divine speaker) to "him" (the pierced one) is resolved in Orthodox Christology through the doctrine of the *hypostatic union*: the one who was pierced on the cross is both God and man, a single divine-human person.

The mourning described in 12:10-14 — personal, familial, all-encompassing — models the Orthodox practice of personal repentance. The sacrament of confession is not a formality but a deeply personal encounter with the pierced Lord, where the penitent sees what sin has done to Christ and responds with genuine grief. The weeping of the families of David and Nathan represents the tears of compunction that the Fathers regarded as a gift of the Holy Spirit.
