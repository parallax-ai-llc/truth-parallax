Orthodox interpretation of Zechariah 1 situates the call to repentance within the patristic theology of *metanoia* — a complete turning of the mind and heart toward God. The Fathers emphasized that the divine invitation "Return to me" is not merely a moral command but an ontological reorientation: the human person, created for communion with God, must turn from the dispersion of sin back to the centering reality of divine presence. The night visions that follow represent the prophetic experience of God's hidden governance of the cosmos.

The horsemen patrolling the earth (1:8-11) represent divine providence exercised through angelic powers. Orthodox angelology, rooted in the writings of Pseudo-Dionysius and developed by Maximus the Confessor and John of Damascus, understands the angelic hierarchy as mediating God's governance of creation. Zechariah's vision provides biblical warrant for this theology: God employs spiritual beings to oversee and direct the course of nations and nature.

The passionate jealousy of God for Jerusalem (1:14) is understood in Orthodox theology not as an imperfection but as the intensity of divine love. God's jealousy is the fiery energy of love that cannot tolerate separation from the beloved. The patristic tradition connects this to the Song of Songs: "Love is strong as death, jealousy fierce as the grave" (8:6). This passionate divine love is the wellspring of the entire prophetic tradition and ultimately of the Incarnation itself.
