## Overview

The Eastern Orthodox tradition reads Zechariah as one of the most richly prophetic books of the Old Testament, its imagery woven deeply into the liturgy of Holy Week, Pascha, and the great feasts. The patristic tradition, from Didymus the Blind to Cyril of Alexandria, provided extensive commentary on Zechariah's visions, reading them as types and shadows of the mysteries of Christ, the Church, and the age to come.

## Liturgical Integration

Zechariah's prophecies are read during the most solemn moments of the Orthodox liturgical year. The entry into Jerusalem on a donkey (9:9) is celebrated at the Feast of the Entry into Jerusalem (Palm Sunday). The pierced one (12:10) and the smitten shepherd (13:7) are central to the Holy Week readings. The fountain for cleansing (13:1) is connected to the baptismal waters and to the blood and water from Christ's side.

## Theological Emphases

The Orthodox reading emphasizes the cosmic scope of Zechariah's vision: the transformation of all creation, the defeat of evil, and the universal recognition of God's kingship (14:9). The patristic reading of the Branch (3:8, 6:12) as Christ connects Zechariah to the theology of theosis — Christ the priest-king who mediates between God and humanity, sanctifying all creation.
