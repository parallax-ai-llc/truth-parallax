Zechariah 8's vision of restored Jerusalem — where the elderly sit in peace and children play in the streets (8:4-5) — embodies the Orthodox eschatological hope of the transfigured creation. This is not merely a political restoration but a glimpse of the Kingdom of God where every dimension of life — age, youth, work, rest — is suffused with divine peace. Orthodox iconography of paradise draws on prophetic visions like this to depict the age to come as one of comprehensive flourishing.

The gathering from east and west (8:7-8) is connected in Orthodox theology to the Eucharistic assembly, which gathers the scattered members of Christ's Body from every direction into one community of worship. The Divine Liturgy's prayer that God would "gather us all into Thy kingdom" echoes the prophetic vision of regathering and embodies it sacramentally in every celebration.

The promise that "ten men from the nations of every tongue shall take hold of the robe of a Jew" (8:23) is read as fulfilled in the nations' coming to Christ, the Jew par excellence. Orthodox missionary theology draws on this vision: the peoples of the world are drawn not by argument or coercion but by the witness of God's presence among His people. The lives of the saints serve as this tangible witness — the "robe" that the nations grasp in their desire to find God.
