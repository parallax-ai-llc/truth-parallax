The vision of the golden lampstand (4:1-14) holds deep liturgical significance in Catholic tradition. The menorah — the seven-branched lampstand — is connected to the perpetual light (*sanctuary lamp*) that burns before the tabernacle in every Catholic church, signifying the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist. The oil flowing from the olive trees into the lamps represents the Holy Spirit who sustains the Church's worship and mission.

The declaration "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit" (4:6) is foundational for Catholic teaching on the relationship between human effort and divine grace. While Catholic theology affirms the necessity of human cooperation with grace (*synergeia*), this verse reminds that the ultimate source of the Church's vitality is the Holy Spirit, not institutional power or political influence. Vatican II's *Lumen Gentium* draws on this prophetic vision to describe the Church as a mystery animated by the Spirit.

The two anointed ones (4:14) — the royal and priestly figures — prefigure Christ who holds both offices in his person. Catholic theology of the *tria munera* (three offices) of Christ as priest, prophet, and king finds prophetic anticipation in Zechariah's pairing of Zerubbabel and Joshua, which points toward their ultimate unification in the one person of Jesus Christ.
