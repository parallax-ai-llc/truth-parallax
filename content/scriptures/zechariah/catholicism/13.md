The fountain "opened for the house of David for sin and uncleanness" (13:1) is a foundational image in Catholic sacramental theology. The Church Fathers, beginning with Ambrose and Augustine, connected this fountain to the baptismal waters and to the blood and water that flowed from Christ's pierced side (John 19:34). Catholic baptismal theology sees in every font a participation in this Zechariah fountain — the cleansing stream opened by Christ's sacrifice that washes away original sin and restores the image of God in the baptized.

The promise of the removal of idols and false prophets (13:2-6) is read in Catholic tradition as describing the purification that the Church effects through its teaching authority (Magisterium) and its pastoral care. The struggle against false teaching has been a constant concern of the Catholic Church, from the early councils that defined orthodox doctrine to contemporary efforts to distinguish authentic faith from distortion.

The smitten shepherd oracle (13:7) — "Strike the shepherd, and the sheep will be scattered" — is quoted by Jesus at the Last Supper (Matthew 26:31) and is central to Catholic Holy Thursday liturgy. The scattering of the disciples at the crucifixion and their regathering at Pentecost enacts the prophetic pattern: the shepherd is struck, the flock disperses, but the risen Lord gathers them again, now empowered by the Spirit for universal mission.
