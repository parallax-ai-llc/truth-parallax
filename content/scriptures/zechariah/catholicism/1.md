Catholic interpretation of Zechariah 1 situates the prophet's call to repentance within the broader economy of salvation. The divine invitation — "Return to me, and I will return to you" (1:3) — is read as an expression of God's prevenient grace, which always precedes and enables the human response. The Catechism (CCC 1430) teaches that conversion is first a work of grace, and Zechariah's opening oracle illustrates this principle: God initiates the return.

The vision of horsemen patrolling the earth (1:8-11) reveals a providential God who actively oversees the affairs of nations. Catholic theology of divine providence, articulated in the Catechism (CCC 302-314), affirms that God governs all creation with wisdom and love. The angelic report that "all the earth remains at rest" (1:11) while Jerusalem still lies in ruins creates a dramatic tension that the angel's intercession addresses — a model for the Catholic practice of intercessory prayer, including the invocation of the saints and angels.

The divine promise of compassion for Jerusalem and anger at the complacent nations (1:14-15) grounds the Catholic understanding of divine justice as inextricable from mercy. God's zeal for His people is not cold judicial reckoning but passionate, covenantal love that insists on both justice for the oppressed and judgment against the oppressor.
