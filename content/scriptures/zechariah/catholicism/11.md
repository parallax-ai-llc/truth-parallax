The shepherd allegory of Zechariah 11 is read in Catholic Passion theology as a prophetic anticipation of Christ's rejection by Israel's leaders. The two staffs, Favor and Union, represent God's gracious covenant and the unity of God's people — both of which are broken when the Good Shepherd is rejected. Catholic commentary connects this directly to the tearing of the Temple veil at the crucifixion (Matthew 27:51), which symbolized the rupture of the old covenant and the inauguration of the new.

The thirty pieces of silver (11:12-13) — "the lordly price at which I was priced by them" — is one of the most specific messianic prophecies fulfilled in the Passion narrative. The Catholic liturgy of Holy Week traces this fulfillment with care: from Judas's bargain with the chief priests (Matthew 26:14-16) to his remorse and the purchase of the potter's field (Matthew 27:3-10). The bitter irony that the price of betrayal was the legal value of a slave underscores the theme of divine humility that pervades Catholic Christology.

The foolish shepherd (11:15-17) is interpreted in Catholic tradition as an antichrist figure — a leader who abandons and exploits the flock. Catholic eschatology, while cautious about specific identifications, affirms the reality of anti-Christian forces that will arise before the Lord's return, and this passage provides prophetic grounding for that expectation.
