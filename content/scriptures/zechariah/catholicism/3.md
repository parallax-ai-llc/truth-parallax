The vision of Joshua the high priest being cleansed from filthy garments and reclothed in pure vestments (3:1-5) is profoundly significant for Catholic sacramental theology. The Catholic reading sees here a type of the sacrament of Reconciliation: the sinner stands before God in soiled garments, the Accuser (Satan) brings charges, but divine mercy intervenes to remove guilt and restore the penitent to a state of grace. The change of garments symbolizes the interior transformation that the sacrament effects.

The figure of Satan as accuser resonates with Catholic angelology and demonology. The Church Fathers identified this scene as an illustration of the cosmic spiritual combat described in Ephesians 6:12, in which the faithful are assailed by spiritual powers that the sacraments and the Church's prayer overcome. The angelic intervention on Joshua's behalf models the role of the guardian angel and the intercession of the saints in Catholic devotion.

The announcement of "the Branch" (3:8) is read Christologically, and the mysterious "stone with seven eyes" (3:9) has been interpreted by Catholic commentators as a reference to Christ the cornerstone (cf. Ephesians 2:20) or to the sevenfold gifts of the Holy Spirit (Isaiah 11:2). The promise of iniquity removed "in a single day" (3:9) points to Good Friday, when Christ's sacrifice accomplished what the entire Levitical system could only prefigure.
