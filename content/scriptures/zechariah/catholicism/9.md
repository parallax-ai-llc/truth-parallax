The Palm Sunday prophecy (9:9) is central to Catholic Holy Week liturgy. The procession of palms reenacts Christ's humble entry into Jerusalem, explicitly fulfilling Zechariah's vision of a king who is "humble and mounted on a donkey." Catholic Christology emphasizes that this humility is not a deficiency but a revelation of God's nature: the God who created the universe enters His royal city on a borrowed colt, redefining kingship itself.

The abolition of "the chariot from Ephraim and the war horse from Jerusalem" (9:10) anticipates the Catholic theology of peace. Christ the King establishes His rule not through military conquest but through the cross — a paradox that the Church's social teaching on war and peace develops. The vision of a kingdom "from sea to sea, and from the River to the ends of the earth" (9:10) supports the Church's claim to universality and its mission to bring the peace of Christ to all nations.

The "prisoners of hope" (9:12) is a phrase that resonates deeply with Catholic spirituality. Hope, in Catholic theology, is a theological virtue — a gift of grace that enables the believer to trust in God's promises even in darkness. The Church's mission to the imprisoned, the suffering, and the marginalized embodies this prophetic image of liberation through hope.
