Catholic reading of Zechariah 10 highlights the divine shepherd motif as anticipating Christ the Good Shepherd and, by extension, the pastoral ministry of the Church. The indictment of false shepherds (10:2-3) resonates with the Catholic understanding of pastoral responsibility: bishops, as successors of the apostles, bear the shepherd's charge to feed and protect the flock. Canon law and the sacrament of Holy Orders institutionalize this prophetic concern.

The gathering of scattered Ephraim and Judah (10:6-12) is read ecclesiologically as the reunification of God's divided people in the one, holy, catholic, and apostolic Church. The ecumenical movement, which the Catholic Church has engaged in vigorously since Vatican II, draws on prophetic visions like this to sustain hope for Christian unity. The promise "I will strengthen the house of Judah, and I will save the house of Joseph" (10:6) becomes a prayer for the restoration of full communion among all who bear Christ's name.

The passage through the sea and the drying up of the River (10:11) — echoing the Exodus — is read typologically as the journey of salvation through baptism. Catholic sacramental theology sees in every crossing of water a participation in the paschal mystery: the passage from slavery to freedom, from sin to grace, from death to life.
