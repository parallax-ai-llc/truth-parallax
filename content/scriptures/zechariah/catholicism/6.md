The four chariots (6:1-8) represent divine judgment executed through cosmic agents, a vision that Catholic theology connects to its understanding of providence and angelic ministry. The chariots emerge from between two bronze mountains — traditionally interpreted as divine justice and mercy — and execute God's purposes throughout the earth. Catholic angelology affirms that God employs angelic beings as instruments of His will, and Zechariah's vision provides prophetic warrant for this understanding.

The crowning of Joshua (6:9-15) is arguably the single most important passage in Zechariah for Catholic Christology and ecclesiology. The Branch who "shall build the temple of the LORD" and "shall bear royal honor and shall sit and rule on his throne, and shall be a priest on his throne" (6:12-13) describes a figure who unites the priestly and kingly offices in his own person. Catholic theology identifies this figure with Christ, the eternal High Priest who is also King of Kings. The Epistle to the Hebrews develops this priest-king Christology at length, and the Catholic Mass enacts it sacramentally: Christ the priest offers himself as the sacrifice, and Christ the king reigns from the altar.

The promise that "those who are far off shall come and help to build the temple" (6:15) is read as a prophecy of the Gentile mission and the building of the universal Church. Every Catholic parish, every mission station, every baptism of a person from "far off" is a fulfillment of this prophetic word.
