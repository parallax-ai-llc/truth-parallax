Catholic interpretation of Zechariah 7 emphasizes the prophet's teaching on the relationship between ritual observance and moral substance. The question about fasting (7:3) occasions a prophetic response that does not abolish fasting but insists on its proper context: justice, mercy, and compassion for the vulnerable (7:9-10). Catholic spiritual tradition maintains a robust practice of fasting — during Lent, on Ember Days, and as voluntary penance — while insisting, with Zechariah, that fasting without charity is empty.

The social justice teaching of 7:9-10 — true judgments, kindness, mercy, and care for widows, orphans, sojourners, and the poor — anticipates Catholic social doctrine as articulated from *Rerum Novarum* (1891) to *Fratelli Tutti* (2020). The prophetic tradition's insistence on justice for the marginalized is the biblical root of the Church's "preferential option for the poor." The Catechism (CCC 2443-2449) draws on the prophetic tradition, including passages like this, to ground the Church's social teaching in divine revelation.

The warning about the ancestors who "refused to pay attention" (7:11) and suffered exile as a consequence serves as a cautionary tale in Catholic preaching. The capacity for spiritual deafness is not confined to ancient Israel; every generation faces the temptation to substitute comfortable religiosity for costly discipleship.
