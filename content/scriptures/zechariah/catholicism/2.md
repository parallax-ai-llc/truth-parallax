The vision of the measuring line (2:1-5) and the promise that Jerusalem will overflow its walls is read in Catholic ecclesiology as a prophecy of the Church's universal expansion. The city that cannot be contained by walls anticipates the Catholic Church's self-understanding as universal (*katholikos*) — a community that transcends every boundary of nation, language, and culture. The promise of God's fiery protection and indwelling glory (2:5) finds sacramental expression in the Eucharistic presence.

The call to "flee from the land of the north" (2:6) is interpreted spiritually in the Catholic tradition as a call to conversion — to leave behind the Babylon of sin and worldly attachment and return to the holy city of God's presence. The monastic tradition, with its emphasis on *fuga mundi* (flight from the world), draws on prophetic texts like this to articulate the radical reorientation of life that Christian discipleship requires.

The remarkable verse 2:11 — "Many nations shall join themselves to the LORD in that day" — provides prophetic warrant for the Catholic Church's universal mission. The inclusion of the nations in the people of God, anticipated here, is fulfilled in the Church's baptismal practice, where people of every nation are incorporated into the Body of Christ.
