Zechariah 12 is central to Catholic theology of the cross. The promise of Jerusalem's deliverance from siege (12:1-9) is read eschatologically as the Church's ultimate triumph over all adversaries, grounded not in human strength but in divine protection. The image of Jerusalem as "an immovable rock" (12:3) resonates with Catholic ecclesiology: the Church built on the rock of Peter will withstand the gates of hell (Matthew 16:18).

The verse "they will look on me, the one they have pierced" (12:10) is perhaps the single most important Old Testament text for Catholic Passion theology. John 19:37 quotes it explicitly in the context of the soldier piercing Christ's side with a lance, from which flowed blood and water. Catholic sacramental theology reads these as symbols of Baptism and Eucharist — the two fundamental sacraments that flow from the pierced side of Christ. The Sacred Heart devotion, with its focus on Christ's wounded heart as the source of mercy, draws deeply on this Zechariah text.

The mourning described in 12:10-14, with its emphasis on personal, family-by-family grief, models the Catholic understanding of repentance as both communal and deeply personal. The sacrament of Reconciliation requires individual confession precisely because sin, while it affects the community, is also an intimate wound that requires personal encounter with divine mercy.
