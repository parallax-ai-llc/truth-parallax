Catholic eschatology engages deeply with Zechariah 14's vision of the consummation of all things. The LORD's standing on the Mount of Olives (14:4), which splits in two to create a valley of escape, is connected in Catholic tradition to the Ascension — Christ ascended from the Mount of Olives (Acts 1:12) and the angels promised He would return in the same way. The splitting of the mountain represents the cosmic transformation that Christ's return will accomplish.

The living waters flowing from Jerusalem (14:8) — half to the eastern sea and half to the western — is read as a symbol of the sacramental grace that flows from the Church to the whole world. Catholic liturgical theology sees in these waters the baptismal stream and the Eucharistic chalice, the two great rivers of grace that nourish the Church on its pilgrimage. The vision echoes Ezekiel 47's river from the Temple, which Catholic tradition connects to the grace flowing from Christ's side.

The final vision of universal worship at the Feast of Booths (14:16-19) and the sanctification of every object in Jerusalem (14:20-21) presents the Catholic eschatological hope: a world wholly consecrated to God, where the distinction between sacred and profane has been overcome. The concluding statement — "there shall no longer be a trader in the house of the LORD of hosts" (14:21) — echoes Jesus's cleansing of the Temple and anticipates the heavenly worship described in Revelation, where God is all in all.
