## Overview

Zechariah is one of the longest and most theologically complex of the Minor Prophets. The prophet, whose name means "the LORD remembers," was a contemporary of Haggai, ministering to the returned exiles in Jerusalem beginning in 520 BCE. The book is notable for its elaborate visionary sequences, its rich messianic imagery, and its influence on New Testament eschatology — particularly the Passion narratives and the book of Revelation.

## Structure

The book divides into two major sections that differ markedly in style:

1. **First Zechariah** (Chapters 1-8): Eight night visions (1:7-6:15), oracles on fasting and restoration (7-8), all precisely dated to 520-518 BCE
2. **Second Zechariah** (Chapters 9-14): Undated oracles of judgment and salvation, featuring the humble king, the pierced one, the smitten shepherd, and the final Day of the LORD

## Cross-Traditional Significance

Zechariah is among the most frequently cited Old Testament books in the New Testament:

- **Judaism**: The visionary tradition influenced apocalyptic literature; the Branch and priestly imagery shaped messianic expectation
- **Christianity**: The triumphal entry (9:9), thirty pieces of silver (11:12-13), the pierced one (12:10), and the smitten shepherd (13:7) are all applied to Christ
- **Islam**: Zakariyya (Zechariah) is a named prophet in the Quran, though identified with the father of Yahya (John the Baptist) rather than the author of this book
- **Academic**: A key text for understanding the development of apocalyptic and messianic thought in Second Temple Judaism

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Visions | Symbolic night visions mediated by an interpreting angel |
| Messianic Prophecy | The Branch, the humble king, the pierced one |
| Temple | The rebuilding and spiritual significance of the Second Temple |
| Eschatology | The final Day of the LORD and the transformation of all creation |
| Restoration | God's faithfulness to Jerusalem and the regathering of His people |
