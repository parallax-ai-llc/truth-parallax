## Commentary on Habakkuk 2: The Fate of the Treacherous

The woes interpreted in Chapter 2 resonate with Jesus's own series of woes against the scribes and Pharisees (Matthew 23), which employ the same prophetic form to condemn religious leaders who have betrayed their calling. Both the pesher and Jesus direct their sharpest criticism not at pagan outsiders but at those within Israel who should have known better — a pattern deeply rooted in the prophetic tradition.

Protestant interpreters find the pesher's emphasis on the Wicked Priest's accumulation of wealth particularly significant in light of the New Testament's sustained critique of riches. Jesus's warnings about the deceitfulness of wealth (Mark 4:19), the camel and the needle's eye (Mark 10:25), and the parable of the rich man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19-31) all address the same concern that animates the pesher: wealth accumulated through injustice brings divine judgment.

The Day of Atonement episode, in which the Wicked Priest attacks the Teacher on the community's holiest day, provides important context for understanding the New Testament's theology of atonement. The pesher's narrative of a corrupt high priest persecuting the righteous on the Day of Atonement creates a powerful ironic contrast: the priest who should be making atonement for the people is instead committing violence against God's chosen teacher. Christian theology would develop this irony further, presenting Christ as both the true High Priest and the sacrifice that supersedes the Temple system.
