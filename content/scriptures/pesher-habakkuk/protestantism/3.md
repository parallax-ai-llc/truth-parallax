## The Righteous Shall Live by Faith

Chapter 3's interpretation of Habakkuk 2:4 brings us to one of the most consequential verses in the history of Western theology. Paul's citation of this verse in Romans 1:17 and Galatians 3:11 provided the scriptural foundation for the Reformation doctrine of justification by faith alone (*sola fide*). The pesher's interpretation — that salvation depends on faithfulness to the Teacher of Righteousness — provides a pre-Christian Jewish reading that both illuminates and contrasts with Paul's use.

The pesher reads "the righteous shall live by his faith" as referring to Torah-observers who demonstrate their faithfulness through adherence to the Teacher's interpretation. Paul, by contrast, reads the verse as proclaiming a righteousness that comes through faith in Christ apart from works of the law. The structural parallel is striking: both interpret *emunah* as a relationship with a specific figure (Teacher or Christ) rather than as an abstract quality. The crucial difference lies in the content of that relationship and its implications for Torah observance.

Martin Luther's breakthrough — his realization that the "righteousness of God" in Romans 1:17 is not the punishing standard by which God judges but the gift by which God justifies — transforms the verse in a way that neither the pesher nor the original Hebrew fully anticipates. Yet the pesher's insistence that salvation depends on relationship with God's appointed teacher provides a Jewish matrix for understanding how Paul could read the verse as he did, even while drawing radically different conclusions.
