## Overview

The Pesher Habakkuk (1QpHab) is one of the most important interpretive texts among the Dead Sea Scrolls, discovered in Cave 1 at Qumran. It provides a verse-by-verse commentary on the biblical book of Habakkuk, interpreting the prophet's words as referring to events in the commentator's own time — particularly the conflict between the Teacher of Righteousness and the Wicked Priest, and the threat posed by the Kittim (likely the Romans).

## Structure

The Pesher Habakkuk follows a consistent interpretive format:

1. **Commentary on Habakkuk 1** (Chapter 1): Interpretation of the prophet's complaints and God's response, identifying the Kittim as the instrument of divine judgment and the Wicked Priest as the adversary of the Teacher
2. **Commentary on Habakkuk 2** (Chapter 2): Interpretation of the woes against the treacherous, applied to the Wicked Priest and other enemies of the community
3. **Commentary on Habakkuk 2:4b and beyond** (Chapter 3): The pivotal interpretation of "the righteous shall live by his faith" as referring to the community's fidelity to the Teacher

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Pesher Habakkuk holds significance across religious and scholarly traditions:

- **Essene**: The community's definitive demonstration of their interpretive method and theological claims
- **Judaism**: Evidence of pre-rabbinic hermeneutical methods and sectarian biblical interpretation
- **Christianity**: Context for understanding the New Testament's use of Habakkuk 2:4 and its hermeneutical methods
- **Academic**: A primary source for understanding the pesher method, Qumran history, and Second Temple interpretation

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Pesher Method | A distinctive mode of biblical interpretation that reads prophecy as coded references to contemporary events |
| Teacher of Righteousness | The authoritative interpreter to whom God revealed the meaning of the prophets |
| Wicked Priest | The persecutor of the Teacher, identified with a specific historical figure (likely a Hasmonean priest-king) |
| Kittim | A foreign military power (most likely Rome) understood as God's instrument of judgment |
| Prophecy Fulfilled | The conviction that biblical prophecy finds its true meaning in the community's present experience |
