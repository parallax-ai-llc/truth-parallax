## The Righteous Shall Live by Faith: Reception History and Analysis

The pesher's interpretation of Habakkuk 2:4 has generated extensive scholarly discussion, both for its intrinsic significance and for its role in the reception history of one of the most consequential verses in Western religious thought. The pesher's reading — linking salvation to faithfulness to the Teacher and Torah observance — provides a crucial data point for understanding the interpretive range available to Paul when he cited the same verse in Romans 1:17 and Galatians 3:11.

Richard Hays, Francis Watson, and others have compared the pesher's and Paul's interpretations of Habakkuk 2:4 in detail. The pesher reads the verse as: the righteous will be delivered from judgment because of their toil and their faithfulness to the Teacher. Paul reads it as: the one who is righteous by faith will live. The crucial ambiguity — does "by faith" modify "the righteous" (the one who is righteous-by-faith) or "shall live" (the righteous shall live-by-faith)? — is resolved differently by each interpreter and continues to generate scholarly debate.

The pesher's acknowledgment of the delayed end (1QpHab VII:5-14) has been analyzed within the broader context of "cognitive dissonance" theory applied to religious movements. Leon Festinger's model — which predicts that failed predictions lead to increased commitment and proselytizing rather than disillusionment — has been applied to the Qumran community by scholars including Albert Baumgarten. The pesher's assurance that the delay itself is divinely ordained represents a sophisticated theological response to unfulfilled eschatological expectation that parallels the early Church's similar adjustment (2 Peter 3:8-9).
