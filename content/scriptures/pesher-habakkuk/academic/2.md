## Commentary on Habakkuk 2: Historical Reconstruction

Chapter 2's series of interpreted woes has provided scholars with the most detailed evidence for reconstructing the conflict between the Teacher of Righteousness and the Wicked Priest. The description of the Wicked Priest — initially legitimate but later corrupted by power, accumulating wealth through plunder, and ultimately suffering a disgraceful end — has been matched against various Hasmonean priest-kings, with Jonathan Maccabee (160-143 BCE) and Alexander Jannaeus (103-76 BCE) being the most common identifications.

The Day of Atonement passage (1QpHab XI:2-8) has been analyzed by scholars for its implications regarding the community's calendar. The pesher states that the Wicked Priest "appeared before them to swallow them up on the Day of Fasting, the Sabbath of their rest." This indicates that the community observed the Day of Atonement on a different date from the Jerusalem Temple — a consequence of their adherence to a 364-day solar calendar rather than the lunisolar calendar used by the Temple establishment. The calendrical dispute, as Jonathan Ben-Dov and others have shown, was one of the defining issues separating the Qumran community from mainstream Judaism.

The rhetorical structure of the pesher's woe interpretations has been analyzed by scholars interested in its literary artistry. Each woe follows a consistent pattern: citation of the biblical text, introductory formula (*pishro* — "its interpretation concerns..."), and application to contemporary figures. This formulaic structure, while repetitive, creates a cumulative rhetorical effect that reinforces the community's conviction that every detail of the prophet's text was divinely crafted to describe their own experience.
