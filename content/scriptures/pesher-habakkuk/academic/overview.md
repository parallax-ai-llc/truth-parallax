## Overview

Academic study of the Pesher Habakkuk has focused on three major areas: the identification of the historical figures mentioned in the text (the Teacher, the Wicked Priest, the Liar, the Kittim), the analysis of the pesher method as a hermeneutical technique, and the reconstruction of the community's history and self-understanding from the commentary's content.

## Key Scholarly Debates

The identification of the Wicked Priest remains one of the most debated questions in Scrolls scholarship. Candidates include Jonathan (160-143 BCE), Simon (143-134 BCE), Alexander Jannaeus (103-76 BCE), and Hyrcanus II (67-40 BCE), with the majority of scholars favoring one of the earlier Hasmonean priest-kings. The identification has implications for dating the pesher, understanding its historical context, and reconstructing the community's history.

The pesher method itself has been analyzed by scholars including Maurya Horgan, George Brooke, and Timothy Lim. Horgan distinguished between "continuous pesharim" (systematic commentaries on a biblical book, like 1QpHab) and "thematic pesharim" (topical compilations of interpreted passages). Brooke has situated the pesher method within the broader context of Second Temple hermeneutics, arguing that it represents a distinctive form of "inspired exegesis" in which the interpreter claims divine guidance comparable to that of the original prophet.
