## Commentary on Habakkuk 1: Scholarly Analysis

The opening chapter of the Pesher Habakkuk has been central to scholarly debates about the identification of the Kittim and the historical context of the pesher. The Kittim are almost universally identified by modern scholars as the Romans, based on the detailed descriptions of their military practices (sacrifice to standards, contempt for rulers, systematic plundering) that match Roman military behavior as described by Josephus, Polybius, and other ancient sources.

The identification of the Kittim with Rome rather than the Seleucid Greeks — an earlier proposal defended by some scholars — has significant implications for dating the pesher. If the Kittim are Romans, the pesher reflects conditions after Pompey's conquest of Jerusalem in 63 BCE, placing its composition in the first century BCE. The paleographic analysis of the manuscript supports this dating, with most scholars assigning 1QpHab to the late first century BCE or early first century CE.

The pesher method itself has been subjected to extensive hermeneutical analysis. Timothy Lim has argued that the pesher operates on the assumption that biblical prophecy is inherently polyvalent — capable of multiple fulfillments — and that the commentator's task is to identify the specific fulfillment intended by God for the "final generation." This hermeneutical assumption distinguishes the pesher from modern historical-critical interpretation, which seeks the meaning intended by the original author, while connecting it to other forms of applied biblical interpretation in ancient Judaism and early Christianity.
