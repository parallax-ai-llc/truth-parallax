## The Kittim and the Wicked Priest

The opening chapter of the Pesher Habakkuk interprets Habakkuk's description of the Chaldeans as a prophecy about the Kittim — the fearsome military power that the community identified with the Romans. Habakkuk's words about a people "bitter and hasty" who march through the earth to seize dwellings not their own were understood as a precise description of Roman military expansion across the Near East.

For the Essene community, the identification of the Kittim with Rome transformed current events into prophetic fulfillment. The Roman armies were not merely a political threat but a divinely appointed instrument of judgment against a corrupt world. The pesher's detailed descriptions of the Kittim — their military tactics, their plundering of conquered peoples, their sacrifice to their military standards — suggest direct observation of or reliable reports about Roman practices.

The chapter also introduces the conflict between the Teacher of Righteousness and the Wicked Priest, interpreting Habakkuk's complaint about violence and injustice as a description of the Teacher's persecution. The Wicked Priest, who "was called by the name of truth when he first arose" but later "became proud," represents a historical figure whose initial legitimacy gave way to corruption — a narrative that reinforced the community's conviction that the Jerusalem establishment had betrayed its calling.
