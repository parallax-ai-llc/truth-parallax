## The Righteous Shall Live by Faith

The pesher's interpretation of Habakkuk 2:4 provides essential context for the Catholic understanding of faith that integrates trust (*fides qua*) with content (*fides quae*) and action. The pesher's reading — that the righteous are saved through their "toil and their faithfulness to the Teacher of Righteousness" — combines interior commitment with exterior practice in a way that resonates with the Catholic rejection of a sharp dichotomy between faith and works.

Catholic theology, articulated definitively at the Council of Trent (Session 6, 1547), teaches that justification involves both faith and the cooperation of the human will with divine grace. The pesher's integration of faithfulness (interior disposition) with "toil" (practical Torah observance) anticipates this Catholic synthesis, even as the specific content differs. James 2:24 — "a person is justified by works and not by faith alone" — expresses a principle structurally similar to the pesher's understanding.

The chapter's treatment of the delayed end also resonates with Catholic theology's engagement with the delay of the parousia. The early Church, like the Qumran community, faced the challenge of unfulfilled eschatological expectations. Catholic theology resolved this tension by emphasizing the Church's ongoing sacramental life as the mode of Christ's presence in the interim — a solution structurally parallel to the pesher's assurance that faithfulness to the Teacher sustains the community through the period of waiting.
