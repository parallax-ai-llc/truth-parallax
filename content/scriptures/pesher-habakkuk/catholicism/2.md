## Commentary on Habakkuk 2: Justice and Accountability

The woes against the Wicked Priest interpreted in Chapter 2 reflect concerns about social justice and priestly accountability that resonate deeply with Catholic social teaching. The Catholic tradition, from the prophetic criticism of unjust wealth in *Rerum Novarum* (1891) to the comprehensive social vision of *Laudato Si'* (2015), maintains the prophetic insistence that wealth accumulated through exploitation brings divine judgment. The pesher's condemnation of the Wicked Priest's plundering anticipates this sustained tradition of prophetic economic critique.

The Day of Atonement episode holds particular significance for Catholic Christology. The image of a corrupt high priest attacking the righteous on the Day of Atonement creates an ironic reversal that the Letter to the Hebrews develops christologically: Christ is the true High Priest who, unlike the corrupt priests of the old covenant, "offered himself" (Hebrews 7:27) as a perfect and permanent sacrifice. The pesher's narrative of priestly failure provides part of the theological context for the early Christian understanding of Christ's priesthood as superseding the Levitical system.

Catholic moral theology also finds in the pesher's condemnation of the Wicked Priest a confirmation of the principle that spiritual authority carries heightened moral responsibility. The Council of Trent's decree on the reform of the clergy and Canon Law's provisions for clerical discipline reflect an ongoing Catholic commitment to holding religious leaders to the prophetic standards that the pesher invokes.
