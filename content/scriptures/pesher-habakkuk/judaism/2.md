## Commentary on Habakkuk 2: Prophetic Woes Reinterpreted

Chapter 2's application of Habakkuk's woes to the Wicked Priest and other contemporary figures exemplifies a mode of prophetic interpretation that would continue to develop in Jewish tradition. The rabbinic principle that "every prophet prophesied for his own generation" (Sanhedrin 99a) stands in tension with the pesher's assumption that the prophet's words were sealed up for a future generation, yet both approaches share the conviction that prophecy speaks to real historical circumstances rather than abstractly.

The charges against the Wicked Priest — accumulating wealth, defiling the Temple, attacking the righteous — reflect broader Second Temple concerns about priestly corruption that appear in multiple Jewish sources. Josephus's descriptions of priestly avarice, the Talmud's critique of certain high-priestly families ("Woe to me from the house of Boethus, woe to me from their clubs," Pesachim 57a), and the Testament of Levi's prophetic condemnation of priestly corruption all testify to a widespread Jewish tradition of holding the priesthood accountable to prophetic standards.

The Day of Atonement episode — the Wicked Priest attacking the Teacher on the community's holiest day — highlights the calendrical dispute that separated the Qumran community from mainstream Judaism. This disagreement was not merely technical but theological: it reflected competing understandings of cosmic order, divine revelation, and legitimate authority over sacred time.
