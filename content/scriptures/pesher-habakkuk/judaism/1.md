## Commentary on Habakkuk 1: A Jewish Perspective

The opening chapter of the Pesher Habakkuk illustrates a hermeneutical approach that is both recognizably Jewish and distinctively sectarian. The practice of reading biblical prophecy as referring to contemporary events has parallels in rabbinic literature — the Talmud (Sanhedrin 97b-98a) applies prophetic passages to the messianic age, and the Targumim frequently update biblical references for their audiences — but the pesher method's exclusive claim that the text refers only to the commentator's present goes beyond rabbinic practice.

From a Jewish perspective, the identification of the biblical Chaldeans with the Kittim (Romans) demonstrates the remarkable fluidity of biblical interpretation in the Second Temple period. Each generation found its own enemies and circumstances reflected in the prophetic text, a hermeneutical practice that would continue throughout Jewish history. The medieval commentators would similarly read prophetic texts in light of their own historical circumstances, though typically with greater attention to the text's original historical meaning.

The chapter's portrayal of the conflict between the Teacher and the Wicked Priest reveals the intensity of internal Jewish disputes over religious authority in the Hasmonean period. The combination of priestly and royal power in the Hasmonean dynasty generated fierce opposition from groups who regarded this merger as illegitimate — a controversy that shaped Jewish political and religious life until the destruction of the Temple in 70 CE.
