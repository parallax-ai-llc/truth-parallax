## The Righteous Shall Live by Faith

The pesher's interpretation of Habakkuk 2:4 is one of the most significant passages in the Scrolls for Jewish-Christian dialogue. The Hebrew word *emunah* (commonly translated "faith" or "faithfulness") carries a rich semantic range that encompasses trust, fidelity, steadfastness, and reliability. The pesher interprets it as faithfulness to the Teacher of Righteousness; rabbinic tradition understands it as faithfulness to the Torah; and Paul reads it as trust in Christ (Romans 1:17, Galatians 3:11).

From a Jewish perspective, the pesher's interpretation of *emunah* as faithfulness to a specific teacher represents a narrowing of the biblical concept. The rabbis, by contrast, applied the verse more broadly: the Talmud (Makkot 24a) cites Rabbi Simlai's statement that Habakkuk established all the commandments on a single principle — faith — suggesting that *emunah* encompasses the entirety of Torah observance. This broader reading preserves the universal scope of the prophetic message while anchoring it in concrete practice.

The pesher's acknowledgment of the delayed end — the "final age" extending beyond expectation — resonates with a challenge that rabbinic Judaism also faced. The failure of messianic expectations associated with Bar Kokhba (135 CE) required a theological reckoning with unfulfilled prophecy. The rabbinic tradition resolved this tension through an emphasis on ongoing Torah study and observance, transforming eschatological expectation into sustained commitment to covenantal life in the present.
