## Overview

Jewish scholarship has engaged the Pesher Habakkuk primarily as evidence of the hermeneutical diversity of Second Temple Judaism. The pesher method, while unique in its specific claim to decode prophetic secrets, shares features with other Jewish interpretive traditions, including midrash, allegory, and typology. Studying the Pesher Habakkuk alongside rabbinic midrash illuminates both the shared assumptions and the sharp differences between sectarian and mainstream Jewish interpretation.

## Key Interpretive Emphases

From a Jewish perspective, the pesher method represents one extreme of a spectrum of interpretive approaches that characterized Second Temple Judaism. While the rabbis also read biblical texts as speaking to their own circumstances — the Talmud regularly interprets prophetic passages with reference to contemporary events — the pesher method's exclusive claim that the text refers only to the commentator's present is distinctive. Rabbinic midrash generally preserves the text's historical meaning alongside its contemporary application; pesher replaces the former with the latter.

The Pesher Habakkuk's treatment of Habakkuk 2:4 ("the righteous shall live by his faith") is particularly significant for Jewish-Christian dialogue, as this verse became central to both Pauline theology and rabbinic teaching. The pesher interprets "faith" as faithfulness to the Teacher of Righteousness, while the rabbis understood it as faithfulness to the Torah, and Paul read it as trust in Christ. The verse's interpretive history illustrates how the same scriptural words could generate radically different theological conclusions within Jewish and Christian traditions.
