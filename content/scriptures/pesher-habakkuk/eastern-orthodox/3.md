## The Righteous Shall Live by Faith

The pesher's interpretation of Habakkuk 2:4 provides Orthodox theology with an important pre-Christian Jewish context for understanding faith (*emunah/pistis*) as a living relationship rather than merely intellectual assent. The Orthodox understanding of faith, as articulated by the Fathers, encompasses trust, commitment, and ongoing participation in the life of God — a holistic concept that shares more with the pesher's understanding of faithfulness to the Teacher than with later Western debates about the relative roles of faith and works.

Orthodox theology understands "the righteous shall live by faith" through the lens of theosis: the righteous "live" not merely in the sense of physical or social survival but in the sense of participating in God's own life. The pesher's more restricted interpretation — faithfulness to the Teacher as the condition of eschatological salvation — represents a partial anticipation of this richer understanding, limited by its sectarian framework but genuine in its recognition that faith is a relational reality, not an individual achievement.

The chapter's treatment of the delayed end resonates with the Orthodox tradition's eschatological patience. The Orthodox Church has never set dates for the parousia, and its liturgical life is structured around the conviction that the Kingdom is both "already" present in the sacraments and "not yet" fully manifested. The pesher's assurance that "the final age will be prolonged beyond all that the prophets said" anticipates this eschatological stance, though Orthodox theology grounds its patience in the reality of Christ's resurrection rather than in faithfulness to a human teacher.
