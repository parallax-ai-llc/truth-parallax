## Overview

The Manusmriti (Laws of Manu), also known as the Manava-Dharmashastra, is the most authoritative and widely studied dharmashastra (legal-ethical treatise) in the Hindu tradition. Attributed to Manu, the mythical progenitor of humanity, the text was likely composed between the 2nd century BCE and 3rd century CE. Its 12 chapters and approximately 2,685 verses cover the entire range of dharmic life, including creation, the duties of the four social classes (varnas), the four stages of life (ashramas), civil and criminal law, penances, karma, and liberation.

## Structure

The 12 chapters address progressively broader dimensions of dharmic order:

| Chapters | Theme |
|----------|-------|
| 1 | Creation and the origin of dharma |
| 2-6 | The duties of the four varnas and four ashramas |
| 7 | The duties of kings and statecraft |
| 8 | Civil and criminal law |
| 9 | The duties of husbands and wives |
| 10 | Mixed castes and occupations |
| 11 | Penances and atonements |
| 12 | Karma, transmigration, and liberation |

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Manusmriti has been received differently across Hindu traditions:

- **Vaishnavism**: Respected as a dharmic authority within Vaishnava legal and ethical frameworks
- **Shaivism**: Engaged selectively, with some Shaiva traditions offering alternative dharmic frameworks
- **Advaita**: Read through the lens of higher and lower knowledge, with ultimate truth transcending social law
- **Dvaita**: Accepted as authoritative within the framework of divinely ordained social order
- **Academic**: Studied critically as a primary source for understanding ancient Indian law and society

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Dharma | Righteous conduct according to one's social position and stage of life |
| Varna | The four social classes and their respective duties |
| Ashrama | The four stages of life from student to renunciant |
| Raja-dharma | The duties and responsibilities of kings |
| Stri-dharma | The duties and rights of women |
| Prayaschitta | Penances for moral and ritual transgressions |
