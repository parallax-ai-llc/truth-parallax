Dvaita Vedanta's interpretation of this chapter on the duties of the householder (grihastha), including marriage rites, domestic sacrifices, and hospitality affirms the divine authority of the Manusmriti's prescriptions as expressions of God's eternal will for the ordering of human society. Madhvacharya's metaphysical framework, which posits real and eternal differences between God, souls, and matter, supports the Manusmriti's differentiation of duties according to the inherent nature (svabhava) of different categories of beings.

For Dvaita interpreters, observing the dharmic prescriptions outlined in this chapter is not merely a social obligation but a form of devotion to Vishnu, who is the ultimate author and guarantor of the dharmic order. The chapter's rules and regulations reflect the divine wisdom that assigns appropriate duties to each category of soul according to their inherent spiritual capacity, facilitating their gradual progress toward liberation through devotion and righteous conduct.
