Advaita Vedanta's reading of this chapter on the origins and duties of mixed castes, occupations in times of distress, and social adaptation operates through the fundamental distinction between conventional (vyavaharika) and ultimate (paramarthika) truth. The chapter's dharmic prescriptions are valid within the realm of conventional social life, guiding those who have not yet attained self-knowledge toward righteous conduct. However, the Advaita jnani who has realized the non-dual nature of Brahman transcends all social categories and prescriptions.

Shankara's commentary tradition treats the Manusmriti's rules as belonging to the sphere of pravritta dharma (the dharma of worldly engagement), which is superseded by nivritta dharma (the dharma of renunciation) for those who seek liberation through knowledge. The chapter's prescriptions thus serve a preparatory function, purifying the mind through righteous conduct and creating the conditions for the arising of discriminative knowledge (viveka) that leads to final liberation.
