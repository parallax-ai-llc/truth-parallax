## Purity Laws in Historical Perspective

Reform Judaism approaches Seder Tohorot as a historical system that governed access to sacred spaces and sacred food in the Temple period. The complex categories of tumah (ritual impurity) and taharah (ritual purity) are studied as reflections of ancient Israelite religious consciousness rather than as operative legal categories. The dissolution of the purity system following the Temple's destruction is understood as part of Judaism's ongoing evolution.

## Reinterpreting Purity

Reform thinkers have reinterpreted the concept of purity in ethical and spiritual terms. Rather than ritual states transmitted through physical contact, purity is understood as moral integrity, spiritual authenticity, and ethical consistency. The mikveh is embraced in some Reform communities as a meaningful ritual for conversion, healing, and life transitions — valued for its symbolic power rather than its halakhic necessity.

## The Niddah Question

Reform Judaism has departed significantly from traditional niddah observance, viewing the menstrual purity laws as reflecting ancient patriarchal assumptions about women's bodies that are incompatible with modern understandings of gender equality and bodily autonomy. Some Reform women have chosen to reclaim mikveh immersion on their own terms, transforming it into a feminist spiritual practice.
