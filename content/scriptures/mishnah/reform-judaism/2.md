## Sacred Time in Reform Practice

Reform Judaism selectively engages with Seder Moed, embracing the spiritual and ethical dimensions of Shabbat and festival observance while adapting specific practices to contemporary life. The core insight of Moed — that time itself can be sanctified through intentional practice — resonates deeply with Reform's emphasis on informed choice and meaningful observance.

## Shabbat as Gift, Not Burden

Reform interpretation of Tractate Shabbat's 39 prohibited labors emphasizes the positive purpose of Shabbat rest rather than the detailed restrictions. Shabbat is understood as a gift of liberation — from work, from commerce, from the relentless demands of productivity — that enables reflection, family connection, study, and worship. Individual Reform Jews are encouraged to develop personal Shabbat practices that bring genuine rest and renewal.

## The Festival Calendar as Ethical Narrative

Reform reading of the festival laws emphasizes their narrative and ethical content. Pesach teaches liberation from oppression, Shavuot celebrates revelation and learning, Sukkot cultivates gratitude and awareness of vulnerability, and the High Holy Days call for moral self-examination and renewal. The specific ritual details are valued as vehicles for these deeper meanings rather than as ends in themselves.
