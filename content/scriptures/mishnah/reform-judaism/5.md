## From Sacrifice to Prayer

Reform Judaism reads Seder Kodashim primarily as a historical document illuminating a phase of Israelite religion that has been superseded by the synagogue and prayer. The early Reform movement explicitly removed references to the restoration of animal sacrifice from its liturgy, understanding prayer, ethical action, and study as the enduring forms of divine service that replaced the Temple cult.

## Ethical Dimensions of Sacrifice

While not advocating for the restoration of sacrifices, Reform scholars find ethical meaning in the sacrificial system's underlying principles. The requirement to offer the best animals, the concept of atonement through acknowledged wrongdoing, and the communal dimension of festival sacrifices all express spiritual truths about devotion, repentance, and community that transcend their original ritual context.

## Animal Welfare

Reform engagement with Kodashim's laws of ritual slaughter (shechitah) connects to contemporary concerns about animal welfare. The rabbinic emphasis on minimizing animal suffering (tza'ar ba'alei chayyim) provides a religious foundation for Reform advocacy on humane treatment of animals, ethical food production, and environmental responsibility.
