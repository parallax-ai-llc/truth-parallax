## Family Law and Gender Equality

Reform Judaism's engagement with Seder Nashim is shaped by its foundational commitment to gender equality. While recognizing the historical significance of these laws, Reform has fundamentally reconceived Jewish family law to eliminate gender-based legal inequities. Women and men have equal rights in initiating and dissolving marriage, and the agunah (chained wife) problem is resolved by granting women full legal agency.

## The Evolving Institution of Marriage

Reform reading of Nashim traces the evolution of Jewish marriage law, noting how the sages themselves modified biblical provisions to better protect women's interests. The ketubah's financial protections, the restrictions on a husband's unilateral power to divorce, and Rabban Gamliel's takkanot (enactments) are read as evidence that Jewish law has always adapted to promote greater justice — a trajectory that Reform extends to its own egalitarian innovations.

## Ethical Principles in Family Relations

Beyond specific legal provisions, Reform draws from Nashim enduring ethical principles: the sanctity of commitment, the importance of consent, the obligation to support one's family, and the necessity of just processes for dissolving relationships. These principles continue to guide Reform rabbis in counseling and in developing contemporary lifecycle rituals.
