## Overview

Reform Judaism approaches the Mishnah as a historically significant document that reflects the creative genius of the rabbinic sages in adapting Jewish law to the post-Temple era. While Reform does not regard the Mishnah as divinely revealed Oral Torah in the Orthodox sense, it deeply values the text as evidence of Judaism's capacity for renewal and the ongoing development of religious thought.

## Interpretive Approach

Reform scholars study the Mishnah using historical-critical methods, examining how its legal rulings reflect the social, economic, and political conditions of Roman Palestine. The Mishnah's internal debates are understood as evidence of a dynamic, pluralistic legal tradition that was comfortable with disagreement and open to change — values that resonate with Reform's own approach to Jewish law and practice.

## Theological Emphases

Reform reading highlights the Mishnah's implicit principle that law must serve human welfare. The flexibility and adaptability demonstrated by the Tannaim — their willingness to reinterpret biblical law in light of changed circumstances — is seen as a precedent for Reform Judaism's own project of ongoing religious reform. The Mishnah demonstrates that tradition and innovation are not opposites but partners in authentic Jewish life.
