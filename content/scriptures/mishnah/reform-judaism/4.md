## Justice and Social Ethics

Reform Judaism finds in Seder Nezikin a rich treasury of principles for social justice. The tort law of Bava Kamma, the labor and property law of Bava Metzia and Bava Batra, and the procedural safeguards of Sanhedrin all express the conviction that a just society requires robust legal protections for the vulnerable and fair mechanisms for resolving disputes.

## The Rights of Workers and the Poor

Reform interpretation highlights Nezikin's protections for laborers, tenants, and the economically disadvantaged. The requirement to pay workers on time, the regulations on fair commerce, the prohibitions against exploitation — these laws articulate a vision of economic justice that Reform connects to its contemporary social action agenda, supporting workers' rights, fair wages, and economic equity.

## Criminal Justice and Human Dignity

The Sanhedrin's elaborate procedural protections for the accused — the requirement of two witnesses, the prohibition of self-incrimination, the preference for acquittal in cases of doubt — embody principles that anticipate modern human rights standards. Reform Judaism invokes these precedents in its advocacy for criminal justice reform, opposing capital punishment and supporting due process protections.
