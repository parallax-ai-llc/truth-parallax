## Overview

The Mishnah is the foundational text of rabbinic Judaism, compiled and redacted by Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi (Judah the Prince) around 200 CE. It represents the first major written collection of the Jewish Oral Torah (Torah she-be'al peh), which according to rabbinic tradition was revealed to Moses at Sinai alongside the Written Torah and transmitted orally through the generations.

## Structure

The Mishnah is organized into six major divisions called Sedarim (Orders), each addressing a broad area of Jewish law:

1. **Zeraim (Seeds)** — 14 tractates on agricultural laws, blessings, and tithes
2. **Moed (Festivals)** — 18 tractates on Shabbat, holidays, and the sacred calendar
3. **Nashim (Women)** — 16 tractates on marriage, divorce, and vows
4. **Nezikin (Damages)** — 20 tractates on civil and criminal law, courts, and ethics
5. **Kodashim (Holy Things)** — 14 tractates on Temple sacrifices, ritual slaughter, and consecrated items
6. **Tohorot (Purities)** — 16 tractates on ritual purity and impurity

## Cross-Traditional Significance

The Mishnah is the shared bedrock of all subsequent rabbinic literature:

- **Orthodox Judaism**: Regarded as divinely authoritative Oral Torah, the basis of the Talmud and halakhic practice
- **Reform Judaism**: Valued as a historical document reflecting the evolution of Jewish thought and practice
- **Conservative Judaism**: Treated as authoritative law that is also subject to historical-critical analysis
- **Academic**: The primary text for studying the development of rabbinic legal reasoning and social history
- **Comparative**: Its legal structures invite comparison with Roman law, Islamic jurisprudence, and other legal traditions

## Key Themes

| Theme | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Oral Torah | The belief that an oral tradition accompanies and interprets the Written Torah |
| Halakhah | The comprehensive legal framework governing all aspects of Jewish life |
| Machloket (Dispute) | The preservation of minority opinions alongside majority rulings |
| Kedushah (Holiness) | The sanctification of everyday life through law and practice |
| Tikkun (Repair) | Legal mechanisms for maintaining social order and justice |
