## Sacrifice Across Religions

Seder Kodashim's sacrificial system finds parallels in virtually every ancient religious tradition. The Vedic yajna (fire sacrifice), the Greek thysia, the Roman sacrificium, the Aztec offering rituals, and the Chinese jisi (sacrificial rites) all involved the ritual presentation of food, animals, or other offerings to the divine. Comparative study reveals common structural elements: consecration, slaughter, offering, and communal consumption.

## The End of Sacrifice

The Jewish transition from sacrifice to prayer following the Temple's destruction parallels similar transitions in other traditions. The Hindu Bhakti movement de-emphasized Vedic fire sacrifice in favor of devotional worship; Buddhism rejected animal sacrifice from its inception; and Christianity reinterpreted the sacrificial system through the theology of Christ's self-offering. Each tradition negotiated the relationship between older sacrificial practice and newer forms of worship.

## Sacred Space and Architecture

Tractate Middot's detailed description of the Temple layout parallels architectural prescriptions in other traditions: the Hindu vastu shastra and temple agamas, the Islamic prescriptions for mosque design, the Christian tradition of church architecture oriented toward the east, and the Buddhist principles governing stupa and temple construction. Each tradition encodes theological meaning in sacred architecture.
