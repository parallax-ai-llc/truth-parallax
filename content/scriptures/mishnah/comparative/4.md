## Comparative Legal Systems

Seder Nezikin's comprehensive civil and criminal code invites comparison with other ancient legal systems. Roman law (the Corpus Juris Civilis), Islamic fiqh (particularly the treatment of torts and contracts), Hindu dharmashastra (Manu's treatment of vyavahara, or legal procedure), and Chinese Confucian-Legalist codes all developed sophisticated systems for adjudicating disputes, assigning liability, and maintaining social order.

## Tort Law Principles

The Mishnah's four primary categories of damage (shor, bor, mav'eh, hev'er — ox, pit, grazing animal, fire) parallel the Roman law categories of delict and the Islamic law of daman (liability). Each system develops principles of causation, foreseeability, and responsibility that, while differing in detail, reveal common human concerns about fair allocation of liability for harm caused by persons, animals, and property.

## Court Procedure and Justice

The procedural safeguards described in Sanhedrin — careful deliberation, witness examination, protection of the accused — parallel developments in Roman criminal procedure, Islamic qada (judicial process), and the common law tradition of due process. The cross-cultural development of procedural protections suggests a convergent recognition that justice requires not only correct outcomes but fair processes.
