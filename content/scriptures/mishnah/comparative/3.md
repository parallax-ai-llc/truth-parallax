## Religious Family Law Systems

Seder Nashim's comprehensive regulation of marriage and divorce parallels family law systems in other religious traditions. Islamic nikah (marriage contract) and talaq (divorce), Hindu vivah (marriage) governed by dharmashastra, and Christian canon law on matrimony all develop detailed legal frameworks for family formation and dissolution rooted in sacred authority. Each system negotiates the relationship between individual autonomy and communal religious norms.

## Marriage Contracts Across Cultures

The Jewish ketubah finds parallels in the Islamic mahr (bride-gift specified in the marriage contract), the Hindu stridhan (bride's wealth), and the Roman dos (dowry). Each institution seeks to protect the economic interests of the wife within the marriage and in the event of dissolution, revealing a cross-cultural recognition that marriage involves economic as well as personal and religious dimensions.

## Levirate Marriage in Comparative Context

The Mishnah's treatment of levirate marriage (yibbum) — the obligation of a man to marry his deceased brother's childless widow — has parallels in ancient Near Eastern law (the Hittite laws, Assyrian law codes) and in some African customary legal systems. This institution reflects a widespread concern for the continuity of family lineage and the protection of widows in patrilineal societies.
