## Agricultural Law Across Traditions

Seder Zeraim's agricultural legislation finds parallels in other religious legal systems that sanctify the relationship between humans and the land. Hindu dharmashastra includes detailed prescriptions for agricultural practice and land use; Islamic law governs zakat on agricultural produce; and medieval Christian canon law addressed tithes and the obligations of landowners to the poor. Each tradition embeds economic justice within a theological framework of divine ownership of the earth.

## The Blessing Tradition

Tractate Berakhot's system of blessings — acknowledging God before eating, upon witnessing natural phenomena, and at set times of prayer — parallels the Islamic system of daily prayer (salat) and du'a (supplication), the Christian grace before meals and the Liturgy of the Hours, and the Hindu sandhyavandana (daily prayers at the junctions of day). Each tradition structures awareness of the sacred through regularized verbal acknowledgment.

## Charity and the Land

The Zeraim provisions for the poor — pe'ah, leket, ma'aser ani — parallel the Islamic zakat system, the Sikh dasvandh (giving a tenth), and Christian tithing traditions. These cross-traditional parallels reveal a widely shared conviction that agricultural abundance carries social obligations and that the earth's productivity must benefit the entire community, not merely the landowner.
