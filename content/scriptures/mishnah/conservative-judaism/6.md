## Purity in Conservative Practice

Conservative Judaism maintains certain purity practices while acknowledging that the full system of tumah and taharah is inoperative without the Temple. The laws of niddah (family purity) are upheld by the movement's standards, though observance varies among Conservative families. The mikveh is required for conversion and encouraged for other life transitions.

## Historical Analysis of Purity

Conservative scholars have contributed significant academic work on the development of purity concepts in ancient Judaism. The relationship between biblical purity laws and their Mishnaic elaboration, the influence of Second Temple sectarian disputes about purity (particularly with the Sadducees and the Dead Sea Scrolls community), and the social implications of purity boundaries are all areas of active Conservative scholarship.

## Contemporary Meaning

Conservative Judaism seeks to preserve the meaningful dimensions of the purity system — its attention to the body, its marking of life transitions, its creation of sacred boundaries — while honestly acknowledging the aspects that reflect superseded worldviews. The mikveh, in particular, has been reclaimed as a powerful ritual resource for marking conversion, healing, and other significant life moments.
