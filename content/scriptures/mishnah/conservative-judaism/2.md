## Festival Observance in Conservative Life

Conservative Judaism maintains the binding character of Shabbat and festival observance while permitting certain adaptations to modern circumstances. The Committee on Jewish Law and Standards has issued landmark teshuvot (legal opinions) on topics such as driving to synagogue on Shabbat and the use of electricity, demonstrating its commitment to halakhic process while responding to the realities of contemporary Jewish life.

## The Second Day of Festivals

A distinctive Conservative halakhic discussion concerns the second day of festivals observed in the Diaspora (yom tov sheni shel galuyot). Conservative authorities have generally maintained this observance while acknowledging its historical origins in calendrical uncertainty that no longer applies, exemplifying the movement's characteristic respect for established practice combined with honest historical analysis.

## Shabbat as Covenantal Sign

Conservative interpretation reads the Shabbat laws of Moed as expressing the covenantal relationship between God and Israel. Shabbat observance is not merely a matter of individual spiritual practice but a communal act of witness — testimony to God's creation of the world and Israel's liberation from Egypt. This communal dimension explains the movement's emphasis on synagogue attendance and shared Shabbat practice.
