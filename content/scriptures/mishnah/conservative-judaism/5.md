## The Temple and Its Legacy

Conservative Judaism studies Seder Kodashim as a record of the Temple service that shaped Israelite religion for centuries. While the Conservative liturgy includes references to the ancient sacrificial service, the movement's prayer books generally express hope for a renewed Temple without specifying the restoration of animal sacrifice, leaving the nature of future worship open to interpretation.

## Kashrut and Ritual Slaughter

The laws of shechitah (ritual slaughter) found in Tractate Chullin remain practically significant for Conservative Judaism, which maintains the observance of kashrut as a binding halakhic obligation. The Conservative movement has also engaged with contemporary ethical questions about industrial animal agriculture, with some authorities arguing that the principle of tza'ar ba'alei chayyim (preventing animal suffering) may render some industrially produced meat halakhically problematic.

## Sacred Service and Daily Life

Conservative interpretation draws from Kodashim the principle that daily acts of eating, working, and caring for the body can be invested with sanctity. The Temple's meticulous attention to the details of sacred service is translated into mindfulness about the details of daily Jewish practice — an approach that bridges the gap between the ancient Temple and the contemporary Jewish home and synagogue.
