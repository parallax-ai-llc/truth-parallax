## Overview

Kabbalistic tradition reads the Mishnah not merely as a legal code but as a repository of hidden spiritual truths encoded within its halakhic structures. The very word "Mishnah" is an anagram of "neshamah" (soul), suggesting that the legal text contains the soul-dimension of Torah. The six orders of the Mishnah are mapped onto the six lower sefirot (from chesed to yesod), revealing a cosmic architecture underlying the legal categories.

## Interpretive Approach

The Zohar and later kabbalistic literature frequently interpret Mishnaic passages as allegories for sefirotic processes. Rabbi Isaac Luria (the Ari) taught that proper study of each halakhah effects a tikkun (rectification) in the spiritual worlds. The Hasidic master Rabbi Nachman of Breslov emphasized that even the most technical legal discussions contain sparks of divine light waiting to be elevated through devoted study and practice.

## Theological Emphases

For the kabbalist, every detail of Mishnaic law — the measures, the categories, the distinctions — corresponds to spiritual realities in the upper worlds. Agricultural laws reflect the flow of divine sustenance; purity laws map the dynamics of holiness and its withdrawal; festival laws encode the rhythms of cosmic time. The student who penetrates beyond the legal surface discovers that the Mishnah is a manual for the soul's journey toward reunion with the divine.
