## Sacred Time and the Sefirot

Kabbalistic interpretation maps the festivals of Seder Moed onto the sefirotic tree. Shabbat corresponds to malkhut (the feminine, receptive dimension of the divine), the three pilgrimage festivals to the three patriarchal sefirot (chesed-Abraham-Pesach, gevurah-Isaac-Shavuot, tiferet-Jacob-Sukkot), and the High Holy Days to the upper sefirot of binah (Rosh Hashanah, the divine mother giving birth to the new year) and keter (Yom Kippur, the day of ultimate transcendence).

## Shabbat and the Supernal Bride

The Zohar's celebrated teaching that Shabbat is the bride (kallah) whom the community of Israel welcomes each Friday evening reveals the mystical depth of the Moed laws. Shabbat observance effects a unification (yichud) between the Holy One, Blessed Be He (Tiferet) and the Shekhinah (Malkhut), healing the fundamental rift in the divine caused by exile and sin. Every Shabbat ritual becomes a theurgic act of cosmic repair.

## The Lulav and the Sefirot

The four species taken on Sukkot — etrog, lulav, hadassim, aravot — are understood by the kabbalists as symbols of the four letters of the divine name YHVH and their corresponding sefirot. Waving the lulav in six directions effects a unification of the six sefirot from chesed to yesod, drawing divine blessing into the world. The sukah itself represents the surrounding light (ohr makif) of the sefirah of binah.
