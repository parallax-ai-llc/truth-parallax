## Gender and Legal Personhood

Academic study of Seder Nashim examines the legal construction of gender in rabbinic law. Scholars analyze how the Mishnah defines women's legal capacities and limitations, constructing a system in which women are both protected and constrained. Feminist scholarship has been particularly productive in analyzing the assumptions about gender, sexuality, and power embedded in these legal texts.

## Marriage and Roman Law

Comparative legal scholars have examined the parallels and divergences between Mishnaic marriage law and contemporaneous Roman marriage practices. Both systems underwent significant changes during the first and second centuries CE, and scholars debate whether rabbinic innovations (such as the ketubah) were influenced by Roman legal developments or represent independent evolution.

## The Sotah Ritual and Its Abolition

The Mishnah's treatment of the sotah (suspected adulteress) ritual is particularly revealing for academic analysis. The text records both the procedure and its abolition by Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, providing evidence for how the rabbis managed the transition from biblical institutions to post-Temple practice. The sotah case illustrates the rabbinic capacity for effectively nullifying biblical law while maintaining theoretical fidelity to the text.
