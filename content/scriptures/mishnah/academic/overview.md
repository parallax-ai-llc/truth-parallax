## Overview

Academic study of the Mishnah treats it as a literary and legal document produced in Roman Palestine around 200 CE. Scholars investigate its compositional history, editorial principles, language, relationship to other rabbinic texts, and the social world of its creators. The Mishnah is studied as evidence for the intellectual, religious, and social history of Jews in the late Second Temple and early rabbinic periods.

## Interpretive Approach

Scholars such as Jacob Neusner, Shamma Friedman, and Judith Hauptman have applied form criticism, redaction criticism, and literary analysis to the Mishnah. Key questions include the relationship between the Mishnah and pre-70 CE Pharisaic practice, the editorial principles guiding Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi's compilation, and the degree to which Mishnaic law reflects actual practice versus idealized legal theory.

## Key Scholarly Questions

Academic research examines the Mishnah's relationship to the Tosefta, the halakhic midrashim, and the two Talmuds. Manuscript studies reveal significant textual variants, suggesting the Mishnah text remained somewhat fluid for centuries after its initial redaction. Comparative legal studies place the Mishnah alongside Roman legal codes, examining parallels in legal categories, methods of argumentation, and institutional structures.
