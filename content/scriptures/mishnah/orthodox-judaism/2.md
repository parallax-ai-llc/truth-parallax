## The Order of Festivals

Seder Moed (Order of Festivals) contains 18 tractates governing the observance of Shabbat, the three pilgrimage festivals (Pesach, Shavuot, Sukkot), the High Holy Days (Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur), minor festivals (Purim, Chanukah-related laws), fast days, and the intermediate days of festivals (Chol HaMoed). Orthodox Judaism treats every detail of these laws as binding divine instruction transmitted through the Oral Torah.

## Shabbat as Foundation

Tractate Shabbat, the largest in this order, enumerates 39 categories of creative labor (melakhot) prohibited on the Sabbath, derived from the types of work used in constructing the Tabernacle. Orthodox interpretation understands these categories not as arbitrary restrictions but as a comprehensive framework for ceasing from creative mastery over the physical world, thereby acknowledging God as the ultimate Creator.

## The Festival Cycle

The festival laws of Moed encode a theological vision of time as sacred. Each holiday commemorates divine acts in history (the Exodus, the giving of Torah, the wilderness sojourn) while simultaneously connecting the Jewish people to the agricultural cycle of the Land of Israel. Orthodox observance maintains this dual historical-agricultural significance, seeing in the festival calendar a divinely ordained rhythm of remembrance, gratitude, and renewal.
