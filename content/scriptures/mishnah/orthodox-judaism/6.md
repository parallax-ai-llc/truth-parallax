## The Order of Purities

Seder Tohorot (Order of Purities) is the largest order by volume, with 16 tractates addressing the complex laws of ritual purity and impurity (tumah and taharah). Key tractates include Kelim (vessels subject to impurity), Oholot (impurity transmitted through overshadowing), Negaim (leprosy-like skin afflictions), Parah (the red heifer), Mikva'ot (ritual baths), and Niddah (menstrual impurity). These laws governed access to the Temple and sacred food.

## Tumah and Taharah as Spiritual Categories

Orthodox interpretation understands tumah (ritual impurity) not as physical uncleanliness but as a spiritual condition related to the encounter with mortality and the withdrawal of holiness. Sources of tumah — contact with death, certain bodily emissions, skin afflictions — all involve the boundary between life and death. The purification process, typically involving water (mikveh) and time, represents spiritual renewal and the restoration of connection to holiness.

## Contemporary Practice

Although most purity laws are suspended without the Temple, the laws of niddah (family purity) remain fully operative in Orthodox life. Monthly immersion in the mikveh following menstruation is considered a cornerstone of Jewish marriage. Additionally, the laws of impurity from a human corpse (tumat met) affect kohanim, who are prohibited from entering cemeteries or coming into contact with the dead.
