## Overview

For Orthodox Judaism, the Mishnah is the written record of the Oral Torah (Torah she-be'al peh) that God revealed to Moses at Sinai alongside the Written Torah. Its authority is thus divine, not merely historical or human. Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi's compilation around 200 CE is understood as the necessary preservation of teachings that had been faithfully transmitted orally for over a millennium, committed to writing only when the exigencies of exile and persecution threatened the chain of transmission.

## Interpretive Approach

Orthodox study of the Mishnah centers on the Talmud (both Babylonian and Jerusalem), which serves as the authoritative commentary on and elaboration of Mishnaic law. The Bartenura and Tosafot Yom Tov are the standard commentaries printed alongside the Mishnah text. Every legal ruling in the Mishnah is treated as binding unless modified by subsequent Talmudic discussion, and the entire system is understood as an integrated, divinely guided legal corpus.

## Theological Emphases

Orthodox interpretation insists on the unity of Written and Oral Torah. The Mishnah is not a human creation reflecting its historical moment but a faithful record of divine teaching. Disputes between sages are understood as legitimate differences in the reception and application of authentic tradition, not as evidence of human invention. The principle of *eilu v'eilu* — "these and these are the words of the living God" — applies to the preserved disagreements within the Mishnah.
