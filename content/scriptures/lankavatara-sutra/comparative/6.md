The teaching on the non-duality of samsara and nirvana challenges the conventional religious assumption that the sacred and the profane are fundamentally separate realms. This challenge resonates with mystical traditions across religions that discover the divine within the ordinary.

The Jewish Hasidic teaching on avodah be-gashmiyut (worship through corporeality) teaches that the divine can be served through all physical activities, including eating, drinking, and sexual relations, when performed with proper intention. This parallels the Lankavatara's teaching that nirvana is found within samsara rather than by escaping from it.

The Christian incarnational theology, which holds that God became fully human in Jesus Christ, sanctifying the material world, provides another parallel. If the divine has entered fully into the material world, then the material world is already the site of the sacred. The Lankavatara's non-duality of samsara and nirvana makes a similar claim from a Buddhist philosophical framework: the very world of suffering is, when rightly perceived, the realm of liberation.

The Daoist concept of the unity of heaven and earth, where the sage moves effortlessly between the transcendent and the mundane, offers yet another resonance. Zhuangzi's sage, who finds the Dao in every creature and every situation, embodies the non-dual awareness that the Lankavatara describes in philosophical terms.
