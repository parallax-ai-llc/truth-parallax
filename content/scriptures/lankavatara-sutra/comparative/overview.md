## Overview

The Lankavatara Sutra's mind-only philosophy and its teaching on an inherent awakened nature within all beings invite rich comparative analysis with philosophical idealism, mystical traditions of inner divinity, and ethical traditions of compassion toward all living creatures.

## Cross-Traditional Parallels

The sutra's teaching that all experienced reality is a projection of consciousness parallels Western philosophical idealism from Berkeley to Kant, the Hindu Advaita Vedanta doctrine that the world is maya (illusion) projected by Brahman, and the Sufi understanding that creation is a mirror of divine self-reflection. Each tradition wrestles with the relationship between mind and external reality.

## Ethical Dimensions

The Lankavatara's chapter on meat-eating, which argues for vegetarianism on grounds of universal compassion and the interconnection of all beings through rebirth, resonates with ethical traditions across religions: the Jain principle of ahimsa, Hindu vegetarian traditions, and contemporary philosophical arguments for animal rights rooted in sentience and suffering.
