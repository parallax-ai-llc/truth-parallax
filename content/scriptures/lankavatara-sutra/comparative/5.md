The ocean metaphor for consciousness finds resonances across contemplative traditions. The Sufi poet Rumi wrote extensively about the relationship between the drop and the ocean, using this image to describe the soul's relationship to God: "You are not a drop in the ocean. You are the entire ocean in a drop." This reversal of the ordinary perspective mirrors the Lankavatara's teaching that individual consciousness, properly understood, is not separate from the vast ocean of awareness.

The Hindu Upanishadic teaching "Tat tvam asi" (You are That) expresses a similar insight through different imagery. The individual self (atman) is identical with the universal reality (Brahman), just as the Lankavatara teaches that the individual alaya-vijnana, when purified, reveals itself as the tathagatagarbha. Both traditions teach that the barrier between individual and universal is illusory.

The Christian mystical tradition of John of the Cross describes the soul's immersion in God as "a living flame of love," using the image of fire consuming a log to describe how the individual self is transformed into the divine. The Lankavatara's teaching on the transformation of consciousness (paravrtti) employs a different metaphor but describes a structurally similar process: the dissolution of the boundary between individual consciousness and ultimate reality.
