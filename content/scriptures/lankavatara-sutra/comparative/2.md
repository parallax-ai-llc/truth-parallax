The Lankavatara's teaching that all experienced phenomena are projections of consciousness invites comparison with philosophical idealism across traditions. Bishop Berkeley's immaterialism, which holds that "to be is to be perceived" (esse est percipi), shares the Yogacara conviction that objects of experience do not exist independently of the consciousness that perceives them.

The Hindu Advaita Vedanta tradition, particularly as articulated by Shankara, teaches that the world of multiplicity is maya (illusion) superimposed by ignorance (avidya) on the one Brahman. While the metaphysical frameworks differ, the basic insight is structurally similar: what appears as an external world is actually the product of consciousness misunderstanding its own nature.

The Western philosophical tradition has grappled with the same questions through the mind-body problem and the debate between realism and idealism. Kant's transcendental idealism, which holds that the mind structures experience through categories that it brings to sensation, provides another parallel. Like the Lankavatara, Kant argues that we never experience things-in-themselves but only things as they appear through the mind's organizing activity.
