The chapter on change connects with the universal human encounter with impermanence that has shaped philosophical and religious thought across traditions. The Greek philosopher Heraclitus taught that "everything flows" (panta rhei) and that one cannot step into the same river twice. This radical impermanence philosophy shares common ground with the Buddhist teaching on anicca that pervades the Lankavatara.

The Jewish tradition addresses impermanence through the concept of hevel (breath, vapor, or vanity) that appears throughout Ecclesiastes: "Vanity of vanities, all is vanity." The Preacher's awareness that all human endeavors are ultimately transient parallels the Buddhist awareness of impermanence, though the Jewish tradition's response, to "fear God and keep his commandments," differs from the Buddhist path of consciousness transformation.

The transformative response to impermanence described in the Lankavatara, the paravrtti or turning-about of consciousness, can be compared with the Christian concept of metanoia and the Islamic concept of tawbah, both of which describe a fundamental reorientation of the person in response to the recognition of the unsatisfactoriness of ordinary existence. Each tradition recognizes that awareness of impermanence and suffering can become the catalyst for profound spiritual transformation.
