The Lankavatara's arguments for vegetarianism based on compassion and the interconnection of all beings through rebirth can be compared with ethical arguments for vegetarianism across religious traditions. The Jain tradition's ahimsa (non-violence) toward all living beings represents the most comprehensive traditional argument for vegetarianism, extending concern to insects, microorganisms, and even plant life in its most rigorous forms.

The Hindu tradition includes both strong arguments for vegetarianism, particularly in the Vaishnavite bhakti tradition, and the acceptance of meat-eating in other contexts, such as the Vedic sacrificial tradition. The Laws of Manu present a nuanced position that recognizes the karmic consequences of meat-eating while acknowledging its prevalence. This ambivalence parallels the Buddhist tradition's own internal debate about the ethics of meat consumption.

Contemporary secular ethical arguments for vegetarianism, particularly those based on animal rights (Peter Singer) and environmental sustainability, echo the Lankavatara's ancient arguments in modern philosophical language. The sutra's teaching that meat-eating generates suffering and impedes spiritual development finds contemporary expression in the growing scientific evidence linking meat consumption to environmental degradation, animal suffering, and human health concerns.
