The use of poetry to convey spiritual teaching in the Sagathakam connects with a universal tradition of sacred verse across religions. The Psalms of the Hebrew Bible, the Quran's rhythmic prose, the Bhagavad Gita's Sanskrit verses, and the hymns of Rumi and Kabir all employ poetic form to communicate spiritual truths that prose alone cannot convey.

The condensation of complex teaching into memorable verses serves a function found across literate and oral religious traditions. The Jewish tradition of the mishna (from the root meaning "to repeat") preserves the oral law in memorizable form. The Islamic tradition values the hifz (memorization) of the Quran. The Hindu tradition of sutra (literally "thread") presents teachings in condensed, aphoristic form designed for memorization and subsequent commentary.

The Sagathakam's concluding position in the Lankavatara parallels the placement of hymns and verses at the conclusion of many religious texts and liturgies. The Christian doxology, the Islamic du'a at the close of prayer, and the Hindu closing mantras all function to seal the teaching in the practitioner's consciousness, ensuring that the essential message is carried forward beyond the moment of reading or hearing. The Lankavatara's final verses serve this same function, distilling the sutra's vast philosophical vision into lines that can be held in the heart and contemplated throughout a lifetime of practice.
