The opening of the Lankavatara Sutra, with its setting on a mythological island and the presence of a demon king seeking wisdom, resonates with narrative patterns found across religious traditions. The motif of the supernatural being who seeks spiritual teaching appears in the Hebrew Bible's account of the Witch of Endor, in the Islamic tradition of Solomon commanding the jinn, and in the Hindu Puranas where demons (asuras) sometimes receive divine teaching.

The transformation of Ravana from the villain of the Ramayana into a sincere seeker of the dharma illustrates the Buddhist principle that spiritual capacity is not determined by one's cosmic status. This parallels the Christian teaching that God's grace can reach anyone, including those conventionally regarded as enemies of the divine, as illustrated by the conversion of Saul of Tarsus into the apostle Paul.

The setting on Lanka, an island associated with both danger and hidden treasure, resonates with the archetype of the sacred island found across cultures: Avalon in Arthurian legend, the Blessed Isles of Greek mythology, and the paradisiacal islands of Celtic tradition. Each represents a liminal space where ordinary rules are suspended and deeper truths can be encountered.
