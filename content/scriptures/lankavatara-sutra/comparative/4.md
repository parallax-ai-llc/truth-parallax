The three natures doctrine invites comparison with other traditions' frameworks for understanding the relationship between appearance and reality. Plato's allegory of the cave presents three levels of reality: the shadows on the wall (comparable to the imagined nature), the objects casting the shadows (comparable to the dependent nature), and the sun that illuminates everything (comparable to the perfected nature).

The Sufi distinction between shari'a (outer law), tariqa (the inner path), and haqiqa (ultimate reality) provides a structural parallel from the Islamic mystical tradition. Each level of understanding reveals a deeper truth about the nature of existence, and the relationship between the levels mirrors the Lankavatara's teaching that the perfected nature is not something other than the dependent nature but the dependent nature correctly perceived.

The Christian mystical tradition of Meister Eckhart distinguishes between God (the personal deity of religious devotion), the Godhead (the transpersonal ground of being), and creatures (the manifest world). Like the three natures, these three levels describe the same reality seen from different perspectives, with the highest perspective revealing what was always true but hidden by the limitations of ordinary perception.
