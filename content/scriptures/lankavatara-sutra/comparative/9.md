The dharani chapter's presentation of sacred syllables with protective power resonates with the cross-traditional practice of using sacred speech for spiritual protection and transformation. The Jewish tradition of sacred names of God, particularly the Tetragrammaton (YHWH) and the names used in Kabbalistic practice, assigns transformative power to specific combinations of letters and sounds.

The Islamic practice of dhikr (remembrance of God) through the repetition of sacred phrases such as "La ilaha illa'llah" (There is no god but God) shares the dharani's principle that concentrated sacred speech can transform consciousness. Both practices employ repetition to focus the mind and create conditions for direct spiritual experience beyond conceptual thinking.

The Christian tradition of sacred formulas, from the Jesus Prayer ("Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner") in Eastern Orthodox hesychasm to the repetition of the Rosary in Catholic devotion, provides another parallel. Each tradition has developed practices of concentrated sacred speech that are understood to have effects beyond their literal meaning, transforming the practitioner through the very act of sustained repetition.
