The tathagatagarbha teaching that all beings possess an inherent seed of awakening parallels the concept of divine indwelling found across mystical traditions. The Christian teaching that the soul is made in the "image of God" (imago Dei), the Quaker concept of "that of God in every person," and the Hindu teaching on the atman as identical with Brahman all affirm that the divine or the absolute is present within every being.

The Sufi concept of the nafs (self) and its relationship to the divine reality (haqiqa) provides another parallel. The Sufi path involves the progressive purification of the nafs from its lowest state (nafs al-ammara, the commanding self) to its highest state (nafs al-mutma'inna, the contented self), which recognizes its identity with the divine. This mirrors the Lankavatara's teaching on the purification of the alaya-vijnana to reveal the tathagatagarbha.

The Kabbalistic concept of the divine sparks (nitzotzot) scattered throughout creation and waiting to be redeemed through human consciousness and action provides yet another resonance. In both the Buddhist and Kabbalistic frameworks, the task of the spiritual practitioner is to recognize and liberate the divine potential that is hidden within the ordinary.
