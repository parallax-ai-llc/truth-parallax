---
title: "Lankavatara Sutra - Chapter 2: Collection of All the Dharmas"
scripture: "lankavatara-sutra"
chapter: 2
---

**1** Then the Bodhisattva Mahamati asked the Blessed One: "Lord, pray tell us about the five dharmas, the three self-natures, the eight consciousnesses, and the two kinds of selflessness, which form the substance of all the inner realization of the Tathagatas."

**2** The Blessed One said: "Mahamati, the five dharmas are: name, appearance, discrimination, right knowledge, and suchness. All things of this world are reducible to these five."

**3** "Name and appearance are with the ignorant the source of discriminating. When appearances are seen and discriminated, names are attached to them, and from names and appearances discrimination arises. This is the way of the ignorant."

**4** "Right knowledge is the knowledge that names, appearances, and discriminations are empty of self-nature. Suchness is the reality that is perceived by the wise when discrimination ceases."

**5** "Mahamati, what are the three self-natures? They are the imagined, the dependent, and the absolute. The imagined self-nature is that which is attached to by the ignorant as real because of their habit of discrimination."

**6** "The dependent self-nature is that which arises from conditions. All things that arise from conditions have no self-nature of their own, just as fire arises from the combination of fuel and air."

**7** "The absolute self-nature is the state of reality as perceived by noble wisdom—free from name, appearance, and discrimination. It is the suchness of things."

**8** "Mahamati, what are the eight consciousnesses? They are: the five sense-consciousnesses—eye, ear, nose, tongue, and body—the mind-consciousness, the manas or discriminating consciousness, and the alaya-vijnana or store-consciousness."

**9** "The alaya-vijnana is like a great ocean. The waves on its surface are the seven active consciousnesses. The ocean itself is the storehouse of all habit-energy, all seeds of past experience. Just as waves arise on the ocean surface while the depths remain undisturbed, so the evolving consciousnesses arise from the alaya."

**10** "When the alaya-vijnana is understood as it truly is—empty of self and empty of the duality of subject and object—then there is a turning-about at the deepest seat of consciousness. This is the inner realization of noble wisdom. This is the state of self-realization in which all dualistic thinking ceases."
