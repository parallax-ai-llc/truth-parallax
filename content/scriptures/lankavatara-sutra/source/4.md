---
title: "Lankavatara Sutra - Chapter 4: On the Nature of Mind"
scripture: "lankavatara-sutra"
chapter: 4
---

**1** Mahamati asked: "Lord, what is the nature of mind? How does mind give rise to the world of appearance?"

**2** The Blessed One said: "Mahamati, the triple world is nothing but mind. What appears as the external world is merely the mind's own manifestation. When this is realized, there is no external world to be grasped at, and no internal self to grasp."

**3** "The mind, as it functions in the everyday experience of the ignorant, is known by various names: mind, thought, consciousness, discrimination, the storehouse, manas, manovijnana, and so forth."

**4** "These are all different names for the workings of one and the same mind. Yet the ignorant, not understanding this, become attached to the names as if each designated a separate reality."

**5** "Mahamati, mind in its pure nature is like a clear mirror. When the mirror is covered with dust, images do not appear clearly. When the dust is wiped away, images are reflected perfectly. So the mind, when covered with defilements, does not reflect reality. When defilements are removed, reality is reflected as it is."

**6** "But even this simile is imperfect, for there is no mirror and there are no images. There is only mind, which in its true nature is free from all images, all reflections, all dualities."

**7** "The mind-only teaching does not mean that there is a substantial mind existing somewhere. It means that what we take to be the external world is nothing other than the activity of mind. When this activity ceases, what remains is the unconditioned—Nirvana."

**8** "Mahamati, the philosophers fall into two extreme views: some assert that everything exists, and some assert that nothing exists. But the Middle Way avoids both extremes."

**9** "Things are neither existent nor non-existent. They are like the horns of a hare—they appear in imagination but have no reality. Yet they are not simply nothing, for they function in the world of appearance."

**10** "When the Bodhisattva understands that the world is mind-only, when he transcends the duality of subject and object, when he ceases to discriminate between self and other, between is and is not—then there arises the self-realization of noble wisdom, which is the turning-about in the deepest seat of consciousness."
