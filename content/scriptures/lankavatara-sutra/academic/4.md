The three natures doctrine presented in this chapter has been the subject of extensive philosophical analysis by scholars of Yogacara Buddhism. Jay Garfield, Mario D'Amato, and others have examined how the trisvabhava framework functions as a hermeneutical tool for interpreting the relationship between appearance and reality in Buddhist philosophy.

Academic analysis has shown that the Lankavatara's presentation of the three natures differs in important ways from the systematized version found in Vasubandhu's Trisvabhavanirdesa (Treatise on the Three Natures). The Lankavatara's treatment is more fluid and less technically precise, supporting the scholarly view that the sutra represents an earlier stage of Yogacara philosophical development.

The chapter's reinterpretation of dependent origination from the mind-only perspective has been analyzed by scholars as a significant philosophical innovation within the Buddhist tradition. Collett Cox's research on the evolution of Buddhist causal theory has shown how the Yogacara reading of pratitya-samutpada represents a departure from earlier Abhidharma interpretations, transforming what was understood as an objective causal process into a description of how consciousness constructs the appearance of causality.
