The Sagathakam has received particular scholarly attention because some researchers have proposed that it represents an originally independent text that was incorporated into the Lankavatara at a relatively late stage. Jikido Takasaki's study of the tathagatagarbha doctrine noted that the verse chapter contains teachings that do not always align precisely with the prose chapters, supporting the hypothesis of independent composition.

Academic analysis of the Sagathakam's poetic form has revealed its relationship to broader Indian Buddhist verse traditions. The chapter employs various Sanskrit meters and stylistic conventions that connect it with the broader corpus of Indian Buddhist kavya (literary poetry), suggesting that its authors were familiar with the conventions of both Buddhist philosophical composition and Indian literary aesthetics.

The Sagathakam's summary function has been compared by scholars with similar verse summaries in other Mahayana sutras, such as the Samadhiraja Sutra and the Avatamsaka Sutra. The practice of providing verse recapitulations of prose teachings reflects the Indian Buddhist pedagogical tradition of combining detailed philosophical analysis with condensed memorizable summaries, ensuring that the teaching could be both studied in depth and carried in memory.
