Academic analysis of the first Collection chapter has focused on its presentation of the Yogacara philosophical framework, particularly the doctrine of vijnapti-matra (consciousness-only). Scholars including Lambert Schmithausen, Nobuyoshi Yamabe, and Dan Lusthaus have debated whether the Lankavatara represents an early or late stage in the development of Yogacara thought, with significant implications for understanding the history of Indian Buddhist philosophy.

The alaya-vijnana concept as presented in this chapter shows features that differ from its treatment in the systematic Yogacara works of Asanga and Vasubandhu. Schmithausen has argued that the Lankavatara represents a transitional stage in which the alaya-vijnana concept was still being developed and had not yet received the systematic treatment it would receive in later Yogacara scholasticism.

The chapter's identification of the alaya-vijnana with the tathagatagarbha has attracted scholarly attention as a philosophical move that creates tensions within the text's own framework. If the alaya-vijnana is a defiled consciousness containing the seeds of samsaric experience, how can it simultaneously be the inherently pure tathagatagarbha? Scholars have debated whether this represents a coherent philosophical position or an unresolved tension reflecting the composite nature of the text.
