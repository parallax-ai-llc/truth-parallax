The chapter on change has been analyzed by scholars in the context of Buddhist theories of impermanence and transformation. Rupert Gethin's research on Buddhist cosmology and psychology has examined how different Buddhist traditions understand the relationship between momentary change and the transformation of consciousness that constitutes awakening.

Academic analysis has noted that the chapter's treatment of change reflects the Lankavatara's characteristic blend of Yogacara and tathagatagarbha thought. Change at the conventional level (the impermanence of conditioned phenomena) is understood through the Yogacara framework, while the ultimate change (the transformation of the alaya-vijnana into the tathagatagarbha) requires the tathagatagarbha framework. This dual-level analysis of change reveals the philosophical sophistication of the sutra's position.

Scholars have also examined the chapter's implications for Buddhist practice theory. The teaching that consciousness can undergo a fundamental transformation (paravrtti) provides the theoretical basis for the claim that buddhahood is achievable, a claim that requires explanation given the Buddhist teaching on the impermanence and non-self of all phenomena. How can a permanent transformation occur in a universe where everything is impermanent? The Lankavatara's answer involves the tathagatagarbha, which is itself beyond impermanence.
