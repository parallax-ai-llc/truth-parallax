The chapter on meat-eating has been studied by scholars of Buddhist ethics as one of the most influential texts in the history of Buddhist vegetarianism. James Stewart's research on Buddhist attitudes toward animals and meat-eating has traced the influence of the Lankavatara's arguments across East Asian Buddhist cultures, showing how this chapter contributed to the establishment of vegetarianism as a normative practice in Chinese, Korean, and Vietnamese Buddhism.

Academic analysis has noted that the Lankavatara's arguments against meat-eating draw on multiple rhetorical strategies: appeals to compassion, appeals to spiritual purity, appeals to the interconnection of all beings through rebirth, and appeals to the opinions of past buddhas. This multi-layered argumentation suggests that the authors were responding to a real debate within Buddhist communities about the appropriateness of meat consumption.

Scholars have compared the Lankavatara's position with the Pali Vinaya's more permissive approach, noting that the difference reflects broader disagreements between Mahayana and non-Mahayana communities about the scope of the bodhisattva's ethical commitment. The historical anthropologist Roel Sterckx has situated the Buddhist vegetarian movement within the broader context of Chinese food ethics, showing how the Lankavatara's arguments were received and adapted within Chinese cultural frameworks.
