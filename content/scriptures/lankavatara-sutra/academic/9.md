The dharani chapter has been studied by scholars of Buddhist ritual and esoteric practice as evidence for the early development of dharani traditions within Mahayana Buddhism. Ronald Davidson's research on Indian esoteric Buddhism has traced the evolution of dharani practice from simple protective formulas to the complex ritual systems of developed Vajrayana, positioning the Lankavatara's dharani chapter as an early stage in this development.

Academic analysis has noted that the dharani material appears to have been added to the sutra at a later stage of its composition, as it differs in style and content from the philosophical chapters. This observation supports the scholarly consensus that the Lankavatara is a composite text that grew over time through the accumulation of different types of material.

The specific dharanis presented in this chapter have been studied by scholars of Buddhist philology, who have compared the Sanskrit, Chinese, and Tibetan versions to reconstruct the original Indic forms. Paul Copp's research on the social history of dharani in medieval China has shown how protective formulas from texts like the Lankavatara were integrated into daily Buddhist practice, inscribed on objects, and used in healing rituals.
