The ocean metaphor for consciousness presented in this chapter has been traced by scholars to early Buddhist and pre-Buddhist Indian philosophical traditions. The image of the mind as an ocean whose surface is disturbed by winds (representing defilements or habitual tendencies) while its depths remain calm appears in various forms across Indian literature. The Lankavatara's specific use of this metaphor to describe the alaya-vijnana represents a creative adaptation of existing imagery for Yogacara philosophical purposes.

Scholars have compared the Lankavatara's ocean metaphor with the similar imagery in the Awakening of Faith in the Mahayana (Dasheng qixin lun), a text attributed to Ashvaghosha but widely regarded by modern scholars as a Chinese composition. The relationship between these two texts has been debated, with some scholars suggesting that the Awakening of Faith drew on the Lankavatara while others argue for independent development of similar imagery.

The chapter's discussion of the transformation of consciousness has been analyzed by scholars through the lens of comparative soteriology. Robert Gimello's work on the Buddhist concept of sudden awakening has examined how the paravrtti described in the Lankavatara relates to the transformation experiences described in other religious traditions, including Christian conversion and Hindu moksha.
