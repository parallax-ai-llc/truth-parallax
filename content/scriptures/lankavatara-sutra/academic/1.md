The opening chapter of the Lankavatara Sutra has been analyzed by scholars as a literary frame that draws on pre-Buddhist Indian narrative traditions. The setting on Lanka and the figure of Ravana connect the sutra with the Ramayana epic tradition, while the Buddhist content transforms this mythological setting into a venue for philosophical teaching. Scholars have debated whether this framing was intended to appeal to audiences familiar with the Ramayana or to deliberately reclaim Hindu mythological space for Buddhist purposes.

The textual history of the opening chapter reveals significant variation across the three Chinese translations and the Tibetan version. Gunabhadra's translation (443 CE) provides the most extensive version of the Ravana episode, while Bodhiruci's translation (513 CE) and Shikshananda's translation (700-704 CE) present shorter versions. These variations suggest that the framing narrative was subject to editorial modification over the sutra's transmission history.

D.T. Suzuki's pioneering English translation and study (1932) introduced the Lankavatara to Western academic audiences, but subsequent scholars have noted that Suzuki's interpretation was shaped by his Zen Buddhist practice, leading him to emphasize certain themes (particularly the concept of self-realization) while underemphasizing the text's Yogacara philosophical framework. More recent translations, including Red Pine's (2012), have aimed for greater philosophical balance.
