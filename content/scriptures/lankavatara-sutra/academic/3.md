The second collection's treatment of the tathagatagarbha doctrine has been extensively analyzed by scholars of Indian Buddhist philosophy. Michael Zimmermann's study of the tathagatagarbha concept has traced its development from early Mahayana texts through the Lankavatara to later systematizations, showing how the concept evolved in meaning and philosophical sophistication over several centuries.

Academic debate has centered on whether the tathagatagarbha teaching represents a genuinely Buddhist doctrine or a concession to Hindu atman (self) philosophy. The Lankavatara itself addresses this concern, noting that the tathagatagarbha teaching differs from the atman because it is taught as a skillful means for those who fear the doctrine of emptiness. Scholars have debated whether this passage represents the text's own critical self-awareness or a later addition designed to preempt philosophical objections.

The concept of pratyatma-arya-jnana (noble wisdom of self-realization) has been compared by scholars with the experiential dimension emphasized in various Indian philosophical traditions. Florin Deleanu's research has examined how this concept relates to the broader Indian discourse on pratyaksha (direct perception) and anubhava (experience), showing how Buddhist and non-Buddhist traditions both valued direct experiential knowledge while framing it within different philosophical contexts.
