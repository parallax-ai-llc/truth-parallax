The fifth collection's treatment of nirvana and non-duality has been studied by scholars of Buddhist philosophy as evidence for the development of the "non-abiding nirvana" concept in Mahayana thought. Paul Williams's research on Mahayana Buddhist philosophy has examined how the Lankavatara's presentation of nirvana as inseparable from samsara contributed to the distinctive Mahayana soteriology that distinguishes it from earlier Buddhist models of liberation.

Academic analysis has also focused on the chapter's teaching about the limits of language. The Lankavatara's insistence that ultimate truth cannot be expressed in words has been compared by scholars with the philosophy of language developed by Bhartrhari, the great Indian grammarian-philosopher, who similarly argued that the highest truth (Shabda-Brahman) transcends linguistic expression while paradoxically being expressed through language.

The relationship between the Lankavatara's philosophical positions and those of Nagarjuna's Madhyamaka has been debated by scholars. While the sutra's mind-only teaching differs from Nagarjuna's thoroughgoing emptiness, the chapters on non-duality and the limits of language show significant convergence with Madhyamaka positions. Thomas Wood's analysis has argued that the Lankavatara's mature philosophical position is closer to Madhyamaka than to systematic Yogacara.
