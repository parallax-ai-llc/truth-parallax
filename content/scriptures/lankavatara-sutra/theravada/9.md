The dharani chapter presents protective formulas and mantras, a practice that has some parallels in the Theravada tradition of paritta (protective chanting). The Theravada canon includes several paritta texts, such as the Ratana Sutta, the Mangala Sutta, and the Metta Sutta, which are chanted for protection against illness, misfortune, and malevolent spirits.

However, the Theravada understanding of paritta differs from the Mahayana concept of dharani. In the Theravada tradition, protective chanting derives its power from the truth of the dharma and the merit of the reciter, not from the intrinsic magical properties of the syllables themselves. The Mahayana dharani tradition, which the Lankavatara represents, attributes more inherent power to the sounds and syllables of the formulas.

The practice of chanting for protection is among the most widely observed practices in Theravada Buddhist countries, performed at temples, households, and public ceremonies. While the specific dharanis of the Lankavatara are not used in Theravada practice, the underlying principle that sacred words have protective power is shared across both traditions, reflecting a pre-sectarian Buddhist practice that predates the Theravada-Mahayana division.
