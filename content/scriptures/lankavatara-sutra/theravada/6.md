The fifth collection discourse addresses themes of non-duality and the nature of nirvana. The Theravada understanding of nibbana as the unconditioned (asankhata) cessation of suffering can be compared with the Lankavatara's presentation of nirvana as the realization of mind-only and the recognition of the tathagatagarbha.

The Theravada tradition categorizes nibbana as one of the four ultimate realities (paramattha dhamma), alongside consciousness, mental factors, and material form. Nibbana is understood as the cessation of all conditioned phenomena, not as a positive reality to be discovered within consciousness. This differs from the Lankavatara's tendency to describe liberation in more positive terms as the uncovering of an inherently pure awareness.

The chapter's non-dual teaching that samsara and nirvana are not ultimately separate can be compared with the Theravada understanding that nibbana is "visible here and now" (sanditthika). The Theravada tradition affirms that liberation can be realized in this very life, but it maintains a clearer distinction between the conditioned realm of samsara and the unconditioned reality of nibbana than the Mahayana non-dual framework allows.
