The third collection discourse explores the three natures (svabhavas): the imagined (parikalpita), the dependent (paratantra), and the perfected (parinishpanna). The Theravada tradition does not use this three-nature framework, instead analyzing phenomena through the categories of the Abhidhamma, which classifies all experience into ultimate realities (paramattha dhammas): consciousness, mental factors, material form, and nibbana.

The Yogacara teaching that the imagined nature is unreal while the perfected nature represents the true way things are can be compared with the Theravada distinction between conventional (sammuti) and ultimate (paramattha) truth. Both frameworks recognize that ordinary perception involves a layer of conceptual construction that obscures the nature of reality, but they differ in how they characterize what remains when this construction is removed.

The chapter's treatment of dependent origination (pratitya-samutpada) from the mind-only perspective reinterprets a teaching that is central to the Theravada tradition. The Theravada understanding of dependent origination emphasizes the twelve links as describing the actual process by which suffering arises and ceases, while the Lankavatara reinterprets these links as projections of consciousness rather than as independently existing processes.
