The final chapter presents the sutra's teachings in verse form (sagathakam), a literary device that has its counterpart in the Theravada tradition. The Pali canon includes extensive verse collections, most notably the Dhammapada, the Theragatha (Verses of the Elder Monks), and the Therigatha (Verses of the Elder Nuns). These poetic compositions serve as memorable summaries of Buddhist teaching.

The Lankavatara's verses condense the sutra's complex philosophical arguments into memorable couplets and stanzas. From a Theravada perspective, this practice of summarizing doctrine in verse serves an important mnemonic function, particularly in oral traditions where texts must be memorized and transmitted without written aids.

The verses of the Sagathakam address many of the same themes found in the prose chapters but often in more direct and accessible language. The Theravada reader may find these verses more approachable than the complex philosophical arguments of the prose sections, though the underlying mind-only and tathagatagarbha doctrines remain distinctly Mahayana in orientation. The poetic form bridges the gap between the traditions by appealing to a shared Buddhist aesthetic of condensed wisdom.
