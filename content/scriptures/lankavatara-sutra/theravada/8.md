The chapter on meat-eating presents detailed arguments against the consumption of flesh, a topic that has been debated within the Theravada tradition. The Pali Vinaya records that the Buddha allowed monastics to eat meat provided three conditions were met: the monk did not see, hear, or suspect that the animal was killed specifically for them. This "three-fold purity" rule established the normative Theravada position.

The Lankavatara's arguments for vegetarianism go beyond the Vinaya regulations, teaching that all beings are interconnected through the cycles of rebirth and that eating flesh is therefore equivalent to eating one's own relatives. This argument from the universality of rebirth is present in some Pali texts as well, particularly in the Jataka tales that illustrate the Buddha's past lives in various animal forms.

Contemporary Theravada communities are divided on the question of vegetarianism. Some, particularly in the Sri Lankan and Chinese-influenced Vietnamese traditions, advocate for vegetarian practice as an expression of compassion (karuna). Others maintain that the Buddha's explicit allowance of meat-eating under the three-fold purity condition should be respected as normative. The Lankavatara's chapter has been cited by both sides in this ongoing debate.
