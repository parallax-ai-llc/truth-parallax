The fourth collection discourse continues the analysis of consciousness and its transformation. The concept of paravrtti (turning about or revolution at the base of consciousness) is central to the Lankavatara's soteriology, describing liberation as a fundamental shift in the relationship between consciousness and its projections.

The Theravada tradition describes liberation differently: through the path moments (magga-citta) that permanently eradicate specific defilements at each stage of awakening. The Theravada model envisions a progressive purification of consciousness rather than a single revolutionary transformation of its deepest stratum. The arahat's consciousness is purified of all defilements but is not described as a fundamentally different kind of consciousness.

The chapter's discussion of buddha-nature as present within all beings continues to raise the Theravada concern about the reintroduction of a permanent self. The Kathavatthu, an Abhidhamma text that refutes various heterodox positions, specifically argues against the claim that there is a luminous consciousness that persists unchanged beneath the flow of conditioned mental events. The Theravada tradition maintains that all consciousness, including the most refined states, is impermanent and conditioned.
