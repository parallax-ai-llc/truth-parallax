The opening chapter sets the scene on the island of Lanka, where the demon king Ravana invites the Buddha to teach. The Theravada tradition has its own accounts of the Buddha visiting Lanka, preserved in the Mahavamsa (Great Chronicle) of Sri Lankan Buddhism. According to this chronicle, the Buddha visited Lanka three times, and his visits sanctified the island as a center of Buddhist civilization.

The Lankavatara Sutra's setting on Lanka thus carries particular resonance for the Theravada tradition, which considers Sri Lanka the guardian of authentic Buddhist teaching. However, the doctrinal content of the sutra, with its emphasis on mind-only philosophy and tathagatagarbha thought, diverges significantly from the Theravada Abhidhamma's analysis of consciousness and reality.

Ravana's role as the host of the Buddha's teaching is noteworthy from a Theravada perspective. The Pali canon includes accounts of non-human beings, including devas and yakshas, receiving the Buddha's instruction. The Atanatiya Sutta describes the Buddha's encounter with the Four Great Kings, who offer protective verses. The motif of a powerful non-human being seeking the dharma is thus common to both traditions.
