## Overview

The Lankavatara Sutra is a Mahayana text not included in the Pali canon, yet its detailed analysis of consciousness and mental processes invites comparison with the Theravada Abhidhamma's own extensive mapping of mind (citta) and mental factors (cetasika). Both traditions share a conviction that understanding the nature of mind is central to liberation.

## Doctrinal Significance

The sutra's teaching on the alaya-vijnana (storehouse consciousness) as the repository of karmic impressions can be compared with the Theravada concept of bhavanga (life-continuum consciousness). While the Abhidhamma does not posit a storehouse consciousness, it does describe how karmic seeds persist across lifetimes through the process of mental continuity.

## Points of Engagement

The Lankavatara's chapter on meat-eating, which provides detailed arguments for vegetarianism based on compassion and the interconnection of all beings, has resonated with Theravada Buddhists who advocate for vegetarian practice despite the Pali Vinaya's allowance of meat under certain conditions.
