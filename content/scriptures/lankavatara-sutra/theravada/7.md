The chapter on change explores the transformation of consciousness that constitutes awakening. The Theravada tradition has its own understanding of mental transformation through the progressive stages of insight (vipassana-nana), which describe how the practitioner's relationship to experience changes as wisdom deepens.

The sixteen stages of insight knowledge in the Theravada Visuddhimagga describe a detailed phenomenology of the contemplative path, from the initial knowledge of arising and passing away through the knowledge of dissolution, fear, danger, and disenchantment, to the culminating knowledge of path and fruition. This progressive phenomenology contrasts with the Lankavatara's description of a single transformative shift (paravrtti) at the base of consciousness.

The chapter's emphasis on impermanence and change as the condition of liberation has parallels in the Theravada tradition's foundational teaching on anicca (impermanence). The Theravada practice of vipassana centers on the direct observation of impermanence in all conditioned phenomena. Both traditions agree that understanding change is essential to liberation, though they differ in their theoretical frameworks.
