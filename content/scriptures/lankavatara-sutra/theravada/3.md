The second collection discourse deepens the philosophical analysis, introducing the tathagatagarbha doctrine alongside the Yogacara framework. The Theravada tradition does not employ the concept of tathagatagarbha (buddha-nature embryo) in its soteriology. The closest Theravada concept is the luminous mind (pabhassara citta) mentioned in the Anguttara Nikaya, which describes the mind as naturally radiant though temporarily obscured by defilements.

The relationship between the alaya-vijnana and the tathagatagarbha that the sutra attempts to establish represents a philosophical synthesis that the Theravada tradition would view with caution. The Theravada position is that the mind is conditioned and impermanent, without an underlying permanent substrate. The tathagatagarbha teaching, which implies an inherently pure essence within beings, risks reintroducing a form of the selfhood (atta) that the Buddha's teaching of anatta was designed to negate.

The chapter's teaching on self-realization (pratyatma-arya-jnana) as the noble wisdom beyond words does connect with the Theravada understanding that nibbana is directly experienced and cannot be fully communicated through concepts. Both traditions affirm that the highest truth is realized rather than merely understood intellectually.
