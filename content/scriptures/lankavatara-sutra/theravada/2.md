The first "Collection of All Dharmas" chapter presents the sutra's central philosophical framework: the teaching that all experienced phenomena are projections of consciousness. The Theravada tradition has its own sophisticated analysis of consciousness (citta) and mental factors (cetasika) in the Abhidhamma, but it does not accept the Yogacara teaching that external objects are mere projections of the mind.

The Theravada Abhidhamma acknowledges the role of consciousness in shaping experience but maintains a form of representational realism: consciousness arises in dependence on sense organs and their objects, both of which are considered to have their own existence. The Lankavatara's radical idealism, which denies the independent existence of external objects, goes beyond what Theravada philosophy is willing to affirm.

The chapter's discussion of the alaya-vijnana (storehouse consciousness) can be compared with the Theravada concept of bhavanga-citta (life-continuum consciousness), which maintains the continuity of the mental stream between active cognitive processes. While both concepts address the question of how consciousness persists, the Theravada formulation avoids the ontological weight that the Yogacara tradition assigns to the alaya-vijnana.
