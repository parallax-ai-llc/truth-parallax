The chapter on meat-eating represents one of the most influential Buddhist arguments for vegetarianism, and it has shaped Mahayana practice throughout East Asia. The sutra presents multiple arguments against consuming flesh: all beings are interconnected through the cycles of rebirth, the consumption of meat generates fear and suffering, meat-eating is incompatible with the bodhisattva's commitment to compassion, and the smell of flesh repels celestial beings and attracts malevolent spirits.

The Mahayana tradition, particularly in Chinese Buddhism, adopted vegetarianism as a normative practice for monastics and devout laypeople, citing this chapter among the scriptural justifications. Emperor Wu of the Liang dynasty (502-549 CE) issued an edict requiring Buddhist monastics to abstain from meat, a decision that transformed Chinese Buddhist practice and distinguished it from the Indian and Southeast Asian traditions.

The chapter's arguments go beyond the narrow question of diet to address the broader Mahayana ethics of compassion. If the bodhisattva vows to liberate all beings, then consuming their flesh is a fundamental contradiction. This integration of dietary practice with the bodhisattva vow represents a distinctive Mahayana contribution to Buddhist ethics.
