The first Collection of All Dharmas presents the foundational Yogacara teaching that all experienced phenomena are projections of consciousness (vijnapti-matra). This is the central Mahayana philosophical insight of the Lankavatara: what we take to be an independently existing external world is actually the display of the alaya-vijnana (storehouse consciousness), which contains the seeds (bija) of all possible experiences.

The eight consciousnesses framework introduced here became one of the most influential philosophical structures in Mahayana Buddhism. The five sense consciousnesses, the mental consciousness (manovijnana), the defiled mental consciousness (manas), and the alaya-vijnana together account for the entire range of sentient experience. This framework was further developed by Yogacara philosophers such as Asanga, Vasubandhu, and later Chinese masters like Xuanzang.

The chapter's analysis of how the mind projects the appearance of an external world is not merely theoretical but has direct soteriological implications. If suffering arises from taking projections of consciousness for independently real objects, then liberation consists in recognizing the projective nature of all experience. This recognition is the beginning of the "turning about" (paravrtti) that constitutes awakening.
