The fifth collection addresses the nature of nirvana and the non-duality of samsara and nirvana, a teaching central to Mahayana soteriology. The sutra teaches that nirvana is not a distant realm to be reached through accumulation of merit and wisdom but the recognition of the mind's own nature, which has been present all along beneath the turbulence of karmic conditioning.

The concept of non-abiding nirvana (apratishthita-nirvana) is implicit in this chapter's teaching. The bodhisattva neither abides in samsara through attachment nor in nirvana through renunciation of the world. This non-abiding, rooted in the simultaneous realization of emptiness and compassion, is the distinctive Mahayana understanding of liberation.

The chapter's teaching on the inexpressibility of ultimate truth connects the Lankavatara with the broader Prajnaparamita tradition. The noble wisdom of self-realization (pratyatma-arya-jnana) cannot be communicated through words because it transcends the subject-object structure that language presupposes. This teaching on the limits of language has profound implications for how the Mahayana tradition understands the relationship between philosophy and practice.
