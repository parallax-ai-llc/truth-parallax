The second collection deepens the philosophical analysis by introducing the tathagatagarbha (buddha-nature embryo) alongside the Yogacara framework. This synthesis is one of the Lankavatara's most significant contributions to Mahayana thought: it identifies the alaya-vijnana, when purified of its adventitious defilements, with the tathagatagarbha, the inherent potential for buddhahood within all beings.

This identification creates a powerful soteriological vision: the very consciousness that generates the illusion of samsara is also the ground of awakening. Liberation is not the destruction of consciousness but its transformation, the recognition of what it has been all along beneath the layers of karmic conditioning. The defiled alaya-vijnana and the pure tathagatagarbha are two descriptions of the same reality, seen from the perspective of ignorance and wisdom respectively.

The chapter also introduces the teaching on pratyatma-arya-jnana (the noble wisdom of self-realization), which describes awakening as a direct, non-conceptual knowing that transcends all philosophical formulations. This teaching has been crucial for understanding how the Mahayana tradition views the relationship between philosophical understanding and liberating experience.
