The fourth collection continues to develop the Yogacara philosophical framework while addressing the practical question of how the transformation of consciousness (paravrtti) actually occurs. The Mahayana practitioner is taught to cultivate a direct seeing into the nature of consciousness that dissolves the habitual patterns of projection and grasping.

The chapter teaches that the alaya-vijnana is like a great ocean, and the seven active consciousnesses are like waves arising from its surface. The winds of habitual tendencies (vasana) stir the ocean into waves, creating the appearance of subject and object, self and world. When the winds of habit cease, the ocean returns to its natural stillness, revealing its inherent clarity.

This ocean metaphor became one of the most influential images in East Asian Mahayana thought, adopted by the Awakening of Faith in the Mahayana (a text some scholars attribute to the Lankavatara's influence) and by numerous Chinese, Korean, and Japanese Buddhist teachers. It provides an accessible image for understanding how the one mind can appear as the multiplicity of phenomena while never losing its fundamental unity.
