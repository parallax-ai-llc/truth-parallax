The dharani chapter presents protective formulas that the Mahayana tradition understands as concentrated expressions of the Buddha's spiritual power. Dharanis serve multiple functions in Mahayana practice: they protect the practitioner from obstacles and negative forces, they concentrate the teaching of the sutra into memorable syllables, and they serve as objects of meditation that focus the mind.

In the broader Mahayana context, the Lankavatara's dharanis represent an early stage in the development of mantra and dharani practice that would eventually flower into the Vajrayana tradition. The principle that sound can embody and transmit spiritual power underlies both the dharani practice described here and the later tantric emphasis on mantra recitation as a primary path to awakening.

The chapter also addresses the protective function of dharanis for the sutra itself. The dharanis are understood as spiritual guardians of the teaching, ensuring that the sutra's transmission remains uncorrupted and that those who recite it are protected from the obstacles that would prevent their understanding. This reflects the Mahayana concern with the preservation and protection of the dharma.
