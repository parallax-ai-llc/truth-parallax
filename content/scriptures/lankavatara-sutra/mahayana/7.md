The chapter on change presents the transformation of consciousness as the essential process of awakening. The Mahayana teaching on paravrtti describes a fundamental revolution at the deepest level of consciousness, where the alaya-vijnana's habitual patterns of projection are reversed and the mind recognizes its own nature.

This transformation is not the addition of something new to consciousness but the removal of the adventitious defilements that obscure its inherent clarity. The analogy of gold purified from ore conveys the Mahayana understanding: the gold (buddha-nature) was always present within the ore (deluded consciousness), but it required the refining process (practice) to reveal it.

The chapter's treatment of impermanence from the mind-only perspective adds a distinctive dimension to the universal Buddhist teaching on change. If all phenomena are projections of consciousness, then impermanence itself is an appearance within the mind. The truly radical change is not the change of external objects but the transformation of the consciousness that projects them. This understanding deepens the Mahayana practitioner's engagement with impermanence as a gateway to liberating wisdom.
