The opening chapter sets the stage for the Buddha's most comprehensive teaching on the nature of consciousness by placing it in the dramatic setting of Lanka, a mythical island associated with Ravana, the demon king of the Indian epic tradition. The sutra transforms this setting from the Ramayana's battlefield into a venue for the deepest Mahayana philosophical teaching, demonstrating the Buddhist practice of recontextualizing existing cultural narratives.

Ravana's request for teaching exemplifies the Mahayana understanding that all beings, regardless of their cosmic status, can aspire to awakening. The demon king's sincerity and devotion model the Mahayana principle that bodhicitta (the aspiration for awakening) can arise in any being, even those conventionally classified as demonic or non-human.

The chapter introduces Mahamati Bodhisattva as the primary interlocutor, establishing the dialogical structure that will carry through the entire sutra. Mahamati's questions represent the inquiries of an advanced bodhisattva seeking to penetrate the deepest levels of Buddhist philosophy, setting the intellectual and spiritual ambition of the text far above introductory teachings.
