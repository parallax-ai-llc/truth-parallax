The Lankavatara Sutra opens with the Buddha arriving at Lanka at Ravana's invitation. In the Tibetan Buddhist tradition, the sutra is preserved in the Kangyur and has been the subject of commentarial attention from scholars within multiple Tibetan schools. The setting on Lanka connects with the Tibetan understanding of the Buddha's capacity to teach in any realm, adapting the dharma to the needs of beings in each specific context.

Ravana's role as the host of the teaching is understood through the Tibetan concept of the "fortunate student" (skal ldan gyi slob ma), a being whose accumulated merit has created the conditions for receiving the deepest teachings. Even a demon king can become a vessel for the dharma when the conditions are ripe, illustrating the Mahayana principle of universal buddha-nature.

The introduction of Mahamati as the primary questioner establishes the sutra's philosophical ambition. In Tibetan Buddhist pedagogical tradition, the quality of questions determines the depth of teaching received. Mahamati's questions demonstrate the understanding of a tenth-bhumi bodhisattva, setting the stage for teachings appropriate to the most advanced practitioners.
