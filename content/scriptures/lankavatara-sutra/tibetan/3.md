The tathagatagarbha teaching of this chapter is central to several Tibetan Buddhist traditions. The Tibetan term for tathagatagarbha, de bzhin gshegs pa'i snying po, is studied across all Tibetan schools but interpreted differently depending on whether one follows the rangtong (self-empty) or zhentong (other-empty) philosophical approach.

The Gelug rangtong interpretation, following Tsongkhapa, understands tathagatagarbha as the emptiness of the mind, not as a positive inherent quality. On this reading, to say that all beings possess buddha-nature means that all minds are empty of inherent existence and therefore capable of transformation into the mind of a buddha. The tathagatagarbha is a potential, not an already-existing reality.

The Jonang zhentong interpretation, following Dolpopa, reads the Lankavatara's tathagatagarbha teaching more literally, understanding buddha-nature as a luminous, permanent reality that is obscured by adventitious defilements but never truly absent. On this reading, the Lankavatara's identification of the purified alaya-vijnana with the tathagatagarbha points to a positive, radiant awareness that constitutes the ultimate nature of all beings. This debate remains one of the most important philosophical discussions in Tibetan Buddhism.
