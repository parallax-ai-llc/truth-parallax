The Sagathakam is studied in Tibetan Buddhist monastic education as a summary of the sutra's philosophical positions. The verse format is valued in the Tibetan scholastic tradition, where philosophical treatises are often composed in verse (tshig bcad) with accompanying prose commentary (mchan 'grel). This format allows for both memorization of essential points and detailed explication of their meaning.

The verses of the Sagathakam address themes that are central to Tibetan Buddhist philosophical debate, including the nature of consciousness, the relationship between buddha-nature and emptiness, and the possibility of non-conceptual knowledge. These topics continue to be debated in Tibetan monastic universities, demonstrating the ongoing relevance of the Lankavatara's philosophical questions.

The chapter's poetic recapitulation of the entire sutra's teaching serves a function similar to the Tibetan practice of "condensed meaning" (bsdus don), where the essential points of a text are summarized for memorization and contemplation. The Tibetan tradition values the ability to hold the entire teaching of a text in mind simultaneously, and the Sagathakam's verse format supports this practice of comprehensive understanding.
