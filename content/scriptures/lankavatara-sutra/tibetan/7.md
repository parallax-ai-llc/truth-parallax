The chapter on change is understood in Tibetan Buddhism through the concept of transformation (gnas 'gyur), which describes both the transformation of consciousness that constitutes awakening and the transformation of the Buddha's teaching over time. The Tibetan tradition recognizes three turnings of the wheel of dharma, each representing a deepening of the teaching, and the Lankavatara is generally classified as belonging to the third turning, which reveals the tathagatagarbha.

The teaching on the transformation of the alaya-vijnana connects with the Tibetan practice of "purifying the six consciousnesses" found in certain tantric meditation practices. In these practices, each consciousness is transformed into a corresponding wisdom: the alaya-vijnana becomes the dharmadhatu wisdom (chos dbyings ye shes), the mirror-like wisdom (me long ye shes) of the purified manas, and so forth.

Tibetan art and iconography express this transformation symbolically through the five buddha families, each representing the transformation of a specific poison into a corresponding wisdom. The chapter's philosophical description of consciousness transformation thus has living expression in the Tibetan tradition's meditative, artistic, and liturgical practices.
