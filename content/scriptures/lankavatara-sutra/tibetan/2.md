The Yogacara teaching on consciousness presented in this chapter is studied in Tibetan monastic universities primarily through the lens of the Madhyamaka critique. The Gelug tradition, following Tsongkhapa, generally treats the Yogacara teaching of mind-only (sems tsam) as a philosophically lower position than the Prasangika-Madhyamaka view, which negates the inherent existence of both subject and object rather than reducing objects to consciousness.

However, the Tibetan tradition also recognizes the Yogacara teaching's practical value for meditation. The analysis of the eight consciousnesses provides a detailed map of mental experience that can inform contemplative practice. The Kagyu and Nyingma traditions have been more sympathetic to Yogacara perspectives, particularly in the context of Mahamudra and Dzogchen meditation.

The alaya-vijnana concept has been the subject of extensive Tibetan debate. Tsongkhapa rejected it as an unnecessary philosophical construct, while the Eighth Karmapa Mikyo Dorje and other Kagyu masters accepted a form of the alaya teaching as compatible with Madhyamaka when properly understood. These intra-Tibetan debates demonstrate the continuing vitality of the Lankavatara's philosophical questions within Tibetan scholasticism.
