## Overview

The Lankavatara Sutra is included in the Tibetan Kangyur and has been studied extensively in Tibetan Buddhist scholastic traditions. It occupies a central role in debates about the relationship between Yogacara and Madhyamaka philosophy, and between the rangtong (self-empty) and zhentong (other-empty) interpretations of emptiness.

## Doctrinal Significance

Tibetan scholars have debated whether the sutra's tathagatagarbha teaching implies an eternal, positive essence within beings or is simply a skillful means to encourage practitioners. The Jonang school's zhentong view, articulated by Dolpopa, drew heavily on the Lankavatara to argue that buddha-nature is a luminous reality merely obscured by adventitious defilements.

## Philosophical Synthesis

The sutra's integration of mind-only and buddha-nature doctrines provides material for Tibetan traditions that seek to harmonize Yogacara and Madhyamaka. The Lankavatara's sophisticated analysis of consciousness levels and its teaching on the transformation of the alaya-vijnana inform Tibetan meditation practices focused on recognizing the nature of mind.
