The ocean metaphor for consciousness that appears in this chapter has been adopted by Tibetan Mahamudra and Dzogchen traditions to describe the nature of mind. The Kagyu teaching on Mahamudra meditation often uses the image of the ocean and waves to describe the relationship between the mind's nature (the ocean's depth) and its display of thoughts and emotions (the surface waves).

The teaching on the transformation (paravrtti) of consciousness at the deepest level parallels the Dzogchen concept of spontaneous liberation (rang grol), where thoughts and appearances self-liberate upon arising when the practitioner rests in the recognition of the nature of mind. Both frameworks describe a shift from identifying with the waves of mental activity to recognizing the ocean-like nature of awareness itself.

Tibetan meditation instructions derived from this understanding emphasize the practice of simply recognizing the nature of each thought as it arises, rather than attempting to suppress or transform thoughts through effort. The Third Karmapa Rangjung Dorje's "Aspiration Prayer of Mahamudra" captures this teaching: thoughts are "the play of the dharmakaya," waves on the ocean of awareness that are never separate from the ocean itself.
