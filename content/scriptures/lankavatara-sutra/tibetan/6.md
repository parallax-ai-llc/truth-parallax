The non-duality of samsara and nirvana presented in this chapter connects with the Tibetan Buddhist teaching on the inseparability of the ground (gzhi), path (lam), and fruition ('bras bu). The ground is the tathagatagarbha, the inherent buddha-nature present in all beings. The path is the process of recognizing this nature through practice. The fruition is the full manifestation of buddhahood, which was always present as the ground.

The chapter's teaching on the inexpressibility of ultimate truth resonates with the Dzogchen concept of the state beyond concepts (spros bral), where the nature of mind is recognized as transcending all philosophical elaboration. The Tibetan master Longchenpa taught that all philosophical views, including Madhyamaka and Yogacara, are ultimately expedient means that must be transcended in the direct recognition of the nature of mind.

The practical implications for Tibetan meditation are significant. If nirvana is not separate from samsara, then the practitioner does not need to achieve a special state or reach a distant goal. The practice is simply to recognize what is already present, a recognition that the Tibetan tradition calls "introduction to the nature of mind" (sems kyi ngo sprod), traditionally given by a qualified master.
