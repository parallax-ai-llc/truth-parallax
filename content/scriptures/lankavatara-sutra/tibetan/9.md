The dharani chapter connects with the extensive Tibetan tradition of mantra recitation, which is one of the most prominent features of Vajrayana Buddhism. The Tibetan understanding of dharani and mantra goes beyond the Lankavatara's presentation, developing elaborate theories of how sound embodies enlightened qualities and how the recitation of sacred syllables transforms the practitioner's body, speech, and mind.

The protective function of dharanis is well established in Tibetan Buddhist practice. Protective mantras (srung 'khor) are recited daily by Tibetan practitioners, and dharani texts are placed in protective amulets (gau) worn on the body. The Tibetan tradition thus extends the Lankavatara's teaching on dharani protection into a comprehensive system of spiritual protection that permeates daily life.

The chapter's presentation of dharanis as concentrated expressions of the sutra's teaching parallels the Tibetan concept of the "essence mantra" (snying po'i sngags), which is understood to contain the entire teaching of a particular deity or sutra in compressed form. The practice of reciting the essence mantra is considered equivalent to reciting the entire text, a principle that reflects the Tibetan understanding of the mantra's power to contain and transmit the fullness of the teaching.
