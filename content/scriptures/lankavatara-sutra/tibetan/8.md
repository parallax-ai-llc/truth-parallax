The chapter on meat-eating has had varying influence on Tibetan Buddhist practice. While many Tibetan masters have advocated vegetarianism, the harsh conditions of the Tibetan plateau, where agriculture is limited, made vegetarian practice difficult for most of Tibetan history. Recent decades have seen a growing movement toward vegetarianism among Tibetan Buddhist communities, led by figures such as the Seventeenth Karmapa Ogyen Trinley Dorje.

The Lankavatara's arguments against meat-eating resonate with the Tibetan Buddhist emphasis on compassion (snying rje) as the foundation of the bodhisattva path. The Karmapa has cited both the Lankavatara and the broader Mahayana teaching on universal compassion in his advocacy for vegetarianism, arguing that modern conditions make vegetarian practice feasible even for Tibetans.

The chapter's argument that meat-eating impedes meditation by generating a coarse mental state connects with the Tibetan yogic tradition's emphasis on dietary purity as a support for advanced meditation practice. Many retreat manuals (sgom rim) in the Tibetan tradition recommend vegetarian or even minimal diets during intensive meditation, suggesting that the relationship between diet and mental clarity has been practically recognized in the Tibetan tradition.
