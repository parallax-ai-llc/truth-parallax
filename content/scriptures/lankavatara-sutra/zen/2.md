The teaching that all phenomena are projections of consciousness is the philosophical foundation of Zen's radical emphasis on mind. When Bodhidharma told Emperor Wu that there was "vast emptiness, nothing holy," he was expressing the Lankavatara's teaching in four words. The emperor's temples, his charitable works, his devotion: all projections of consciousness. And the consciousness that projects them? Also empty.

The eight consciousnesses described in this chapter provide a map of the mind that Zen practice explores from the inside. In zazen, the practitioner encounters the five sense consciousnesses as they arise and pass. The mental consciousness weaves its narratives. The defiled mental consciousness (manas) whispers "I, me, mine." And beneath it all, the great ocean of the alaya-vijnana stirs with the waves of habitual tendencies.

Zen does not ask the practitioner to understand this map intellectually but to directly perceive the projective activity of consciousness in real time. When you sit in zazen and watch a thought arise, that thought is the alaya-vijnana manifesting a wave. When you see the thought without grasping it and let it dissolve, that is the beginning of the turning-about (paravrtti) that the sutra describes.
