The tathagatagarbha teaching of this chapter addresses the question that drives every Zen practitioner: if all beings possess buddha-nature, why are we not already awake? The Lankavatara's answer is that the alaya-vijnana, stirred by habitual tendencies accumulated over beginningless time, projects the appearance of a world of separate objects and a separate self, obscuring the tathagatagarbha that is its own deepest nature.

This is the same question that the Third Patriarch Sengcan addressed in his Faith in Mind (Xinxinming): "The great way is not difficult; it only avoids picking and choosing." The habitual tendencies that obscure buddha-nature are nothing other than the mind's habit of picking and choosing, preferring and rejecting, grasping and pushing away.

The chapter's teaching on self-realization (pratyatma-arya-jnana) is what Zen calls kensho: the direct seeing of one's own nature. This seeing cannot be transmitted through words because it is the recognition of the very awareness that reads these words. It is always already present, always already complete. The Lankavatara provides the philosophical framework; Zen provides the practice that makes it real.
