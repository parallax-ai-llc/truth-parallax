The verse chapter condenses the Lankavatara's teaching into pointed, memorable lines that function like a collection of koans. Each verse captures a facet of the sutra's vision: mind-only, buddha-nature, the transformation of consciousness, the inexpressibility of truth. A Zen practitioner can take any single verse and sit with it for a lifetime.

The poetic form itself teaches something about the relationship between language and truth. Poetry, like the koan, uses language against itself, employing rhythm, image, and paradox to point beyond conceptual content to direct experience. The Sagathakam's verses are not explanations of the Lankavatara's philosophy but evocations of the experience that the philosophy attempts to describe.

The final verses of the sutra bring us back to where we began: the mind itself, sitting on the island of Lanka, which is this very cushion, this very moment. The entire Lankavatara, with all its philosophical complexity, comes down to a single point: look at your own mind. What do you see? If you answer with words, you have missed it. If you remain silent, you have also missed it. What then? This is the koan the Lankavatara leaves us with, and it is the koan we sit with in every period of zazen.
