The chapter on meat-eating has had a significant impact on Zen practice, particularly in the Chinese Chan and Korean Seon traditions, where vegetarianism became normative for monastics. The Japanese Zen tradition, following different historical circumstances, generally does not require vegetarianism, though many Zen centers worldwide now emphasize plant-based diets as an expression of the bodhisattva's compassion.

From a Zen perspective, the question of meat-eating is not merely ethical but is intimately connected to the practice of awareness. What are you eating? Where did it come from? What suffering was involved in its production? These questions, asked with full attention, transform the act of eating from mindless consumption into a practice of engaged awareness.

The oryoki (formal Zen eating ceremony) embodies this awareness. Each movement is precise, each bite is received with gratitude, and the chant before the meal reminds the practitioner of the countless beings and conditions that made the food possible. Whether the meal includes meat or not, the practice of eating with full awareness is itself a profound expression of the Lankavatara's teaching on the interconnection of all beings.
