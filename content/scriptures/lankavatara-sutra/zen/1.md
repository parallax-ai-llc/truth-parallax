The Lankavatara Sutra opens on the island of Lanka, and from a Zen perspective, this island is the mind itself. The demon king Ravana's request for teaching is the deepest aspiration of consciousness seeking to understand its own nature. Before Bodhidharma brought this sutra to China, before he sat facing a wall for nine years, this text was waiting to become the seed of Zen.

According to tradition, Bodhidharma transmitted the Lankavatara Sutra to his successor Huike as the essential text for practice. This makes the Lankavatara the first dharma text of the Chan lineage, the sutra that the founder considered most essential for understanding the nature of mind. Before the Diamond Sutra rose to prominence through Huineng, the Lankavatara defined what Chan was about.

The opening chapter's establishment of Mahamati as the questioner sets a tone that resonates with Zen practice. Mahamati does not seek intellectual understanding; he seeks the direct realization of the noble wisdom that the Buddha embodies. This orientation toward lived realization rather than conceptual knowledge is the heart of both the Lankavatara and the Zen tradition it helped to birth.
