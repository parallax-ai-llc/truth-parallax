The three natures taught in this chapter provide a Zen practitioner with a precise tool for understanding delusion and awakening. The imagined nature is the layer of conceptual overlay that we project onto experience: "This is good, this is bad, this is me, this is not me." The dependent nature is experience as it actually flows, moment to moment, arising and passing. The perfected nature is the dependent nature seen without projection.

In zazen, these three natures are not philosophical abstractions but direct experiences. When you sit and the mind narrates, judges, and categorizes, that is the imagined nature at work. When you let go of the narrative and simply sit with what is, you encounter the dependent nature: sensations arising and passing, breath coming and going, sounds appearing and disappearing. And in moments of clear, open awareness, free from all commentary, the perfected nature reveals itself.

The chapter's teaching on dependent origination from the mind-only perspective reinforces the Zen insight that the chain of causation that binds us to suffering is not an external cosmic process but the activity of our own consciousness. Breaking the chain is not a cosmic event but an intimate one: the moment-to-moment choice to see through the mind's projections.
