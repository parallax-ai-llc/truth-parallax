The non-duality of samsara and nirvana taught in this chapter is the philosophical basis of the Zen insistence that ordinary life is itself the practice ground. If nirvana is not separate from samsara, then awakening is not separate from delusion, and the monastery is not separate from the marketplace. This is what Zen means when it says, "Before enlightenment, chop wood, carry water. After enlightenment, chop wood, carry water."

The teaching that the noble wisdom of self-realization cannot be expressed in words is the justification for Zen's "special transmission outside the scriptures." If the highest truth transcends language, then it cannot be transmitted through sutras alone but requires the direct encounter between teacher and student, mind to mind, in the living moment.

Yet here is the paradox that defines Zen's relationship to the Lankavatara: the sutra uses thousands of words to teach that words cannot capture the truth. Zen's response to this paradox has been to develop methods, koans, mondo, hua tou, shikantaza, that use words and concepts to point beyond words and concepts, like using a thorn to remove a thorn.
