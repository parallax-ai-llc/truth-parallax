The dharani chapter may seem out of place in a sutra that emphasizes direct realization beyond words, but from a Zen perspective, dharanis function in a way similar to mantras and koans: they concentrate the mind on a single point, breaking through the discursive thinking that ordinarily dominates awareness.

In Zen practice, the repetition of a single phrase, whether a dharani, a mantra like "Namu Amida Butsu," or a koan like "Mu," serves to exhaust the thinking mind's attempts to find conceptual resolution. When the thinking mind gives up, something else can emerge: the direct perception of reality that the Lankavatara calls pratyatma-arya-jnana (self-realization of noble wisdom).

The protective function of dharanis also resonates with the Zen understanding that practice creates a field of awareness that naturally protects the practitioner from harmful states of mind. When attention is fully engaged with the present moment, there is no room for the habitual patterns of anxiety, craving, and aversion that cause suffering. The dharani's protection is not magical but psychological: the concentrated mind is inherently protected from its own projections.
