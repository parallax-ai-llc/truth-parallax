The ocean metaphor that appears in this chapter has become one of the most important images in Zen teaching. The mind is like a great ocean; thoughts are like waves. The winds of habitual tendencies stir the surface into turbulence, but the depths remain undisturbed. Zazen is the practice of settling into the depths while the waves continue to arise at the surface.

The chapter's teaching on the transformation of consciousness (paravrtti) describes what happens in the moment of kensho. The practitioner does not acquire something new but sees what has always been present. The ocean does not become the ocean through the calming of waves; it was always the ocean, even in the midst of the wildest storm. Kensho is not the creation of buddha-nature but its recognition.

This recognition is what Zen means by "seeing one's original face before one's parents were born." The question points beneath the accumulated layers of conditioning, habit, and identity to the awareness that precedes all of them. The Lankavatara provides the philosophical vocabulary for describing this awareness; zazen provides the method for encountering it directly.
