The chapter on change speaks directly to the Zen practitioner's experience on the cushion and in daily life. Everything changes. The breath changes, the thoughts change, the sensations change, the seasons change, the body ages and dissolves. Zen practice is the thorough realization of this change, not as a philosophical position but as lived reality.

The transformation described in this chapter is not merely the impermanence of external things but the transformation of the consciousness that perceives them. In Zen, this is the shift from seeing the world as a collection of fixed objects to experiencing reality as a continuous flow of interdependent arising. Dogen called this "the dropping off of body and mind" (shinjin datsuraku).

The practical question for the Zen practitioner is: how does this transformation actually occur? The Lankavatara teaches that it happens through the cessation of habitual projections. Zen translates this into practice: sit still, let the projections arise without grasping them, and eventually the habit of projection exhausts itself. What remains is the mind's own luminous nature, which was never absent but only unrecognized.
